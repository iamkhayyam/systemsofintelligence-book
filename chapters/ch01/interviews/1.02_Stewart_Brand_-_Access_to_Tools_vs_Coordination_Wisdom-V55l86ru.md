# Interview 2: Stewart Brand
## "Access to Tools vs. Coordination Wisdom"

**Expert:** Stewart Brand, Whole Earth Catalog, Long Now Foundation  
**Duration:** 42 minutes  
**Location:** Long Now Foundation, San Francisco  
**Interview Date:** [TBD]

---

## Pre-Interview Context

### Expert Background
- Creator of Whole Earth Catalog (1968-1972, 1980-1984)
- Co-founder of The WELL (early online community)
- Co-founder of Long Now Foundation (thinking in 10,000-year timeframes)
- Author: "How Buildings Learn," "The Clock of the Long Now," "Whole Earth Discipline"
- Photographer for NASA (early Earth from space images)

### Key Concepts to Explore
- "Access to tools" philosophy and its evolution
- Community as coordination layer
- Long-term thinking vs. short-term optimization
- Infrastructure vs. crisis
- Pace layering (Fast/Slow framework)

### Interview Objectives
1. Show evolution from "access to tools" to "coordination wisdom"
2. Use Whole Earth Catalog as historical coordination example
3. Contrast with Facebook as coordination failure
4. Introduce long-term thinking as coordination with future
5. Provide practical vision for coordination infrastructure

## ACT I: THE WHOLE EARTH INSIGHT
**Duration: 10 minutes**

### SEGMENT 1: Creating the Catalog (4 min)

**[00:30 - 00:45 - Visual: A-CAM on Brand, looking at the interviewer, then slightly off-camera as he recalls. Gentle, warm lighting.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let's go back to 1968. You're 29 years old, and you create the Whole Earth Catalog. What was the vision?"

**BRAND:**
"Well, I'd just come back from working with NASA, and I'd seen something that changed everything for me—the Earth from space. This tiny blue marble, all of us on it together. It was a profound shift in perspective.

**[00:45 - 01:00 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand's face, a slight smile. Then C-CAM, wide shot, showing Brand and the Long Now space. Insert: NASA 'Earthrise' photo or 'Blue Marble' photo.]**

The environmental movement was starting to bubble up, but it was mostly about protest—'stop this, ban that.' I wanted something generative: 'here are tools to build the world you want.' A positive, proactive approach.

**[01:00 - 01:30 - Visual: A-CAM on Brand. He gestures slightly with his hands as he speaks.]**

So the Whole Earth Catalog was born. The subtitle said it all: 'Access to Tools.' If it helped you be self-sufficient, learn something new, or simply *do* something yourself—whether that was building a house, growing food, or understanding complex systems—we reviewed it. We wanted to empower individuals."

**[01:30 - 01:45 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What kind of tools were you featuring?"

**BRAND:**
"Everything! [Chuckles softly] We had camping gear, calculators—the HP-35 was a big deal, a slide rule in your pocket!—how-to books on everything from organic farming to geodesic dome construction, educational materials, even early synthesizers. We weren't selling the tools ourselves, mind you—we were pointing you to them, providing critical reviews, and saying 'this works, this doesn't, here's why.' It was about informed choice."

**[01:45 - 02:30 - Visual: Insert Cam: Close-ups of original Whole Earth Catalog pages. We see reviews for geodesic domes, a detailed spread on gardening tools, an early HP calculator, perhaps a book on cybernetics. The pages are well-worn, authentic. A-CAM returns to Brand.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"It became the bible of the counterculture. Why do you think it resonated so deeply?"

**BRAND:**
"Because it wasn't just products. It was permission. Permission to build your own life, to think for yourself, to trust your own judgment, to experiment. It was saying, 'You don't have to wait for institutions to solve your problems; here are the means to solve them yourself.'

**[02:30 - 03:00 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, a thoughtful expression. He pauses briefly.]**

Steve Jobs famously said it was like Google in paperback form—35 years before Google. And he was right, in a way. But it was more than just search. It was curated wisdom, filtered through a particular sensibility, a particular set of values."

**[03:00 - 03:30 - Visual: A-CAM on Brand, then C-CAM showing a stack of various WEC editions on a nearby table.]**

### SEGMENT 2: The Coordination Surprise (3 min)

**[03:30 - 03:45 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"But the catalog became more than just reviews. What happened that surprised you?"

**BRAND:**
"The surprise was the letters. We'd publish reviews, and readers wouldn't just buy the tools; they'd write back to us. And we published those letters. They'd say things like:

**[03:45 - 04:30 - Visual: Insert Cam: Close-ups of actual user letters and responses published in the Catalog. Highlight the handwritten or typed nature, the direct address. A-CAM on Brand, animated as he describes the letters.]**

- 'I tried that dome design—here's what I learned when the snow load was too heavy.'
- 'I modified this particular tool—here's the improvement I made, and why.'
- 'I'm trying to solve this problem with composting toilets—anyone have ideas or experience?'

And then, other readers would respond to *those* letters. Conversations emerged across the country—people learning from each other, teaching each other, coordinating their efforts and knowledge."

**[04:30 - 04:45 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, leaning in.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the community became the product?"

**BRAND:**
"The community became the coordination layer! That's the key insight.

**[04:45 - 05:15 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, emphasizing his point. He gestures with his hands.]**

The catalog gave you access to tools—that's individual capability. But the letters, the dialogue, the shared experience—that gave you access to collective wisdom. What actually works in practice, what problems people hit, what solutions they discovered. That's coordination: learning together, not just individually."

**[05:15 - 05:30 - Visual: A-CAM on Brand, a slight, knowing smile.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Did you design it that way?"

**BRAND:**
[Chuckles, a wry smile] "No! We thought we were making a product catalog. What we accidentally created was a coordination platform. The coordination emerged from the structure—tools, reviews, letters, and the community that formed around it. It was an emergent property."

**[05:30 - 05:45 - Production note: This is the key insight—pause to let it land. Visual: C-CAM, wide shot of Brand, then a slow zoom into a highlighted section of a WEC page showing letters.]**

### SEGMENT 3: The Personal Computer Revolution (3 min)

**[05:45 - 06:00 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The catalog ended in 1984—the same year the Macintosh launched. Coincidence?"

**BRAND:**
"Not entirely. The personal computer, in many ways, fulfilled the Whole Earth vision in hardware. Steve Jobs literally said the Whole Earth Catalog inspired Apple. The Mac was a tool for individual empowerment—a 'bicycle for the mind,' as he put it—exactly what we championed. It was about giving individuals incredible power."

**[06:00 - 06:30 - Visual: Insert Cam: Archival footage of Steve Jobs introducing the Macintosh, or an early Mac commercial. A-CAM on Brand, nodding.]**

"But here's what we didn't anticipate: when everyone has incredibly powerful tools, coordination becomes the bottleneck."

**[06:30 - 06:45 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, a slight furrow in his brow.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What do you mean by 'coordination becomes the bottleneck'?"

**BRAND:**
"In 1968, most people had limited tools. Giving them access to better tools was revolutionary—it increased their capability dramatically. By 2000, everyone had powerful computers, then the internet. The limitation wasn't tool access anymore—it was coordination. How do we work together effectively? How do we learn collectively at scale? How do we make decisions together? We never built that infrastructure."

**[06:45 - 07:30 - Visual: A-CAM on Brand, then a quick montage of early internet interfaces (AOL, GeoCities) followed by the rise of social media logos. Music becomes slightly more urgent.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And then social media promised to solve that connection problem..."

**BRAND:**
"And made it worse. Because they optimized for connection, for engagement, for attention—not for coordination. They gave everyone a megaphone, but no one a conductor's baton."

**[07:30 - 07:45 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, a look of concern. He pauses, letting the thought hang.]**

## ACT II: THE COORDINATION CRISIS
**Duration: 22 minutes**

### SEGMENT 4: Facebook vs. Whole Earth (6 min)

**[07:45 - 08:00 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let's talk about Facebook specifically. You've called it 'access to attention without coordination wisdom.' Explain that distinction."

**BRAND:**
"Okay, let's compare the Whole Earth Catalog to Facebook directly. It's a useful contrast:

**[08:00 - 08:30 - Visual: C-CAM, Brand stands and walks to a large whiteboard or screen. He picks up a marker. Graphics begin to appear on screen/whiteboard, showing two columns: 'Whole Earth Catalog' and 'Facebook'.]**

**Whole Earth Catalog:**
- **Individual:** It gave you access to tools—books, equipment, knowledge. It amplified your personal capability.
- **Collective:** Through the letters section, it fostered community learning. People shared experiences, problems, solutions. That's coordination.
- **Cultural:** It had a clear point of view, a set of values: self-sufficiency, ecological awareness, appropriate technology, long-term thinking.
- **Coordination:** All three of these elements worked together. It was a coherent system."

**[08:30 - 09:30 - Visual: Brand points to the 'Whole Earth Catalog' column on the whiteboard. Graphics animate to highlight each point as he speaks. Then he moves to the 'Facebook' column.]**

"Now, **Facebook:**
- **Individual:** It gives you access to attention. You can broadcast to everyone, share your thoughts, your life. It amplifies your personal voice.
- **Collective:** ??? [Brand draws a question mark on the whiteboard] What's the coordination mechanism? There isn't one, really. It's designed for viral spread, for engagement, for reaction—not for shared understanding or collective action.
- **Cultural:** ??? [Brand draws another question mark] What are its core values, beyond growth and engagement? It's largely value-neutral, or rather, it reflects *all* values, which means it coordinates none.
- **Coordination:** Failed. It's connection without coordination. It's a megaphone without a meeting agenda."

**[09:30 - 09:45 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"But Facebook has groups, events, pages—isn't that coordination?"

**BRAND:**
"Those are connection tools, not coordination tools. There's a crucial difference:

**[09:45 - 10:30 - Visual: Brand returns to the whiteboard, drawing a Venn diagram or two distinct circles: 'Connection' and 'Coordination'. He points to them.]**

**Connection:** You can communicate with people. You can find people who think like you. You can share information.
**Coordination:** You can achieve shared understanding. You can make collective decisions. You can take collective action towards a common goal.

Facebook enables connection, yes. But the algorithm optimizes for engagement, for keeping your eyes on the screen, not for coordination. So what do you get?
- Echo chambers: coordination *within* tribes, but not *across* them.
- Viral spread: connection without understanding, often leading to misinformation.
- Performative outrage: engagement without learning, just reaction.

Connection is not coordination. It's a prerequisite, but it's not the thing itself."

**[10:30 - 11:00 - Visual: C-CAM on Brand and the whiteboard, showing the distinction clearly. He looks at the camera, making eye contact. Music becomes slightly more serious.]**

### SEGMENT 5: The Infrastructure Test (6 min)

**[11:00 - 11:15 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand, who is still by the whiteboard.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How do you know if something is true infrastructure or just a crisis waiting to happen? Is there a test?"

**BRAND:**
"Yes! I think there are three questions you can ask. Let's call it the 'Infrastructure Test.'

**[11:15 - 11:30 - Visual: Brand writes 'The Infrastructure Test' at the top of the whiteboard. Graphics appear on screen.]**

**Question 1: Does it enable coordination toward flourishing?**
- Take roads: Yes. They coordinate movement between places, enabling commerce, social interaction, getting people where they need to go. That's flourishing.
- Traffic jams: No. That's connection without coordination. Everyone's on the road, but nobody's moving efficiently. It's a failure of coordination.
- Facebook: No. It connects people, but does it reliably coordinate them toward collective flourishing? Often, it seems to do the opposite—polarization, anxiety, division."

**[11:30 - 12:30 - Visual: Brand points to the first question. Graphics show icons for roads (green check) and traffic jams (red X). Then a Facebook logo (red X). He moves to the second question.]**

"**Question 2: Does it work across timeframes?**
- An electricity grid: Yes. It coordinates power generation and consumption second-by-second, but it's also designed to last for decades, coordinating future demand with present investment.
- Flash crashes in markets: No. That's when coordination fails across timeframes, when short-term algorithms destabilize long-term market health.
- Social media: No. It optimizes for 'now'—quarterly earnings, daily active users, real-time engagement. It actively damages long-term attention spans, civic discourse, and mental health. It's a coordination failure with the future."

**[12:30 - 13:30 - Visual: Brand points to the second question. Graphics show icons for power grid (green check) and market crash (red X). Then a social media icon (red X). He moves to the third question.]**

"**Question 3: Does it coordinate diverse participants?**
- Language: Yes. It coordinates meaning between vastly different minds, allowing us to build shared realities.
- Miscommunication: No. That's a failure to coordinate meaning.
- Markets: Yes. They coordinate supply and demand between millions of buyers and sellers, often with conflicting interests, towards a collective outcome.
- Monopolies: No. When one party controls everything, there's no true coordination; there's just control.
- Facebook: No. It coordinates within filter bubbles, within echo chambers. It struggles to coordinate across difference, often amplifying division rather than bridging it."

**[13:30 - 14:30 - Visual: Brand points to the third question. Graphics show icons for language (green check) and miscommunication (red X). Then a Facebook logo (red X). He steps back from the whiteboard, looking at the camera.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"By this test, Facebook is definitely crisis, not infrastructure."

**BRAND:**
"Absolutely. And here's the scary part: we're treating it like infrastructure. We're building society on top of a coordination crisis.

**[14:30 - 15:00 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, his expression serious.]**

Imagine building a city on top of a traffic jam. That's what we're doing with democracy, with education, with public health—building on platforms that inherently create coordination failure. It's a profound misallocation of our collective effort."

**[15:00 - 15:15 - Production note: This is the alarming realization. Visual: C-CAM, wide shot of Brand and the whiteboard, then a slow zoom out to emphasize the scale of the problem. Music becomes more somber.]**

### SEGMENT 6: The Three Bodies of Technology (5 min)

**[15:15 - 15:30 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand, who gestures to the whiteboard.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've talked about the 'three bodies' of technology. Break down what's actually needed to build true coordination infrastructure."

**BRAND:**
"Right. We tend to focus on just one part. But there are three essential 'bodies' that need to be coordinated.

**[15:30 - 15:45 - Visual: Brand draws three large, interconnected circles on the whiteboard. He labels the first circle.]**

**Body 1: Individual Capability (Tools)**
"This is what we've built for 50 years, and we're incredibly good at it:
- Personal computers: They amplify individual thinking.
- Smartphones: They amplify individual connection and access to information.
- Apps: They amplify individual productivity, creativity, communication.

**[15:45 - 16:30 - Visual: Brand points to the first circle. Graphics appear, showing icons for PCs, smartphones, apps. He then labels the second circle.]**

Moore's Law, exponential growth, tools everywhere. We've mastered this. But it's only one leg of the stool."

**Body 2: Collective Coordination (Wisdom)**
"This is what we forgot to build, or at least, didn't prioritize:
- How do we coordinate toward shared goals, not just individual ones?
- How do we learn collectively what's worth doing, what actually works?
- How do we make decisions together at scale, with diverse perspectives?

**[16:30 - 17:15 - Visual: Brand points to the second circle. Graphics appear, showing abstract representations of collective learning, shared goals, decision-making. He then labels the third circle.]**

We have almost no robust infrastructure for this. Social media gives us connection, but not coordination."

**Body 3: Cultural Values (Purpose)**
"And this is what we often lost sight of entirely:
- What's worth amplifying? What are we actually building towards?
- What timeframe matters? Are we optimizing for now, or for the long term?
- What kind of future are we trying to create?

**[17:15 - 18:00 - Visual: Brand points to the third circle. Graphics appear, showing concepts like generational impact, long-term vision, collective flourishing. He then draws arrows connecting all three circles.]**

We optimized for quarterly profits, for immediate engagement, not for generational flourishing. That's a failure of purpose."

**BRAND:**
"Real infrastructure coordinates all three:
- Tools enable individual capability.
- Coordination enables collective action and learning.
- Values guide both toward a meaningful, flourishing future.

**[18:00 - 18:45 - Visual: C-CAM, wide shot of Brand and the complete diagram on the whiteboard, showing the interconnectedness. He looks at the camera.]**

Social media gave us powerful tools without robust coordination or clear, long-term values. That's why it's a crisis. It's a two-legged stool trying to stand."

## ACT III: BUILDING COORDINATION WISDOM
**Duration: 10 minutes**

### SEGMENT 9: Long-Term Thinking as Coordination (4 min)

**[24:45 - 25:00 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then cut to Brand (A-CAM) walking towards the 10,000-year clock prototype, placing a hand on it.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You founded the Long Now Foundation to think in 10,000-year timeframes. How does long-term thinking relate to coordination?"

**BRAND:**
"Long-term thinking *is* coordination with the future. Most technology, most of our economy, optimizes for 'now'—quarterly earnings, daily active users, real-time engagement. This is, by definition, a coordination failure with future generations."

**[25:00 - 25:45 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand's hand on the clock mechanism. C-CAM, wide shot of Brand and the impressive clock prototype. He looks at the clock thoughtfully.]**

"We're building a clock that will tick for 10,000 years. Why? To create a coordination point between present and future. It's a monument to long-term responsibility.

When you start thinking in centuries, or millennia:
- You can't optimize for quarterly profits. That becomes absurd.
- You *must* coordinate with generations you'll never meet, whose problems you can only dimly imagine.
- You start asking fundamentally different questions: What's worth building that lasts? What knowledge is truly enduring? What actions today will resonate positively for a hundred, a thousand, ten thousand years?

**[25:45 - 26:45 - Visual: A-CAM on Brand, looking directly at the camera, then back to the clock. Graphics appear, showing a timeline stretching far into the future.]**

This is coordination wisdom: coordinating present actions with long-term consequences. It's about expanding our circle of responsibility across time."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How does this apply to AI and social media today?"

**BRAND:**
"Current platforms optimize for engagement today. Coordination platforms, built with a Long Now perspective, would optimize for flourishing across generations.

**[26:45 - 27:30 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, his expression earnest.]**

The questions change entirely:
- Not: How do we maximize time on site?
- But: How do we help humans coordinate toward long-term goals, like climate stability or intergenerational equity?

- Not: How do we increase daily active users?
- But: How do we create coordination that serves children we'll never meet, ensuring they inherit a viable, thriving planet?

Different optimization, radically different outcomes. It's the difference between a quick sugar rush and building a healthy body."

**[27:30 - 28:00 - Visual: C-CAM, wide shot of Brand and the clock, emphasizing the scale of time. Music becomes more expansive and hopeful.]**

### SEGMENT 10: The Coordination Tools We Need (3 min)

**[28:00 - 28:15 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand, who has moved slightly away from the clock, facing the camera.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Practically, what specific coordination tools should we be building right now?"

**BRAND:**
"We need to shift our focus from individual amplification to collective coordination. Here are three categories:

**[28:15 - 28:30 - Visual: Brand gestures with his hands. Graphics appear on screen, listing the categories.]**

**1. Long-Term Thinking Platforms:**
"Tools that explicitly coordinate the present with the future:
- Systems that show the long-term consequences of short-term actions, making those impacts visible and tangible.
- Platforms that enable decision-making across timeframes, allowing us to 'negotiate' with future generations.
- Tools that connect generations through shared, enduring projects, like the clock itself."

**[28:30 - 29:15 - Visual: Brand points to the first category. Graphics show visualizations of future impacts, intergenerational project timelines.]**

**2. Collective Sense-Making Tools:**
"Tools that coordinate diverse perspectives, not just echo chambers:
- Platforms designed to help people understand opposing viewpoints, not just argue them.
- Systems that facilitate coordination across difference, helping 'enemies' find common ground and shared goals.
- Tools that synthesize vast amounts of information into collective wisdom, not just noise."

**[29:15 - 30:00 - Visual: Brand points to the second category. Graphics show network diagrams where diverse nodes are connecting, visualizations of shared understanding emerging from disparate data.]**

**3. Wisdom Amplification Systems:**
"Tools that coordinate knowledge with judgment, with *wisdom*:
- Not just more information access—we're drowning in that.
- But coordination of information with human judgment, with ethical frameworks, with historical context.
- Helping people learn what's truly worth knowing, what's enduring, what leads to flourishing."

**[30:00 - 30:45 - Visual: Brand points to the third category. Graphics show knowledge graphs, curated learning paths, systems that highlight ethical considerations. He looks at the interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"These sound utopian. Can they actually be built?"

**BRAND:**
"They're harder than engagement optimization, yes. Much harder. But that's precisely the point. Easy optimization gave us the coordination crisis. Hard coordination will give us infrastructure that lasts, that serves humanity for the long haul."

**[30:45 - 31:15 - Visual: B-CAM, close-up on Brand, a determined look. Music becomes more resolute.]**

### SEGMENT 11: The Next 50 Years (3 min)

**[31:15 - 31:30 - Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, then back to Brand, who looks out thoughtfully, perhaps towards a window or the clock.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've seen 50 years of incredible technological change. What does coordination wisdom look like 50 years from now? What's at stake?"

**BRAND:**
"We stand at a critical juncture. There are two very distinct possibilities for the next 50 years:

**[31:30 - 31:45 - Visual: Brand gestures with his hands, indicating two paths. Graphics appear on screen, showing 'Path 1' and 'Path 2'.]**

**Path 1: Coordination Failure**
- We keep optimizing tools without building coordination infrastructure.
- Individual capability keeps increasing exponentially—AI, biotech, you name it.
- But collective coordination keeps failing, leading to greater polarization, more intractable global problems, and eventually...
- Eventually: Collapse from a coordination crisis. We become too powerful to coordinate, and we destroy ourselves."

**[31:45 - 32:30 - Visual: Brand points to 'Path 1'. Graphics show a chaotic, fragmented future, perhaps a broken network. His expression is somber.]**

**Path 2: Coordination Wisdom**
- We learn to build robust coordination infrastructure.
- Individual capability coordinates seamlessly with collective wisdom.
- Both coordinate with long-term, generational values.
- Eventually: A flourishing we can't yet imagine. A truly global, wise civilization capable of solving its biggest challenges."

**[32:30 - 33:15 - Visual: Brand points to 'Path 2'. Graphics show a harmonious, interconnected future, thriving ecosystems, collaborative human endeavors. His expression brightens slightly.]**

### SEGMENT 5: The Infrastructure Test (6 min)

**[11:15 - 11:30 - Visual: Brand writes 'The Infrastructure Test' at the top of the whiteboard. Graphics appear on screen.]**

**Question 1: Does it enable coordination toward flourishing?**
- Take roads: Yes. They coordinate movement between places, enabling commerce, social interaction, getting people where they need to go. That's flourishing.
- Traffic jams: No. That's connection without coordination. Everyone's on the road, but nobody's moving efficiently. It's a failure of coordination.
- Facebook: No. It connects people, but does it reliably coordinate them toward collective flourishing? Often, it seems to do the opposite—polarization, anxiety, division."

- Social media: No. It optimizes for 'now'—quarterly earnings, daily active users, real-time engagement. It actively damages long-term attention spans, civic discourse, and mental health. It's a coordination failure with the future."

**[12:30 - 13:30 - Visual: Brand points to the second question. Graphics show icons for power grid (green check) and market crash (red X). Then a social media icon (red X). He moves to the third question.]**

- Monopolies: No. When one party controls everything, there's no true coordination; there's just control.
- Facebook: No. It coordinates within filter bubbles, within echo chambers. It struggles to coordinate across difference, often amplifying division rather than bridging it."

## INTERVIEWER'S QUESTION CARDS

### Act I: The Whole Earth Insight
1. "Let's go back to 1968. You're 29 years old, and you create the Whole Earth Catalog. What was the vision?"
2. "What kind of tools?"

4. "But the catalog became more than reviews. What happened?"
5. "So the community became the product?"
6. "Did you design it that way?"

8. "What do you mean?"
9. "And then social media promised to solve it..."

### Act II: The Coordination Crisis
10. "Let's talk about Facebook specifically. You've called it 'access to attention without coordination wisdom.' Explain."
11. "But Facebook has groups, events, pages—isn't that coordination?"
12. "How do you know if something is true infrastructure or just a crisis waiting to happen? Is there a test?"
13. "By this test, Facebook is definitely crisis, not infrastructure."
14. "You've talked about the 'three bodies' of technology. Break down what's actually needed to build true coordination infrastructure."
15. "Looking back, how did the Whole Earth Catalog coordinate these three bodies—individual capability, collective coordination, and cultural values?"
16. "If you were building the Whole Earth Catalog today, for the internet age, what would that coordination infrastructure look like?"
17. "Does anything like this exist today?"

### Act III: The Long Now
18. "You founded the Long Now Foundation to think in 10,000-year timeframes. How does long-term thinking relate to coordination?"
19. "How does this apply to AI and social media?"
20. "Practically, what specific coordination tools should we be building right now?"
21. "These sound utopian. Can they actually be built?"
22. "You've seen 50 years of incredible technological change. What does coordination wisdom look like 50 years from now? What's at stake?"
