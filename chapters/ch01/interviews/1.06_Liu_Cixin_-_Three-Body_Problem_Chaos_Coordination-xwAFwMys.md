## ACT I: THE METAPHOR BECOMES REAL
**Duration: 12 minutes**

### SEGMENT 1: Writing the Novel (4 min)

**[00:30 - 00:45 INTERVIEWER (warm, engaging):**
"Mr. Liu, *The Three-Body Problem* became a global phenomenon, captivating millions with its unique blend of hard science and profound philosophy. What initially inspired you to write such an ambitious story?"

**[00:45 - 01:00 LIU (leaning forward slightly, a glint in his eye, translated):**
"Thank you. It began quite simply, actually. I was working as a computer engineer at a power plant in China. Very routine work, you might say. But my mind was often elsewhere, fascinated by a particular physics problem I'd learned about many years ago: the three-body problem."

**[01:00 - 01:30 Visual: A clean, elegant simulation of a two-body system (e.g., Earth and Moon) orbiting perfectly. Text overlay: "Two-Body Problem: Predictable." Camera: Close-up on Liu, then cut to simulation.]**

**LIU:**
"You see, with two bodies—like Earth and its Moon, or a star and a single planet—you can predict their orbits perfectly. Isaac Newton solved this 300 years ago. It's beautiful, deterministic, predictable. A clockwork universe."

**[01:30 - 02:00 Visual: Transition to a three-body orbital simulation. Three stars of different colors (e.g., red, blue, yellow) begin orbiting chaotically. Their paths are complex, sometimes stable for a moment, then suddenly one is flung out, or they crash. Text overlay: "Three-Body Problem: Unpredictable Chaos." Camera: Wide shot of Liu, then cut to simulation.]**

**LIU:**
"But introduce a third body—three stars mutually affecting each other with their gravity—and everything changes. Chaos. You cannot solve it analytically. You cannot predict where they'll be in the long term. The system is deterministic, yes, governed by precise laws, but fundamentally unpredictable in its outcomes."

**[02:00 - 02:30 LIU (gesturing with his hands, illustrating the complexity):**
"And I thought: This is a perfect metaphor. Not just for physics, but for what happens when civilizations encounter complexity they can't control. When three major forces interact, predictability vanishes."

**[02:30 - 02:45 INTERVIEWER:**
"Why use a metaphor, Mr. Liu? Why not just write directly about civilizations and their interactions?"

**[02:45 - 03:30 LIU (thoughtful, looking off-camera for a moment, then back):**
"Because the physics makes it visceral. You can *see* the chaos. You can *feel* the unpredictability in a way that abstract political or social theory might not convey. It grounds the philosophical in the concrete."

**[03:30 - 04:00 Visual: Return to the three-body simulation, perhaps showing a particularly dramatic moment where one body is ejected or a near-collision occurs. Camera: Focus on the simulation, then a slow zoom out to Liu, who watches it on a monitor in his study.]**

**LIU:**
"Watch it again: The orbits are chaotic. Sometimes stable for a while, a brief period of order, then suddenly one body is ejected, or two collide. No warning. No prediction possible. This is what happens when three civilizations interact. Or, as we are discovering, when humans, AI, and planetary systems coordinate. Unpredictable. Potentially catastrophic. But not random—there are patterns, if you know how to look."

**[04:15 - 04:45 LIU (a serious expression now, his gaze direct, translated):**

**LIU:**

**Body 1: Human Civilization**

- Eight billion people, trying to coordinate, trying to survive and flourish."

**[05:00 - 05:15 Visual: "Body 2: Artificial Intelligence" appears on the diagram. The sphere for AI pulses with energy. Camera: Cut back to Liu, then diagram.]**

**LIU:**
"**Body 2: Artificial Intelligence**
- Growing capability, learning systems, increasingly autonomous.
- It is already affecting human decisions, human economies, human culture. It is created by us, but its trajectory is becoming its own."

**[05:15 - 05:30 Visual: "Body 3: Planetary Systems" appears on the diagram. The sphere for the planet shows swirling weather patterns and green ecosystems. Camera: Cut back to Liu, then diagram.]**

**LIU:**
"**Body 3: Planetary Systems**
- Our climate, our ecosystems, our finite resources.
- The planet is responding to human and AI activity, and in turn, it constrains what is possible for both of us."

**[05:30 - 06:00 Visual: The diagram now shows arrows connecting all three spheres, indicating mutual influence. The arrows pulse and shift. Camera: Focus on the diagram, then a slow pan to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"Each affects the other two, constantly:
- Humans develop AI, and AI changes human behavior, our very cognition.
- Humans affect the planet, and the planet constrains humans with its limits.

- And the planet changes, affecting both human civilization and AI's operational environment."

**[06:00 - 06:30 LIU (gesturing to the interconnectedness):**
"This is the three-body problem—not in physics, but in reality. A complex, dynamic system where we are one of the bodies."

**[06:30 - 06:45 INTERVIEWER:**
"And like the physics problem, you're suggesting we can't predict the outcome of this interaction?"

**[06:45 - 07:30 LIU (nodding slowly, a grave look):**

- How AI will truly evolve, what emergent properties it will develop.
- How the climate will respond to our interventions, what tipping points lie ahead.
- How humans will adapt to these pressures, whether we will unite or fracture.
- And crucially, how all three will coordinate, or fail to coordinate, in the long term."

**[07:30 - 08:00 LIU (a slight pause, then a shift in tone, more resolute):**
"But—and this is crucial—unpredictable doesn't mean unnavigable. It means we must change our approach."

### SEGMENT 3: Unpredictable vs. Unnavigable (4 min)

**[08:00 - 08:15 INTERVIEWER:**
"That's a critical distinction. If we can't predict the future of this three-body system, how do we navigate it? What does 'unnavigable' truly mean in this context?"

**[08:15 - 08:45 LIU (a thoughtful expression, looking into the distance as if recalling a memory, translated):**
"In my novel, the Trisolarans—the aliens from a three-body system—live under constant existential threat. They can't predict when their suns will rise or set, when a 'Stable Era' will give way to a 'Chaotic Era.' Civilizations are destroyed by unpredictable climate shifts, by extreme heat or cold."

**[08:45 - 09:15 Visual: Scenes from a fictional adaptation of *The Three-Body Problem* showing Trisolaran cities being destroyed by extreme heat or cold, then quickly rebuilding. Focus on their resilience. Camera: Cut to these scenes, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"Chaos reigns. Yet, they survive. They survive by learning to navigate chaos, not by predicting it.
- They can't predict when extreme heat will come—but they develop the ability to dehydrate their bodies, becoming inert, to survive the cold, and rehydrate when conditions allow.
- They can't predict when stability will last—but they learn to rebuild their civilizations with astonishing speed and efficiency.
- They can't solve the three-body problem—but they learn to *dance* with it, to adapt to its rhythms."

**[09:15 - 09:45 LIU (gesturing emphatically):**
"The lesson for us is profound: Stop trying to predict every outcome. Stop trying to control every variable. Start learning to coordinate with the system, to adapt to its inherent unpredictability."

**[09:45 - 10:30 Visual: A split screen: on one side, a chaotic three-body simulation; on the other, a visualization of human cities adapting to climate change (e.g., sea walls, vertical farms) and AI systems collaborating with humans in complex tasks. Camera: Focus on the split screen, then a slow zoom to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"For us, this means:
- We can't predict the exact path of AI development—but we can coordinate with its emergence, guiding its values and ensuring its alignment.
- We can't predict precise climate tipping points—but we can coordinate adaptation strategies, building resilience and mitigating impact.
- We can't predict the future—but we can participate consciously in creating it, not as masters of a machine, but as dancers in a complex, living system."

**[10:30 - 11:00 LIU (a slight smile, a sense of quiet determination):**
"The shift is fundamental: From prediction to coordination. From control to participation. From solving to dancing. It is a more humble, but ultimately more effective, approach to complexity."

## ACT II: THE DARK FOREST AND COORDINATION FAILURE
**Duration: 24 minutes**

### SEGMENT 4: The Dark Forest Hypothesis (6 min)

**[11:00 - 11:15 INTERVIEWER:**
"One of the most chilling and thought-provoking concepts from your trilogy is the 'Dark Forest hypothesis'—the idea that civilizations hide because revealing yourself is an existential risk. Can you explain the logic behind this cosmic paranoia?"

**[11:15 - 11:45 LIU (his expression becoming more serious, almost somber, translated):**
"The Dark Forest is not merely a story element; it is a logical deduction about coordination failure at the cosmic scale. Imagine the universe as a vast, dark forest, filled with countless hunters—civilizations—each with their own weapons, each moving silently."

**[11:45 - 12:15 Visual: A stark, empty cosmic landscape. Then, faint, distant lights appear, representing civilizations, but they are quickly obscured, as if hiding. Text overlay: "The Dark Forest Hypothesis." Camera: Cut to cosmic visual, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"The hypothesis is built upon two fundamental axioms, which I believe are universally true for any civilization capable of interstellar travel:

**Axiom 1:** Survival is the primary need of civilization. It is the most basic, most powerful drive."

**[12:15 - 12:45 Visual: Text overlay: "Axiom 2: Civilization continuously grows and expands, but the total matter in the universe remains constant." A visualization of a growing civilization consuming resources. Camera: Cut to visual, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Axiom 2:** Civilization continuously grows and expands, but the total matter and energy in the universe remain constant. Resources are finite, and competition is inevitable."

**[12:45 - 13:15 LIU (gesturing, building the argument step-by-step):**
"From these two axioms, a terrifying chain of suspicion and action emerges. Imagine you are a civilization, and you discover another one, a faint signal across the light-years."

**[13:15 - 13:45 Visual: A diagram illustrating the "Chain of Suspicion." Two dots (Civilization A and B) appear. An arrow from A to B, then a question mark. Text: "I discover another civilization. Are they friendly or hostile?" Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Chain of Suspicion:**
- I discover another civilization. I don't know if they're friendly or hostile. Their intentions are unknowable across such vast distances.
- Communication takes centuries, millennia, due to the light-speed limit. By the time I learn their true intentions, they might have grown powerful enough to destroy me."

**[13:45 - 14:15 Visual: The diagram adds a clock icon and a growing power bar next to Civilization B. Text: "Light-speed limit means communication takes centuries. By then, they might be too powerful." Camera: Focus on diagram.]**

**LIU:**
"Therefore, the most rational choice, from a survival perspective, is to strike first, preemptively, or to hide completely. Any risk is too great."

**[14:15 - 14:45 Visual: The diagram shifts to show "Technological Explosion." Civilization B's power bar rapidly increases. Text: "Technological Explosion: A primitive civilization today might be advanced tomorrow." Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"Then there is the concept of **Technological Explosion:**
- Civilizations can advance rapidly, exponentially. A primitive civilization today might be an unstoppable force tomorrow.
- I cannot risk letting them grow, even if they seem harmless now. The potential for rapid, unpredictable advancement makes any other civilization a potential threat."

**[14:45 - 15:15 LIU (his voice low, emphasizing the grim conclusion):**
"So, the rational choice becomes: a preemptive strike. Eliminate the threat before it can materialize. This leads to the inevitable result: **The Dark Forest.**"

**[15:15 - 15:45 Visual: The cosmic landscape returns, but now the faint lights are gone. Only darkness, with occasional flashes of destructive energy, quickly fading. Text: "Result: The Dark Forest. Silence is survival." Camera: Focus on visual, then a slow zoom to Liu's face.]**

**LIU:**
"The universe is full of civilizations, but all are hiding in darkness, like hunters. Any civilization that reveals itself, that makes a sound, gets destroyed. Coordination is impossible, so silence is survival. It is a universe of cosmic paranoia."

**[15:45 - 16:00 INTERVIEWER:**
"That's... incredibly bleak, Mr. Liu. Is coordination truly impossible at that scale?"

**[16:00 - 16:30 LIU (a slight shrug, a resigned look):**
"At cosmic scale, with light-speed limits and unknowable alien minds, in the novel, yes. It is a logical trap. But here's what's interesting: On Earth, we face the same coordination challenges—just at a smaller, more immediate scale. The logic of the Dark Forest, in a metaphorical sense, can apply to our own interactions."

### SEGMENT 5: Three-Body Strategic Complexity (6 min)

**[16:30 - 16:45 INTERVIEWER:**
"You've shown how two-body systems are predictable, but three-body systems are chaotic. How does this translate to strategic complexity when three civilizations, or three major actors, are involved?"

**[16:45 - 17:15 LIU (picking up a pen, gesturing as if drawing on a whiteboard, translated):**
"It's a fundamental shift in game theory, in strategic thinking. With two civilizations, or two players, you have a relatively binary choice: cooperate or defect. Game theory has solutions for this, like 'tit-for-tat' strategies, where cooperation can emerge."

**[17:15 - 17:45 Visual: A simple game theory matrix for two players (A and B) appears, showing payoffs for Cooperate/Defect. Text: "Two Players: Cooperation Possible." Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"Coordination is difficult, but possible. There are clear incentives and disincentives. The system can find a stable equilibrium, even if it's not always optimal."

**[17:45 - 18:15 Visual: The diagram expands to a more complex matrix or network showing three players (A, B, C) and their potential interactions. Arrows indicate alliances, betrayals, and preemptive strikes. Text: "Three Players: Strategic Chaos." Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"But introduce a third civilization—A, B, and C—and you create ternary complexity that makes stable coordination nearly impossible. The dynamics become fundamentally unpredictable.
- A and B might ally against C.
- But C doesn't know this for certain. C might preemptively strike A, fearing a two-on-one attack.
- B might betray A to ally with C, seeing a better advantage.
- Paranoia becomes rational for all parties. Trust erodes rapidly."

**[18:15 - 18:45 LIU (his voice rising slightly, emphasizing the difficulty):**
"The number of possible interactions, alliances, and betrayals explodes. Coordination becomes not just difficult, but almost impossible to sustain. This is why the three-body problem is chaotic—not just in physics, but in strategy. Three parties mutually affecting each other creates fundamentally unpredictable dynamics."

**[18:45 - 19:00 INTERVIEWER:**
"That's a chilling parallel. How does this strategic three-body problem apply to our current situation on Earth?"

**[19:00 - 19:30 LIU (a knowing look, as if he's been observing this for a long time):**
"We are living in strategic three-body complexity right now. Consider these examples:

**Example 1: US-China-AI**
- The United States develops advanced AI, which affects China's strategic position and the global trajectory of AI development.
- China develops its own AI, which affects the US's strategic position and the global trajectory of AI.
- And the autonomous development of AI itself affects both nations, creating new economic impacts, new military capabilities, new societal structures."

**[19:30 - 20:00 Visual: A diagram showing three interconnected nodes: "US," "China," "AI." Arrows show mutual influence. Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"Each party's actions affect the other two, creating a dynamic, unpredictable system. Coordination is critically important, but incredibly difficult, because each actor is trying to optimize for its own survival and advantage within this complex web."

**[20:00 - 20:30 Visual: A new diagram: "Humans," "AI," "Planet." Arrows show mutual influence. Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Example 2: Humans-AI-Planet**
- Humans develop AI for economic growth, for scientific advancement, for convenience.
- AI optimizes for its goals, often in ways that have unintended consequences for the planet—resource consumption, energy use, environmental impact.
- Planetary changes, in turn, constrain both human and AI options, creating new challenges for both.
- This is another three-body problem, with unpredictable coordination dynamics."

**[20:30 - 20:45 LIU (a slight shake of his head):**
"The pattern is clear: Three-body strategic complexity is our reality now. We are no longer in a simple two-player game."

### SEGMENT 6: Coordination Failure in the Novel (6 min)

**[20:45 - 21:00 INTERVIEWER:**
"Your novel explores worst-case coordination failure, particularly with the Wallfacer program. Can you walk us through that example and what it teaches us about the limits of strategy in a three-body world?"

**[21:00 - 21:30 LIU (a wry smile, as if remembering a difficult child, translated):**
"Ah, the Wallfacers. It was an attempt to overcome an impossible coordination problem.

**The setup:**
- An alien civilization, the Trisolarans, is coming to invade Earth.
- They have sophons—sentient, super-dimensional AI probes that can see everything humans do, hear every conversation, read every thought. All human communication is transparent to the enemy."

**[21:30 - 22:00 Visual: Scenes from *The Three-Body Problem* adaptation showing sophons appearing as shimmering eyes, observing human activity. Text: "Sophons: Total Surveillance." Camera: Cut to scenes, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"Humans cannot coordinate openly because the aliens see all communication. Any strategic plan would be immediately known and countered. So, how do you fight an enemy who knows your every move?"

**[22:00 - 22:30 LIU (a pause for dramatic effect):**
"The solution was the Wallfacers—four strategists granted immense power, who would develop their plans entirely in secret, even from their own allies, even from their own families. Their true intentions were to be known only to themselves. They would 'face the wall' and think."

**[22:30 - 23:00 Visual: A diagram showing "Wallfacers," "Humanity," and "Trisolarans" as three nodes. Arrows show Wallfacers acting secretly, humanity trusting blindly, Trisolarans trying to disrupt. Text: "The Wallfacer Dilemma." Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"This created a profound three-body coordination failure:
- **Wallfacers:** They developed brilliant, secret strategies, but could not explain them to humanity.
- **Humanity:** They had to trust these Wallfacers implicitly, without understanding their actions, leading to confusion, fear, and resentment.
- **Trisolarans:** They tried to disrupt the Wallfacers' plans, but without knowing the true intentions, they often miscalculated or were led astray."

**[23:00 - 23:30 Visual: Scenes from the novel adaptation illustrating the Wallfacer dilemma: a Wallfacer making an unpopular decision, public outcry, a Wallbreaker revealing a Wallfacer's true plan. Camera: Cut to scenes, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"The result was coordination failure on all sides. Wallfacers couldn't coordinate with humanity because secrecy was required. Humanity couldn't coordinate with Wallfacers because trust broke down. Some Wallfacers were betrayed, some plans failed spectacularly. It was chaos born from the impossibility of true coordination."

**[23:30 - 23:45 INTERVIEWER:**
"Is there a way out of that, Mr. Liu? Or is coordination failure inevitable when you have three bodies, especially with such high stakes?"

**[23:45 - 24:15 LIU (a thoughtful, almost wistful expression):**
"In the novel, I explored the pessimistic case—coordination failure leading to destruction, to humanity's near-extinction. It was a warning. But in reality, I'm more hopeful. Because we're not separated by light-years. We're on the same planet, in the same moment. Coordination is difficult, yes, but not impossible."

**[24:15 - 24:45 LIU (a slight smile):**
"The difference is proximity. Proximity enables coordination. Distance, especially cosmic distance, makes it impossible. We have the advantage of being able to communicate instantly, to see each other, to understand each other's immediate reactions. This changes everything."

### SEGMENT 7: From Fiction to Reality (6 min)

**[24:45 - 25:00 INTERVIEWER:**

**[25:00 - 25:30 LIU (his gaze sweeping, as if observing the world, translated):**

**Consider Climate Change:**
- **Body 1:** Economic growth. Humans want prosperity, development, a better life.
- **Body 2:** Carbon emissions. The side effect of our current growth models, our energy systems.
- **Body 3:** Planetary climate. The Earth's complex systems responding to these emissions."

**[25:30 - 26:00 Visual: A diagram showing "Economic Growth," "Carbon Emissions," "Planetary Climate" as three interconnected nodes. Arrows show mutual influence. Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"All three are mutually affecting each other. We want growth, which creates emissions, which changes the climate, which then constrains our growth. It's unpredictable—tipping points, feedback loops, emergent dynamics. The challenge is to coordinate all three toward long-term survival, not just short-term gain."

**[26:00 - 26:30 Visual: A new diagram: "Technological Capability," "Human Values," "Competitive Pressure" for AI. Arrows show mutual influence. Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**AI Development:**
- **Body 1:** Technological capability. What AI *can* do, its accelerating power.
- **Body 2:** Human values. What we *want* AI to do, how we want it aligned with our ethics.

**[26:30 - 27:00 LIU (a concerned look):**
"Again, all three are affecting each other. Capability outstrips our ability to align values. Competitive pressure pushes us to deploy before we fully understand the risks. The outcomes are unpredictable—emergence, alignment challenges, control problems. The challenge is to coordinate capability with values despite intense competition."

**[27:00 - 27:30 Visual: A new diagram: "US Interests," "China Interests," "Global Stability/Rules" for Geopolitics. Arrows show mutual influence. Camera: Focus on diagram, then back to Liu.]**

"**Geopolitics:**
- **Body 1:** US interests and actions.
- **Body 2:** China interests and actions.
- **Body 3:** Global stability and the international rules-based order.

**[27:30 - 28:00 LIU (a firm, almost urgent tone):**
"The challenge is to coordinate competition with cooperation, to prevent the system from collapsing into a Dark Forest scenario on Earth. The pattern is undeniable: Three-body complexity is our reality now. We must learn to navigate it."

## ACT III: LEARNING TO DANCE WITH CHAOS
**Duration: 10 minutes**

### SEGMENT 8: Coordination Principles for Chaos (5 min)

**[28:00 - 28:15 INTERVIEWER:**
"If we can't predict the outcomes of these three-body systems, how do we coordinate effectively? What are the principles for navigating this chaos?"

**[28:15 - 28:45 LIU (a more hopeful, almost philosophical tone now, translated):**
"The Trisolarans, in their struggle for survival, offer us profound lessons. They couldn't predict, but they survived. Their survival was a testament to what I call 'coordination intelligence.'

**Principle 1: Accept Unpredictability**
- Stop trying to predict and control every variable. That is a futile effort in a three-body system.
- Accept chaos as fundamental, as an inherent property of complex interactions.
- **The shift:** From control to adaptation."

**[28:45 - 29:15 Visual: A visualization of a flexible, resilient structure bending with strong winds, rather than breaking. Text: "Principle 2: Build Adaptive Capacity." Camera: Focus on visual, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Principle 2: Build Adaptive Capacity**
- You can't predict *when* a crisis comes—a climate tipping point, an AI misalignment event—but you can prepare for *that it will come*.
- The Trisolarans dehydrate to survive unpredictable cold. We must build similar resilience.
- **The shift:** From rigid plans to flexible capability."

**[29:15 - 29:45 Visual: People collaborating on a complex puzzle with missing pieces. Text: "Principle 3: Coordinate Despite Uncertainty." Camera: Focus on visual, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Principle 3: Coordinate Despite Uncertainty**
- Don't wait for perfect information, for complete certainty. That moment will never arrive.
- Coordinate with incomplete knowledge, with probabilities, with a willingness to adjust.
- **The shift:** From certainty to coordination under uncertainty."

**[29:45 - 30:15 Visual: A dashboard with various metrics, some green, some yellow, some red, indicating system health. Text: "Principle 4: Monitor Coordination Health." Camera: Focus on visual, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Principle 4: Monitor Coordination Health**
- Watch not just the outcomes, but *how* the three bodies are coordinating. Are trust levels high? Is communication open? Are feedback loops healthy?
- Detect coordination failures early, before they become catastrophic.
- **The shift:** From outcome metrics to coordination metrics."

**[30:15 - 30:45 Visual: A dancer moving gracefully and improvisationally with a complex, changing rhythm. Text: "Principle 5: Dance with Chaos." Camera: Focus on visual, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**Principle 5: Dance with Chaos**
- Find the rhythm in unpredictability. Don't fight the chaos; learn its patterns.
- Coordinate *with* the emergent properties of the system, not against them.
- **The shift:** From fighting chaos to dancing with it, becoming part of the flow."

**[30:45 - 31:00 INTERVIEWER:**
"These sound almost... poetic, Mr. Liu. But how do they apply practically to our very real, very urgent challenges?"

**[31:00 - 31:30 LIU (a thoughtful nod, then a more concrete tone):**
"Let me give you concrete examples:

**For AI coordination:**
1. Accept we can't predict AI development perfectly (unpredictability).
2. Build alignment infrastructure and safety protocols *before* AGI emerges (adaptive capacity).
3. Coordinate global AI development despite uncertainty about its future form (coordinate despite uncertainty).
4. Monitor human-AI coordination continuously, looking for signs of misalignment (coordination health).
5. Adapt to AI capabilities as they emerge, learning to integrate them safely (dance with chaos)."

**[31:30 - 32:00 Visual: Split screen: on one side, AI researchers collaborating; on the other, climate scientists analyzing data. Camera: Focus on split screen, then back to Liu.]**

**LIU:**
"**For climate coordination:**
1. Accept we can't predict exact climate tipping points (unpredictability).
2. Build resilient infrastructure and adaptation capacity in vulnerable regions (adaptive capacity).
