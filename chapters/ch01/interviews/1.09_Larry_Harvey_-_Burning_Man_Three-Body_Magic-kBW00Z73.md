# Interview 9: Larry Harvey  
## "Burning Man and the Three-Body Magic"

**Expert:** Larry Harvey, Founder of Burning Man

## Pre-Interview Context

### About Larry Harvey
- Created Burning Man in 1986 (burned 8-foot wooden man on SF beach)
- Grew event from 20 people to 70,000+ annually
- Developed the 10 Principles (radical inclusion, gifting, decommodification, etc.)
- Philosopher of temporary community and participatory culture
- Passed in 2018, but teachings continue through community

### Note on Interview
This interview would have been conducted with Larry Harvey before his passing, or is conducted with current Burning Man leadership channeling his philosophy and teachings.

### Key Concepts
- Radical self-expression + Communal effort + Temporary infrastructure
- The 10 Principles as coordination framework
- "Playa magic" as emergent coordination
- Temporary cities as coordination laboratories
- Participation vs. spectacle

### Interview Objectives
1. Use Burning Man as concrete coordination example
2. Show how three bodies create "magic" (emergence)
3. Contrast with typical events (optimize one or two bodies)
4. Apply lessons to technology platforms
5. Provide model for coordination architecture

**[00:00] [Visual: Burning Man time-lapse—empty desert, city rises, art installations, the burn, leave no trace. Music swells, then fades slightly under V.O.]**

**HARVEY (V.O.):**
"Burning Man works because we coordinate three elements most events keep separate: radical self-expression, communal effort, and temporary infrastructure. You can't have the magic with just two. But when all three coordinate—when people express themselves within a community that builds shared infrastructure—something emerges that none could create alone. We call it 'the playa magic,' but it's really just what happens when you design for three-way coordination."

## ACT I: THE PLAYA MAGIC
**Duration: 12 minutes**

### SEGMENT 1: The First Burn (4 min)

**[00:30] INTERVIEWER:**
"1986, Baker Beach in San Francisco. You and some friends burn an 8-foot wooden man. How did that become Burning Man?"

**[00:37] HARVEY:**
"It was summer solstice. My friend Jerry James and I had been through breakups—we were processing heartbreak. We decided to build a wooden man and burn it on the beach. It was a personal ritual, a gesture of release, a way to put something behind us and look forward.

**[00:50] [Visual: Archival photos from the first burn on Baker Beach – grainy, intimate, showing the small group and the burning effigy.]**

We didn't announce it. We just built it and lit it on fire. It was a quiet, almost private act.

**[01:00] [Camera: Slow zoom on a photo of the original Man burning.]**

But something unexpected happened. People saw the flames from down the beach, drawn by the primal allure of fire. They came over, curious. By the time the man burned down to embers, there were maybe 20 people standing in a circle, watching, mesmerized.

**[01:20] [Visual: Transition to a modern shot of people gathered around a small fire on the playa, looking engaged.]**

**And they stayed.** After the burn, people didn't just disperse. They started talking, sharing stories, connecting with strangers. This spontaneous community formed around the fire, a temporary tribe united by a shared, inexplicable experience.

**[01:40] HARVEY:**
"That was the insight: It wasn't about the fire itself. It was about what the fire created—a coordination point for community. The fire was the infrastructure, the burning was the self-expression, and the people who gathered were the community. All three, in nascent form, were there."

**[02:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"So you did it again the next year?"

**[02:03] HARVEY:**
"We did. And the next. Each year, it grew a little bigger, a little more elaborate. More people came, more art appeared. By 1990, we had a few hundred people, and the police, bless their hearts, decided our little ritual was getting a bit too... *radical* for Baker Beach. They shut down the burn.

**[02:25] [Visual: Archival footage/photos of the 1990 beach burn being dispersed by police, a sense of playful defiance.]**

So, we packed up our Man, and with the help of a few intrepid souls, we moved it to the Black Rock Desert in Nevada. A vast, empty canvas.

**[02:40] [Visual: Aerial shot of the Black Rock Desert, vast and stark.]**

**And that's when it really became Burning Man.** The desert forced us to confront the challenges of scale, of survival, of building something from nothing. It pushed us to formalize the coordination that had been so spontaneous on the beach."

### SEGMENT 2: Discovering the Three Bodies (4 min)

**[03:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"When did you realize it was about coordinating three specific elements?"

**[03:05] HARVEY:**
"It took years to articulate, to put words to the feeling, but the pattern was there from the beginning. We just didn't have the language for it yet.

**[03:15] [Visual: Montage of early Burning Man photos – diverse people, early art, rudimentary camps.]**

**Early years (1986-1990):**
- People came to express themselves (art, costumes, performance, personal rituals). That was the individual spark.
- Community formed around shared experience, around the fire, around the sheer audacity of it all. That was the collective heart.
- Minimal infrastructure (just the burn itself, maybe a few tents). That was the bare bones.

**[03:40] HARVEY:**
"We noticed: The magic, that feeling of profound connection and possibility, happened when all three were present and working together.

**[03:50] [Visual: Split screen showing contrasting images: a beautiful art piece alone vs. people interacting with it; a bustling camp vs. a chaotic, unorganized one.]**

**Years we had great art but weak community:** Felt hollow, like a gallery opening where everyone just looked and left. Narcissistic.
**Years we had strong community spirit but no infrastructure:** Chaos, conflicts over territory, people getting lost, medical emergencies with no clear response. Good intentions, bad outcomes.
**Years we coordinated all three:** Magic—experiences people remembered for life, moments of transcendence, a sense of belonging that was almost spiritual.

**[04:25] [Camera: Close up on Harvey, looking reflective.]**

**The realization:** You need all three, coordinating, not just existing in parallel:
1.  **Radical self-expression** (the individual's creative impulse, their unique gift)
2.  **Communal effort** (the collective building, sharing, and caring for one another)
3.  **Temporary infrastructure** (the physical and social systems that enable it all, from the city grid to the principles of gifting and leave no trace).

**[04:50] HARVEY:**
"This became our coordination architecture. It wasn't a blueprint we started with, but a pattern we discovered through trial and error, through observing what worked and what didn't in the crucible of the desert."

### SEGMENT 3: Why Temporary Matters (4 min)

**[05:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Why 'temporary'? Why not build permanent infrastructure, especially as the event grew so large?"

**[05:07] HARVEY:**
"Ah, 'temporary' is not a limitation; it's a liberation. Because permanence creates attachment, hierarchy, and ossification. It's the enemy of evolution.

**[05:20] [Visual: Time-lapse of Black Rock City—from empty playa, roads appear, camps rise, art is installed, then reverse time-lapse showing it all disappear, leaving pristine desert.]**

**Permanent infrastructure:**
- Creates ownership ('this is mine,' 'I built this, so I control it').
- Creates hierarchy ('I'm in charge of this permanent structure').
- Creates resistance to change ('we've always done it this way, why change now?').
- **Result:** Coordination becomes rigid, bureaucratic, and eventually, stifling. It becomes about maintaining the *thing*, rather than fostering the *experience*.

**[05:55] HARVEY:**
"Temporary infrastructure, on the other hand, fosters a completely different mindset:
- It creates gifting ('I built this for us, for this moment, and then it will be gone').
- It creates equality ('we all build and dismantle together, we are all temporary stewards').
- It creates evolution ('next year we try something new, we learn, we adapt').
- **Result:** Coordination stays fluid, dynamic, and focused on the present experience. It's about the journey, not the destination."

**[06:25] [Camera: Wide shot of the playa, showing the vastness and the temporary nature of the city.]**

**The teaching:** Nothing is permanent. Everything is coordination. The desert reminds us of our impermanence, our fleeting presence.

**[06:40] HARVEY:**
"When you build knowing you'll dismantle, you build differently:
- Not for control, but for gift.
- Not for permanence, but for experience.
- Not for ownership, but for community.
- Not for profit, but for participation.

**[07:00] HARVEY:**
"This is coordination wisdom: Hold lightly, coordinate consciously, evolve continuously. It's a radical act in a world obsessed with accumulation and permanence."

## ACT II: THE COORDINATION ARCHITECTURE
**Duration: 22 minutes**

### SEGMENT 4: Self-Expression Without Community = Narcissism (5 min)

**[07:20] INTERVIEWER:**
"You've said 'self-expression without community becomes narcissism.' Show us what that looks like."

**[07:27] HARVEY:**
"Go to any major city. You'll see self-expression everywhere, in countless forms:
- Street performers performing for tips, a transaction, not a shared experience.
- Graffiti artists marking territory, often without permission, for personal recognition.
- Influencers creating content for likes, for validation, for a metric.
- Artists selling in galleries, where the art becomes a commodity, the viewer a customer.

**[07:55] [Visual: Montage of urban self-expression examples: street musician with hat out, graffiti tag, selfie-taker, art gallery with price tags.]**

**HARVEY:**
"This is self-expression, yes. But is it coordinating with community in a meaningful way? Is it contributing to a shared fabric?

**[08:10] HARVEY:**
"Usually no:
- The performer wants money; the audience is a means to an end.
- The graffiti artist wants recognition; the community is a backdrop for their ego.
- The influencer wants followers; the audience is a metric to be optimized.
- The gallery artist wants sales; viewers are customers, not co-creators.

**[08:35] HARVEY:**
"The pattern is clear: Self-expression optimized for individual gain, for personal glory, not for community coordination or collective benefit.

**[08:45] [Camera: Close up on Harvey, a slight frown of concern.]**

**Result:** Narcissism—'look at me!' without 'how does this serve us?' It's a monologue, not a dialogue. It's about extraction, not contribution.

**[09:00] [Visual: Transition to Burning Man footage: an artist explaining their piece to an engaged group, people collaborating on a mural, a performer inviting audience participation.]**

**Contrast with Burning Man:**
- An artist builds an installation, often spending years and thousands of dollars, and then gifts it to the community. There's no expectation of payment, no sales.
- **Motivation:** Contributing to a shared experience, sparking joy, provoking thought, creating a moment for others.
- **Result:** Self-expression that serves community, that becomes a catalyst for connection, that transforms the individual act into a collective gift.

**[09:40] HARVEY:**
"The difference is profound: Coordination with community transforms narcissism into gift, into contribution, into something truly generative."

### SEGMENT 5: Community Without Infrastructure = Chaos (5 min)

**[09:50] INTERVIEWER:**
"What about the opposite—community without infrastructure? What happens then?"

**[09:55] HARVEY:**
"We learned this the hard way, out in the desert. In the early 90s, we had this incredible spirit, this burgeoning community. Everyone wanted to help, everyone was excited.

**[10:10] [Visual: Archival photos from chaotic early desert years – cars stuck, unorganized camps, people looking a bit overwhelmed, maybe a dust storm.]**

But we had almost no infrastructure for coordination. It was a beautiful, well-intentioned mess.

**[10:20] HARVEY:**
"Result:
- People duplicating efforts, building the same thing twice, or working at cross-purposes.
- Resources wasted, because there was no central way to know who needed what.
- Conflicts over territory, over noise, over resources, because there were no clear boundaries or agreements.
- Medical emergencies with no coordinated response, people getting lost, real dangers in a harsh environment.
- **Chaos from good intentions.** A community can't thrive on good vibes alone; it needs structure to channel that energy effectively."

**[11:00] [Camera: Harvey gestures broadly, indicating the vastness of the playa.]**

**The lesson:** Community spirit isn't enough. You need coordination infrastructure. You need the scaffolding that allows collective action to be effective, not just enthusiastic.

**[11:15] [Visual: Time-lapse of DPW (Department of Public Works) building roads, laying out the city grid, setting up infrastructure.]**

**What we built, piece by piece, out of necessity:**

**Infrastructure for Coordination:**
- **City grid layout:** Coordinates placement of camps, art, and services, making a navigable city.
- **Department of Public Works (DPW):** Coordinates building roads, setting up safety perimeters, managing heavy equipment.
- **Rangers:** Coordinate safety, mediate disputes, provide information, act as non-confrontational community guides.
- **Emergency Services (Medical, Fire, Law Enforcement):** Coordinate health response, fire safety, and security.
- **Leave No Trace principles and MOOP (Matter Out Of Place) sweeps:** Coordinates cleanup, ensuring the desert is returned to its pristine state.
- **The infrastructure enables community to coordinate at scale, to self-organize effectively.**

**[12:10] HARVEY:**
"Without it:
- Look at Occupy Wall Street: Great community, powerful message, but no sustainable infrastructure, and it eventually collapsed under its own weight.
- Many festivals: Good vibes, but poor coordination leads to chaos, safety issues, and a bad reputation.

**[12:30] [Visual: Aerial shot of Black Rock City, perfectly laid out, bustling with activity.]**

**With it:**
- 70,000 people coordinate to build a temporary city in a week.
- Live together with minimal conflict, often in extreme conditions.
- Dismantle it completely in days, leaving no trace.
- **Infrastructure enables coordination at scale, turning potential chaos into emergent order.**"

### SEGMENT 6: Infrastructure Without Self-Expression = Bureaucracy (5 min)

**[13:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"And infrastructure without self-expression? What does that look like?"

**[13:05] HARVEY:**
"That's most of modern life! That's the world we live in every day.

**[13:10] [Visual: Montage of sterile, bureaucratic spaces: corporate conference room, DMV waiting area, airport security line, cubicle farm.]**

**Corporate events:**
- Perfect infrastructure (venues, schedules, logistics, catering). Everything runs on time, efficiently.
- No self-expression (business casual, professional behavior, scripted interactions). You're expected to conform.
- No real community (networking, transactional relationships, not genuine connection).
- **Result:** Efficient but soulless. It's functional, but it doesn't inspire, doesn't transform. It's a machine, not a living organism.

**[13:50] HARVEY:**
"Or consider government services:
- Infrastructure for coordination (laws, processes, bureaucracy, forms, regulations). Designed for order and fairness.
- No self-expression (follow the rules, fill out the form correctly, don't deviate). Individuality is a hindrance.
- No community (anonymous, impersonal interactions). You're a number, a case file.
- **Result:** Functional but dehumanizing. It gets the job done, but at what cost to the human spirit?"

**[14:20] [Camera: Harvey looks out at the playa, then back to the interviewer, a knowing look.]**

**The pattern:** Infrastructure optimized solely for efficiency, for control, not for human flourishing, not for the vibrant, unpredictable spark of individual creativity.

**[14:35] [Visual: Transition to Burning Man footage: DPW workers in hard hats, but with colorful costumes or decorated vehicles; a Ranger smiling and helping someone.]**

**Contrast with Burning Man:**
- Yes, we have infrastructure (city layout, safety protocols, medical services, sanitation). We *must* have it to survive.
- But our infrastructure serves self-expression and community. It's designed to *enable* radical freedom, not to suppress it.
- Not the other way around. The infrastructure is a servant, not a master.

**[15:10] HARVEY:**
"Take the Man itself, our central effigy.
- **Infrastructure:** It's built to burn safely, engineered for stability, visible from everywhere, a landmark.
- But it exists to enable: Community gathering, shared experience, cathartic release, a focal point for collective meaning-making.
- **Infrastructure serves coordination, not control.** It's a framework for freedom, not a cage."

### SEGMENT 7: All Three Coordinating = Magic (7 min)

**[15:40] INTERVIEWER:**
"When all three coordinate—self-expression, community, infrastructure—what emerges? What is this 'playa magic' you speak of?"

**[15:50] HARVEY:**
"That's when the alchemy happens. That's when the 'playa magic' manifests. It's not supernatural; it's emergent. It's what happens when you design for possibility. Let me give you examples:

**[16:05] [Visual: Footage of a fantastical Art Car, glowing at night, people dancing on it, others waving as it passes.]**

**Example 1: Art Cars (Mutant Vehicles)**
- **Self-expression:** Someone spends a year building a giant glowing octopus car, or a pirate ship on wheels, or a rolling cupcake. It's their wildest dream made real.
- **Community:** They don't just drive it around for themselves. They offer rides to anyone, becoming a mobile gathering space, a spontaneous party, a way to explore the playa together.
- **Infrastructure:** It's registered with the Department of Mutant Vehicles, follows safety rules, has lights, coordinates its movement within the city grid.
- **Emergence:** A mobile art piece that creates spontaneous community experiences, serendipitous encounters, and moments of pure joy that are impossible to plan or predict. It's a gift that keeps on giving, literally moving through the community.

**[17:00] [Visual: Footage of a vibrant Theme Camp: people interacting, a unique structure, a sign inviting participation, a gift being offered.]**

**Example 2: Theme Camps**
- **Self-expression:** A group of friends creates a unique experience—a silent disco, a tea house, a workshop on radical empathy, a giant slip-n-slide. It's their collective creative vision.
- **Community:** They gift this experience to passersby, creating connections, fostering interaction, offering respite or stimulation. It's a micro-community within the larger city.
- **Infrastructure:** The camp is placed in the city grid, adheres to Leave No Trace principles, manages its own waste, coordinates with the city for power and water.
- **Emergence:** These camps become vibrant neighborhoods with distinct character, offering endless opportunities for serendipitous discoveries, deep conversations, and a profound sense of belonging. They are the living cells of Black Rock City.

**[17:55] [Visual: Dramatic footage of the Man burn: the crowd gathered, the fire rising, faces illuminated, then the Temple burn: quieter, more reflective, people leaving offerings.]**

**Example 3: The Burn Itself (The Man and The Temple)**
- **Self-expression:** The Man is a massive, iconic art installation, often with a different theme each year. The Temple is a sacred space for grief and remembrance, built by artists.
- **Community:** 70,000 people gather in a vast circle, united in anticipation, in shared catharsis, in collective awe. For the Temple, it's a quieter, more personal, yet still collective, release.
- **Infrastructure:** A massive safety perimeter, fire safety teams, medical personnel, Rangers, all coordinating to ensure the safety of tens of thousands.
- **Emergence:** Collective catharsis, shared transcendence, a profound sense of unity, an ineffable 'magic' that words can barely capture. It's a ritual that binds us, a moment of collective consciousness.

**[18:50] HARVEY:**
"What makes it magic? None of these could exist with just two bodies:
- An art car without community: Just a vehicle, a private toy.
- A theme camp without infrastructure: An unsafe, chaotic mess, quickly collapsing.
- The burn without self-expression: Just a bonfire, a destructive act, not a transformative ritual.

**[19:15] HARVEY:**
"All three coordinating: That's when experiences emerge that change people's lives. That's when you touch something deeper than mere entertainment."

**[19:25] INTERVIEWER:**
"What are people actually experiencing? What is the 'magic'?"

**[19:30] HARVEY:**
"They're experiencing emergence—something created by coordination that none of the parts contain on their own. It's the feeling of being part of something vastly larger than yourself, yet simultaneously more authentically *you*.

**[19:45] [Camera: Close up on Harvey, his eyes sparkling with conviction.]**

**The magic is:**
- Belonging (the deep human need for community).
- Freedom (the radical self-expression that allows you to be truly seen).
- Trust (the infrastructure that creates a safe container for that freedom and belonging).
- **Coordinating to create:** An experience of profound possibility, a glimpse of a different way to live, to organize, to be human.

**[20:15] HARVEY:**
"People leave Burning Man thinking: 'If we can coordinate to create this temporary city in the desert, this vibrant, gifting, self-organizing culture, what else is possible? What else can we build together?'

**[20:30] HARVEY:**
"That's the real gift: Not just the art or the party, but the proof that coordination, when designed consciously, can create emergence. It's a laboratory for human potential.

**[20:45] HARVEY:**
"And that proof changes how people see the world. It plants a seed of possibility that they carry back into their everyday lives."

## ACT III: LESSONS FOR TECHNOLOGY
**Duration: 10 minutes**

### SEGMENT 8: What Technology Platforms Get Wrong (5 min)

**[21:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"What can technology platforms learn from Burning Man's coordination architecture? Where do they fall short?"

**[21:08] HARVEY:**
"Most platforms optimize for one or two bodies, never all three. They pick a lane, and in doing so, they miss the magic. They miss the human.

**[21:20] [Visual: Graphic: Platform comparison matrix, showing checkmarks/X's for Self-Expression, Community, Infrastructure for each platform.]**

**Take Facebook, for example:**
- ✓ Self-expression (you can post, share photos, express opinions).
- ✗ Real community (algorithmic feeds prioritize engagement over genuine connection, echo chambers, curated personas). It's a broadcast platform, not a truly communal one.
- ✗ Healthy infrastructure (optimizes for engagement metrics, not for human flourishing, leading to addiction, misinformation, and mental health issues).
- **Result:** Self-expression without coordination with true community or healthy infrastructure often devolves into narcissism, anxiety, and division.

**[22:10] HARVEY:**
"Or Reddit:
- ✓ Community (subreddits, shared interests, passionate groups).
- ✓ Infrastructure (voting systems, moderation tools, clear rules for each community).
- ✗ Self-expression (often anonymous, can be toxic, encourages mob dynamics, discourages nuanced individual voice).
- **Result:** Community with infrastructure, but without authentic, accountable self-expression, can lead to mob dynamics, echo chambers, and a race to the bottom in terms of discourse.

**[22:50] HARVEY:**
"And then there are the vast corporate platforms, the enterprise solutions:
- ✓ Infrastructure (professional, efficient, secure, designed for specific tasks).
- ✗ Self-expression (sterile, corporate-approved language, no room for individual voice or creativity).
- ✗ Community (transactional, not relational; colleagues, not a true community).
- **Result:** Infrastructure without humanity or genuine connection leads to bureaucracy, disengagement, and a feeling of being a cog in a machine.

**[23:30] HARVEY:**
"What's missing across the board is the conscious, intentional coordination of all three. They treat them as separate levers, not as an integrated system.

**[23:40] [Visual: Graphic: 'Burning Man-Inspired Platform' with three interlocking circles: Self-Expression, Community, Infrastructure, and 'Emergence' in the center.]**

**What would coordinated platforms look like? A Burning Man-Inspired Platform:**
- **Self-expression:** Tools for authentic creativity and voice, where individuality is celebrated and encouraged, not just monetized.
- **Community:** Designed for real connection and collaboration, fostering empathy and shared purpose, not just algorithmic engagement.
- **Infrastructure:** Governance that serves both self-expression and community, prioritizing human flourishing over extraction or control.
- **Coordination:** All three working together, dynamically, adaptively.
- **Emergence:** Experiences that transform users, that inspire them, that make them feel more alive and connected, not just entertained or informed."

### SEGMENT 9: The Coordination Principles (5 min)

**[24:30] INTERVIEWER:**
"Burning Man has 10 Principles. Are these coordination principles? How do they fit into this framework?"

**[24:38] HARVEY:**
"Absolutely! Though we didn't frame them that way initially. They emerged from the needs of the community, from the lessons learned in the desert. They are, in essence, our operating system for coordination. Let me show you:

**[24:55] [Visual: Graphic: The 10 Principles listed, with concise 'Coordination Framework' annotations next to each.]**

**The 10 Principles as Coordination Framework:**

1.  **Radical Inclusion:** Coordinate with everyone. No exclusion, no gatekeeping. The more diverse the input, the richer the emergent outcome.
2.  **Gifting:** Coordinate through giving. Not transactions, not quid pro quo, but unconditional generosity. It builds trust and reciprocity.
3.  **Decommodification:** Coordinate outside money. Remove the transactional layer to foster genuine human connection and creativity.
4.  **Radical Self-Reliance:** Coordinate with yourself first. Take responsibility for your own needs. This empowers individuals to contribute, rather than be a burden.
5.  **Radical Self-Expression:** Coordinate your truth. Be authentic, be unique. This is the fuel for the collective creative fire.
6.  **Communal Effort:** Coordinate with others. Work together, build together, share the load. This is the engine of the community.
7.  **Civic Responsibility:** Coordinate with the whole. Care for the community, uphold its values, contribute to its safety and well-being. This is the social infrastructure.
8.  **Leave No Trace:** Coordinate with the environment. Be mindful of your impact, clean up after yourself, respect the land. This is ecological coordination.
9.  **Participation:** Coordinate actively. Don't just spectate; engage, contribute, create. This is the dynamic energy that makes it all happen.
10. **Immediacy:** Coordinate in the present. Be here now, experience the moment, connect directly. This fosters genuine human interaction, unmediated by screens or expectations.

**[26:30] HARVEY:**
"The pattern is undeniable: Each principle is about a specific facet of coordination—with self, with others, with the environment, with the present moment.

**[26:45] HARVEY:**
"Together: They create a robust coordination architecture that enables 70,000 people to coordinate successfully, to build a city, to create magic, and then to make it all disappear, leaving no trace."

**[27:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Could these principles apply to technology platforms?"

**[27:05] HARVEY:**
"They should! Imagine social media built on these principles:

-   **Radical inclusion:** Everyone can participate, not just the most profitable users or those who fit a certain demographic.
-   **Gifting:** Share value freely, knowledge, art, support, not just extract data or attention.
-   **Decommodification:** Focus on human connection and shared experience, not transactions, advertising, or endless consumption.
-   **Participation:** Encourage users to create, to contribute, to build, not just to consume passively.
-   **Immediacy:** Foster real, unmediated connection, not algorithmic mediation that filters and manipulates.

**[27:50] HARVEY:**
"Different principles, different platform, different outcomes. The current platforms are built on principles of extraction and attention. Burning Man is built on principles of contribution and connection.

**[28:05] HARVEY:**
"The lesson is clear: Coordination principles determine coordination outcomes. Choose wisely what you build your world upon."
