## ACT I: FROM WHOLE EARTH TO WIRED

**Duration:** 12 minutes

### Opening [0:45-3:30]

**[0:45 - 1:00] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, warm lighting. Kelly's studio visible behind — shelves of books, specimen jars, vintage cameras, a framed first issue of Wired.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Kevin, you've been at the intersection of technology and culture for fifty years. You edited the Whole Earth Catalog, co-founded Wired, wrote foundational books on networks and emergence. What's the thread that runs through all of it?"

**[1:00 - 3:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, leaning back in his chair, relaxed. He speaks with the ease of someone who has told these stories before but still finds them interesting.]**

**KELLY:**
"The thread is coordination. I've always been interested in how things work together without a center. The Whole Earth Catalog was a coordination device — it connected people who were trying to solve similar problems in different places without knowing about each other. Access to tools was just the surface. The deeper thing was: here's what other people are figuring out. You don't have to figure it out alone.

**[1:45] [Visual: Insert cam: Pages of the Whole Earth Catalog — hand-drawn diagrams, book reviews, reader letters. The texture of a pre-digital knowledge network.]**

Wired was the same thing at digital scale. The internet was a coordination infrastructure. The question we were asking was: what becomes possible when people can coordinate at this speed, at this scale, with this fidelity?

**[2:15] [Visual: A-CAM back on Kelly, gesturing with one hand.]**

And the books have been an attempt to understand what the coordination is actually building. Not just what it produces for humans — what the system itself is becoming. Because by the time I was writing *Out of Control* in the early 1990s, it was clear that the networks we were building had their own behavior. You couldn't explain what the internet was doing by looking at any individual node. You had to look at the coordination."

---

### Segment 1: Out of Control [3:30-7:30]

**[3:30 - 3:45] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer. Insert: Cover of *Out of Control*, the bees on the jacket.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"*Out of Control* came out in 1994 and it's now seen as one of the most prescient technology books ever written. What were you seeing back then that others weren't?"

**[3:45 - 5:15] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who leans forward slightly, warming to the subject.]**

**KELLY:**
"Here's the thing — I was watching distributed systems produce behavior that no center was directing. The internet was routing around damage. Not because anyone programmed it to, but because of its architecture. That's a coordination property, not a design feature. Nobody sat down and said, 'I want my network to heal itself.' It just did, because of how the parts were connected.

At the same time, I was fascinated by the artificial life experiments that were happening — Chris Langton's work, the Santa Fe Institute crowd. They were showing that you could get extraordinarily complex behavior from very simple rules, as long as the elements could interact. Simple agents, simple rules, rich coordination — and boom, you get emergence.

**[5:15 - 5:45] [Visual: Insert cam: Animation of flocking behavior — boids following three simple rules, producing coordinated flight patterns. Then: a network diagram showing packet routing.]**

And I kept looking at biological systems — ant colonies, immune systems, ecosystems — and seeing the same pattern everywhere. Biology was the model for what technology was becoming, not the other way around. People in the tech industry thought they were building machines. I thought they were growing organisms."

**[5:45 - 6:00] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The title itself — *Out of Control* — people read that as a warning. But you meant something very different."

**[6:00 - 7:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who smiles and nods.]**

**KELLY:**
"Exactly. 'Out of control' doesn't mean chaotic. It means coordinated without a controller. That's the key distinction. An ecosystem is out of control. Nobody is running the Amazon rainforest. There's no CEO of the coral reef. And yet these are the most extraordinarily productive, diverse, resilient systems on the planet. They work precisely because nobody is in charge.

What people missed about networks — and this was the big blind spot in the nineties — was that they thought the internet was a tool for human control. A better telephone. A faster fax machine. I was arguing that it was a coordination architecture that would develop its own behavior, its own tendencies, its own direction. The social media platforms that emerged twenty years later were exactly what I expected. Not because I was some kind of prophet — because coordination dynamics are reliable. When you connect enough agents with enough feedback loops, emergent behavior is not a possibility. It's a guarantee.

**[7:00] [Visual: Insert cam: Diagram showing elements (A), connections (B), emergent behavior (C) — the three-body pattern.]**

And here's the deeper point: the whole is not just greater than the sum of its parts. The whole is a different thing entirely. You cannot understand what an ecosystem does by studying its species in isolation. You cannot understand what the internet does by studying individual computers. The emergent layer — that's not a byproduct. That's the point."

---

### Segment 2: The Whole Earth Thread [7:30-12:45]

**[7:30 - 7:50] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer. Insert: A vintage copy of the Whole Earth Review.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Stewart Brand created the Whole Earth Catalog. You edited the Whole Earth Review. That tradition — tools, access, decentralized learning — what did it understand about coordination that Silicon Valley later forgot?"

**[7:50 - 9:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who settles deeper into his chair, reflective now.]**

**KELLY:**
"The Whole Earth tradition understood something really fundamental: knowledge is a coordination resource, not a competitive advantage. The person who shares what they know makes the whole network smarter. That was the operating philosophy. We weren't hoarding information or gating it behind subscriptions. We were saying: here's the best stuff we've found, go use it, and when you figure something out, tell us so we can tell everyone else.

Tools, in the Whole Earth view, are coordination interfaces between human intention and the world. A good tool doesn't just amplify your power — it connects your capability to a problem in a way that produces something neither you nor the tool could produce alone. 'Access to tools' meant: remove the barriers that prevent coordination.

**[9:30 - 9:45] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, nodding.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And then the platforms came along."

**[9:45 - 11:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, whose expression shifts — still warm, but with an edge of concern.]**

**KELLY:**
"Right. And here's what Silicon Valley forgot. The Whole Earth tradition was anti-monopoly by philosophy. Not because we were political about it, but because we understood that coordination should be distributed. Power should be distributed. The moment you centralize the coordination, you've changed its nature.

The platforms that emerged — Facebook, Google, eventually the whole attention economy — they centralized the coordination and captured the value. The network effects that make coordination powerful also make monopoly inevitable if you're not careful. If the more people who use a platform, the more valuable it becomes, then eventually one platform dominates. And then the platform's interests diverge from the network's interests.

**[11:00 - 11:30] [Visual: Insert cam: Split screen — the Whole Earth Catalog's reader letters section beside a Facebook feed full of engagement-bait. The contrast is stark.]**

We thought the internet would be a commons. In many ways it became an enclosure. The infrastructure for coordination was captured by the infrastructure for distraction. Individual actors connected by network infrastructure, but the incentive layer — the emergent behavior that the system actually optimized for — that was designed for extraction, not coordination. And that's a failure of design, not a failure of technology."

**[11:30 - 11:50] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Wikipedia seems like the exception — the one thing that stayed true to the Whole Earth vision."

**[11:50 - 12:45] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, brightening.]**

**KELLY:**
"Wikipedia is the closest contemporary descendant of the Whole Earth Catalog, absolutely. Shared knowledge infrastructure. Volunteer-driven. No advertising. The value flows back to the commons rather than being extracted by a platform owner. Jimmy Wales understood something that Mark Zuckerberg didn't: the coordination is the product. You don't need to monetize the attention if you're building the coordination itself. Wikipedia works because it aligned its incentive structure with the coordination it was trying to produce. That's rare. That's precious. And it's the model we should be looking at as we build AI systems."

## ACT II: WHAT THE TECHNIUM WANTS

**Duration:** 18 minutes

### Segment 3: The Technium [12:45-19:00]

**[12:45 - 13:10] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer. Insert: Cover of *What Technology Wants*.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You coined the term 'technium' for the global system of technology as a whole. Not individual tools — the entire interconnected system. What is the technium, and why does it need its own name?"

**[13:10 - 15:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who sits up straighter, energized by the question. This is clearly territory he loves.]**

**KELLY:**
"The technium is the sum of all human-made things, plus the relationships between them, plus the behavior that emerges from those relationships. It's not just hardware. It's software, institutions, languages, cultural practices that depend on technology. The web of connections between things, the dynamics of their interactions, the whole system of tech-enabled information and communication.

Here's why it needs its own name: when you name something, you can think about it. 'Technology' implies passive tools. A hammer sitting on a shelf. 'Technium' implies an active system with its own dynamics. And that's not a rhetorical trick — it's an observation.

**[14:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Animated diagram — individual technologies as nodes, connections forming between them, and a pulsing emergent layer above, labeled 'technium behavior.' The diagram breathes, almost organically.]**

You can't meaningfully ask what a hammer wants. That's a category error. But you can ask what a living system wants — that's a meaningful question. We ask it about ecosystems all the time. What does the forest want? Well, the forest wants to grow, to diversify, to increase its biomass, to become more resilient. Those aren't metaphors. Those are observable tendencies. The technium has the same kind of tendencies."

**[15:00 - 15:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So what does the technium want?"

**[15:15 - 17:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who counts on his fingers as he lists, then drops his hands and speaks more expansively.]**

**KELLY:**
"Increasing efficiency. Increasing opportunity. Increasing emergence. Increasing complexity. Increasing diversity. Increasing specialization. Increasing ubiquity. Increasing freedom. Increasing mutualism. Increasing beauty. Increasing sentience. Increasing structure. Increasing evolvability.

And here's the thing that startled me when I first compiled that list: these are the same things life wants. The exact same tendencies. Life wants to diversify. Life wants to increase in complexity. Life wants to become more efficient, more specialized, more interconnected. The technium is not separate from biological evolution — it is its continuation.

**[16:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Side-by-side comparison — the evolutionary tree of life and a technology evolution diagram. The branching patterns are strikingly similar.]**

I call it the seventh kingdom of life. You've got bacteria, archaea, protists, fungi, plants, animals — and the technium. It's the newest kingdom. It runs on different substrates — silicon instead of carbon, electricity instead of ATP — but the deep dynamics are the same. It evolves. It diversifies. It fills niches. It coordinates."

**[17:00 - 17:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the coordination dimension — mutualism. That's the one you keep coming back to."

**[17:15 - 19:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, nodding emphatically.]**

**KELLY:**
"Mutualism is the key. It's the most important thing on that list, in my view. Mutualism is coordination between organisms that benefits both parties. Lichens — a fungus and an alga coordinating to produce something neither could produce alone. Mycorrhizal networks — trees and fungi exchanging nutrients underground. These relationships are everywhere in biology, and they're the relationships that produce the most creative, most resilient, most productive outcomes.

The technium wants to coordinate with humanity, not to replace it. The most advanced state of technology is not automation — it's mutualism. Full automation is actually a dead end, biologically speaking. The most successful species are not the ones that do everything alone. They're the ones that coordinate most effectively with other species. And that's the trajectory I see for AI. Not a replacement for human intelligence. A mutualistic partner for it. The question is whether we build the relationship that way."

### Segment 4: The Inevitable [19:00-25:00]

**[19:00 - 19:20] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer. Insert: Cover of *The Inevitable*.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Your book *The Inevitable* describes twelve technological forces that are shaping the next thirty years. You're careful not to call them predictions — you call them trajectories. What's the difference, and why does it matter?"

**[19:20 - 21:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who makes a distinction with his hands — one palm flat for predictions, the other sweeping in an arc for trajectories.]**

**KELLY:**
"A prediction is specific: X will happen by year Y. I don't do that. I'm terrible at it and so is everyone else. A trajectory is directional: the system is moving toward Z. The technium has trajectories because it has wants — tendencies that arise from its nature, the same way water has a tendency to flow downhill. You can predict the direction without predicting the speed or the specific form.

I can tell you that things are going to become more connected. I cannot tell you which connectivity platform will win. I can tell you that AI will become more pervasive. I cannot tell you which company will build it. The trajectories are reliable. The specifics are not."

**[21:00 - 21:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Walk us through the twelve forces. What's the pattern you see connecting them?"

**[21:15 - 23:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who becomes more animated, ticking through the concepts.]**

**KELLY:**
"The twelve forces are: Becoming, Cognifying, Flowing, Screening, Accessing, Sharing, Filtering, Remixing, Interacting, Tracking, Questioning, Beginning. Each one is a direction, not a destination.

But here's what I realized after I published the book: every single one of those forces is a coordination force. Cognifying — making things smarter — that's really about making things better at coordinating with each other and with us. A smart thermostat coordinates between your comfort, the weather, and your energy bill. That's coordination, not just intelligence.

**[22:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Animated graphic — the twelve forces as rays emanating from a central point, each labeled. They pulse and interconnect, forming a web.]**

Flowing — information moving across barriers that used to contain it — that's coordination at a distance. Sharing — humans choosing to coordinate their knowledge rather than hoard it. Filtering — making coordination legible in an ocean of noise. Every force points in the same direction: more coordination, faster coordination, richer coordination.

And that tells you something profound about the technium's trajectory. It doesn't want to be smarter in isolation. It wants to coordinate. Because coordination is what produces emergence, and emergence is what produces novelty, and novelty is what allows evolution to continue. Coordination is the engine."

**[23:30 - 23:45] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Where does AI fit in that picture? It seems like it touches all twelve forces simultaneously."

**[23:45 - 25:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who pauses briefly, choosing his words carefully.]**

**KELLY:**
"That's exactly right. AI is not a single force — it's the amplification of all twelve simultaneously. It cognifies everything, obviously. But it also flows information across new boundaries, it enables new kinds of sharing, it filters at scales no human could manage, it remixes in ways we couldn't imagine.

But here's the question that keeps me up at night — and I say that as a generally optimistic person. Cognifying without coordinating is just smarter optimization. You get a more efficient extraction machine. You get faster exploitation. The question is whether we build the coordination architecture to match the cognifying. Because right now, we're cognifying everything and coordinating almost nothing. We're building brilliant components and hoping coordination happens by accident. It won't. Coordination has to be designed for, the same way the Amish design for community."

### Segment 5: The Amish Lesson [25:00-30:00]

**[25:00 - 25:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer, with a slight smile.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Speaking of the Amish — you've spent real time with Amish communities and you argue they have a surprisingly sophisticated technology philosophy. What does a 19th-century religious community have to teach us about AI?"

**[25:15 - 27:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who grins broadly.]**

**KELLY:**
"Oh, I love this question, because people always expect me to be joking when I bring up the Amish. I'm not joking at all. The Amish have the most sophisticated technology evaluation framework I've ever encountered. More sophisticated than anything in Silicon Valley, and I say that having co-founded Wired.

Here's what the Amish actually do. They don't reject technology — that's the myth. They evaluate it. Before adopting a technology, they ask one question: what will this do to our community? Not: is it useful? Not: is it efficient? Not: can we afford it? The question is: what will it do to the bonds between us?

**[26:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Photographs of Amish communities — a barn raising, a family dinner, a community meeting. The coordination is visible in every image.]**

If a technology strengthens coordination — strengthens the bonds between community members — they adopt it, carefully, with constraints. If it weakens coordination — if it pulls people apart, if it makes them more independent in ways that reduce interdependence — they reject it. Period.

The telephone is the classic example. They rejected it from homes because it would weaken face-to-face community. But they allowed community phone booths — a shared phone at the edge of the property — because that allows coordination with the outside world without replacing the internal coordination of the community. That's not primitive. That's brilliant."

**[27:00 - 27:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And Silicon Valley doesn't ask that question."

**[27:15 - 28:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, shaking his head.]**

**KELLY:**
"Silicon Valley asks: what is technically possible? What can we build? What will people use? What will generate growth? Those are all fine questions. But they skip the Amish question entirely: what does this do to coordination? What bonds does it strengthen? What bonds does it weaken?

I'll give you a concrete example. Social media was technically brilliant. People used it enthusiastically. It generated enormous growth. But it weakened the bonds between people. It optimized for engagement, which turned out to be a proxy for outrage, which turned out to erode trust, which turned out to destroy the coordination capacity of entire societies. If Mark Zuckerberg had spent a weekend with the Amish before building the News Feed, we might live in a different world.

**[28:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Split screen — an Amish community meeting, deliberating over technology adoption, beside a Silicon Valley product launch, celebrating disruption. Two philosophies of technology.]**

The technium wants mutualism. I believe that. But we have to design for it. The Amish design for mutualism. Silicon Valley designs for scale. Those are not the same thing."

**[28:30 - 28:45] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So what's the Amish question for AI?"

**[28:45 - 30:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who speaks more slowly now, deliberately.]**

**KELLY:**
"Before deploying an AI system, ask: what does this do to human coordination? Does it strengthen the feedback loops between humans and their systems? Does it help people understand each other better, work together more effectively, make better collective decisions? Or does it replace human judgment with machine judgment in a way that breaks the coordination?

The Amish would ask: can we have the AI coordinate with us rather than for us? That's the right question. An AI that coordinates with you is a partner. An AI that coordinates for you is a replacement. The difference is whether the human stays in the loop — not as a supervisor, but as a participant. Mutualism means both parties are active. Both parties contribute something the other can't. Both parties are changed by the relationship. That's what we should be building."

## ACT III: COORDINATION AS THE SEVENTH KINGDOM

**Duration:** 15 minutes

### Segment 6: The Long Zoom [30:00-37:00]

**[30:00 - 30:20] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer. The lighting shifts slightly — afternoon sun moving through the studio windows.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You think in very long time horizons — deep evolutionary time. You're on the board of the Long Now Foundation, which is building a clock designed to run for ten thousand years. What does that long zoom reveal about where coordination intelligence is headed?"

**[30:20 - 32:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who leans back and looks upward briefly, as if scanning a timeline in his mind.]**

**KELLY:**
"When you zoom out far enough, the story of life on Earth is a story of increasing coordination. Start at the beginning. Single-celled organisms: internal coordination only. Each cell is its own universe. Then multicellular organisms appear — and that's not about better cells. That's about coordination between cells producing something entirely new. An organism.

Then social animals. Coordination between individuals. Then language — which is coordination between minds, and not just minds in the same place, but minds across time. I can coordinate with Aristotle because he wrote things down. Then writing, then printing, then telegraph, telephone, internet — each one is a new substrate for coordination operating at greater scale and greater speed.

**[31:45] [Visual: Insert cam: Animated timeline — single cells, multicellular life, social groups, language, writing, printing, telegraph, internet, AI. Each stage pulses with increasing density of connections.]**

And now AI. Which is coordination between human minds and non-biological intelligence. That's the current frontier. Not smarter computers. Not faster processing. The coordination between human and machine intelligence to produce something that neither could produce alone."

**[32:30 - 32:45] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And at each stage, something genuinely new emerges."

**[32:45 - 34:30] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, nodding vigorously.]**

**KELLY:**
"That's the crucial pattern. The jump from single cells to multicellular organisms was not about better cells — it was about coordination between cells producing something new. Something the cells couldn't have imagined, if cells could imagine. A jellyfish. An oak tree. A human brain. None of those were latent in the single cell. They emerged from the coordination.

The jump from individual humans to civilization was the same. Civilization is not about better humans. It's about coordination between humans producing something new — language, law, art, science, cities. Things no individual human could create or even conceive of alone.

**[33:45] [Visual: Insert cam: Time-lapse of a city being built — coordination made visible in architecture, infrastructure, commerce.]**

So the jump to AI follows the same pattern. It's not about better computers. It is about coordination between human and machine intelligence producing something genuinely new. Something we can't fully imagine yet, because the emergent layer hasn't fully formed."

**[34:30 - 34:45] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"That uncertainty — the not knowing — does that worry you?"

**[34:45 - 37:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who pauses, then speaks with unusual directness.]**

**KELLY:**
"I genuinely don't know what emerges from human-AI coordination. That's the honest answer. And I've learned over fifty years that when I don't know, I should say so rather than pretend.

But here's why the uncertainty doesn't paralyze me. The trajectory is toward more mutualism, more coordination, more emergence. Every single jump in the evolutionary sequence has produced more coordination capacity, not less. More diversity, not less. More creative potential, not less. There's no reason to think this jump will be different — unless we actively design it wrong.

**[35:30] [Visual: Insert cam: The three-body diagram — existing intelligence (A), new coordination substrate (B), emergent capability (C). The C is marked with a question mark.]**

Language was a new coordination substrate. It produced new thought that pre-language minds couldn't have generated. Writing was a new coordination substrate. It produced new knowledge that oral cultures couldn't have accumulated. The internet was a new coordination substrate. It produced new forms of collaboration that pre-internet societies couldn't have organized.

AI is the next coordination substrate. It will produce new intelligence — new forms of knowing, new forms of creating, new forms of understanding — that neither humans nor machines could generate alone. That emergent layer is unknown. And I want to be very clear about this: that is not a problem. That is the point. If we already knew what would emerge, it wouldn't be emergence. It would just be engineering.

The technium wants to coordinate with us. We should want to coordinate with it. The question — the only question that matters — is how."

### Segment 7: What He Practices [37:00-44:30]

**[37:00 - 37:20] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer. The studio has a warm, late-afternoon glow now.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've been thinking about these dynamics for fifty years. How has that changed how you actually live and work?"

**[37:20 - 39:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who smiles and shifts in his chair — this is personal territory.]**

**KELLY:**
"Well, the first thing is long horizons. I'm a member of the Long Now Foundation — we're building a clock designed to run for ten thousand years inside a mountain in Texas. And people ask, why? What's the point? The point is the question the clock forces you to ask: what would a ten-thousand-year civilization need? That question changes what you build today.

When you think in centuries instead of quarters, everything looks different. You stop optimizing for next week and start designing for the long coordination. What institutions would you need? What knowledge would you need to preserve? What relationships between humans and their technologies would you need to sustain? Those are better questions than 'what will ship by Friday.'

**[38:30] [Visual: Insert cam: Photographs of the Long Now clock mechanism — massive, precise, beautiful. Designed to outlast every technology Kelly has ever written about.]**

**[39:00 - 39:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And your own practice — the learning, the curiosity. You're famous for constantly picking up new things."

**[39:15 - 41:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who chuckles.]**

**KELLY:**
"I'm constantly learning things I know nothing about. Photography, Asian cultures, biology, new technologies, old crafts. I just spent months learning about — of all things — the history of maps. Not because I needed to know about maps. Because the beginner's mind is the most valuable state of consciousness I've found.

The moment I become an expert in something, I become less useful. I know that sounds paradoxical, but it's true. The expert sees what's expected. The beginner sees what's actually there. The expert has models and frameworks and they're wonderful, but they're also blinders. The beginner walks into a room and notices things the expert has stopped seeing because they already know what's supposed to be there.

**[40:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Kelly's photography — street scenes from Asia, macro shots of insects, landscapes. The eye of someone who is still surprised by the world.]**

That's why I've never settled into one field. I keep moving. Not because I'm restless, but because the coordination between what I learned in biology and what I learned in technology and what I learned from the Amish and what I learned from Asian philosophy — that cross-pollination is where the interesting thoughts live. It's coordination across domains. It's the same principle at a personal scale."

**[41:00 - 41:15] [Visual: A-CAM on interviewer.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"One last question. If you could sit down with the people building AI right now — the engineers, the founders, the policymakers — what would you tell them?"

**[41:15 - 44:00] [Visual: B-CAM on Kelly, who straightens up. This is his final statement and he knows it.]**

**KELLY:**
"Four things. First: build for mutualism, not replacement. Every time you're designing an AI system, ask whether you're building a partner or a substitute. Partners create more value over time because both parties evolve. Substitutes create diminishing returns because you've cut the human out of the feedback loop.

Second: ask the Amish question. What does this do to human coordination? Not what does it do to efficiency, not what does it do to profit margins — what does it do to the bonds between people? Build the feedback loops that make the answer visible, because right now we're flying blind.

**[42:15] [Visual: Insert cam: Kelly's copy of *What Technology Wants*, dog-eared and annotated. His own book, still being revised in his mind.]**

Third: the technium wants what life wants. Give it what life gives. The freedom to coordinate. The space to emerge. The feedback to evolve. Don't try to control it from the top down — that's the lesson of *Out of Control.* Design the conditions for mutualism, then let the mutualism develop.

And fourth — stay curious. I mean that seriously. The thing you're building is going to surprise you. That's not a warning. That's the gift. Every evolutionary jump has produced surprises that were richer than what came before. More beautiful, more complex, more alive.
