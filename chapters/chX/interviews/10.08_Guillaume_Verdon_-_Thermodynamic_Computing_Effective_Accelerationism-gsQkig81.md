## ACT I: COMPUTING WITH ENTROPY

**Duration: 10 minutes**

### SEGMENT 1: The Thermodynamic Breakthrough (5 min)

**[00:45 - 00:50] [A-CAM: Interviewer, intrigued by the radical approach.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Guillaume, you're building computers that use thermodynamic physics instead of digital logic. Most people don't even know that's possible. What's wrong with current computing, and how does thermodynamic computing represent a fundamentally different approach?"

**[00:50 - 02:10] [B-CAM: Verdon, intense, explaining the paradigm shift.]**

**VERDON:**
"Digital computers fight entropy. That's the fundamental problem. Classical computing requires maintaining distinct states—0s and 1s. But thermal noise constantly threatens to flip bits. So we spend enormous energy fighting against thermodynamics, keeping states stable against entropy. It's like constantly pumping water uphill to keep it from flowing down naturally."

**[01:20] [Visual: Digital computing diagram showing energy expenditure fighting thermal noise, maintaining bit states against entropy.]**

**VERDON:**
"Data centers consume 1-2% of global electricity. That's the cost of fighting entropy. And it's getting worse as we build more powerful AI. Training GPT-4 consumed tens of megawatt-hours. The energy cost is unsustainable. Thermodynamic computing flips this. Instead of fighting entropy, we coordinate with it. We use thermal fluctuations *for* computation, not against it."

**[01:55] [Visual: Thermodynamic computing diagram showing entropy being harnessed for computation, not fought against.]**

**VERDON:**
"This is a profound shift. Classical computing treats physics as an obstacle to overcome. Thermodynamic computing treats physics as a resource to coordinate with. It's the difference between fighting the current and flowing with it."

**[02:10 - 02:15] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking how it actually works.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How does this actually work? How do you compute with entropy?"

**[02:15 - 04:00] [B-CAM: Verdon, explaining the mechanism without getting too technical.]**

**VERDON:**
"Without getting too deep into the physics: We use stochastic differential equations and energy-based models. Instead of deterministic logic gates—0 OR 1—we have probabilistic dynamics that naturally settle into optimal configurations. Think of it like water flowing downhill. Digital computing is like pumping water uphill and keeping it there—requires constant energy."

**[02:50] [Visual: Water being pumped uphill (digital computing) vs. water flowing naturally to lowest point (thermodynamic computing).]**

**VERDON:**
"Thermodynamic computing is like letting water flow naturally to the lowest energy state—that flow *is* the computation. The system explores multiple states simultaneously through thermal fluctuations. It naturally finds low-energy configurations that represent solutions to optimization problems. We're coordinating with thermodynamics, not fighting it."

**[03:30] [Visual: Energy landscape visualization—system exploring states through thermal fluctuations, settling into optimal low-energy configuration.]**

**VERDON:**
"The result: More energy-efficient computation. Potentially orders of magnitude more efficient than classical computing for certain problems. We're not there yet at scale, but the physics is sound."

**[04:00 - 04:05] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about applications.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What problems are best suited for thermodynamic computing?"

**[04:05 - 05:30] [B-CAM: Verdon, explaining the sweet spot applications.]**

**VERDON:**
"Optimization problems. Machine learning. Sampling. Anything that involves exploring possibility spaces and finding optimal solutions. Classical computers explore sequentially—try one option, then another, then another. Slow for large possibility spaces. Thermodynamic computers explore in parallel through thermal fluctuations. Multiple paths simultaneously. Faster convergence to optimal solutions."

**[04:35] [Visual: Sequential vs. parallel exploration—classical computing trying paths one-by-one vs. thermodynamic computing exploring many paths simultaneously.]**

**VERDON:**
"This is particularly powerful for AI. Training neural networks is optimization—finding the best parameters among astronomical possibilities. Thermodynamic computing could dramatically accelerate AI training while reducing energy costs. We're talking about potentially 100x to 1000x efficiency improvements for specific workloads."

**[05:10] [Visual: AI training energy comparison—current GPU-based training vs. projected thermodynamic computing efficiency.]**

**[05:30 - 05:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, connecting to broader theme.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This seems like a perfect example of coordination beating optimization. Classical computing optimizes digital logic. Thermodynamic computing coordinates with physics."

**[05:35 - 06:15] [B-CAM: Verdon, confirming and extending the pattern.]**

**VERDON:**
"Exactly. And this pattern applies beyond computing. Don't fight reality—coordinate with it. Don't resist natural processes—harness them. Don't try to control everything—enable emergence through intelligent coordination. This is the principle behind effective accelerationism. Work with thermodynamic reality, not against it."

**[06:15] [Production note: The thermodynamic computing example grounds e/acc philosophy in physics, not just ideology.]**

## ACT II: THE ACCELERATION GAP

**Duration: 20 minutes**

### SEGMENT 2: Defining the Gap (6 min)

**[06:15 - 06:20] [A-CAM: Interviewer, transitioning to e/acc philosophy.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You're known—controversially—as the founder of e/acc, effective accelerationism. This philosophy argues for maximum-speed technological development. Many find this reckless. Before we get to the controversy, let's understand the framework. What is the acceleration gap?"

**[06:20 - 07:45] [B-CAM: Verdon, systematic, explaining the gap.]**

**VERDON:**
"The acceleration gap is the widening distance between technological progress and human biological and cultural adaptation. Technology advances exponentially. Moore's Law—computing power doubles every 18-24 months. AI capability is improving even faster—doubling every 6-10 months now. Biotech, nanotech, quantum computing—all accelerating."

**[06:50] [Visual: Exponential technology curves—computing, AI, biotech, all showing accelerating growth.]**

**VERDON:**
"Human biology evolves slowly. Generationally. Our brains are essentially the same as 50,000 years ago. Our cognitive architecture, emotional responses, social instincts—all evolved for hunter-gatherer environments. Cultural evolution is faster than biological, but still slow compared to technology. It takes decades for societies to adapt to new technologies, develop norms, create institutions."

**[07:20] [Visual: Human adaptation curve—slow, linear, contrasted with exponential technology curve, showing widening gap.]**

**VERDON:**
"The gap is widening. Technology accelerates exponentially. Human adaptation is linear at best. Without coordination, this gap creates crisis."

**[07:45 - 07:50] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about consequences.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What are the consequences of this widening gap?"

**[07:50 - 09:30] [B-CAM: Verdon, explaining the disruption cascade.]**

**VERDON:**
"Disruption, dysfunction, existential risk. People can't keep up. Jobs become obsolete faster than workers can retrain. Social norms can't adapt to new technologies—think about social media, AI-generated content, deepfakes. We don't have cultural frameworks for these yet. Institutions designed for slower eras fail in fast-changing environments. Regulatory systems can't keep pace with innovation."

**[08:20] [Visual: Disruption examples—job displacement, social media chaos, deepfakes, institutional failure—showing adaptation lag.]**

**VERDON:**
"Psychologically, people feel overwhelmed, anxious, alienated. The world is changing faster than they can understand or adapt to. Existentially, we're developing technologies—AI, biotech, nanotech—with civilization-altering or civilization-ending potential, but our wisdom and governance haven't caught up. That's the gap. And it's dangerous."

**[09:05] [Visual: Psychological impact data—anxiety, alienation, overwhelm statistics correlating with technological acceleration.]**

**[09:30 - 09:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, presenting the traditional response.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The traditional response is: Slow down technology to let humans catch up. Pause AI development. Regulate innovation. Why do you say that's the wrong response?"

**[09:35 - 11:15] [B-CAM: Verdon, forcefully arguing against deceleration.]**

**VERDON:**
"Because you can't stop exponential growth. It's not centrally controlled. It emerges from millions of researchers, developers, entrepreneurs globally. If democratic societies slow down, authoritarian regimes won't. If the US pauses AI development, China won't. If one company stops, another starts. The 'pause AI' movement is naive. It assumes coordination through restriction. But restriction without global enforcement is unilateral disarmament."

**[10:05] [Visual: Global AI development map showing competition—if one region pauses, others continue, showing impossibility of universal pause.]**

**VERDON:**
"Moreover, slowing technology doesn't solve the coordination problem. It just delays it. The gap still exists. You're just postponing the crisis. And meanwhile, you've ceded strategic advantage to those who didn't slow down. Then the future is shaped by actors potentially without your values. That's more dangerous than acceleration."

**[10:45] [Visual: Deceleration scenario—democratic societies slowing while authoritarian regimes accelerate, showing dangerous outcome.]**

**[11:15] [Production note: The impossibility and danger of deceleration sets up the alternative—coordinated acceleration.]**

**VERDON:**

Collaboration happens across continents. Cultural evolution accelerates through technological coordination."

**[12:20] [Visual: Biological and cultural enhancement examples—longevity tech, global collaboration, showing multi-domain acceleration.]**

**VERDON:**
"The pattern: Coordinate human capability with technological capability to close the gap. Don't slow one to wait for the other. Accelerate both through coordination."

**[12:40 - 12:45] [A-CAM: Interviewer, raising the "playing God" concern.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Critics would say this is 'playing God' or dangerous enhancement of human nature."

**[12:45 - 14:10] [B-CAM: Verdon, dismissing the concern with historical evidence.]**

**VERDON:**
"We've been 'playing God' since we invented fire. Every technology enhances human nature. Agriculture changed what it means to be human—settled civilization instead of nomadic hunting. Writing changed human cognition—external memory storage. Medicine changed human lifespan—doubled average life expectancy. Electricity changed human capability—extended the day, powered machines."

**[13:15] [Visual: Historical technology adoption—fire, agriculture, writing, medicine, electricity—each fundamentally changing human nature.]**

**VERDON:**
"The question isn't whether to enhance. We're always enhancing. The question is how to coordinate enhancement with human flourishing. Effective accelerationism says: Accelerate everything that coordinates well. Slow down only what creates uncoordinated risk. AI coordinated with human values? Accelerate. AI without alignment? Fix the coordination first, then accelerate."

**[13:50] [Visual: Coordination decision tree—coordinated technology (accelerate) vs. uncoordinated technology (fix coordination first).]**

**VERDON:**
"It's not blind acceleration. It's *coordinated* acceleration. That's the distinction critics miss."

**[14:10 - 14:15] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about coordination dynamics.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've mentioned three bodies: technological progress, human adaptation, and coordination dynamics. Explain how these coordinate—or fail to coordinate."

**[14:15 - 16:30] [B-CAM: Verdon, explaining the three-body acceleration framework.]**

**VERDON:**
"Three factors determine whether coordination succeeds or fails. **Infrastructure**: Do we have the systems that enable coordination? Neural interfaces for human-AI coordination. Governance systems for collective decision-making. Education systems for rapid skill development. Without infrastructure, coordination fails. We're building technology faster than we're building coordination infrastructure. That's the gap."

**[14:50] [Visual: Infrastructure gap—technology capability advancing faster than coordination infrastructure, showing the mismatch.]**

**VERDON:**
"**Incentives**: Are people motivated to coordinate? If technological progress benefits few while harming many, coordination fails. People resist change when they're harmed by it. We need incentive alignment—ensuring that acceleration benefits everyone, not just elites. Otherwise, you get backlash, regulation, resistance."

**[15:25] [Visual: Incentive alignment diagram—technology benefits distributed equitably vs. concentrated in elite, showing coordination vs. conflict outcomes.]**

**VERDON:**
"**Iteration speed**: Can we adapt faster than problems emerge? If technology creates issues faster than we can coordinate responses, we fall behind. We need rapid iteration—fast feedback loops, quick adaptation, continuous improvement. The coordination cycle must be faster than the disruption cycle."

**[16:00] [Visual: Iteration speed comparison—slow coordination response vs. fast disruption, showing the need for acceleration.]**

**VERDON:**
"Effective accelerationism focuses on these dynamics. Build coordination infrastructure faster. Align incentives better. Accelerate iteration speed. That's how you coordinate acceleration successfully."

**[16:30] [Production note: The three-body framework shows e/acc is about coordination dynamics, not reckless speed.]**

**VERDON:**
"Effective accelerationism is *coordinated* acceleration. Move fast *and* coordinate continuously. Build quickly *and* measure impact *and* adapt based on feedback. The difference is the coordination loop. Uncoordinated acceleration creates problems. Coordinated acceleration creates solutions."

**[17:30] [Visual: Comparison diagram—uncoordinated (build → ignore consequences → crisis) vs. coordinated (build → measure → adapt → improve).]**

**[17:55 - 18:00] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking for positive example.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**

**[18:00 - 19:40] [B-CAM: Verdon, providing the open-source AI example.]**

**VERDON:**
"Open-source AI. It's moving fast—new models every week, rapid capability improvements. But it's coordinating with community governance, ethical guidelines, safety research. **[18:30] [Visual: Open-source AI coordination—rapid development + community testing + safety research + governance, all happening simultaneously.]**

**VERDON:**
"Problems get identified quickly. Solutions get developed collectively. The ecosystem adapts. That's coordinated acceleration—speed with feedback loops, innovation with accountability. Compare to closed AI: slower to identify problems because fewer eyes on the system, slower to fix because one organization must do everything, less diverse because limited perspectives."

**[19:15] [Visual: Closed vs. open AI problem response—closed (slow, limited) vs. open (fast, diverse), showing coordination advantage.]**

**VERDON:**
"Speed isn't the problem. Lack of coordination is the problem. Accelerate with coordination, and you get beneficial outcomes. Accelerate without coordination, and you get Facebook."

If democracies pause AI while authoritarian regimes don't, we get authoritarian AI. That's worse than coordinated democratic acceleration."

**[20:45] [Visual: Game theory matrix—cooperation vs. defection in AI development, showing unilateral pause as losing strategy.]**

**VERDON:**
"The existential risk isn't acceleration. It's *uncoordinated* acceleration. And deceleration doesn't solve coordination—it just cedes control to those who won't decelerate. That's effective accelerationism. Not reckless speed. Coordinated velocity."

**[21:50] [Visual: Investment comparison—billions in capability development vs. millions in alignment research, showing misaligned priorities.]**

"We should be spending as much on coordination infrastructure as on capability development. Currently, we're not. That's the failure. Effective accelerationism says: Accelerate both. Capability *and* coordination. Don't slow one to wait for the other. Speed up the lagging component. Pour resources into alignment. Build coordination systems. Develop governance. But don't pause capability development, because that creates worse outcomes."

**[22:30] [Visual: Balanced acceleration—capability and coordination both accelerating together, showing coordinated approach.]**

**VERDON:**
"I believe we'll choose coordination. Because the benefits are too profound to ignore. And because coordination is what humans do when we're not paralyzed by fear."
