## Interview Overview

Dr. Timnit Gebru, founder of the Distributed AI Research (DAIR) Institute and former co-lead of Google's Ethical AI team, has documented how AI systems that appear technically "fair" can amplify social injustice when they coordinate with existing power structures. Through the lens of facial recognition, algorithmic hiring, and surveillance systems, Gebru reveals why technical fairness without social justice analysis creates systems that are "fair" by narrow metrics while deepening inequality. This interview explores the three bodies of AI injustice and what it means to coordinate AI development with social consciousness.

The technology wasn't neutral. It wasn't just a tool that could be made "fair" with better data. It was encoding existing biases, amplifying existing inequalities, and being deployed in ways that harmed the people it worked worst for. It wasn't just a bug; it was a feature of how these systems were conceived and deployed within existing power structures.

### How "Fair" Algorithms Amplify Inequality

Let me break down the coordination failure systematically. There are three bodies that need to work together, and currently they don't. I often draw them like this.

**Body 1: Technical Systems (The Algorithm Layer)**

This is what AI companies focus on. This is the realm of the engineers, the data scientists. It includes:
*   AI models and training data
*   Optimization objectives and loss functions
*   Performance metrics and benchmarks
*   The algorithms themselves

When companies talk about "fairness," they almost exclusively mean fairness at *this* layer. They're talking about equal accuracy across demographic groups. Balanced error rates. Calibrated predictions. They want the numbers to look good.

And to be clear: Technical fairness matters. If your facial recognition works better on white faces than Black faces, that's a problem. If your hiring algorithm has different accuracy for men and women, that's a problem. We absolutely need to address these technical biases.

But technical fairness is not enough. Not even close. It's a necessary but insufficient condition for justice.

**Body 2: Social Context (The Power Layer)**

This is what AI companies consistently ignore, or actively try to avoid. This layer is about the real-world environment in which the technology is deployed. It asks:
*   Who builds AI and for whom?
*   Whose data, whose values, whose benefit are prioritized?
*   What are the existing inequalities and power structures in society?
*   What is the historical context of structural oppression?

This layer asks: Who has power? Who makes decisions? Whose interests are served by this technology?

Example: Facial recognition might be technically "fair"—meaning it has equal accuracy across demographics. But if it's deployed by police departments with a history of over-policing Black communities, that "fair" system coordinates with existing power structures to amplify harm. The technical fairness doesn't account for the social context, the history of racialized policing, the power imbalance.

**Body 3: Systemic Outcomes (The Impact Layer)**

This is what AI companies externalize. They build, they deploy, and they often ignore or deny the real-world consequences. This layer asks:
*   Who gets harmed, who benefits?
*   How does inequality get amplified or reduced?
*   What are the real-world consequences for real people?
*   What are the feedback loops and long-term effects on communities?

And here's the critical insight: **You cannot predict systemic outcomes from technical fairness alone.** You have to understand how the technical system coordinates with social context to create outcomes.

A "fair" algorithm coordinating with an unfair power structure creates unfair outcomes. Technical fairness without social justice analysis is not just insufficient—it's dangerous, because it gives a veneer of legitimacy to systems that amplify oppression. It allows companies to say, "Look, our algorithm is fair!" while communities suffer.

Let me walk you through exactly how this coordination failure plays out with facial recognition. This is the clearest example I know.

**Phase 1: The Technical Framing (Wrong)**

Early facial recognition systems had accuracy problems:
*   99% accurate on white male faces
*   65% accurate on Black female faces

The technical framing says: "This is a bias problem. We need to fix the algorithm." The solution is purely technical: Get more diverse training data. Retrain the model. Improve accuracy across demographics.

Result: New systems achieve 95% accuracy on Black female faces. Problem solved, right?

Wrong. We've solved the technical problem while potentially making the social problem worse. We've made the tool more efficient for harm.

**Phase 2: The Social Context (What We Ignored)**

Who uses facial recognition?
*   Law enforcement (police departments, ICE, FBI)
*   Corporations (retail stores, employers, landlords)
*   Governments (border control, welfare systems, voting)

Where is it deployed?
*   Disproportionately in Black and brown communities
*   In over-policed neighborhoods
*   At protests and political gatherings
*   In low-income areas, often without consent or even public knowledge.

Why is it deployed?
*   Surveillance and control
*   Identifying "suspects" and "threats"
*   Monitoring and tracking people, often for pre-crime or predictive policing.

The social context: Facial recognition is a surveillance technology deployed primarily to monitor and control marginalized communities by institutions with a history of oppression. This context is critical, and it's almost always ignored by the developers.

**Phase 3: The Systemic Outcomes (The Real Story)**

Now let's coordinate the technical system with the social context to understand the actual outcomes:

**Before technical "fix":**
*   Facial recognition: 65% accurate on Black faces
*   Deployment: Over-policed Black communities
*   Outcome: Some false positives, but also many missed identifications. The technical failure somewhat limited the harm.
*   Harm: Significant but somewhat limited by technical failure.

**After technical "fix":**
*   Facial recognition: 95% accurate on Black faces
*   Deployment: Still over-policed Black communities
*   Outcome: More accurate identification of more people. More efficient surveillance. More arrests.
*   Harm: More arrests, more surveillance, more control. The technical improvement *amplified* the existing harm.

The technical improvement—making the system "fair"—coordinated with existing power structures to amplify harm. More accurate facial recognition means more efficient surveillance of already over-surveilled communities. It means more people are caught in the dragnet, more lives are disrupted, more injustice is perpetrated.

**INTERVIEWER:** So you're saying the technical fix, in this case, made things worse?

**GEBRU:** I'm saying the technical fix *without social justice analysis* made things worse. We improved the tool without changing who uses it, how they use it, or why they use it. We made the weapon more precise without questioning if it should be aimed at these communities at all.

It's like making a more accurate weapon and calling that "fairness." Yes, the weapon now works equally well regardless of the target's race. But if it's being aimed at marginalized communities, that "fairness" just means more efficient oppression. It's a perverse definition of fairness.

**Phase 4: The Three-Body Analysis (The Right Framing)**

The right question isn't: "How do we make facial recognition technically fair?"

The right question is: "How does facial recognition coordinate with power structures to create systemic outcomes?"

This requires asking:

**Before building:**
*   Who will use this?
*   What power do they have?
*   What is their relationship to the communities being surveilled?
*   What is the history of that relationship? (e.g., history of policing in Black communities).

**During deployment:**
*   Where is it actually being deployed?
*   Who is being surveilled and by whom?
*   What are the immediate impacts on individuals and communities?
*   What feedback loops are emerging?

**After deployment:**
*   Who was harmed? Who benefited?
*   Did it amplify or reduce inequality?
*   What were the second-order effects?
*   What did we miss in our initial analysis?

This three-body analysis reveals: Even "fair" facial recognition, when coordinated with oppressive power structures, creates oppressive outcomes. The solution isn't better algorithms. The solution is coordinating technical development with social justice—asking whether we should build this at all, and if so, with what safeguards, accountability, and community control. Sometimes, the answer is simply: don't build it.

Facial recognition is the clearest example, but this pattern repeats everywhere. Let me give you three more.

**Example 1: Algorithmic Hiring**

**Technical layer:** An AI screens resumes, predicts job performance, and is trained on historical hiring data. It optimizes for "best candidates."

**Social context:** That historical data *encodes past discrimination*. "Best candidates" often means "people like those we hired before," who were disproportionately white and male. The algorithm learns to perpetuate these patterns.

**Systemic outcomes:** The algorithm screens out qualified candidates from underrepresented groups. It amplifies existing workplace inequality. It creates a feedback loop: biased hiring leads to biased training data, which leads to more biased hiring.

Amazon tried this. Built an AI hiring system. It learned to downgrade resumes that mentioned "women's" organizations or attended women's colleges. Why? Because historical data showed those candidates were less likely to be hired. The algorithm was technically "accurate"—it correctly predicted who would be hired based on past patterns. But it coordinated past discrimination with present hiring to create future inequality.

**Example 2: Credit Scoring**

**Technical layer:** An AI predicts loan default risk, using hundreds of variables, optimizing for accurate risk assessment.

**Social context:** The algorithm can't legally use race, so it uses proxies: zip code, name, shopping patterns, social networks. These proxies *correlate with race and class* due to structural inequality and historical practices like redlining.

**Systemic outcomes:** It denies loans to people in marginalized communities. It's "objectively" based on risk, but that risk reflects historical oppression and systemic disadvantage. It perpetuates wealth inequality across generations.

The algorithm doesn't "see" race, but it sees everything correlated with race. It's technically "fair"—same algorithm for everyone. But it coordinates historical inequality with present lending to create future disparity.

**Example 3: Content Moderation**

**Technical layer:** An AI flags "harmful" content, trained on labeled examples, optimizing for removing "bad" content while keeping "good" content.

**Social context:** The training data reflects dominant culture's norms. What counts as "harmful" is culturally specific. Marginalized communities use language differently, often reclaiming slurs or using specific vernacular that can be misinterpreted.

**Systemic outcomes:** It disproportionately censors marginalized voices. LGBTQ+ content is flagged as "sexual" when it's simply identity expression. Black vernacular is flagged as "threatening." Political speech from marginalized groups is flagged as "extreme."

The algorithm is technically "accurate"—it correctly identifies content that looks like the "harmful" examples it was trained on. But it coordinates dominant culture's norms with platform power to silence marginalized voices, effectively policing their speech.

**The Pattern Across All Examples:**

This is the fundamental coordination failure:
1.  Technical system optimized for narrow objective.
2.  Deployed in context of existing power imbalances.
3.  Coordinates with power structures to amplify inequality.
4.  Defended as "objective" or "fair" because technically accurate.

Technical fairness without social justice analysis creates systems that are "fair" by one metric while amplifying injustice by every metric that actually matters.

Now let me talk about why this coordination failure persists. It's not just ignorance. It's incentive structure.

**Current AI Development Model:**

**Who benefits:**
*   Tech companies (profit from AI products, market dominance)
*   Investors (returns on AI investments, increased valuations)
*   Privileged users (AI works well for them, enhances their existing power)

**Who bears costs:**
*   Marginalized communities (harmed by AI systems, increased surveillance, discrimination)
*   Workers (exploited in AI supply chain, precarious labor)
*   Environment (damaged by AI energy consumption, resource extraction)

**The coordination failure:**
*   Benefits are internalized (captured by companies and investors).
*   Costs are externalized (borne by communities without power, without a voice).
*   There is no inherent coordination between those who benefit and those who bear costs.

This is exactly like environmental pollution. Companies profit from polluting, communities bear the health costs, and without regulation, companies have no incentive to coordinate their profit-seeking with environmental justice. They will continue to pollute as long as it's profitable.

Same with AI. Companies profit from deploying AI, communities bear the social costs, and without accountability, companies have no incentive to coordinate technical development with social justice. They will continue to deploy harmful systems as long as it's profitable and they face no repercussions.

**INTERVIEWER:** So how do we create that accountability? What mechanisms are needed to force this coordination?

**GEBRU:**
**VISUAL:**
*   **[36:00] A-CAM:** Gebru, outlining solutions with conviction.
We need coordination mechanisms that force those who benefit to coordinate with those who bear costs. We need to shift the power dynamic.

This means:

**Community participation:** People affected by AI systems must coordinate in their development. Not as an afterthought, not as "user testing," but as co-designers with real power, from conception to deployment.

**Impact assessment:** Before deployment, comprehensive analysis of how technical systems will coordinate with social context to create systemic outcomes. And that analysis must be done *with and for* affected communities, not just by internal teams.

**Accountability mechanisms:** Real consequences for harmful coordination. Not just "oops, we'll do better," but legal liability, regulatory enforcement, criminal penalties where appropriate. We need independent oversight.

**Benefit sharing:** If communities bear the costs of AI development, they should share in the benefits. Data dividends, profit sharing, community ownership. This isn't charity; it's justice.

This isn't radical. This is basic justice. Those who profit from a system should coordinate with those who bear its costs. It's about rebalancing power.

Let me paint you a picture of what coordinated AI development looks like. Not hypothetical—we're building this at DAIR right now.

**Before Building: Coordination with Communities**

Traditional approach:
*   Tech company decides what to build.
*   Builds it.
*   Deploys it.
*   Maybe asks for feedback later, as an afterthought.

Coordinated approach:
*   Start with community needs and priorities.
*   Ask: What problems do you face? What solutions do you need?
*   Co-design with people who will be affected, from the very beginning.
*   Build with community participation and oversight at every stage.

**Example:** We're working with communities affected by welfare surveillance systems. Not building "better" surveillance, but working with community members to understand: What do you actually need? What would help vs. harm?

The answer isn't "more accurate fraud detection." It's "systems that ensure people get the benefits they're entitled to without dehumanizing surveillance, without punitive measures."

Completely different problem. Completely different solution. Only visible when you coordinate with affected communities from the start, when you center their lived experience.

**During Development: Coordination Across Layers**

For every AI system, coordinate:

**Technical development:**
*   What algorithms, what data, what metrics?
*   But also: What are we optimizing for and why? What are the ethical implications of these choices?

**Social context analysis:**
*   Who will use this? Who will be affected?
*   What power dynamics does this coordinate with?
*   What is the historical context of this technology and its deployment?

**Outcome monitoring:**
*   What are we measuring beyond technical performance?
*   How do we track disparate impacts on different communities?
*   What feedback loops might emerge, and how do we mitigate negative ones?

All three coordinating throughout development, not technical first and social as an afterthought. It's an iterative, integrated process.

**During Deployment: Ongoing Coordination**

Traditional approach:
*   Deploy and monitor technical metrics: accuracy, latency, uptime.

Coordinated approach:
*   Deploy with community oversight.
*   Monitor social impacts, not just technical performance.
*   Create robust feedback mechanisms for affected communities, with real power to influence.
*   Be ready to shut down if coordination fails, if the system is causing unacceptable harm.

**Example:** If we deploy a system and discover it's harming marginalized communities, we don't just "fix the bias." We ask: Should this system exist at all? Can it be coordinated with justice, or is the harm inherent?

Sometimes the answer is: This system cannot be coordinated with justice. Then we shut it down. That's a difficult but necessary decision.

**After Deployment: Accountability and Iteration**

Continuous coordination:

**Impact assessment:**
*   What actually happened?
*   Who was harmed? Who benefited?
*   Did outcomes match intentions?
*   What did we miss in our initial analysis?

**Community feedback:**
*   Regular check-ins with affected communities.
*   Power to raise concerns and demand changes.
*   Real consequences for ignoring feedback.

**Iterative improvement:**
*   Not just technical iteration.
*   But coordination iteration.
*   How do we better coordinate technical capability with social justice?

This is ongoing work, not a one-time fix. It requires constant vigilance and a commitment to justice.
