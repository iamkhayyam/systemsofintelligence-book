## ACT I: WHAT IN-Q-TEL IS — AND WHY IT HAD TO BE INVENTED

**Duration:** 12 minutes
**Objective:** Establish In-Q-Tel as a coordination architecture — not a spy agency investment arm, but a solution to a genuine three-body coordination failure

### Opening [0:00-3:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** In-Q-Tel is often described as the CIA's venture capital arm. That framing makes it sound like the intelligence community is just trying to make money. What's the actual problem it was designed to solve?

**ANONYMOUS:** The problem is a timing and language mismatch. The intelligence community operates on classified requirements, multi-year procurement cycles, and security constraints that make commercial partnerships nearly impossible. The commercial technology world operates on 18-month product cycles, open development, and market signals that have nothing to do with national security needs.

Before In-Q-Tel, the gap between these two worlds was widening every year. The intelligence community would identify a need. They'd go through the procurement process. By the time a contract was awarded and a system was built, the commercial technology had advanced three generations and the classified system was already obsolete.

Meanwhile, commercial companies were building exactly what the intelligence community needed — but didn't know it. They had no visibility into classified requirements. The intelligence community had no mechanism for telling them.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** So you had two bodies that needed each other but couldn't find each other.

**ANONYMOUS:** Precisely. And the reason they couldn't find each other wasn't incompetence. It was structural. The classification system exists for good reason. You can't broadcast what the intelligence community needs — the adversary reads the same newspapers, monitors the same procurement databases. But if you can't signal need, you can't attract supply. That's the market failure In-Q-Tel was invented to solve. We're the C — the coordination layer that translates between the intelligence community and the commercial sector without compromising either.

**[02:00] [Visual: The timing mismatch diagram — government procurement cycle (5-7 years) vs. commercial development cycle (18 months). The gap growing over time. In-Q-Tel as the coordination bridge.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** Walk me through what the landscape looked like in 1999, before In-Q-Tel existed. What was actually failing?

**ANONYMOUS:** Let me give you an example I can discuss because it's in the public record. In the late 1990s, the intelligence community recognized that geospatial intelligence — satellite imagery, overhead reconnaissance — was going to require commercial-grade processing. The classified systems were custom-built, expensive, and slow to update. Meanwhile, startups in Silicon Valley were building geographic information systems for commercial applications — urban planning, logistics, environmental monitoring. The capabilities were converging. But there was no mechanism for the intelligence community to say, "That thing you're building for FedEx — we need something like that, with different requirements we can't discuss."

The traditional procurement response would have been: write a classified specification, clear a defense contractor, build something from scratch. Five years, hundreds of millions of dollars, and by the time it was deployed, the commercial version was better.

In-Q-Tel was designed to short-circuit that entire failure mode.

### Segment 1: The Translation Problem [3:00-8:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** So the core function is translation. But how do you translate between a classified world and a commercial world when the classified side can't reveal what it actually needs?

**ANONYMOUS:** That's the art. And I want to be careful about how I describe this, because the art itself is part of what we protect.

The general principle: most classified problems are structurally isomorphic to commercial problems. The domains are different. The data types are different. The stakes are different. But the underlying coordination challenge — the shape of the problem — is often the same.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** Can you give me an example?

**ANONYMOUS:** I'll use ones that are public. After September 11th, the intelligence community's most urgent need was connecting data across agencies that had never shared information. The classified version of that problem involves connecting signals intelligence, human intelligence, imagery intelligence, and open source intelligence databases that were designed — sometimes deliberately — to be incompatible.

The unclassified version? Enterprise data integration. Connecting a company's sales database to its logistics database to its customer service records to its financial reporting. Same structural problem: disparate data sources, different schemas, different access controls, different cultures of use. The shape is identical.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And that's where Palantir comes in.

**ANONYMOUS:** Palantir is the public example, yes. They were solving the commercial version — fraud detection, financial analytics, enterprise data integration. In-Q-Tel recognized the structural isomorphism. The investment was made. The intelligence community adopted a version of what Palantir was building commercially.

The classified need was never disclosed to the company's engineering team in its original form. It didn't need to be. Because the commercial problem and the classified problem had the same solution architecture.

**[05:30] [Visual: The isomorphic problem diagram — classified need on one side, commercial application on the other, In-Q-Tel's translation in the middle. The same coordination structure, different domains.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** That's elegant in theory. But in practice, there must be cases where the isomorphism isn't clean — where the classified need has wrinkles the commercial version doesn't.

**ANONYMOUS:** Constantly. And that's where the coordination gets subtle. The commercial version gives you the 80 percent solution. The last 20 percent — the adaptation to adversarial environments, the hardening against deception, the performance under conditions the commercial market never encounters — that's where classified work happens, with cleared engineers, inside secure facilities. But you're building on a foundation that was developed at commercial speed, with commercial investment, by commercial engineers. The intelligence community couldn't have built that foundation alone. Not at that pace. Not at that scale.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** You mentioned the classification constraint — that the company's engineers can't get clearances fast enough. How does that actually work in practice?

**ANONYMOUS:** A Top Secret clearance takes, on average, nine months to a year. For some compartmented programs, longer. A startup engineer in San Francisco cannot wait a year to begin a conversation about a project. They'll have changed jobs twice by then. So the traditional model — clear everyone, bring them inside the fence — is incompatible with the velocity of commercial development.

Our model inverts that. We don't bring the engineers inside the classified world. We bring the classified need outside, in translated form, into the commercial world. The engineer never needs to know the classified application. They solve their commercial problem. We ensure the commercial problem is structured so its solution maps to the classified need.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** That requires extraordinary pattern recognition on your side — seeing the shape of a classified problem inside a commercial product roadmap.

**ANONYMOUS:** That's what we hire for. The people who work at In-Q-Tel have an unusual profile. They need to understand both worlds deeply enough to see the structural correspondence. An investment partner at In-Q-Tel needs to read a classified requirements document from the intelligence community and then walk into a pitch meeting in Palo Alto and recognize that the startup's natural language processing tool for customer sentiment analysis is, at a structural level, the same problem as the classified requirement they read that morning. That's not a common skill set.

**[07:00] [Visual: The Keyhole/Google Earth example — the satellite imagery analysis need (classified) mapped to the commercial geographic information system (unclassified). The same coordination structure producing Google Earth and intelligence community capability simultaneously.]**

### Segment 2: What Makes It Work [8:00-12:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** In-Q-Tel has been operating for 25 years now. Most government-Silicon Valley partnerships fail spectacularly. What's different about this one?

**ANONYMOUS:** Three things. And I'll describe them as coordination principles, because that's what they are.

First — patience as strategy. We invest before the need is urgent. In the intelligence community, if you wait until the crisis to find the technology, you've already lost. Our investment horizon is three to five years before the intelligence community will formally require the capability. By the time the need is acute, the company is mature, the product is tested, the relationship is established. We've already done the coordination work. The crisis hits, and the coordination architecture is already in place.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** That runs counter to everything in venture capital, which is about urgency and speed to market.

**ANONYMOUS:** It does. And that's why we're structured as a not-for-profit. We don't need to return capital to LPs on a ten-year fund cycle. We don't need to optimize for the fastest exit. We can afford to be patient, because our return on investment isn't measured in equity multiples. It's measured in whether the intelligence community has the capability it needs when it needs it. Patience is the competitive advantage. You can't rush coordination. You can only prepare the architecture so that when the signal arrives, the coordination happens quickly.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** What's the second principle?

**ANONYMOUS:** We provide more than money. We provide non-dilutive support, which matters to startups that don't want to give up equity. But more importantly, we provide something no commercial VC can offer: we provide the intelligence community as a customer. Not publicly — but a demanding, sophisticated, technically literate customer who will stress-test the product in ways the commercial market never would.

A commercial customer wants the product to work under normal conditions. We test it under adversarial conditions. We find the failure modes the commercial market would never discover. And here's the part that surprises people: this makes the product better for everyone. The robustness testing we put a product through — the edge cases, the adversarial inputs, the failure scenario analysis — all of that feeds back into the commercial product. The company's other customers benefit from the intelligence community's rigor without ever knowing it was applied.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And the third?

**ANONYMOUS:** We don't require exclusivity. The company sells to whoever it wants — commercial customers, other governments, our competitors. This is counterintuitive. Why would the intelligence community invest in a company and then let it sell to everyone?

Because a company that only serves the intelligence community becomes a captive contractor. It stops competing in the commercial market. It stops innovating. It becomes dependent on classified funding, which makes it brittle. The commercial market is the engine that keeps the technology alive, keeps it advancing, keeps it relevant. We need the company to stay commercial. The coordination depends on it.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** So the three-body structure is: intelligence community needs, In-Q-Tel's translation and investment, and the commercial market that keeps the whole system alive.

**ANONYMOUS:** Exactly. Remove any one of the three and the coordination collapses. Remove In-Q-Tel and the intelligence community can't access commercial technology at speed. Remove the commercial market and the technology stagnates. Remove the intelligence community need and there's no signal to coordinate around. It's a genuinely three-body system. None of the components is sufficient alone. The coordination is what produces the capability.

## ACT II: HOW THE COORDINATION ACTUALLY WORKS

**Duration:** 16 minutes
**Objective:** The mechanics of In-Q-Tel's coordination work — and what it reveals about the structure of technology adoption

### Segment 3: Finding the Right Companies [12:00-18:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** Let's talk about the selection process. A normal VC looks at market size, team, product-market fit, growth metrics. What does In-Q-Tel look for that a commercial investor doesn't?

**ANONYMOUS:** Everything a commercial VC looks for — plus two additional tests that most investors have never considered.

The first is what I'll call the adversarial test. Most commercial technology is built for cooperative environments. Users who want the product to work. Data that is accurate and complete. Conditions that are stable and predictable. That's the assumption underlying virtually every product in Silicon Valley.

Intelligence community use cases are fundamentally adversarial. The adversary doesn't want your technology to work. The data may be deliberately misleading. The conditions are designed to break your assumptions. Every input could be a deception. Every signal could be noise crafted to look like signal.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** So you're looking for companies whose technology can withstand hostile use.

**ANONYMOUS:** We're looking for companies whose architecture was built with some awareness that the environment might not be cooperative. Sometimes that's explicit — cybersecurity companies, for instance, are built adversarial by design. But often it's implicit. A company doing anomaly detection in financial transactions is already dealing with adversarial inputs — fraudsters designing transactions to evade detection. A company doing content authentication is already in an adversarial environment — deepfakes, synthetic media, manipulated imagery.

These companies have already confronted a version of the problem the intelligence community faces. Their engineering reflects it. Their architectures are more resilient. They've thought about adversarial inputs at the design level, not as an afterthought.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** What's the second test?

**ANONYMOUS:** Generalization. And this one is harder to assess. Most commercial technology is optimized for its training distribution — it works brilliantly on problems that resemble the data it was built on. Intelligence work constantly encounters out-of-distribution problems. New adversaries with new techniques. Novel situations with no precedent. Operating environments that shift without warning.

A technology that can only perform within its training distribution is operationally useless in an intelligence context. It will work in the test environment and fail in the field. We've seen this repeatedly.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** This sounds closely related to what Francois Chollet has described with the ARC Prize — the difference between narrow pattern matching and genuine generalization.

**ANONYMOUS:** I'm familiar with Chollet's work. And yes, there's a deep structural parallel. What Chollet is measuring — the ability to solve novel problems that require abstraction beyond training data — is precisely what the intelligence community needs from its technology. We've been selecting against brittleness for 25 years. We didn't use Chollet's vocabulary, but we were testing for the same property. Can this system handle something it hasn't seen before? If the answer is no, we're not interested. A system that memorizes its training distribution is a liability in the field, no matter how impressive the benchmarks look.

**[15:00] [Visual: The In-Q-Tel investment selection framework — commercial criteria plus adversarial robustness plus generalization. The overlap with Chollet's ARC Prize criteria is noted.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** What does this selection process produce across a whole portfolio?

**ANONYMOUS:** A portfolio of companies that are more robust than their commercial peers. Not necessarily more capable — more resilient. And here's something that surprises people outside the intelligence community: the companies that survive our adversarial evaluation tend to outperform commercially too. The venture returns on In-Q-Tel's public portfolio are strong by any standard.

Why? Because adversarial robustness is a proxy for genuine generalization capability. A system that can withstand adversarial inputs is a system that has learned something real about its domain — not just the statistical shortcuts that work on benchmark datasets. The commercial market rewards that kind of robustness too. It just doesn't test for it explicitly the way we do.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** So the intelligence community's paranoia, for lack of a better word, is actually good product discipline.

**ANONYMOUS:** I'd say rigor rather than paranoia. But yes. The assumption that the environment is adversarial forces engineering discipline that cooperative-environment assumptions don't require. It's an expensive way to build software. But it produces systems that actually work when the stakes are real.

### Segment 4: The Dual-Use Tension [18:00-23:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** The technologies In-Q-Tel invests in end up being used commercially by billions of people. Google Earth came from intelligence community investment. Palantir is used by governments and corporations worldwide. Is that a feature or a problem?

**ANONYMOUS:** Both. Simultaneously. And I want to be honest about that, because the temptation in my position is to emphasize the positive and minimize the negative. Let me resist that temptation.

The positive loop is real and powerful. Intelligence community investment forces technology to solve hard problems. Hard problems produce more robust solutions. Those solutions enter the commercial market and serve billions of users. The intelligence community benefits because it can draw on commercial scale — the massive R&D investment, the talent pool, the rapid iteration cycles — that it could never fund alone. Commercial customers benefit because intelligence community requirements forced a level of rigor into the product that no commercial customer would have demanded. Everyone wins.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And the other side?

**ANONYMOUS:** Technologies built for intelligence community applications can be — and have been — repurposed for surveillance of civilians. This is documented. Palantir has been used for immigration enforcement in ways that raise serious civil liberties questions. Facial recognition technology, developed in part for identifying adversaries in operational environments, gets deployed against domestic protesters. Predictive analytics tools designed to anticipate terrorist activity get applied to predictive policing in communities that never consented to that surveillance.

The coordination architecture that makes intelligence community capability possible — the one I've been describing for the last twenty minutes — has a structural gap. The three bodies in our model are the intelligence community, In-Q-Tel, and the commercial market. Notice who's not in that architecture: the public. Civil liberties. Democratic accountability. The people who are affected by how these technologies are ultimately deployed.

**[20:00] [Visual: The dual-use technology loop — intelligence community investment, commercial development, broader deployment. The governance gap as the third body missing from the loop.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** Is that something In-Q-Tel can address, or is it outside your mandate?

**ANONYMOUS:** Our mandate is national security. That's clear and explicit. We are not a civil liberties organization. We are not a governance body. We invest in technologies that protect the country. The question of how those technologies are subsequently governed in the commercial sphere — that is genuinely outside our mandate.

But I'll say this: the absence of a governance coordination layer is itself a coordination failure. And we are in the business of recognizing coordination failures. The same three-body framework that explains why In-Q-Tel was necessary — a gap between two bodies that couldn't coordinate — explains why dual-use governance is necessary. The capability body and the deployment body are coordinated. The democratic accountability body is not in the loop. That's a structural gap, and structural gaps get exploited. By adversaries, by commercial interests, by governments — including our own.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** Does the AI version of this tension worry you more than previous technology waves?

**ANONYMOUS:** Significantly more. And here's why. Previous dual-use technologies — satellite imagery, data analytics, natural language processing — had relatively constrained application spaces. You needed specific infrastructure, specific data, specific expertise to deploy them at scale.

AI is general-purpose. The same large language model that helps the intelligence community analyze foreign language documents can generate synthetic propaganda at scale. The same computer vision system that identifies military equipment in satellite imagery can track individuals through a city. The same reasoning engine that connects intelligence dots can automate surveillance of political dissidents. The generality is the problem. Previous technologies were dual-use. AI is omni-use.

And the governance architecture — the coordination between capability and democratic accountability — is not just a step behind. It's a generation behind. Wiener identified this problem in 1950. We've had seventy-five years to build the coordination layer. We haven't.

### Segment 5: The 25-Year View [23:00-28:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** In-Q-Tel has been operating for 25 years. You have a longitudinal view that almost nobody else has. How has the coordination challenge evolved?

**ANONYMOUS:** Three distinct phases. And they correspond to different failure modes.

1999 to roughly 2008: the problem was access. Commercial technology was developing capabilities the intelligence community needed, but there was no coordination mechanism between classified needs and commercial development. In-Q-Tel solved the access problem. We built the bridge. That was the first decade.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And the bridge worked.

**ANONYMOUS:** It worked well. The Keyhole investment is the paradigm case. The intelligence community needed commercial satellite imagery processing. A startup in San Francisco had the technology. We made the investment in 2003. Google acquired the company in 2004. The intelligence community got its capability. Google got Google Earth. The coordination was clean.

2008 to roughly 2019: the problem shifted from access to pace. Mobile computing, cloud infrastructure, social media, the app economy — commercial technology began moving faster than the intelligence community could absorb it. Every quarter, the relevant technology landscape shifted. In-Q-Tel expanded to handle higher volume and faster cycles. We went from making a handful of investments a year to dozens.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And now?

**ANONYMOUS:** 2020 to the present. The problem is no longer access or pace. The problem is coordination at scale. AI is developing faster than any previous technology wave. The number of relevant companies isn't in the dozens — it's in the thousands. The dual-use implications are broader and more immediate than anything we've seen. The adversarial exploitation of AI capabilities is happening in near-real-time, not over years.

And our model — patient investment, careful bilateral translation, relationship-building over years — is under pressure. Because the fundamental assumption of our model is that we have time. Time to identify the right companies. Time to build relationships. Time to translate needs. Time to test, adopt, integrate. AI is compressing all of those timelines simultaneously.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** Is the model breaking?

**ANONYMOUS:** I wouldn't say breaking. I'd say being stress-tested at a level its designers didn't anticipate. The patience that was our competitive advantage assumed a certain velocity of technological change. When that velocity exceeds the coordination architecture's capacity to respond, patience becomes a liability. You're being patient while the adversary is being fast.

**[26:00] [Visual: The three eras — access (1999), pace (2010), scale (2020). Each era's coordination challenge mapped to the In-Q-Tel response. The current era's challenge: the gap between the model's pace and AI development's pace.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** So what does the coordination architecture need to become?

**ANONYMOUS:** That's the question we're actively working on, and I'm limited in what I can say. But conceptually: the patient investor model was designed for a world where the intelligence community could identify a need three to five years before it became urgent. In the AI domain, the need-to-urgency timeline is compressing to months. Sometimes weeks. The coordination architecture needs to match that velocity without sacrificing the rigor that makes it work.

How you do that — how you maintain the adversarial testing, the careful translation, the non-disclosure of classified requirements, all at ten times the speed — that's the institutional challenge. And I'll be honest: I don't think we've solved it yet. We're adapting. We haven't fully adapted.

## ACT III: WHAT THE INTELLIGENCE COMMUNITY SEES IN AI THAT OTHERS DON'T

**Duration:** 14 minutes
**Objective:** The specific AI coordination failures visible from the intelligence community's vantage point

### Segment 6: Adversarial AI in the Wild [28:00-36:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** The intelligence community watches adversaries use and abuse AI in real time. What are you seeing that the commercial AI world isn't?

**ANONYMOUS:** I need to be careful here, because the specific intelligence is classified. But I can describe the categories, because the categories themselves are known.

The first category: synthetic media for influence operations. And I want to be precise about what this means, because the public conversation about deepfakes is missing the real threat. The real threat is not a convincing deepfake of a world leader. Those are detectable, they get debunked, they're dramatic but tactically limited.

The real threat is hyper-targeted synthetic content at scale. A foreign intelligence service — and I'm not specifying which one — can now generate thousands of unique, culturally specific, psychologically targeted messages for specific communities within a target country. Each message uses the right tone, the right cultural references, the right emotional triggers for that specific audience segment. Each message is unique, so no single detection algorithm catches the pattern. The volume overwhelms any manual review process.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** How is this different from traditional propaganda?

**ANONYMOUS:** Scale and specificity. Traditional influence operations required human operators who understood the target culture. You needed native speakers, cultural experts, people who could craft messages that resonated. That constrained the scale. You could target broad audiences with crude messaging, or narrow audiences with sophisticated messaging. You couldn't do both.

AI eliminates the scaling constraint. You can now have sophisticated, culturally nuanced, psychologically targeted messaging at a volume that was previously impossible. The three-body coordination that makes this work is: AI generation capability, targeting intelligence about the audience, and the information ecosystem vulnerabilities that allow the content to reach its targets. Adversaries have coordinated all three. Defenders have not.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** What about the technical attacks — adversarial inputs, data poisoning?

**ANONYMOUS:** This is the category that should terrify the commercial AI world and largely doesn't. Adversarial examples — inputs specifically designed to fool AI classifiers in ways that are invisible to human observers — are not a theoretical concern. They are deployed in the field. I can't tell you by whom or against what systems. But this is happening now, against operational systems, by sophisticated actors who understand exactly how to exploit the gap between a model's training distribution and its deployment environment.

Data poisoning is the slower, more insidious version. Corrupting the training data — or the fine-tuning data, or the reinforcement learning signal — to degrade model performance or insert backdoors. This doesn't require access to the model's architecture. It requires access to the data the model learns from. And in an era of web-scraped training data, the attack surface for data poisoning is effectively the entire internet.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And model extraction — querying public APIs to reconstruct proprietary models?

**ANONYMOUS:** That's a known technique, yes. If you can query a model repeatedly, you can build a shadow model that approximates its behavior. It's not perfect reconstruction. But it's sufficient for understanding the model's decision boundaries, which is sufficient for crafting adversarial inputs that exploit those boundaries. The coordination failure here is straightforward: public model access was designed for cooperative users. Adversaries are not cooperative users.

**[32:00] [Visual: The influence operation three-body diagram — AI generation capability (A), targeting intelligence (B), information ecosystem vulnerability (C). The adversarial coordination that exploits the gap.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** What's the current state of defense against all of this?

**ANONYMOUS:** I'll give you the honest assessment, which is not the reassuring one.

Detection models: it's an arms race, and the offense currently has the advantage. Generating synthetic content is cheaper and faster than detecting it. The asymmetry favors the attacker.

Platform coordination: slow, fragmented, and jurisdictionally complicated. Platforms have different incentives, different technical capabilities, different legal obligations, and different relationships with government. Coordinating a coherent defense across platforms is a governance problem as much as a technical one.

Public awareness: improving, but lagging behind the capability curve by years. People know deepfakes exist. They don't understand that the real threat is the content that doesn't look fake because it was generated to be indistinguishable from authentic human expression.

The honest assessment — the one I would give in a classified briefing if I were still giving classified briefings — is that the coordination architecture for defending information ecosystems against AI-enabled influence operations does not yet exist at the required scale. The pieces are there. They are not coordinated.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** That's a striking admission from someone inside the intelligence community.

**ANONYMOUS:** The intelligence community's job is accurate assessment, not reassurance. Accurate assessment sometimes means acknowledging that the coordination you need doesn't exist yet.

### Segment 7: What Should Be Built [36:00-42:00]

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** From 25 years of coordinating intelligence community needs with commercial technology — what coordination architectures does the AI ecosystem most urgently need that it doesn't currently have?

**ANONYMOUS:** Three things. And I'll rank them by urgency, not by feasibility.

First — adversarial robustness as a baseline, not as a feature. Every AI system deployed in any context where adversaries exist — and that includes every public-facing system — needs to be tested against adversarial inputs before deployment. Not as an afterthought. Not as a compliance checkbox. As a design requirement, baked into the development process from the beginning.

The intelligence community learned this the hard way with every previous technology wave. Cryptographic systems that were tested only against cooperative inputs. Communications systems that assumed benign network conditions. Surveillance systems that assumed the targets wouldn't adapt. Every time, the adversary exploited the gap between the cooperative assumption and the adversarial reality. Every time, we rebuilt with adversarial robustness included. Every time, it was more expensive after the failure than it would have been to build it in from the start.

AI is on the same trajectory. The commercial AI industry is currently in the "cooperative assumption" phase. The adversarial exploitation phase is already beginning. The rebuild phase will be expensive and painful.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** What's the second?

**ANONYMOUS:** The dual-use governance layer. I described the gap earlier — the coordination architecture between AI capability and democratic accountability doesn't match the deployment architecture. The technologies In-Q-Tel has funded over 25 years — satellite imagery analysis, pattern recognition, natural language processing, behavioral prediction — are now in commercial products used by billions of people. The governance architecture for those technologies has not kept pace with the deployment architecture.

And I want to be precise about what I mean by governance here. I don't mean regulation. Regulation is a single tool in a governance architecture. What's needed is a coordination layer — a C body in the three-body framework — that sits between capability and deployment and ensures that democratic accountability is in the loop for decisions about how these technologies are used.

The three bodies: capability development is A. Deployment context is B. Democratic accountability is C. Right now, A and B are tightly coordinated. C is not in the architecture at all for most AI deployment decisions. That's a structural failure, and it's going to produce consequences proportional to the capabilities involved.

**[39:00] [Visual: The three bodies of AI governance — capability (A), deployment (B), democratic accountability (C). C disconnected from the coordination loop.]**

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** And the third?

**ANONYMOUS:** The pace coordination problem. This is the one that keeps me awake. AI development is moving faster than the institutions designed to coordinate with it. Congress cannot legislate at AI speed. Courts cannot adjudicate at AI speed. International bodies cannot negotiate at AI speed. Intelligence agencies cannot assess at AI speed — and we're supposedly the fast-moving part of government.

The coordination architecture between AI development pace and institutional response capacity is the most urgent unsolved problem in this space. And it's not a technology problem. It's a structural problem. The institutions were designed for a world where technology changed slowly enough for deliberation, debate, and democratic process. That world may no longer exist.

In-Q-Tel's patient investor model was one response to a version of this problem. We demonstrated that you could coordinate government needs with commercial pace if you built the right coordination architecture. But our model was designed for a world where the pace gap was manageable — years, not weeks. The current pace gap may be beyond what any existing coordination architecture can handle.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** That sounds like you're describing a coordination failure that doesn't have an obvious solution.

**ANONYMOUS:** I'm describing a coordination failure that doesn't have an existing solution. Whether it has a possible solution is a different question. I believe it does. But it requires institutional innovation at a pace that institutions are not historically capable of. The coordination architecture needs to evolve as fast as the technology it's trying to coordinate. That's a bootstrapping problem — you need the thing you're trying to build in order to build it.

**INTERVIEWER (B-CAM):** How do you think about that in terms of the intelligence community's posture? What do you carry forward from this vantage point?

**ANONYMOUS:** We have been watching coordination failures in technology for 25 years. We have seen, in classified detail, what happens when capability outpaces the coordination architecture designed to govern it. We've seen it in adversary states. We've seen it in allied states. We've seen it in our own operations.

AI is not unique in kind. It is the same pattern, running faster, at higher stakes, with broader impact. The intelligence community has one advantage in this environment — maybe the only advantage that matters. We have already seen the adversarial use cases that the commercial world hasn't imagined yet. We know what a sufficiently motivated adversary will do with these capabilities, because we've watched them begin.

The question — the only question that matters — is whether we can coordinate that knowledge into the commercial development process before the adversaries coordinate their exploitation against us. That's a race. And I'm not confident we're winning it.
