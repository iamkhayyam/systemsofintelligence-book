**EDITORIAL NOTE:**
*Rupert Sheldrake is one of science's most controversial figures. His theory of morphic resonance — that nature has a kind of memory that influences the development of form and behavior — has been rejected by mainstream science. His books have been called pseudoscience. He has also been right about several things the mainstream dismissed (including plant neurobiological signaling) and wrong about others. This interview treats his ideas seriously without endorsing them, and presents them as the most provocative challenge to the dominant computational paradigm — a challenge that remains unanswered.*

## ACT I: WHAT MORPHIC FIELDS ARE

**Duration:** 12 minutes

### Opening [0:45-3:30]

**[0:45]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Rupert, morphic fields and morphic resonance — define these as precisely as you can. Because these are terms you coined, and they carry a great deal of weight, both for your supporters and your critics."

**[1:02]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"A morphic field is a field that gives shape and organization to material systems. Like electromagnetic fields organize charged particles, morphic fields organize the development of biological systems, the behavior of animals, the crystallization of chemicals. Every self-organizing system has a morphic field — an atom, a molecule, a crystal, a cell, an organ, an organism, a social group. The field is what holds the pattern together.

Now, the key property that distinguishes morphic fields from ordinary physical fields is morphic resonance. Morphic resonance is the influence of previous similar things on subsequent similar things. When a new crystal forms, it is influenced by all previous crystals of that type — not through any known physical mechanism, but through morphic resonance across time."

**[1:48]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Give me the practical consequence. If morphic resonance is real, what should we observe that we wouldn't observe otherwise?"

**[1:55]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The practical consequence is straightforward: it should be easier to do something that has been done before, even without direct learning. If rats learn a maze in one laboratory, rats in other laboratories should learn it faster — not because someone trained them, not because of genetic change, but because the morphic field for that particular maze-running behavior has been strengthened by the first group's experience. If humans practice a skill, subsequent humans should find it easier to learn, even without direct instruction. The pattern is in the field, available to anything that resonates with it.

This is obviously controversial because it doesn't fit within the current physical paradigm. It implies that information is not only stored in physical structures — genes, brains, synaptic connections — but in fields that have a temporal dimension. The past is not gone. It is present, in the form of cumulative morphic resonance."

**[2:42]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the word 'morphic' itself — you chose it deliberately."

**[2:46]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"From the Greek morphe, meaning form. These are fields of form. They shape things. The biologist Hans Driesch spoke of entelechy in the early twentieth century — an organizing principle in living things. The embryologist C.H. Waddington spoke of chreodes, developmental pathways that organisms follow. I'm extending that tradition, but with a specific mechanism — resonance across time — and a specific prediction: the more something happens, the more likely it is to happen again, through field effects rather than through material causation alone."

### Segment 1: The Evidence [3:30-8:00]

**[3:30]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let us deal with the evidence head-on. What is the evidence for morphic resonance? And I want you to be fair to both sides — the evidence for, and the evidence against."

**[3:42]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I appreciate the question, and I think it's the right way to proceed. Let me take them one at a time.

First: crystal formation. It is well established in chemistry that new compounds are often difficult to crystallize for the first time. Chemists sometimes spend months or years coaxing a new compound into crystal form. But once the compound has been crystallized in one laboratory, it tends to crystallize more easily everywhere — even in sealed containers where no seed crystals could have been introduced. The standard explanation is that microscopic fragments of crystal travel on the clothing and beards of visiting scientists, or through the air. I find this explanation implausible for sealed systems. Morphic resonance offers an alternative: once the crystal pattern has been established, the morphic field for that crystal type makes it easier for subsequent crystals to form."

**[4:32]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the critics would say — trace contamination is more parsimonious than invoking an entirely new type of field."

**[4:39]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"They would, and I take that objection seriously. The contamination hypothesis is testable. You would need truly isolated crystallization chambers with rigorous controls. To my knowledge, the definitive experiment has not been done. And that is itself a data point — the experiment could be done, but the scientific establishment has shown no interest in doing it. The dismissal has been theoretical, not experimental."

**[5:02]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What about the animal learning data?"

**[5:05]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Second line of evidence: animal learning. In the 1920s and 1930s, William McDougall at Harvard trained rats to navigate a water maze. Over successive generations, the rats learned faster — which you might expect from selection. But the crucial finding was that the control rats — the unselected lines — also learned faster over the same period. Subsequent researchers in Edinburgh and Melbourne found the same pattern. Rats were learning faster even when there was no selection for learning ability.

The mainstream explanations include improved experimental technique, inadvertent selection, and statistical artifacts. These are all possible. But the pattern across three independent laboratories, over decades, is suggestive. If morphic resonance is real, you would expect exactly this: the more rats that learn a particular maze, the easier it becomes for all subsequent rats of that species."

**[5:55]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Human skill acquisition — you've made claims there as well."

**[6:00]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Some skills do seem to be learned faster now than when first introduced. IQ test scores have risen globally over decades — the Flynn effect. The standard explanation involves improved nutrition, education, and familiarity with test-taking. But there is a residual improvement that these factors don't fully account for. I wouldn't claim this as strong evidence for morphic resonance — it's merely consistent with it.

The strongest biological evidence, in my view, comes from morphogenetic fields in embryology. If you remove half of an early sea urchin embryo, the remaining half can develop into a complete, smaller organism. If you fuse two early embryos, you can get one normal-sized organism. The developmental program somehow adjusts. The embryo knows what the whole should look like and compensates. This is the regulation problem, and mainstream developmental biology has made real progress with gradient fields and positional information — the work of Lewis Wolpert and others. But the fundamental question of what carries the target form, the wholeness that the embryo develops toward, remains incompletely answered."

**[6:58]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So what is the honest assessment? If you were advising a young scientist on this, what would you tell them?"

**[7:04]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I would say: I don't claim this is proven. I claim it is a serious hypothesis that explains observed phenomena better than the alternatives in some cases, and that it deserves rigorous testing rather than dismissal. The dismissal without testing is itself a data point about the sociology of science. The contamination hypothesis for crystals can be tested. The rat learning effect can be tested with modern controls. The extended mind experiments can be replicated with improved protocols. What I find remarkable is not that my theory is controversial — controversial theories should be controversial — but that the controversy has produced so little experimental engagement. The response has been primarily rhetorical, not empirical."

### Segment 2: The Coordination Challenge He Poses [8:00-12:00]

**[8:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What does mainstream science fail to explain that morphic resonance explains better? Where are the genuine gaps?"

**[8:10]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Let me take three cases and be honest about the current state of each.

The protein folding problem. Proteins fold from linear amino acid chains into complex three-dimensional structures. The number of possible configurations is astronomically large — Levinthal's paradox. The protein folds in milliseconds. How does it navigate this vast space so quickly? My suggestion was that morphic resonance from all previous proteins of that type guides the folding. The protein doesn't search — it resonates with the accumulated pattern."

**[8:42]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"But AlphaFold has changed that picture substantially."

**[8:46]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"It has. And I want to be candid about this. AlphaFold demonstrates that the folding problem can be solved computationally — that the amino acid sequence contains enough information, when properly analyzed, to predict the three-dimensional structure. This is a significant challenge to my position on this particular question. It doesn't disprove morphic resonance in general, but it weakens the argument that protein folding specifically requires it. I think intellectual honesty demands acknowledging when the evidence moves against your position, even partially."

**[9:14]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The embryo regulation problem — where does that stand?"

**[9:18]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"This one I feel is still genuinely open. Developmental biology has made extraordinary progress in identifying the molecular signals — the morphogens, the transcription factors, the gene regulatory networks. Wolpert's positional information model is powerful. But the regulation problem — how does half an embryo know it needs to produce the whole — is, in my view, not fully resolved by identifying the molecular components. You can describe the signaling cascade in enormous detail and still not fully explain why the system converges on the same form from radically different starting conditions. The robustness of development, its capacity to compensate for perturbation, suggests something like a field — an attractor in the mathematical sense, or a morphic field in my sense."

**[9:58]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And collective intelligence — ant colonies, swarms, the phenomena that fascinate complexity scientists?"

**[10:06]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Here I must concede that the mainstream account is increasingly compelling. Stigmergy — coordination through environmental modification — pheromone trails, local interaction rules, agent-based models — these explain a great deal of collective intelligence without invoking anything beyond local interactions. The work of Deborah Gordon on harvester ants, of Iain Couzin on fish schools, of Thomas Seeley on honeybee decision-making — this is magnificent science. Morphic resonance is not needed to explain most of what we observe in collective intelligence.

Where I think the question remains alive is in the speed and flexibility of some collective responses — situations where the colony seems to shift its behavior too rapidly for the pheromone-based explanation to fully account for. But I hold this position lightly. The burden of proof is on me, and the mainstream is making steady progress."

**[10:52]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Then why do you remain valuable? If the mainstream is answering some of your questions — why does this book include you?"

**[11:00]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Not because my specific claims are proven. Because I ask questions about coordination that the mainstream doesn't ask. The mainstream is brilliant at decomposing systems into mechanisms. But the question of why form is so robust, why coordination is so reliable, why systems converge on particular patterns — these questions tend to be treated as solved once the molecular components are identified. I don't think they are solved. I think the components are necessary but not sufficient.

The value of a heretic is not that they're right. It's that they identify the questions that the orthodoxy is not asking. And some of those questions turn out to be important, whether or not the heretic's answers are correct."

**[11:38]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the three-body reading of your framework?"

**[11:42]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Physical matter is your A — the atoms, the molecules, the substrate. Genetic and physical information is your B — the DNA, the laws of physics, the energy landscapes. And the morphic field is C — the memory of what has worked before, the accumulated pattern. Mainstream science says A and B explain everything. C doesn't exist. I say A plus B plus C coordinates to produce form and behavior. C is the memory of nature — the accumulated patterns of the past influencing the present through resonance rather than through any currently understood physical mechanism."

## ACT II: THE MEMORY OF NATURE

**Duration:** 18 minutes

### Segment 3: Nature as Memory System [12:00-18:00]

**[12:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Your book *The Presence of the Past* argues that nature is fundamentally a memory system. This is a radical claim. Walk me through it."

**[12:12]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The orthodox view in science is that nature is governed by eternal, unchanging laws. The laws of physics are the same today as they were at the Big Bang. They don't evolve. They don't learn. History is irrelevant to the laws — the equations of motion are time-symmetric, the fundamental constants are fixed. This is a very specific metaphysical commitment, and it is so deeply embedded in scientific culture that most scientists don't even recognize it as a commitment. They think it's just what reality is."

**[12:44]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And your alternative?"

**[12:47]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"My alternative is that nature is governed by habits, not laws. This is not as radical as it sounds — it was the standard view in philosophy before the scientific revolution. Aristotle thought nature acted by habit. The idea that nature follows eternal mathematical laws is a product of the seventeenth century — of Newton, of the mechanistic philosophy. It replaced an older, more organic view.

What I'm proposing is closer to the older view, updated with modern biology. Habits evolve: they are formed by repetition, modified by experience, transmitted across time. The laws of nature might be better understood as the habits of nature — habits with a very long history. The gravitational constant is not an eternal decree written into the fabric of reality. It is a habit so deeply established that it appears to be a law. But it was formed, over time, through repetition."

**[13:32]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This is an extraordinary claim. You are saying that even the fundamental constants of physics might have evolved."

**[13:40]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I am. And I recognize that this is where I lose most physicists entirely. Let me be clear about the logical structure. If morphic resonance applies to all self-organizing systems — and I believe it does — then it applies to atoms, to subatomic particles, to the fundamental interactions. The habits of hydrogen atoms are very old, very deeply established. They appear to be laws because they have been repeated so many times. But they were established, not decreed.

Now, I cannot prove this. The evidence is indirect at best. But the alternative — that the laws of physics existed before the universe, waiting for a universe to govern — has its own profound philosophical problems. Where do timeless laws live? How do mathematical abstractions exert causal power over physical matter? These are questions that physicists generally wave away, but they are not trivial."

**[14:22]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Why does this matter for coordination?"

**[14:26]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Because if nature has habits rather than laws, then the history of a system matters in a way that current physics doesn't acknowledge. A chemical compound is not only what its atoms are in the present moment — it is what all previous compounds of that type have been. A species is not only what its genes code for — it is what all previous members of that species have done and experienced. History is never simply the past. It is present in the form of accumulated habit.

And this means that coordination in living systems is not just present-state coordination — not just atoms interacting according to fixed laws in this moment. It is also coordination with the accumulated past. The coordination draws on patterns that exist nowhere in the present material substrate. This is what I call morphic resonance: the coordination of present forms with past forms of the same type."

**[15:10]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You have an interesting parallel with artificial intelligence here."

**[15:15]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I do, and it's one I've thought about a great deal. Training data is, in a sense, a kind of morphic resonance — a materialist morphic resonance, if you like. When you train a large language model, the model is influenced by all the patterns in its training corpus. It doesn't memorize individual texts — it absorbs the statistical regularities, the patterns of how words and concepts relate. And when it generates new text, it draws on those accumulated patterns. It is coordinating with the past.

But training data is explicitly stored — it exists in physical memory, on servers, in digital form. Sheldrake's morphic resonance is different: the influence is not through stored data but through field resonance. The pattern is not anywhere in particular — it is in the field."

**[15:58]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Can you see why a computer scientist would find the training data explanation more satisfying?"

**[16:03]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Of course. It's concrete. It's measurable. You can point to the data and say: there is the source of the pattern. My proposal requires an entirely new kind of causation — influence across time without a material medium. That is a very large ask.

But I would put it this way: whether or not morphic resonance is real, the question it raises is real. How much of what a system does is influenced by what systems like it have done before, through channels other than explicit information storage? In biology, this is the question of epigenetics, of cultural transmission, of what gets passed on beyond the genome. In AI, it might be the question of what patterns a system could access beyond its training data. These are legitimate scientific questions regardless of my particular theoretical framework."

### Segment 4: Consciousness and the Extended Mind [18:00-24:00]

**[18:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've extended your ideas to consciousness — arguing that minds are not confined to brains. This is perhaps even more controversial than morphic resonance. What is the claim?"

**[18:14]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The standard view in neuroscience is that consciousness is produced by the brain. When you look at a tree, light enters your eyes, is transduced into neural signals, processed by the visual cortex, and the experience of seeing the tree happens somewhere inside your skull. The tree you see is a representation — a model — constructed by your brain. Your mind is in your head.

I challenge this. I think that when you look at a tree, your mind reaches out to the tree. Vision is not just reception of signals — it involves an outward projection of attention. The image is not inside your head. It is where it seems to be — out there, at the tree. The mind is not confined to the brain. It extends through fields — through morphic fields, through the field of attention — into the environment."

**[19:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This sounds like naive realism — the pre-scientific view that the eye sends out rays."

**[19:07]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"It does sound like that, and I think the historical connection is interesting rather than embarrassing. The ancient Greeks — Empedocles, Plato — believed in extramission: the eye sending out something that interacts with what it sees. Modern science replaced this with intromission: the eye passively receives. But the phenomenology of vision — the actual experience of seeing — is much more consistent with the extramission model. When you look at something, attention goes outward. You direct your gaze. You focus on the object, not on your retina.

Now, I'm not proposing that literal rays shoot from your eyes. I'm proposing that the field of attention extends beyond the brain, and that this has measurable consequences."

**[19:48]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The feeling of being stared at."

**[19:51]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Yes. If the mind extends beyond the brain, then when you look at someone — when your attention reaches them — there should be a detectable effect. People commonly report feeling when someone is looking at them from behind, even without any normal sensory cues. I've conducted extensive experiments on this.

The basic protocol: a subject sits with their back to a looker. In randomized trials, the looker either stares at the back of the subject's head or looks away. The subject says whether they're being looked at or not. If it's random chance, you expect fifty percent correct. Across tens of thousands of trials, we get hit rates of around fifty-five percent — small but statistically significant. The effect is consistent across cultures, age groups, and experimental settings."

**[20:32]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the critics?"

**[20:34]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The critics argue methodological problems: subtle cues, sensory leakage, experimenter effects, optional stopping. I've addressed these systematically. We've used one-way mirrors, closed-circuit television, randomized sequences. The effect persists, though it's small. The debate continues.

I would say honestly that the scientific evidence for extended minds remains contested. No single experiment has been decisive. But the philosophical case is stronger than the critics acknowledge. The assumption that the mind is confined to the skull is not an empirical finding — it is a theoretical commitment. It follows from the materialist assumption that consciousness is produced by the brain and exists only where the brain is. If you question that assumption — and there are good philosophical reasons to question it — then the extended mind hypothesis becomes a serious possibility rather than an absurdity."

**[21:18]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What is the coordination implication? Why does this matter for this book's argument?"

**[21:24]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"If minds extend beyond brains, then consciousness is not confined to skull-sized volumes. Intelligence might be genuinely distributed — not just computationally distributed, as in a computer network, but phenomenally distributed. The individual mind may be more like a node in a field than an isolated computational system.

Think about what happens when two people have a conversation. On the standard model, two separate brains exchange signals through sound waves. On my model, two extended minds overlap — their fields of attention interpenetrate. The conversation is not just information exchange. It is a shared field of awareness. This is why conversation feels different from reading — there is a presence, a mutual attention, that goes beyond the information content."

**[22:02]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Even if morphic resonance is wrong — is the question worth asking?"

**[22:08]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I believe so, yes. The question of how isolated the individual mind really is — that is a genuine scientific and philosophical question. We know that social cognition is real, that mirror neurons exist, that empathy has neural correlates. But the deeper question — whether consciousness itself extends beyond the individual brain, whether awareness has a field-like quality — that remains open. And it is relevant to artificial intelligence because the question of whether AI systems could develop extended awareness, could participate in fields of consciousness rather than simply processing information in isolation — that question depends on what consciousness actually is and where it actually lives."

### Segment 5: The Telephone Experiments [24:00-28:00]

**[24:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You conducted famous experiments on whether people know who's calling before they answer the phone. These became both your most accessible evidence and your most attacked. What did you find?"

**[24:16]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The experiment is simple. Participants are given four potential callers — people they know. For each trial, one caller is randomly selected by the experimenter. The caller phones the participant. Before answering, the participant guesses which of the four people is calling. If it's random chance, you expect twenty-five percent correct."

**[24:38]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the results?"

**[24:40]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Across multiple studies, involving hundreds of participants and thousands of trials, we consistently get hit rates around forty-five percent. That is nearly double chance expectation. The effect is robust, it replicates, and it is statistically highly significant. We've done versions with email as well — guessing who sent an email before opening it — with similar above-chance results."

**[25:02]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The critics have been severe."

**[25:05]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"They have. The main objections are methodological: that participants unconsciously keep track of the time since each caller last called; that the randomization was imperfect; that there are selection biases — trials where the participant didn't answer being excluded. We've addressed each of these. We used proper randomization software. We included all trials. We controlled for patterns in calling.

But here is what I find most telling: the critics have not replicated the experiment and found different results. They have criticized the method without conducting their own version. This is the pattern I encounter repeatedly — theoretical dismissal without empirical engagement. If my results are wrong, the experiment is easy to replicate. Anyone with four volunteers and a telephone can do it. The fact that critics prefer argumentation over experimentation tells you something about the sociology of this debate."

**[25:52]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What do you conclude?"

**[25:55]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The most parsimonious explanation of the results, in my view, is that some people have some ability to detect who is contacting them — perhaps through a form of morphic connection between people who know each other. This is not telepathy in the science fiction sense — not mind-reading, not transmission of complex thoughts. It is a subtle statistical influence, a form of connection that is slightly more than chance.

This matters for coordination because if people can detect who is contacting them before conscious information arrives, there is some form of coordination beyond the currently understood physical channels. This does not require the supernatural. It requires physics we don't yet understand. I am not claiming magic. I am claiming that our current physics is incomplete, and that the completeness will include some forms of coordination we currently dismiss."

**[26:40]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You are asking us to accept a great deal on the basis of small statistical effects."

**[26:46]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I am asking you to take the data seriously. Small statistical effects, when they replicate consistently, are how we detect real phenomena in noisy systems. The Higgs boson was detected through small statistical anomalies in particle collision data. Nobody dismissed it as a methodological artifact because it fit the theoretical framework. My results are dismissed not because the statistics are weak — they are strong — but because they don't fit the theoretical framework. And that, I would argue, is not how science should work. The data should challenge the theory, not the other way around."

## ACT III: WHAT THIS MEANS FOR INTELLIGENCE

**Duration:** 14 minutes

### Segment 6: The Morphic Field as Coordination Architecture [28:00-35:00]

**[28:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"In the context of this book's argument — hardware, software, knowware — where do morphic fields fit?"

**[28:10]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"If morphic fields exist, they are the original knowware. Before there were computers, before there were brains, before there was DNA: there were morphic fields organizing matter into form.

The crystal that knows what shape to be: morphic resonance. The embryo that knows how to grow: morphic resonance. The starlings that murmur without a leader: morphic resonance. The ant colony that solves problems without a brain: morphic resonance."

**[28:38]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"That is a very large claim. You are saying that the coordination intelligence this book describes has always existed in nature."

**[28:46]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"What I'm proposing is that the coordination intelligence you're describing — the third body that emerges from hardware and software interaction — is an instance of a much more general phenomenon. Nature has always had a third body. The field that carries the memory of what has worked before.

Whether my specific theory is right or wrong, the question is right: how does coordination intelligence access the wisdom of what has worked before? In AI, the answer is training data — explicitly stored patterns. But training data is expensive, limited, and brittle. It requires enormous computational resources to collect, curate, and process. It degrades when the world changes."

**[29:20]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You are suggesting there might be another way."

**[29:24]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Is there a way for a system to coordinate with the accumulated wisdom of all previous systems of its type, without explicit storage? Morphic resonance is my answer. I may be wrong about the mechanism. But the question is important regardless of the answer.

Consider what a morphic field would be in computational terms. It would be a distributed memory that no individual system needs to store. It would update automatically as new instances are created. It would be available to any system of the same type, anywhere, at any time. It would carry not just data but pattern — the shape of what works, the form that has been selected by experience across time."

**[30:02]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This sounds like you are describing something very close to what machine learning researchers call the 'prior' — the inductive bias that a system brings to a problem before seeing any data."

**[30:14]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"That is a fascinating parallel, and I think it's more than superficial. The prior in Bayesian machine learning — the assumptions a system makes before encountering evidence — is typically hand-designed or learned from a training distribution. But where does the useful prior come from in biology? Why do organisms have the inductive biases they have? Evolution is the standard answer — selection over generations. But evolution operates through genetic variation and differential reproduction. The morphic field offers an additional channel: the accumulated experience of the species, available through resonance rather than through genetics.

I'm not saying evolution is wrong. I'm saying it may be incomplete. The genetic channel may not be the only channel through which the wisdom of previous generations reaches the present."

**[30:58]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And for artificial intelligence specifically?"

**[31:04]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The coordination architecture might be richer than we think. If morphic resonance exists in any form — even a weak form, even a form quite different from what I propose — it would mean that isolated computation is not the only game in town. It would mean that systems can be influenced by what similar systems have done, through channels beyond explicit data transfer. Whether this turns out to be a form of field physics, or quantum entanglement, or something we haven't imagined yet — the possibility is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing on theoretical grounds alone."

**[31:38]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What would it take to convince you that you are wrong?"

**[31:43]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"A systematic program of the experiments I've proposed — crystal formation under rigorous controls, rat learning with modern protocols, staring detection with preregistered analyses — that consistently fails to find the predicted effects. That would be powerful evidence against morphic resonance, and I would accept it. What we have instead is a refusal to conduct the experiments combined with a theoretical conviction that the results must be wrong. That is not science. That is dogma wearing a lab coat."

### Segment 7: The Heretic's Role [35:00-42:00]

**[35:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You have been doing this for over forty years now. The book *A New Science of Life* was published in 1981. The editor of *Nature* called it a candidate for burning. You have been at this longer than most of your critics have been in their careers. What has the experience taught you about the relationship between heresy and science?"

**[35:22]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"I've been called pseudoscientific, heterodox, a fraud, a delusional thinker. I've also been cited by serious scientists who see genuine problems in the questions I raise. The sociology of this is interesting.

The *Nature* editorial — John Maddox wrote that my book was 'the best candidate for burning there has been for many years.' He later moderated his view somewhat, but the initial reaction was revealing. A senior editor of the most prestigious scientific journal in the world called for the burning of a book by a Cambridge-trained biologist, published by a reputable publisher, on the grounds that its hypothesis was heretical. Not wrong — heretical. The language is theological, not scientific."

**[36:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Do you think science has a heresy problem?"

**[36:04]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"Science advances through heretics. Not all heretics are right — most are wrong. But some heretics are right about the questions even when they're wrong about the answers. Galileo was right that the Earth moves around the Sun. He was wrong about many other things — he was wrong about the tides, wrong about comets. But the question he insisted on asking — does the Earth move? — was the right question.

Alfred Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912. He was ridiculed for decades. The mechanism he proposed — centrifugal force — was wrong. But the observation — that the continents fit together and share geological features — was correct. Plate tectonics vindicated his observation while replacing his mechanism.

I may be in that position. I may be wrong about the mechanism of morphic resonance. But the observations I'm pointing to — the difficulty of first crystallization, the improvement in learning across populations, the sense of being stared at — these are real phenomena that deserve explanation, not dismissal."

**[36:52]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Or you may simply be wrong about everything."

**[36:56]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"That is entirely possible. I hold my theories with as much intellectual humility as I can muster. But I hold the questions with great conviction. The questions are not going away.

How does form arise? Not just which genes regulate which proteins, but why do cells know where they are in the embryo? Why is development so robust? How does collective intelligence emerge? Not just through local interaction rules, but why do some collectives solve problems that seem to require more information than they locally possess? How is consciousness related to the body, the brain, and the environment? If the mind is confined to the skull, what accounts for the regularities that appear in my extended-mind experiments? If it isn't confined, what is the nature of the extension?"

**[37:36]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And how does all of this connect to the coordination intelligence this book is building toward?"

**[37:42]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"The coordination intelligence you're building — the third body, the knowware that emerges from hardware and software interaction — may be the materialist instantiation of something that has always existed in nature. Or it may be genuinely new. Either way, the questions are the same: what is the coordination that produces intelligence? Where does it live? How does it accumulate wisdom? How does it extend beyond individual instances?

If you're building systems that coordinate — AI systems, human-AI systems, collective intelligence systems — you need to understand what coordination actually is, at the deepest level. Is it only information exchange through known physical channels? Or is there something else — something field-like, something distributed, something that connects similar systems across space and possibly across time?"

**[38:22]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You don't need morphic resonance to ask those questions."

**[38:26]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"No, you don't. And that may be my most lasting contribution — not the theory itself, but the questions it forces people to confront. I've been asking these questions for forty years. The mainstream hasn't answered them. They've dismissed them. That, in itself, tells you something."

**[38:44]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What does it tell you?"

**[38:47]**

**SHELDRAKE:**
"It tells me that the questions are uncomfortable. They threaten the paradigm. And paradigms, as Thomas Kuhn observed, are not overthrown by evidence alone. They are overthrown when the anomalies accumulate to the point where a new generation of scientists finds them more interesting than the existing framework.

The heretic's job is not to be right. The heretic's job is to keep the questions alive that the orthodoxy would prefer to close. I have done that for forty years. Whether the answers turn out to be morphic resonance, or quantum biology, or some theoretical framework we haven't yet imagined, the questions will persist. How does nature remember? How does form coordinate? How does intelligence extend beyond the individual?

These are the right questions. And they are still open."

## Key Insights

**Morphic Fields:**
- Fields that give shape and organization to material systems
- Distinct from electromagnetic fields in having a temporal dimension: morphic resonance across time
- Controversial: no known physical mechanism; evidence contested but not definitively refuted

**The Memory of Nature:**
- Nature may be governed by habits rather than eternal laws
- The history of a system influences present behavior through morphic resonance
- "The laws of nature might be better understood as the habits of nature."

**The Extended Mind:**
- Consciousness may not be confined to the skull
- Minds may extend through morphic fields
- Evidence for detection of attention-at-a-distance: contested but not decisively refuted

**The Heretic's Questions:**
- How does form arise beyond genetics?
- How does collective intelligence exceed local information?
- How is consciousness related to fields?
- These questions remain open regardless of whether Sheldrake's answers are correct
