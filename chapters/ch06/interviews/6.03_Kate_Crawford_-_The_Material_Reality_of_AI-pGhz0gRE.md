## Interview Overview

Dr. Kate Crawford, Distinguished Research Professor at USC Annenberg and author of "Atlas of AI," has traced artificial intelligence back through its full material supply chain—from rare earth mines in the Congo to data centers consuming massive energy to exploited labor in factories and annotation warehouses. In this conversation, Crawford reveals how AI is profoundly material, requiring extraction, manufacturing, and labor that remain invisible to most users. Through the lens of GPT-3's training, Amazon warehouse AI, and the global supply chain, she shows how computational advancement coordinates with material extraction and social inequality to create a technology that serves the powerful while externalizing costs to marginalized communities and the planet itself.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD)**

So, I started following the chain backward. It's a bit like detective work. AI runs on servers. Servers require chips. Chips require rare earth minerals. Minerals require mining. Mining requires labor. And suddenly, the "cloud" wasn't ethereal anymore. It was:

*   **01:50:** Cobalt mines in the Democratic Republic of Congo.
*   **02:00:** Lithium extraction in Chile.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, shakes her head slightly)**

The guide didn't know. Or perhaps, more accurately, nobody wanted to talk about it. It wasn't part of the narrative.

That's when it clicked. I understood: The tech industry has built AI on a foundation of extraction and exploitation, and they've made that foundation deliberately invisible. They talk about "the cloud" as if it's ethereal, weightless, immaterial. They want us to believe it's magic.

But there is no cloud. There are data centers consuming enormous energy, built with minerals extracted by exploited labor, running on infrastructure that spans the globe. AI isn't immaterial. It's profoundly material. And the materiality has been deliberately hidden, obscured by a narrative of pure software innovation.

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** It begins with **Stage 1: Resource Extraction.**

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, pointing to the map)**

**CRAWFORD:** AI requires a vast array of rare earth minerals: cobalt for batteries and electronics, lithium for power storage, copper for wiring and circuits, and a host of other rare earth elements for semiconductors.

**(SCREEN CAM - MAP ZOOMS IN ON DRC, CHILE. PHOTOS OF MINING OPERATIONS APPEAR.)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** Where do these come from? The Democratic Republic of Congo produces 70% of the world's cobalt. I documented mines there where:

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** Workers, including children, dig by hand. There's no safety equipment, no labor protections. They're exposed to toxic materials daily. Wages are a few dollars a day, if that. The conditions are dangerous, exploitative, and often deadly.

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** In Chile, lithium extraction involves massive water consumption in already desert regions, leading to the destruction of indigenous lands and severe environmental devastation. The benefits flow mostly to foreign corporations, while the costs are borne by local communities and the environment. This is the absolute foundation of AI. Every AI system, every algorithm, every "smart" device, begins here, with this extraction.

**(SCREEN CAM - MAP TRANSITIONS TO MANUFACTURING HUBS: CHINA, TAIWAN, SOUTH KOREA. FACTORY FOOTAGE/PHOTOS.)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** Next, **Stage 2: Manufacturing.** These raw minerals are processed and transformed into chips, servers, and devices. Where? Primarily in China, Taiwan, South Korea. In factories where:

**(SCREEN CAM - MAP TRANSITIONS TO GLOBAL DATA CENTER LOCATIONS. ENERGY CONSUMPTION CHARTS APPEAR.)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** Then, **Stage 3: Energy Consumption.** The entire infrastructure runs on massive amounts of energy.

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** Data centers globally consume 1-2% of all electricity, and that's growing rapidly. Training large AI models has an enormous carbon footprint. And inference—running those models continuously—is a constant energy drain. All of this requires massive cooling systems, which consume even more energy and often vast amounts of water. And where does that energy come from? Often coal, natural gas, fossil fuels. The "clean" tech industry running on dirty energy.

**(SCREEN CAM - MAP TRANSITIONS TO E-WASTE DUMP SITES, OFTEN IN GLOBAL SOUTH. FOOTAGE/PHOTOS OF E-WASTE.)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** Finally, **Stage 4: E-Waste.** The lifecycle ends in waste. Obsolete servers, devices, chips—all containing toxic materials that require careful disposal. Instead, much of it is shipped to developing countries.

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** There, informal recycling operations expose workers to severe toxins, leading to widespread environmental contamination. The material lifecycle of AI is a continuous loop: Extraction → Manufacturing → Operation → Waste. And at each stage, we find exploitation, environmental damage, and costs externalized to the Global South and marginalized communities.

### [08:30 - 11:00] Writing "Atlas of AI"

**(B-CAM - INTERVIEWER)**

**INTERVIEWER:** It's a truly comprehensive, and frankly, disturbing picture. You documented all of this in your book, "Atlas of AI." The title itself suggests a mapping of this hidden territory. What was your core thesis in writing it?

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, holds up her book, "Atlas of AI")**

**CRAWFORD:** I wrote "Atlas of AI" to document this full, interconnected system. The title is deliberate: an atlas maps territory. I wanted to map the *territory* of AI—not just the algorithms, but the physical infrastructure, the labor, the power dynamics that shape it.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, puts the book down, gestures with her hands as if outlining points)**

The book traces AI through five critical lenses:
1.  **Earth:** The planetary costs—the mining, the energy, the environmental damage.
2.  **Labor:** The human costs—the workers in mines, factories, and the often-invisible annotation warehouses.
3.  **Data:** The extraction of information from people, often without their consent or fair compensation.
4.  **Classification:** How AI systems categorize, sort, and ultimately control people, often reinforcing existing biases.
5.  **Power:** Who benefits, who decides, and crucially, who bears the costs of this technology.

My central thesis is that AI is fundamentally an extractive industry, just like oil or mining. It extracts value—from the earth, from human labor, from people's data—and it concentrates that value, those profits, in the hands of a few powerful corporations, while externalizing the immense costs to everyone else: to marginalized communities, to the environment, to the planet itself.

**(B-CAM - INTERVIEWER, looking serious)**

**INTERVIEWER:** That's a very strong claim, Dr. Crawford. To call AI an "extractive industry" rather than a purely technological one.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, leans forward, intense, direct)**

**CRAWFORD:** It's not a metaphor. It's literally true. Consider the characteristics of extractive industries:
*   They remove resources from the earth.
*   They exploit labor to process those resources.
*   They concentrate profits among owners and investors.
*   They externalize environmental and social costs.
*   They often operate with minimal regulation or accountability.

AI does all of this. The only difference is we've been trained to think of AI as "immaterial" software, as something that exists purely in the digital realm, so we don't see the extraction. We don't see the physical footprint. But once you see it, once you understand this full material reality, you can't unsee it. Every AI system, from the simplest app to the most complex large language model, is built on extraction. The fundamental question then becomes: Who benefits, and who bears the costs?

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** AI investment is in the hundreds of billions annually. AI energy consumption is projected to double in the next five years. AI hardware production is accelerating, and AI model sizes—from GPT-3 to GPT-4 and beyond—are growing exponentially.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, gestures emphatically)**

Each increase in AI capability, each new "breakthrough," requires:
*   More minerals extracted.
*   More energy consumed.
*   More labor exploited.
*   More environmental damage.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, direct to camera, serious)**

It's not costless. The costs are enormous. We're just externalizing them—to workers in mines, to communities near factories, to the Global South, to the planet itself. If we don't account for the material reality of AI, if we don't coordinate computational advancement with material sustainability and social justice, we are building a technology that will ultimately destroy the very conditions for its own existence. That's not hyperbole. That's physics and economics. We cannot continue to extract infinitely from a finite planet.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, draws strong connecting arrows between the three power circles)**

**CRAWFORD:** The **Coordination of Power** is what creates this self-reinforcing system. Those who control computational power (the tech companies) *need* material power (the resources, the infrastructure). Those who control material power (mining companies, chip manufacturers, energy providers) *profit* immensely from computational power's demand. And those who control social power (executives, investors, policymakers) benefit from and reinforce both computational and material power. The result is a system where power coordinates across all three layers to concentrate benefits and externalize costs.

**(B-CAM - INTERVIEWER)**

**INTERVIEWER:** And those who bear the costs, the miners, the factory workers, the communities affected—they have no power in this equation?

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, intense, direct)**

**CRAWFORD:** Exactly. The coordination is profoundly asymmetric. Workers in cobalt mines have no say in AI development. Communities near factories have no say in what gets manufactured or under what conditions. People whose data is scraped have no say in how it's used or whether they're compensated. The Global South has no say in the e-waste dumped on its shores. This is coordination without consent. This is power coordinating with itself to extract value from those without power, and to externalize the harms onto them.

*   They predict which workers are likely to quit.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, returns to the three-body diagram, now with a hopeful but firm tone)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** **Social Ethics:** Who benefits from AI, who bears the costs, who makes the decisions, and how we distribute power.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, gestures to all three layers)**

**(SCREEN CAM - Graphic: "Accountability Framework" with three pillars.)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** **2. Accountability:** Companies must be held responsible for the entire coordination system—not just their algorithms, but their full material and social impact. **(SCREEN CAM - Graphic: "Justice" pillar highlighted.)**

**CRAWFORD (V.O.):** **3. Justice:** Benefits must coordinate with burdens. Those who profit must bear the costs. Communities affected by extraction or waste must participate in decisions. Workers must have power and protection. Environmental damage must be remediated. And data creators must be compensated for their contributions.

**(A-CAM - CRAWFORD, gestures to the entire framework)**

**CRAWFORD:** This isn't radical. This is basic accountability. If you profit from a system, you are responsible for its full impact.
