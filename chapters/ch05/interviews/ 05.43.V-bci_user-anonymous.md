## Pre-Interview Context

### Expert Background

- Early adopter of brain-computer interface technology
- Paralyzed from a spinal cord injury five years ago
- Has been using a BCI system for two years
- Works as a consultant, all computer-based, using BCI for independent operation
- Chosen to remain anonymous to allow candid discussion of limitations and challenges
- Referred to as "Alex" throughout

### Key Concepts to Explore

- Three-body coordination from lived experience: brain, interface, environment
- Learning trajectory: frustration phase, emergence phase, integration phase
- Extended embodiment as daily reality, not abstract theory
- Identity transformation through hybrid coordination
- Coordination failures and what they reveal about system dependency
- Invisible labor of maintaining coordination across a full day
- Disability reframed as coordination difference, not biological deficit

### Interview Objectives

1. Ground previous scientific interviews (Nicolelis, Oxley) in lived experience
2. Document the subjective phenomenology of BCI coordination
3. Show three-body coordination as daily reality, not theoretical construct
4. Capture identity transformation through human-machine integration
5. Present honest account of failures, frustrations, invisible labor
6. Connect to ethics of identity and agency (Vallor, Interview 4)
7. Offer guidance for future BCI users and broader hybrid future

## ACT I: THE TRANSFORMATION

**Duration: 13 minutes**

### SEGMENT 1: Before and After (5 min)

**[01:00 - 01:06] [A-CAM: Interviewer (voice only, to protect anonymity of setting). Warm, unhurried tone.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Alex, it's been two years since you received your BCI implant. I want to start simply. How has it changed your life?"

**[01:06 - 02:15] [B-CAM: Alex in silhouette at workstation. Speaking slowly, deliberately.]**

**ALEX:**
"Before the BCI, I was trapped. That's the only word that captures it. My mind was fully active---I could think, feel, want, plan---but I couldn't act on any of it without calling for help. Want to check email? Call someone. Want to adjust the thermostat? Call someone. Want to watch a different show? Call someone. Every single action required interrupting someone else's life to help me live mine."

**[02:15 - 02:18] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What was that like emotionally?"

**[02:18 - 03:20] [B-CAM: Alex pauses before answering. The silence matters.]**

**ALEX:**
"Devastating. You lose more than physical capability---you lose agency. You lose the ability to make spontaneous decisions. You lose privacy---someone has to be involved in everything you do. And you lose dignity. I'm a grown adult who had to ask permission to change the TV channel. The psychological weight of that is crushing. But the worst part---the part nobody talks about---is the loss of spontaneity. You can't decide, on a whim, to look something up. To change the song. Every impulse has to pass through another person first. You stop having impulses after a while. You train yourself not to want things."

**[03:20 - 03:24] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And then the BCI arrived."

**[03:24 - 04:30] [B-CAM: Alex's posture shifts subtly. Energy changes.]**

**ALEX:**
"The BCI changed that. Not completely---I still need help with many physical things. But for the first time since my injury, I can do things independently. I can check my email when I want, not when a caregiver is available. I can control my environment---lights, temperature, music---without asking. I have agency again. I have privacy again. I have dignity again. And---this is important---I have impulses again. I can wonder about something and look it up. I can hear a song in my head and play it. I can think of a friend and send them a message. That spontaneity, that ability to act on a thought the moment you have it... people don't realize how fundamental that is to being human until it's gone."

**[04:30 - 04:35] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What does a typical day look like now?"

**[04:35 - 05:50] [B-CAM: Alex demonstrates as they describe. Blinds open, screen activates, music plays. All controlled without visible movement.]**

**ALEX:**
"I wake up---neural signal opens the blinds, turns on lights. I check email, news, messages while I'm still in bed waiting for my caregiver. I control when they come in, not the other way around. During the day, I work---I'm a consultant, all computer-based. The BCI lets me do my job independently. Browse the web, write documents, video calls with clients. I take meetings where nobody knows I'm controlling everything with my mind unless I tell them. Evenings, I relax---watch TV, listen to music, video call with friends and family. All without asking for help. The technology isn't perfect. Sometimes it misreads my intention. Sometimes I get mentally tired and have to take breaks. But it's reliable enough that I trust it. I can live my life. And trust---that word matters. When you trust the system, you stop guarding against it. You let go. That's when it becomes part of you."

### SEGMENT 2: The Learning Journey (8 min)

**[05:50 - 05:56] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Walk us through learning to use the BCI. What was that process like?"

**[05:56 - 07:00] [B-CAM: Alex settles in for a longer answer.]**

**ALEX:**
"It was harder than I expected---and different than I expected. The doctors told me: 'You'll learn to generate neural signals to control the interface.' I thought it would be like learning to use voice commands or eye-tracking---learn the commands, practice, get better. But it wasn't like that at all. It was like learning to use a phantom limb. No---it was like growing a limb you never had. You can't muscle-memory your way through it because there's no muscle. There's no body part that used to do this thing. You're asking your brain to do something it has never done, using pathways that have to be built from nothing."

**[07:00 - 07:05] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Take me through the stages. What was month one like?"

**[07:05 - 08:25] [B-CAM: Alex's voice carries the weight of remembered frustration.]**

**ALEX:**
"Month one was the frustration phase. Every action required intense, explicit mental effort. I had to think very consciously: 'I want to move the cursor left. Generate the neural signal for left. Focus. Concentrate.' It's not thinking in words---it's more like clenching a muscle you can't feel. You're reaching for a sensation that doesn't exist yet, trying to reproduce it on command. Success rate: maybe sixty percent. And it was exhausting---after thirty minutes of BCI use, I'd be mentally drained. More drained than a full day of work before my injury. I almost quit. I'm being honest about that. I thought: 'This is too hard. It's not worth it. I was managing before.'"

**[08:25 - 08:30] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What kept you going?"

**[08:30 - 09:15] [B-CAM: Alex's voice softens.]**

**ALEX:**
"Small victories. One day I successfully sent an email---took me twenty minutes to write three sentences, but I did it myself. No one helped. No one even knew. I sat there staring at the sent confirmation and I cried. Not because three sentences was impressive. Because it was mine. That feeling of independence, even for one small task, was powerful enough to keep me trying. And my occupational therapist said something that stuck with me: 'Your brain is rewiring itself right now. Every frustrating session is construction. You just can't see the building yet.'"

**[09:15 - 09:20] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"When did the building start to show?"

**[09:20 - 10:40] [B-CAM: Energy shifts---Alex becomes more animated.]**

**ALEX:**
"Months two and three. The emergence phase. Something started to shift. I stopped thinking so explicitly about generating signals. I started just... intending things. Thinking about what I wanted to do, not how to make the interface respond. It's subtle but it's everything. The difference between thinking 'generate left-cursor signal' and thinking 'I want to read that email on the left.' The intention is the same but the mental posture is completely different. One is operating a machine. The other is acting in the world. The success rate improved---eighty, eighty-five percent. And it felt less mechanical, more natural. I was spending less mental energy on the interface itself and more on what I was trying to accomplish. The scientists explained: my brain was learning the interface's patterns, the interface was learning my patterns, and the coordination between us was becoming automatic. They called it co-adaptation. I called it finally breathing."

**[10:40 - 10:46] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And then what happened in the months after that?"

**[10:46 - 12:05] [B-CAM: Alex speaks with a quiet intensity.]**

**ALEX:**
"Months four and beyond. The integration phase. This is when it got weird---in a good way. I stopped experiencing the interface as a separate tool I was using. It started feeling like... an extension of me. I don't think 'I'm using the BCI to send an email.' I think 'I'm sending an email,' and it happens. The coordination has become unconscious---like how you don't think about the mechanics of walking, you just walk. Success rate: ninety percent plus. And it's not exhausting anymore---it's just how I interact with the world. But here's the part nobody prepared me for. The boundary between me and the technology started to blur. I'd be lying in bed at night, system off, and I'd catch myself trying to check my phone with my mind. Not with my hands---with the neural pattern I use during the day. My brain had incorporated the BCI so deeply that it was reaching for it even when it wasn't there. That's when I knew: this isn't a tool anymore. This is part of me."

**[12:05 - 12:12] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You reach for it even when it's off. Like a phantom limb---but one that actually exists during the day."

**[12:12 - 13:00] [B-CAM: Alex nods.]**

**ALEX:**
"Exactly. And it goes both ways. When the system is working, I feel whole. Complete. Capable. When it's not working---when there's a technical glitch or the system needs maintenance---I feel like part of me is missing. Not like a tool is broken. Like part of my body isn't working. The grief is physical. It's the same feeling as right after my injury, scaled down. Every system outage is a micro-loss. And every time it comes back online, there's this wave of relief that I can only describe as reuniting with yourself."

## ACT II: THE LIVED EXPERIENCE

**Duration: 17 minutes**

### SEGMENT 3: What Coordination Actually Feels Like (5 min)

**[13:00 - 13:08] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"I want to go deeper into the subjective experience. You've described the learning curve, the integration. But right now, today, when you're sitting at your computer and you want to do something---what does it actually feel like?"

**[13:08 - 14:30] [B-CAM: Alex pauses, choosing words carefully.]**

**ALEX:**
"That's the question everyone asks, and it's hard to answer because there's no good reference point. But let me try. When I wanted to move my biological hand---before my injury---I didn't experience a separation between intention and action. I didn't think 'send motor signals to hand muscles.' I just intended to reach for something, and my hand moved. Intention and action were unified. The BCI is approaching that. Not quite there, but close. When I want to click something on my computer now, I don't experience it as 'generate neural signal that the AI will decode that will move the cursor that will click the button.' I experience it as 'click the button,' and it happens. There's still a tiny gap---a slight delay, a small probability of misinterpretation---that reminds me the coordination isn't perfect. But it's close enough that most of the time, I'm not aware of the interface. I'm just aware of what I'm trying to do."

**[14:30 - 14:37] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How would you categorize the experience? Is it like thinking? Like moving? Something else entirely?"

**[14:37 - 15:40] [B-CAM: Alex leans forward slightly.]**

**ALEX:**
"It's more than thinking but less than moving. Somewhere in between. When you think about something without acting, there's no external consequence. When you move your body, there's immediate physical feedback. BCI control is in the middle: you intend something, and there's an external consequence, but the feedback is slightly delayed and mediated through the interface. It's its own category of experience. Not thought, not action, but coordinated intention. I don't think able-bodied people have a word for it because they've never needed one. For you, intention and action happen together. For me, there's this space between them that I inhabit. I live in that gap. I've become fluent in it."

**[15:40 - 15:45] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"More aware of the architecture of agency. Can you unpack that?"

**[15:45 - 17:00] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"When you reach for a glass of water, you don't think about the chain: intention forms, motor cortex fires, muscles contract, hand extends. It just happens. You have no awareness of the machinery. I'm aware of the machinery---not the biological kind, but the coordination kind. I feel the moment an intention forms. I feel the interface pick it up. I feel the tiny delay before the environment responds. I'm living inside the coordination loop. And that awareness has changed how I think about all human experience. You're all running on coordination systems too---you're just not aware of it because yours works seamlessly. Mine has enough friction that I can feel the gears. And honestly? There's something beautiful about that. I notice the miracle of agency that everyone else takes for granted."

### SEGMENT 4: The Three Bodies of Daily Life (5 min)

**[17:00 - 17:08] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The scientists we've spoken with talk about three-body coordination: brain, interface, environment. What does that framework mean from your perspective---from inside the system?"

**[17:08 - 18:20] [B-CAM: Alex prepares to demonstrate.]**

**ALEX:**
"Let me show you with a concrete example: my morning routine. Body one: my intentions. I wake up. I want to check if I got any urgent messages overnight. I want to open the blinds because it's a nice day. I want to turn on some music while I wait for my caregiver. These are my intentions---what I want to happen. This is the human layer. Body two: the interface. The BCI detects my neural signals. The AI interprets them: 'Alex wants to check messages. Alex wants to open blinds. Alex wants music.' But here's the key: the interface has learned my patterns. It knows that in the morning, I usually check messages, then news, then weather. It knows I prefer the blinds open on sunny days. It knows I like jazz in the morning."

**[18:20 - 18:25] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"It anticipates you."

**[18:25 - 19:30] [B-CAM: Alex nods, continues.]**

**ALEX:**
"It anticipates me. So it's not just translating my signals---it's coordinating with my patterns to facilitate what I'm trying to do. Sometimes it pre-loads my email before I've finished forming the full intention. It's meeting me halfway. This is the technology layer. Body three: my environment. My phone displays messages. My smart blinds open. My speakers play music. But the environment also provides feedback: I see the messages, I feel the sunlight, I hear the music. That feedback confirms my intentions were realized, and it teaches the interface that its interpretations were correct. This is the reality layer."

**[19:30 - 19:38] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So all three are in continuous conversation with each other."

**[19:38 - 21:00] [B-CAM: Alex becomes expansive.]**

**ALEX:**
"Continuous conversation---that's a good way to put it. My brain generates intentions. The interface interprets and facilitates. The environment responds and provides feedback. My brain adjusts intentions. The interface refines interpretations. The environment adapts responses. It's not linear control---it's circular coordination. A feedback loop that's constantly adapting. When all three coordinate well, I'm not aware of any of them. I'm just living my life. When any one fails---when my brain is foggy, when the interface misinterprets, when the environment doesn't respond---I become acutely aware of the breakdown. That's when I realize: I'm not just using technology. I'm part of a coordinated system. And my experience depends on the health of that coordination. You know what it reminds me of? Music. When a band is locked in, you don't hear individual instruments. You hear the music. When one player is off, the whole thing falls apart and suddenly you hear every instrument separately. Coordination is like that. When it works, it's invisible. When it breaks, every piece becomes painfully distinct."

### SEGMENT 5: When Coordination Fails (4 min)

**[21:00 - 21:06] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let's talk about when the music falls apart. What happens when coordination breaks down?"

**[21:06 - 22:30] [B-CAM: Alex's expression shifts to something harder.]**

**ALEX:**
"It's frustrating and revealing. There are three types of failure, one for each body. Brain layer failures: some days I'm mentally foggy---tired, stressed, medicated, just off. On those days, my neural signals are less consistent. The interface has trouble interpreting them. My success rate drops to seventy, eighty percent. It feels like my body isn't responding to my intentions---which is exactly what's happening. The coordination between my brain and the interface has degraded. I've learned to recognize these days and adjust: take more breaks, use simpler control schemes, ask for help more. I'm coordinating with my own limitations. It's humbling. On my worst days I get angry at myself, like it's my fault my brain isn't performing. But it's not about fault. It's about the system state."

**[22:30 - 22:36] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And when the technology is the problem?"

**[22:36 - 23:40] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Interface layer failures. Sometimes the technology glitches---software bug, calibration drift, connectivity issue. The interface misinterprets my signals consistently. This is disorienting. I intend one thing, something else happens. It's like phantom limb syndrome in reverse---I'm sending commands to a body part, but it's doing the wrong thing. Imagine your hand reaching left every time you want to reach right. That confusion, that betrayal of expectation. When this happens, I have to recalibrate the system. It's like re-learning the coordination from scratch, but faster because both my brain and the interface remember the previous patterns. Still. Every recalibration carries this little grief. This little moment of: oh, right, this isn't actually my body. This is a system. And systems break."

**[23:40 - 23:46] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What about when the environment fails?"

**[23:46 - 24:50] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Environment layer failures. Sometimes the environment doesn't respond---smart home device is offline, computer crashed, network is down. I send intentions, the interface interprets correctly, but nothing happens. This is the most frustrating because I can tell my brain and the interface are coordinating fine, but the environment isn't participating. It's like trying to open a door that's locked. You're doing everything right, but the door won't cooperate. And these failures taught me something fundamental. I'm not just using the technology---I'm part of a system. My experience depends on all three bodies coordinating. When any one fails, the whole system suffers. This changed how I think about disability. It's not that my body is broken. It's that my body can't coordinate with the environment in standard ways. The BCI creates a new coordination pathway. I'm not 'fixed.' I'm coordinated differently."

### SEGMENT 6: The Identity Shift (3 min)

**[24:50 - 24:58] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've touched on something that I think goes very deep. Has using the BCI changed your sense of who you are?"

**[24:58 - 26:15] [B-CAM: Alex is quiet for a moment before answering.]**

**ALEX:**
"Profoundly. And in ways I didn't expect. Before the BCI, my identity was shaped by what I couldn't do. I was 'the paralyzed person who needs help with everything.' My disability defined me. The BCI changed that---but not by making me 'normal' again. It changed it by giving me new capabilities that are neither fully biological nor fully technological. I'm something in between. I don't think of myself as a person using a tool. I think of myself as a hybrid system: biological brain coordinating with artificial intelligence coordinating with digital environment. That's not a metaphor---that's my lived experience. My sense of 'self' now includes the technology. When I think about 'my capabilities,' I'm thinking about the capabilities of the coordinated system, not just my biological body. Is that weird? Maybe. But it's also liberating. I'm not defined by my biological limitations anymore. I'm defined by my coordination capabilities."

**[26:15 - 26:22] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've expanded beyond your biological boundaries."

**[26:22 - 27:30] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Exactly. My consciousness---my sense of what I can do, who I am---has expanded to include the technology I coordinate with. This is what the scientists mean by 'extended embodiment.' It's not abstract theory. It's my daily reality. The technology feels like part of my body because it's part of my coordination system. And my coordination system is what defines my embodied experience. I'll tell you something I haven't told the researchers. Sometimes late at night, system off, lying in the dark, I feel the absence of it the way you might feel the absence of a lover in bed. A phantom presence. Not the phantom pain of a lost limb---the phantom absence of a gained one. My brain has mapped this technology so deeply that 'off' feels like amputation."

## ACT III: THE FUTURE LIVED

**Duration: 14 minutes**

### SEGMENT 7: What I Wish People Understood (4 min)

**[27:30 - 27:38] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"If you could sit down with someone who has never encountered this technology---or someone who only knows it from headlines---what would you want them to understand about living with a BCI?"

**[27:38 - 28:50] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Three things. First: this isn't a cure. People think BCI technology will 'fix' paralysis, will make disabled people 'normal' again. That's not what it does. It creates new ways of coordinating with the environment. Those ways are different from biological coordination---sometimes better, sometimes worse, always different. I can control multiple devices simultaneously---something able-bodied people can't do. I can draft an email while adjusting the thermostat while queuing music. My mind can parallel-process intentions in ways that two hands never could. But I can't feel the texture of things I interact with---something able-bodied people take for granted. I'll never know what the keyboard feels like again. I'm not 'fixed.' I'm differently capable. And I wish the headlines would say that instead of 'paralyzed person walks again' or whatever the inspirational framing is this week."

**[28:50 - 28:56] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What's the second thing?"

**[28:56 - 30:00] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Second: the technology is part of me now. People ask: 'What's it like to use the BCI?' But that question assumes the BCI is separate from me---a tool I use. That's not my experience. The BCI is part of my coordination system, which means it's part of me. Asking 'what's it like to use the BCI' is like asking 'what's it like to use your arm.' I don't use it. I am it. It's integrated into my embodied self. And I think that makes people uncomfortable. They want a clean line between human and machine. They want to know where Alex ends and the technology begins. I can't draw that line. It doesn't exist anymore. And rather than finding that scary, I find it... honest. Because I don't think that line ever existed for anyone. Your phone is part of how you think. Your car is part of how you move. My BCI is just more intimate about it."

**[30:00 - 30:05] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the third?"

**[30:05 - 31:15] [B-CAM: Alex's voice carries fatigue just describing it.]**

**ALEX:**
"Third: coordination is exhausting. Even though the technology has become natural, maintaining the coordination requires mental energy. On high-use days, I'm mentally exhausted by evening---not from the tasks themselves, but from the continuous coordination. People don't realize: able-bodied interaction with the environment is unconscious. BCI interaction is mostly unconscious, but there's a thin layer of conscious attention required to maintain coordination. It's like... you know how if you're driving in a foreign country, on the wrong side of the road, you can do it but by the end of the day you're wiped out? Even though driving itself is automatic? There's this thin layer of vigilance. That's my every day. Over a full day, that adds up. It's invisible labor that no one sees. I look like I'm just sitting at my computer. Nobody knows that underneath, my brain is running a constant coordination protocol on top of everything else."

### SEGMENT 9: What I Want Next (3 min)

**[33:30 - 33:37] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Looking forward---what improvements do you want to see? What would make the biggest difference in your daily life?"

**[33:37 - 34:40] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Better coordination, not better technology. Let me explain. Tighter brain-interface coordination: faster learning, more accurate interpretation, less mental effort. I want the coordination to be as unconscious as biological motor control. Current state: ninety percent success rate, slight mental effort. Goal: ninety-nine percent success rate, zero mental effort. That last ten percent sounds small. It's not. It's the difference between driving in a foreign country and driving at home. It's the difference between fluent and native."

**[34:40 - 34:46] [A-CAM]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What about the environment side---the third body?"

**[34:46 - 35:55] [B-CAM]**

**ALEX:**
"Smarter interface-environment coordination. I want the interface to be more contextually aware. To understand what I'm trying to accomplish in my life, not just what neural signals I'm generating. Example: I'm video calling my mom. The interface should know that means I want good lighting, minimal background noise, stable connectivity. It should coordinate with my environment to optimize for that context without me explicitly controlling every parameter. Right now I control devices individually. I want the system to coordinate my entire environment to support my intentions. And I want richer feedback. Right now, the feedback is mostly visual. I want haptic, auditory, even neural feedback that creates the sensation of touch. The scientists are working on bidirectional BCIs that send sensory information back to the brain. That would close the coordination loop. Right now I can reach out but I can't feel what I'm touching. Half a body."
