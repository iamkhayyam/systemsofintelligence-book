## "Virtue Ethics for Coordinated Intelligence: Beyond Rules and Optimization"

**Chapter 5: Human-Systems Intelligence Interaction**

**Visual Style:** Philosophical depth meets practical wisdom—abstract concepts grounded in concrete examples

**Guest Bio:**
Dr. Shannon Vallor is Professor of Philosophy at the University of Edinburgh and author of "Technology and the Virtues: A Philosophical Guide to a Future Worth Wanting." Her work bridges ancient virtue ethics with contemporary technology ethics, arguing that AI challenges require not new ethical rules but renewed cultivation of human wisdom, courage, and practical judgment.

**Core Thesis:**
AI ethics fails when it tries to encode ethics into systems as rules or utility functions. Ethics is coordination between personal virtue, technological capability, and social context. We need AI that participates in ethical reasoning while humans maintain ethical responsibility.

## ACT I: THE ETHICS CRISIS

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Objective:** Establish why current AI ethics frameworks fail, and why the failure is structural, not incidental

### Opening [0:45-3:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Dr. Vallor, the technology industry has embraced AI ethics with remarkable enthusiasm. Every major company has published principles, hired ethicists, formed review boards. And yet the problems persist. Bias, manipulation, social harm. What is going wrong?"

**VALLOR:**
"What is going wrong is that we are trying to solve a coordination problem with optimization tools. And I mean that quite precisely.

The dominant approach in the industry is utilitarian in spirit, even when it does not call itself that. The idea is: define a good outcome, build a metric for it, and optimize. Facebook optimized for engagement, which was their proxy for user satisfaction. The optimization succeeded beautifully. Users spent more time on the platform, clicked more, shared more. And the result was addiction, polarization, measurable harm to adolescent mental health.

They optimized the wrong thing. But the deeper problem is that they thought ethics could be reduced to optimization at all."

**[01:45] [Visual: Vallor turns to the whiteboard. Writes 'Ethics = Optimization?' and draws a large X through it.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the utilitarian framing is the core mistake?"

**VALLOR:**
"It is one of two mistakes. The other is equally seductive. The deontological approach says: instead of optimizing outcomes, program rules. Do not lie. Respect privacy. Treat people fairly. These are genuinely important principles. But watch what happens when you try to implement them.

An autonomous vehicle encounters a situation where it must choose between passenger safety and pedestrian safety. A content moderation system must decide whether a particular post constitutes free speech or incitement. A hiring algorithm must determine what counts as fair when the historical data reflects decades of systemic discrimination.

Rules cannot handle this. They are too rigid for the contextual complexity of real ethical situations. Every rule needs exceptions, every exception requires judgment, and judgment is precisely what rules were supposed to replace."

**[02:45] [Visual: Vallor writes 'Ethics = Rules?' with another X through it.]**

### Segment 1: Ethics as Coordination [3:00-6:30]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"If ethics is neither optimization nor rule-following, then what is it?"

**VALLOR:**
"Ethics is the practiced capacity to coordinate multiple values in context. It is the ability to navigate competing goods, to understand particular situations in their specificity, and to make wise judgments that serve human flourishing.

This is what Aristotle called phronesis — practical wisdom. Not theoretical knowledge of the good, but the lived capacity to perceive what a situation demands and to act accordingly. It is not a formula. It is a skill developed through experience, reflection, and practice."

**[03:30] [Visual: Vallor writes 'Ethics = Coordinated Wisdom' on the whiteboard. No X this time.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And this connects to AI how, exactly?"

**VALLOR:**
"Every ethical situation involving AI requires coordination among three bodies. I use that language deliberately because none of these can be collapsed into the others.

The first body is individual virtue — the personal layer. These are character traits: wisdom, courage, compassion, justice, temperance. Not rules you follow but capacities you develop through practice. Wisdom lets you see situations clearly. Courage lets you act despite difficulty. Compassion lets you enter another person's experience. Justice lets you weigh competing interests with care. These are cultivated over a lifetime through experience and honest reflection."

**[04:15] [Visual: First circle appears on the whiteboard. Vallor labels it 'Human Virtue'.]**

**VALLOR:**
"The second body is AI systems — the technological layer. AI can process vast quantities of data, recognize patterns humans would miss, maintain consistency at scale, optimize within defined parameters. These are genuinely powerful capabilities. But AI cannot develop wisdom. It cannot feel compassion. It cannot understand context the way a person who has lived in the world understands it. AI is an ethical amplifier — it scales human decisions, for good or ill, but it cannot replace human judgment."

**[04:55] [Visual: Second circle. 'AI Capability'.]**

**VALLOR:**
"The third body is social context — the reality layer. Ethical situations do not happen in a vacuum. They happen in specific social contexts shaped by cultural norms, power dynamics, historical injustices, systemic effects. What is ethical in one context may not be in another. A content moderation decision appropriate in one cultural setting might be deeply harmful in another. A hiring algorithm that appears fair in isolation may perpetuate systemic inequality in practice.

This is the coordination reality — where actual outcomes materialize and where ethics is ultimately judged."

**[05:40] [Visual: Third circle. 'Social Context'. Arrows connecting all three.]**

**VALLOR:**
"Ethics emerges from coordinating all three. Human virtue guiding AI capability within social context to produce outcomes that serve flourishing. When we try to reduce ethics to a single body — just optimize the algorithm, just follow rules, just respect cultural norms — we fail. Ethical coordination requires all three, held in dynamic tension."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"That is a much more demanding picture than most ethics frameworks offer."

**VALLOR:**
"It is. And that is appropriate, because the ethical challenges posed by AI are genuinely difficult. The desire for a simple framework — a checklist, a set of principles you can laminate and post on the wall — is understandable. But it is a desire for false comfort. Ethics has always been hard. What AI does is raise the stakes and the speed."

### Segment 2: The Aristotelian Foundation [6:30-10:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You mentioned Aristotle. Some might wonder why a philosopher who lived twenty-four centuries ago is relevant to artificial intelligence."

**[06:45] [Visual: Vallor picks up a worn copy of the Nicomachean Ethics from her desk.]**

**VALLOR:**
"Aristotle understood something that most contemporary ethicists have forgotten, or at least underemphasized. He understood that ethics is not primarily about knowing what is right. It is about becoming the kind of person who can perceive and enact what is right in particular circumstances. The question is not 'what should I do?' in the abstract. It is 'what does this situation, with all its particularities, demand of me — and do I have the character to respond well?'

That distinction is everything when it comes to AI. We keep asking: how do we make AI ethical? How do we program AI to do the right thing? And those are the wrong questions. The right question is: how do we cultivate the human capacities needed to coordinate wisely with AI? What kind of people must we become?"

**INTERVIEWER:**
"In your book, you identify what you call 'technomoral virtues.' Can you walk us through that concept?"

**VALLOR:**
"Technomoral virtues are the classical virtues reinterpreted for an age of powerful technology. Honesty in the age of deepfakes requires not just commitment to truth but a cultivated capacity to discern fabrication. Humility in the age of algorithmic recommendation means recognizing the limits of your perspective even when your information diet confirms your beliefs. Justice in the age of automated decisions requires understanding how technical systems encode discrimination invisible to those who benefit from it."

**[08:30] [Visual: Vallor lists on the whiteboard: Honesty, Humility, Justice, Courage, Empathy, Self-control, Flexibility, Perspective]**

**VALLOR:**
"Each virtue is ancient. Aristotle would recognize every one. But the contexts in which they must be exercised are radically new. The virtues are not new — the practices through which we cultivate them must be."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And this is more than just a philosophical argument. You are making a practical claim about AI governance."

**VALLOR:**
"Entirely practical. If the people designing, deploying, and overseeing AI systems lack practical wisdom — if they cannot perceive the ethical dimensions of what they are building, if they lack the courage to act on that perception, if they lack the justice to consider those who will be most affected — then no framework, no checklist, no review board will save us. Ethics review is necessary but not sufficient. What is sufficient is a culture of virtue among the people doing the work."

## ACT II: VIRTUE ETHICS FOR AI

**Duration:** 22 minutes
**Objective:** Develop the positive framework — what virtue-guided AI coordination looks like in theory and practice

### Segment 3: Beyond the Trolley Problem [10:00-15:30]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let us take a concrete case. Content recommendation algorithms are perhaps the most consequential AI systems currently deployed. How does a virtue ethics approach differ from what we have now?"

**[10:15] [Visual: Graphics showing social media recommendation flow — user data in, engagement metrics cycling, content becoming progressively more extreme.]**

**VALLOR:**
"This is an excellent test case because it reveals the failures of both dominant approaches with painful clarity.

The utilitarian approach says: maximize engagement. The algorithms are extraordinarily good at it. They discovered that outrage and fear are the most reliable drivers. Users become polarized, anxious, addicted. The optimization succeeded. The ethics failed. Because engagement is not flourishing."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the rule-based approach?"

**VALLOR:**
"Program rules: do not recommend misinformation, do not harm mental health, respect autonomy. These sound excellent until you try to implement them. How do you define misinformation when a contested finding may be established fact tomorrow? What constitutes harm to mental health when distressing content may be important in the long term? Respecting autonomy means what — giving people what they want, or what serves their interests? These are questions of judgment, not engineering."

**[11:45] [Visual: Vallor draws two columns on the whiteboard — 'Optimization' and 'Rules' — with their respective failure modes listed beneath.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So what does virtue ethics offer instead?"

**VALLOR:**
"Virtue ethics reframes the question entirely. Instead of asking 'what metric should we optimize?' or 'what rules should we enforce?' it asks: what virtues should guide the design and operation of this system?

If the recommendation system were guided by wisdom, it would seek to understand what genuinely serves user wellbeing rather than merely what maximizes engagement. If guided by temperance, it would recommend in moderation rather than overwhelming users or exploiting psychological vulnerabilities. If guided by justice, it would surface diverse perspectives rather than narrowing the user's world to an echo chamber. If guided by compassion, it would consider emotional impact rather than treating human attention as a resource to be extracted."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"But algorithms do not have character traits."

**VALLOR:**
"Precisely. And that is the point. The algorithm does not need virtue — but the humans who design, train, deploy, and oversee it absolutely do. The algorithm amplifies whatever values are embedded in it, whether those values are explicit or implicit. Currently, the implicit value is engagement maximization, which is a proxy for advertising revenue. That is a vice, not a virtue, being amplified at planetary scale.

The coordination works like this: humans provide the virtues — wisdom, judgment, values. AI provides pattern recognition, consistency, scale. Social context provides feedback on actual outcomes. The algorithm does not need to be wise. But it needs to be built and guided by wise people, operating within institutional structures that reward wisdom rather than extraction."

**[13:30] [Visual: Vallor draws the three-body diagram again, this time specifically for recommendation systems: 'Design Team Virtue' / 'Algorithm Capability' / 'User & Social Context'.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"That shifts the burden from the technology to the people building it."

**VALLOR:**
"It shifts the burden to where it belongs. We have been engaged in a kind of moral outsourcing — trying to make the technology ethical so that we do not have to be. That is precisely backwards. Technology does not have moral agency. Humans do. The question is whether we are cultivating that agency or allowing it to atrophy."

### Segment 4: Designing for Coordination [15:30-21:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How do we design AI systems that coordinate with human virtue rather than eroding it? What does that look like in practice?"

**VALLOR:**
"I would offer three design principles, each grounded in a different virtue.

The first is transparency, grounded in the virtue of honesty. AI systems should make their reasoning visible to the humans who depend on them. Not black boxes that produce inscrutable outputs, but systems that show their work. When a doctor uses an AI diagnostic system, that system should surface not just a recommendation but the reasoning behind it — the patterns it detected, the evidence it weighted, the confidence levels. This enables the doctor to apply their clinical wisdom, their knowledge of this particular patient, their understanding of context that the algorithm cannot access."

**[16:15] [Visual: Example of transparent AI interface — a medical diagnostic system showing its reasoning chain alongside confidence intervals.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This is essentially making the AI's reasoning available for human judgment."

**VALLOR:**
"Exactly. The transparency is not an end in itself. It is the condition for coordination. You cannot coordinate wisely with something you cannot see or understand. Opacity destroys the possibility of ethical coordination because it forces humans to either trust blindly or reject entirely, neither of which is wise.

The second principle is what I call maintained agency, grounded in the virtue of practical wisdom. AI should support human decision-making, not supplant it. Keep humans in the coordination loop, particularly for consequential decisions.

Consider an AI system assisting judges with bail decisions. The AI can analyze recidivism patterns, flag relevant case factors, identify comparable cases. These are genuine contributions. But the judge makes the final determination, applying their experience with this particular defendant, their understanding of the local context, their perception of factors the data cannot capture. The coordination is: AI pattern recognition plus human judgment yields better decisions than either could achieve alone."

**[17:30]**

**VALLOR:**
"But the system must be designed so that human judgment is genuinely exercised, not merely rubber-stamped. If the default is to accept the AI's recommendation and deviation requires justification, you have created the appearance of oversight while systematically undermining it. Genuine coordination requires understanding human-machine psychology, not just technical architecture."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the third principle?"

**VALLOR:**
"Ethical feedback loops, grounded in the virtue of humility. Design systems that learn from ethical outcomes, not merely technical metrics.

Take a hiring AI. The standard approach measures prediction accuracy against predefined metrics. A virtue-informed approach asks: Did people hired thrive? Did the process feel fair? Did diversity improve? Did the team become more creative? These are harder to measure. They require patience and qualitative judgment. But they are what actually matters."

**[18:45]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This sounds like it requires fundamentally changing how AI development works."

**VALLOR:**
"It does. Current AI development optimizes technical performance — accuracy, speed, scale. What I am proposing is optimization for coordination quality: how well does this system coordinate with human virtue and social context to serve flourishing?

That means including ethicists and affected communities in design from the start. Measuring success by human outcomes, not just benchmarks. Iterating on ethical feedback. And sometimes — the part that makes engineers uncomfortable — accepting lower technical performance if it enables better ethical coordination."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Can you give an example of that tradeoff?"

**VALLOR:**
"A highly accurate but opaque diagnostic algorithm versus a slightly less accurate but transparent one. If the transparent system enables doctors to coordinate their clinical wisdom with the AI's pattern recognition, the overall outcome may be substantially better. We have been so focused on optimizing AI performance in isolation that we have neglected the human-AI coordination system. The coordination system is what actually produces ethical outcomes."

**[20:30] [Visual: Diagram comparing 'AI performance optimization' versus 'Coordination quality optimization' — the latter shows a wider circle encompassing human judgment, social outcomes, and system learning.]**

### Segment 5: Cultivating Virtue in the AI Age [21:00-26:30]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"If AI amplifies human virtue or vice, then cultivating virtue becomes an urgent practical matter, not just a philosophical ideal. But technology itself seems to make virtue harder to cultivate. How do you reconcile that?"

**VALLOR:**
"You have identified the central tension. Technology changes the landscape of virtue cultivation — it makes some virtues harder to develop, some easier, and it reveals the need for capacities we may not have named before. Let me give you three cases.

The first is attention and self-governance. In Aristotle's time, temperance primarily meant moderating physical appetites — food, drink, sensual pleasure. Today, the most consequential form of temperance is the governance of attention. Social media platforms, streaming services, infinite scroll interfaces — all engineered by some of the most talented designers in history to capture and hold human attention. The entire business model depends on overriding your capacity for self-regulation.

Developing temperance now requires cultivating the ability to resist algorithmic manipulation. Digital sabbaths, attention training, conscious consumption practices. The virtue is ancient, but the practices must be updated for an environment in which billion-dollar industries are working to erode precisely this capacity."

**[22:15] [Visual: Historical progression — a Greek symposium where temperance governs the mixing of wine, dissolving into a modern scene of a person scrolling through a phone at 2 AM.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the virtue is constant but the obstacle has changed."

**VALLOR:**
"The obstacle has changed and, crucially, it has been engineered. Aristotle worried about natural appetites. We must worry about manufactured ones — desires that have been deliberately created and amplified by systems designed to be irresistible. That raises the stakes enormously.

The second case is empathy. We developed it through millennia of face-to-face interaction — reading body language, hearing vocal tone, sharing presence. Today, much interaction is mediated by technology with radically compressed bandwidth. The platforms incentivize performance over authenticity. You react rather than respond. Developing empathy now requires intentional counterpractice — seeking face-to-face interaction, assuming good faith online, remembering there is a suffering human being behind every screen name."

**[23:30]**

**VALLOR:**
"The third case is the most concerning: practical wisdom itself. Historically, wisdom came from lived experience — making mistakes, observing consequences, developing judgment. Today, AI provides answers instantly. Why struggle with a problem when you can ask a language model? Why develop expertise when you can outsource judgment?"

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You are describing a kind of deskilling."

**VALLOR:**
"Exactly. Cognitive deskilling — the atrophy of capacities we no longer exercise because technology exercises them for us. The risk is catastrophic, because the entire coordination framework depends on humans having wisdom. If we outsource judgment, we lose the very capacity that makes ethical coordination possible. We become passengers in a vehicle with no driver.

Developing wisdom now means resisting the temptation to outsource judgment. Using AI as scaffolding to be gradually removed, not a permanent crutch."

**[25:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This connects back to your coordination framework. If humans lose their virtue, the three-body system collapses."

**VALLOR:**
"Precisely. The coordination fails if any of the three bodies is degraded. If the AI is poorly designed, the coordination fails. If the social context is ignored, it fails. And if humans lose the virtue needed to guide the coordination — if we become passive consumers of AI outputs rather than active agents of ethical judgment — then the entire framework collapses. We end up with powerful technology guided by no one.

This is why I argue that virtue cultivation is not a luxury or an afterthought. It is the precondition for everything else in AI ethics. You can have the best-designed AI system in the world, and if the people operating it lack wisdom, the outcomes will be harmful. Education, practice, institutional support for the development of human character — these are AI governance measures as surely as any regulation or audit."

### Segment 6: The Medical AI Case [26:30-32:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let us ground this in a specific case. You have written extensively about medical AI. Walk us through how virtue ethics plays out in a clinical setting."

**VALLOR:**
"Medicine is the ideal test case because it has always been about coordinating technical knowledge with human judgment. Physicians have navigated this tension for centuries — between the science and the art of medicine. AI intensifies it.

Here is the scenario. A physician is using an AI diagnostic system. A patient presents with a complex set of symptoms. The AI analyzes the symptoms against the patient's medical history, cross-references the latest research, and recommends a diagnosis and treatment plan. What should the physician do?"

**[27:15] [Visual: Medical consultation room — a physician at a desk, an AI diagnostic display on one screen, a patient's chart on another.]**

**VALLOR:**
"The utilitarian temptation says: follow the AI recommendation. It is trained on millions of cases. Its pattern recognition exceeds human capability. Maximize expected outcomes by deferring to the superior classifier.

But this ignores everything that makes medicine ethical. It ignores the patient's values, their tolerance for risk, the meaning they assign to different outcomes. A treatment that maximizes lifespan may not maximize flourishing if it destroys quality of life. Optimization requires a single metric, and human lives cannot be reduced to one."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the rule-based approach?"

**VALLOR:**
"Medical ethics has explicit rules — respect patient autonomy, do no harm, maintain confidentiality, act for the patient's benefit. These are important constraints. But they do not tell the physician what to do when the rules conflict. If the AI recommends an aggressive treatment that may extend survival but will likely cause significant suffering, how do you balance beneficence against non-maleficence? If the patient makes a choice the physician believes is medically unwise, how do you balance autonomy against duty of care? Rules set the boundaries. They do not navigate the space within them."

**[28:45]**

**VALLOR:**
"The virtue ethics approach coordinates all three bodies. The physician brings medical virtue — years of clinical experience, wisdom about what treatments actually help people live well, not merely longer. The AI brings pattern recognition across millions of cases, synthesis of current research. The patient brings their values, their life goals, their understanding of what a good life means to them.

The physician does not follow the AI. They coordinate. They use the AI's insights to inform clinical judgment, apply wisdom to understand this patient in this moment, engage the patient in shared decision-making. And they arrive at a recommendation that serves this patient's flourishing — which may or may not align with what the AI would recommend alone."

**[30:00] [Visual: Diagram showing coordination — three overlapping circles: 'Clinical Virtue' / 'AI Diagnosis' / 'Patient Context', with 'Wise Decision' at the intersection.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The result is better than any single input."

**VALLOR:**
"That is genuine coordination. Better than the physician alone, better than the AI alone, better than the patient alone. The coordination produces something none of the parts could produce independently.

But the coordination only works if the physician maintains their clinical virtue. If physicians become dependent on AI recommendations, if they stop exercising judgment, the coordination collapses. The AI becomes an authority rather than a tool. And that is dangerous, because the AI does not know this patient. It knows patterns. The physician knows this person."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the maintenance of human expertise is not just professionally important — it is ethically essential."

**VALLOR:**
"Ethically essential. If we allow AI to deskill physicians — if we create a generation of doctors who have never developed the deep clinical judgment that comes from years of unassisted practice — we will have optimized the component while destroying the system. The human-AI coordination system will produce worse outcomes than the human alone would have, because the human component has been hollowed out."

## ACT III: BUILDING COORDINATED ETHICS

**Duration:** 12 minutes
**Objective:** Move from theory to practical implementation and address fundamental limitations

### Segment 7: Practical Implementation [32:00-37:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How does an organization actually implement this? If I am leading a technology company and I come to you and say, 'We want to take a virtue ethics approach to our AI products,' what do you tell me?"

**VALLOR:**
"I tell you three things, and I warn you that none of them is easy.

The first step is a virtue-centered design process. Instead of beginning with the question 'what can this AI do?' — which is where nearly every project starts — begin with 'what virtues should guide this AI's development and use?' Gather your stakeholders. And I mean all of them — developers, designers, users, and most importantly the communities who will be most affected by the system. Identify the relevant virtues together. What wisdom is needed here? What justice considerations are at stake? What forms of temperance are required? What courage will it take to act on what you discover?

This becomes the ethical framework for the project. Not a list of rules to be checked off, but a set of virtues to coordinate around throughout the entire lifecycle."

**[33:00] [Visual: Workshop scene — diverse stakeholders around a table, whiteboard covered with virtue-related questions and post-it notes.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"That sounds like it could become performative — another form of ethics theater."

**VALLOR:**
"It absolutely can, and often does. Which is why the second step matters: coordination architecture. You must design the system itself to enable genuine coordination between human virtue and AI capability.

This means genuine visibility into AI reasoning — not post-hoc explanations but real transparency. Keeping humans in the loop for consequential decisions in ways that support rather than undermine judgment. Building feedback mechanisms that capture whether people are actually flourishing. And creating reflection points where everyone reassesses whether the system serves its purpose.

This is not just technical architecture. It is ethical architecture."

**[34:30]**

**VALLOR:**
"The third step is ongoing virtue cultivation, and this is where most organizations fail because it requires sustained investment in people rather than systems. Train developers not just in technical skills but in ethical reasoning and contextual awareness. Support users in developing the wisdom to coordinate with AI effectively. Create communities of practice — internal and cross-organizational — where people can share ethical challenges, discuss difficult cases, and develop judgment collectively.

And do this continuously. Not a one-time training everyone forgets by the following quarter. Ongoing, embedded, valued — the way a medical school weaves ethics through the entire curriculum, not a single course."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"This sounds considerably more demanding than publishing a set of AI principles."

**VALLOR:**
"It is. Virtue ethics is inherently more demanding than either utilitarianism or deontology because it requires continuous judgment, reflection, and adaptation. You cannot check the ethics box and move on. You cannot outsource ethical responsibility to a review board and return to optimizing metrics.

But that difficulty is appropriate. The problems we face with AI are genuinely hard. They involve real tradeoffs between real values affecting real people. Anyone who tells you AI ethics is simple is either selling something or has not understood the problem. The virtue ethics approach acknowledges the genuine difficulty and provides a framework for navigating it through coordinated human judgment."

### Segment 8: The Limits of AI Ethics [37:00-40:30]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What are the fundamental things AI cannot do ethically, even with perfect coordination?"

**VALLOR:**
"There are three limitations that I believe are not merely current technical constraints but structural features of what AI is. And recognizing them is essential for maintaining the kind of human agency that ethical coordination requires.

First, AI cannot understand meaning. AI processes patterns in data. It can identify statistical regularities with extraordinary precision. But it does not grasp what those patterns signify for human flourishing. It does not understand what suffering means to the person experiencing it, what dignity means to the person whose dignity is at stake, what justice means to the community that has been denied it. This is not a matter of more data or better architecture. Meaning is constituted by lived experience, and AI does not have lived experience."

**[38:00]**

**VALLOR:**
"Second, AI cannot make value judgments. When values genuinely conflict — privacy against security, freedom against safety, individual rights against collective welfare — someone must decide which matters more in this particular situation. AI can map the tradeoffs with great clarity. It can show you the consequences of different choices. But it cannot tell you which values to prioritize, because that determination requires a commitment to a vision of the good that emerges from human experience and reflection. And we must not pretend otherwise. When an AI system appears to make value judgments, what it is actually doing is implementing value judgments that were made, explicitly or implicitly, by the humans who designed and trained it."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the third?"

**VALLOR:**
"AI cannot take moral responsibility. When ethical decisions cause harm — and some inevitably will, because ethics involves real tradeoffs with real stakes — someone must be accountable. Someone must answer for what happened, learn from it, make amends. AI cannot do this because it does not have moral agency. It does not choose in the morally relevant sense. It does not bear guilt or obligation. The responsibility remains with the humans who built, deployed, and oversaw the system.

This means that the fantasy of autonomous ethical AI — the idea that we can build systems that make ethical decisions independently and thereby relieve us of moral burden — is not just technically implausible. It is morally incoherent. You cannot delegate moral responsibility to an entity that cannot bear it."

**[39:30]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So there is an ethical limit to AI capability that is not merely a current limitation."

**VALLOR:**
"Yes. And recognizing that limit is itself a form of wisdom — perhaps the most important form of wisdom in our current moment. The temptation is enormous. The systems are impressive. The desire to believe that we can build our way out of ethical difficulty is deeply human. But we must resist it. We must maintain human ethical agency and responsibility while coordinating with AI's genuine capabilities. That is the narrow path, and it is the only path to AI that genuinely serves human flourishing."

### Segment 9: The Vision [40:30-43:30]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"What does success look like? If we get this right — if we actually build a culture of virtue-guided AI coordination — what does that future look like?"

**VALLOR:**
"I envision a future in which AI amplifies human virtue rather than eroding it. Where technology enables us to be wiser, more compassionate, more just — not by replacing our ethical capacities but by extending them. Not artificial wisdom, but augmented wisdom."

**[41:00] [Visual: Montage — ethical AI applications across domains: a physician using a transparent diagnostic system, a teacher with an AI tutor that adapts to each student's needs, citizens engaging with civic AI that makes policy tradeoffs visible.]**

**VALLOR:**
"In medicine, AI helps physicians apply clinical wisdom at scale while deepening rather than replacing clinical judgment. In education, AI helps teachers recognize each student's needs, enabling personalized attention that was impossible in a class of thirty. In governance, AI helps citizens see the consequences of policy choices, enabling wiser democratic deliberation.

And in daily life, AI helps us resist manipulation rather than succumbing to it. It supports our attention rather than fragmenting it. It serves as coordination infrastructure for human virtue rather than as a substitute for it."

**INTERVIEWER:**

Achieving it requires designing AI for coordination rather than autonomy, cultivating virtue in developers and institutions, maintaining human ethical agency in the face of enormous pressure to surrender it. The question is not how do we make AI ethical. The question is how do we coordinate AI capability with human virtue in social context to serve flourishing. That is the virtue ethics approach. And it is, I believe, our best path to technology that genuinely serves human good."
