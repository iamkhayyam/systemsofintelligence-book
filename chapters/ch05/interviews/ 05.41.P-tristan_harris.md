**[00:00-02:10] Opening Question**

**INTERVIEWER:** You've called social media platforms "coordination traps," which is a striking phrase. Most people experience these platforms as personal choices—they open an app, they scroll, they close it. Where does coordination enter the picture, and why do you frame it that way rather than simply calling these products addictive?

**[B-CAM - Close-up on Harris, visibly energized by the question]**

**HARRIS:** The framing matters enormously. If you call it addiction, you're putting the problem inside the individual. "You're addicted, you lack willpower, put your phone down." That's a two-body story: one person, one device, fix the person.

But what's actually happening is structural. When I was at Google, I watched brilliant engineers—people who cared about the world—build features that systematically degraded human capacity to think clearly, to be present, to govern ourselves. They weren't doing it on purpose. They were optimizing engagement metrics because that's what the system rewarded.

The word "trap" is deliberate. A trap works because the individual incentive leads you in—the cheese looks good—but the collective outcome is catastrophic. Every person checking their phone at dinner thinks they're making an individual choice. But when everyone at the table is doing it, you've lost something no individual can recover alone. That's coordination failure.

### Segment 1: The Two-Body Trap (4 min)

**[02:10-06:15]**

**INTERVIEWER:** Walk us through how you saw this play out inside the industry. When a product team at Google or Facebook designs a new feature, what's the mental model they're working with, and where does it break down?

**[VISUAL: Whiteboard - Harris draws two circles: "Technology" and "Users"]**

**HARRIS:** The standard mental model is what I'd call two-body thinking. You have the technology on one side and the user on the other. The product team asks: "Is this feature good for users?" That's the entire frame. Technology, users, done.

So take notifications. The team says, "Users like getting notifications about friend activity. When we send notifications, users come back to the app. They report feeling more connected. Let's send more notifications." And within that two-body frame, it's completely reasonable. Individual user gets value. Ship it.

**INTERVIEWER:** And that analysis isn't wrong, exactly—

**HARRIS:** It's not wrong. It's incomplete. It's dangerously incomplete. Because they're ignoring the third body entirely—social dynamics. How individual behaviors coordinate to create collective outcomes that nobody chose and nobody wants.

Here's what actually happens with notifications when you add that third body to the analysis:

**Individual level**: You get a notification, you check your phone, you feel a moment of connection. Positive experience. Check.

**Social level**: Everyone gets notifications. Everyone checks their phones constantly. You're at dinner and three people reach for their pocket in the span of two minutes. Nobody is present with each other. The ambient hum of a conversation disappears because everyone's attention is being auctioned off to whoever pinged them last.

The feature that's good for individuals destroys collective presence. And here's the really pernicious part—once everyone else is checking their phone, your incentive to stay present collapses too. Why would you sit there being the only one not looking at your screen? So the coordination pattern becomes self-reinforcing.

**[VISUAL: Harris adds third circle: "Social Dynamics" - shows coordination arrows between all three]**

**INTERVIEWER:** So the third body—social dynamics—isn't just an afterthought. It fundamentally changes the evaluation?

**HARRIS:** It changes everything. And this pattern repeats across every major feature these platforms have shipped:

**Infinite scroll**: Convenient for individuals—no need to click "next page." But collectively, it creates compulsive checking behavior. People report picking up their phones and losing forty-five minutes without realizing it. The feature eliminates the natural stopping cues that help individuals self-regulate.

**Autoplay**: Seamless for individuals—your next video starts automatically. But collectively, it creates binge behavior. Netflix's own research showed that autoplay was correlated with sleep disruption across their user base. You remove the moment of choice—"Do I want to watch another one?"—and the default becomes endless consumption.

**Algorithmic feeds**: Personalized for individuals—you see content tailored to your interests. But collectively, it creates filter bubbles and polarization. Everyone lives in a different informational reality, curated to maximize their engagement, which means curated to confirm their existing beliefs and trigger their emotional responses.

The features aren't designed to be harmful. They're designed to maximize engagement without ever asking the coordination question: how does individual engagement aggregate into social outcomes?

### Segment 2: The Like Button Case Study (6 min)

**[06:15-12:05]**

**INTERVIEWER:** Let's go deep on one specific example. The Like button—it seems so innocuous. A thumbs up. How do you analyze that through this three-body lens?

**HARRIS:** The Like button is the perfect case study because it's so simple on the surface and so devastating in its coordination effects. Let me walk you through it body by body.

**[VISUAL: Animation showing Like button evolution]**

**HARRIS:** **Body 1: Individual Psychology.**

Humans crave social validation. This isn't a design flaw—it's how we evolved. We're social primates. We need to know: "Do people approve of me? Am I accepted by my group?" These are deep, legitimate psychological needs.

The Like button provides instant, quantified feedback on that need. You post something, you get validation. Little dopamine hit. Positive reinforcement loop. At the individual level, this feels genuinely good. You share a photo of your kid, your friends heart it, you feel seen. What's wrong with that?

**INTERVIEWER:** Nothing, in isolation.

**HARRIS:** Exactly. In isolation, nothing. Now—

**Body 2: Platform Mechanics.**

Facebook needs engagement. More posts, more time on platform, more ad impressions, more revenue. The Like button is an engagement engine. People post more frequently because they want likes. They check back obsessively to see if new likes have arrived. They spend more time on the platform because the variable reward schedule—"Did I get more likes since I last checked?"—is the same psychological mechanism that makes slot machines compelling.

At the platform level, this is perfect. Engagement climbs. Revenue climbs. Investors are happy. Ship it to two billion people.

**HARRIS:** **Body 3: Social Coordination.**

Now watch what happens when you coordinate individual psychology with platform mechanics across billions of users simultaneously. This is where the three-body dynamics produce outcomes that nobody designed and nobody wanted.

**Phase 1—Behavior Change**: People start unconsciously optimizing for likes. They don't post what's authentic—they post what gets likes. The selfie from the perfect angle. The hot take that generates reactions. The performance of a life that looks enviable. This isn't conscious for most people. The reinforcement loop just gradually shapes what they share.

**Phase 2—Social Norms Shift**: When everyone is performing for likes, authenticity becomes the exception rather than the norm. The social contract of the platform silently rewrites itself. It's no longer "share your life with friends." It's "perform a likeable version of your life for an audience." And once that norm takes hold, anyone who posts authentically—"I'm struggling today"—gets fewer likes and learns to stop.

**Phase 3—Psychological Impact**: Because likes are public and quantified, comparison becomes inevitable. You see that your post got 12 likes and your friend's got 200. You feel inadequate. Teenagers especially internalize this—their sense of social worth becomes tied to a number that refreshes in real time. The research on this is devastating. Anxiety, depression, body image disorders, all correlated with social media use in adolescents.

**Phase 4—Collective Trap**: Social media has now transformed from a connection platform into a performance platform. Authenticity is dead. Mental health is suffering. But everyone is trapped because leaving means losing your social graph, your connections, your access to community. The switching costs are enormous. So people stay in a system that's making them miserable because the coordination dynamics won't let them leave individually.

**[VISUAL: Diagram showing cascade from individual behavior to collective harm]**

**INTERVIEWER:** So the Like button created all of this?

**HARRIS:** The Like button didn't create this alone. The Like button *coordinated* individual psychology with platform mechanics with network effects to produce this. And that distinction is the whole point.

If you evaluate the Like button as a two-body problem—"Do users like getting likes?"—the answer is yes, and you ship it. If you evaluate it as a three-body coordination problem—"How does this feature coordinate user psychology with social dynamics to produce systemic outcomes?"—you see the catastrophe coming.

This is why I say technology ethics fails when treated as a two-body problem. You have to ask the coordination question. Always.

## ACT II: THE COORDINATION PATTERNS OF HARM (20 minutes)

### Segment 3: Outrage and Polarization (5 min)

**[12:05-17:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** Let's move from social validation to something arguably more dangerous—political polarization. You've called this the greatest coordination failure of our time. Make the case.

**HARRIS:** Polarization is the canonical example of coordination producing emergent harm that no individual actor intended. It's also the one that threatens democratic self-governance itself, which is why I think it's the most urgent.

**[VISUAL: Screen showing social media feed with political content]**

**HARRIS:** Start with the algorithmic coordination. Social media algorithms optimize for engagement—clicks, shares, comments, time on platform. That's their objective function. And the research is unambiguous: outrage content generates two to three times more engagement than neutral content. Divisive content gets shared at dramatically higher rates than nuanced content. A study from MIT found that false news stories spread six times faster than true ones on Twitter, and the primary driver was novelty and emotional arousal.

So the algorithm learns—not through malice, through optimization—show people content that triggers outrage. That's what maximizes the metric you're optimizing for.

**INTERVIEWER:** The algorithm is doing exactly what it was designed to do.

**HARRIS:** Precisely. Now layer on the psychological coordination. Humans have negativity bias—we evolved to pay more attention to threats than to opportunities, because missing a threat could kill you. We also have deep tribal instincts—we bond with our in-group by identifying threats from out-groups.

Outrage content activates both circuits simultaneously. "The other side is threatening everything you care about!" Your attention locks on. Your amygdala fires. You share it with your tribe because sharing threat information is a deeply evolved social behavior. You engage because you feel compelled to defend your group.

The algorithm coordinates with your deepest psychological architecture to keep you in a state of perpetual outrage. Not because it's trying to make you angry—because angry people are engaged people, and engaged people are profitable people.

**HARRIS:** Now here's where three-body dynamics produce civilizational-scale consequences. When billions of people are simultaneously being shown outrage content, all sharing it with their tribes, all engaging with divisive narratives:

**Phase 1**: Filter bubbles form. You only see content that confirms your worldview, because that's what you engage with, and the algorithm gives you more of what you engage with. The information environment splits.

**Phase 2**: The other side becomes caricatured. You never see the reasonable people on the other side—you only see the most extreme examples, because those generate the most outrage and engagement. Your mental model of the other side becomes a cartoon.

**Phase 3**: Empathy breaks down. You literally cannot understand how any rational person could think differently, because the information environment you live in makes the other side look insane. Cross-partisan friendships decline. Family relationships fracture over politics.

**Phase 4**: Shared reality collapses. We can't agree on basic facts. We can't agree on what problems exist. We can't govern ourselves because governance requires enough shared reality to negotiate and compromise.

**[VISUAL: Animation showing polarization cascade across network]**

**HARRIS:** None of this was designed. No engineer at Facebook sat down and said, "Let's destroy American democracy." But the coordination architecture—algorithm optimizing for engagement, psychology responding to outrage, social dynamics amplifying tribalism—produces exactly that outcome. It's emergent. And it's terrifying precisely because no one is in control of it.

**INTERVIEWER:** How do you interrupt a coordination pattern that's running on autopilot across billions of people?

**HARRIS:** You have to redesign the coordination architecture itself. All three bodies, simultaneously.

**Technology**: Stop optimizing for engagement. Optimize for understanding, for nuance, for bridge-building. Algorithmically promote content that exposes people to the best arguments on multiple sides, not the most outrageous strawmen.

**Psychology**: Design for our better angels—curiosity, empathy, the desire to understand—not our worst instincts. When someone is about to share an article they haven't read, prompt them: "Want to read this first?" That small friction reduces misinformation sharing by 25 percent.

**Social dynamics**: Measure success by collective outcomes—shared understanding, democratic health, social cohesion. Not by how many hours people spent doom-scrolling.

This requires redesigning the coordination architecture from the ground up. And no one platform can do it alone, which is why regulation is essential.

### Segment 4: The Race to the Bottom of the Brainstem (5 min)

**[17:00-22:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** You coined a vivid phrase that's entered the public conversation—"the race to the bottom of the brainstem." Unpack that for us. What does it describe, and why is it structural rather than incidental?

**HARRIS:** The race to the bottom of the brainstem is the competitive dynamic that makes this crisis self-reinforcing. Here's how it works.

Every app on your phone is competing for one thing: a slice of your finite attention. You have maybe sixteen waking hours. Your attention is the scarce resource. Every platform, every app, every notification is in a zero-sum competition for that resource.

Now, what's the most reliable way to capture human attention? You go lower on the brainstem. You bypass the prefrontal cortex—the part that does planning, reflection, wisdom—and you target the limbic system and the reptilian brain. Fear. Outrage. Social validation. Sexual arousal. Novelty. These are the deep, old circuits that evolution wired to be difficult to resist.

**INTERVIEWER:** And this is a race because—

**HARRIS:** Because if your competitor goes lower and you don't, you lose. If TikTok's algorithm serves more dopamine-triggering content per minute than YouTube's, users migrate. If Instagram exploits social comparison more effectively than Snapchat, Snapchat loses engagement. Each platform is under constant competitive pressure to exploit deeper psychological vulnerabilities.

It's the structure of an arms race. No individual participant wants the escalation, but the competitive dynamics make restraint irrational. If you unilaterally adopt humane design—add friction, respect attention, include stopping points—your engagement drops and your competitor eats your market share.

**[VISUAL: Game theory diagram showing race to the bottom]**

**HARRIS:** This is what people miss when they say, "Just build better products." The competitive structure punishes responsibility. Revenue is proportional to attention captured. Capture less attention, make less money, your board replaces you.

The race to the bottom of the brainstem isn't a choice individual companies are making. It's a coordination failure at the industry level. A tragedy of the commons where the commons is human attention itself.

**INTERVIEWER:** And individuals can't just opt out.

**HARRIS:** That's the trap. People say, "Just put your phone down." But opting out of these platforms now means opting out of social life, professional networking, news, civic participation. You can't get a job without LinkedIn. You can't stay connected to your kid's school without a group chat.

Telling individuals to resist is like telling someone to hold their breath underwater. The coordination architecture itself has to change.

### Segment 5: The Business Model Problem (5 min)

**[22:00-27:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** You're pointing at the advertising business model as the root of the coordination failure. How exactly does the revenue model create misalignment, and what are the alternatives?

**HARRIS:** The advertising model creates a specific coordination trap.

**[VISUAL: Diagram of advertising business model as three-body system]**

**HARRIS:** **Body 1**: Users want connection, information, entertainment. **Body 2**: Platforms need advertising revenue. **Body 3**: Advertisers want attention and influence.

The platform coordinates these by maximizing time on platform and attention capture. More attention equals more ad impressions equals more revenue.

But notice: the platform's financial incentive is now aligned with the advertiser, not the user. The user wants to connect with friends and put the phone down. The advertiser wants the user to keep scrolling past ads. The platform sides with the advertiser because that's where the money comes from.

This creates perverse coordination at every level. Platforms optimize for addiction because addicted users generate more ad revenue. Content that triggers anxiety and outrage beats genuinely valuable content because anxious people scroll more. User wellbeing and platform profit are structurally misaligned.

It's not that the people at these companies don't care. I know many of them. But the business model creates incentives that override individual good intentions. This is a systems problem, not a people problem.

**INTERVIEWER:** So what replaces it? The advertising model funds the free internet that billions of people depend on. You can't just remove it without offering something.

**HARRIS:** There's no single clean replacement. But there are business models that better align platform success with user wellbeing:

**Subscription models**: Users pay directly. The platform succeeds by serving users, not selling their attention to advertisers. Substack is an early example—writers are rewarded for genuine value, not outrage clicks.

**Public utility models**: For information infrastructure essential to democratic functioning—think public libraries—there's a case for public funding. The platform optimizes for social good because that's its mandate.

**Time well spent models**: Success measured by whether users accomplish their goals and feel good about time spent. A social media platform that celebrates when users connect and disconnect, rather than one that keeps them scrolling for hours.

**[VISUAL: Alternative business model diagrams showing aligned incentives]**

**HARRIS:** The key insight: align the incentives so that coordinating user psychology with platform mechanics with social dynamics creates wellbeing, not harm.

And I want to push back on the assumption that humane design is less profitable. When you respect users, they trust you more. They recommend you more. They stay longer as customers—not longer per session, but longer over their lifetime. The current model is extractive—it burns through user trust for short-term engagement. A humane model could be generative—building trust that compounds over time.

### Segment 6: The Humane Technology Principles (5 min)

**[27:00-32:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** At the Center for Humane Technology, you've been developing design principles that offer an alternative path. What does humane technology actually look like in practice? Give us the framework.

**HARRIS:** We've identified three core principles for coordinating technology with human wellbeing. And I want to emphasize—these aren't abstract ideals. Each one is implementable today with existing technology.

**Principle 1: Time Well Spent.**

Technology should help people accomplish their goals and disconnect, not maximize time on platform. This sounds obvious, but it's the exact opposite of how every major platform currently operates.

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side comparison of current vs. humane design patterns]**

**HARRIS:** Current design uses infinite scroll, autoplay, and push notifications—all engineered to keep you engaged indefinitely, to eliminate natural stopping points, to make the default behavior continued consumption.

Humane design creates clear goals, completion markers, and natural stopping points. Imagine opening a social app and seeing: "Here are the 15 updates from your close friends since you last checked. You've seen them all. Go live your life." That's a product that respects you. It coordinates your intentions with platform features with your actual life goals.

The success metric shifts from "time on platform" to "did the user accomplish what they came to do and feel good about their time spent?"

**INTERVIEWER:** Principle two?

**HARRIS:** **Principle 2: Authentic Social Connection.**

Technology should strengthen genuine human relationships, not replace them with performance and comparison.

Current design centers public metrics—likes, follower counts, view numbers. It incentivizes broadcast communication over intimate exchange. It rewards performance over authenticity.

Humane design prioritizes private interactions, small groups, and meaningful conversation. It eliminates metrics that enable social comparison. It actively encourages face-to-face connection. Imagine a platform that notices you've been messaging a friend frequently and suggests: "Looks like you and Sarah have a lot to catch up on. Want to schedule a coffee?"

The coordination architecture changes from "Human connection needs coordinated with platform features to produce engagement" to "Human connection needs coordinated with platform features to produce relationship health."

**HARRIS:** **Principle 3: Information Integrity.**

Technology should promote understanding and shared reality, not polarization and misinformation.

Current design optimizes for engagement, which means optimizing for outrage and division. It creates filter bubbles. It algorithmically amplifies extreme content because extreme content generates more clicks.

Humane design promotes nuance. It exposes users to diverse perspectives presented charitably. It slows down viral spread so that verification can keep pace with distribution. It rewards understanding over reaction—imagine an algorithm that promotes content not because it made people angry but because readers reported they learned something new or understood a different viewpoint.

The coordination shifts from "human attention coordinated with algorithmic amplification to produce engagement" to "human understanding coordinated with information architecture to produce democratic health."

**INTERVIEWER:** These principles all require optimizing for multiple values simultaneously. That's harder than maximizing a single metric.

**HARRIS:** Much harder. And that's exactly the point. The current system is simple—maximize engagement—and that simplicity is what makes it destructive. Because when you reduce a complex human system to a single optimization target, you get Goodhart's Law on a civilizational scale. The metric becomes the target, the target becomes divorced from the actual goal, and you optimize your way into catastrophe.

Humane technology requires holding multiple values in tension: individual wellbeing, platform sustainability, social health, democratic functioning, human dignity. All at once. That's coordination, not optimization. And it's the only path forward.

## ACT III: THE FUTURE OF HUMAN-TECHNOLOGY COORDINATION (12 minutes)

### Segment 7: The Implementation Challenge (4 min)

**[32:00-36:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** Critics would say these principles sound wonderful in a whitepaper but face impossible headwinds in the real market. Why haven't platforms adopted them, and what breaks the deadlock?

**HARRIS:** Three structural obstacles, and I want to name them honestly because pretending this is easy would be dishonest.

**First: Competitive dynamics.** If one platform unilaterally adopts humane design and its competitors don't, users might migrate to the more addictive platform. It's a prisoner's dilemma. The platform that exploits psychology most effectively captures the most attention and wins the market. This is why I keep saying it's a race to the bottom—the competitive structure punishes restraint.

Breaking this requires collective action. Regulation that sets a floor—minimum standards for all platforms—so that no company gains competitive advantage from exploitation. Industry standards, like we have for food safety or automotive safety. And cultural pressure from users who demand better.

**[VISUAL: Game theory diagram showing prisoner's dilemma and regulatory solution]**

**HARRIS:** **Second: Measurement challenges.** It's trivially easy to measure engagement—time on platform, clicks, shares, impressions. These numbers update in real time. Product teams can A/B test and see results in hours.

It's extraordinarily hard to measure wellbeing. How do you quantify "life satisfaction" or "relationship quality" or "democratic health"? These unfold over months and years, not minutes. They're influenced by thousands of factors beyond any single platform.

Platforms optimize what they can measure. If we want them to optimize for wellbeing, we need better measurement tools—longitudinal studies, wellbeing surveys built into platform use, epidemiological-style tracking of social health indicators. This is an active research frontier and it's making progress, but we're not there yet.

**HARRIS:** **Third: Coordination complexity.** Even with the best intentions, humane technology requires navigating genuine value conflicts.

Users want different things—some come for entertainment, others for professional networking, others for news. Society holds competing values—free speech versus harm prevention, privacy versus accountability. Platforms have legitimate business constraints—they need to be sustainable enterprises.

There's no algorithm that resolves these tensions. It requires ongoing human judgment, democratic deliberation, and institutional accountability. This is why we need both better regulation and what Shannon Vallor calls techno-moral virtue—the cultivation of wisdom in the people building and governing these systems.

**INTERVIEWER:** What's the realistic path forward, given all of that?

**HARRIS:** Three coordinated interventions, all happening simultaneously.

**Individual level**: Digital literacy, attention practices, conscious technology consumption. Not as a substitute for structural change—but as a complement to it. Users who understand how manipulation works are harder to manipulate and more likely to demand better.

**Platform level**: Humane design principles, business model innovation, radical transparency about algorithmic effects. Some companies are beginning to move here. Apple's Screen Time features, Instagram hiding like counts in some markets—these are small steps, but they signal that the industry feels the pressure.

**Societal level**: Regulation, enforceable standards, independent auditing, legal liability for coordination harms. Europe's Digital Services Act is an important template. The U.S. is behind but the political will is building across party lines, because both left and right recognize that their voters are being manipulated.

All three levels have to move together. Individual action alone fails because the platforms are too powerful. Self-regulation alone fails because competitive dynamics prevent it. Regulation alone fails because it's too slow and blunt. Coordinated action across all three levels is the only viable path.

### Segment 8: The Regulatory Imperative (4 min)

**[36:00-40:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** Let's go deeper on regulation. You mentioned fiduciary duty for platforms—that's a provocative idea. What would it actually mean in practice?

**HARRIS:** We regulate every other industry that has asymmetric power over human wellbeing. Doctors have fiduciary duties to patients. Financial advisors have fiduciary duties to clients. These duties exist because the professional has knowledge and power that the individual doesn't, and without legal obligation, the incentive to exploit that asymmetry is too strong.

**[VISUAL: Graphics showing regulatory framework comparisons across industries]**

**HARRIS:** Technology platforms have more asymmetric power over human psychology than any doctor or financial advisor. They have teams of PhD behavioral scientists designing engagement features. They have real-time data on your emotional state, your vulnerabilities, your habits.

A platform fiduciary duty would mean: you cannot knowingly design features that exploit psychological vulnerabilities for profit. You cannot optimize for engagement at the demonstrated expense of user mental health. You cannot deploy features affecting children without independent safety review.

Enforcement: regular algorithmic audits by independent bodies, mandatory transparency reporting on wellbeing impacts, and meaningful penalties proportional to revenue—not the token fines we see now.

**INTERVIEWER:** What about algorithmic accountability?

**HARRIS:** Platforms should be legally required to study and report the social effects of their algorithms. Right now, these algorithms operate as black boxes. Even the companies themselves often don't fully understand what their recommendation systems are optimizing for in practice.

Algorithmic accountability means: What is this algorithm actually optimizing for? What are the measurable downstream effects on polarization, mental health, democratic discourse? Make that public. Let researchers, regulators, and citizens see what these systems are doing. We're flying blind—we know the harms exist, but we can't trace them because the platforms won't open the black box.

**HARRIS:** And the third regulatory pillar: business model constraints, especially for children.

Consider restricting or banning advertising-based business models for platforms serving minors. A thirteen-year-old should not be the product being sold to advertisers. Subscription-only for youth platforms. Public funding for critical educational information infrastructure. Age-appropriate design codes with real teeth—the UK's is a useful model.

**INTERVIEWER:** The industry pushback on all of this will be intense. "You're stifling innovation. You're imposing government control on speech."

**HARRIS:** Of course. And I take those concerns seriously—I'm a technologist, I believe in innovation. But we've regulated every other industry that created systemic harm, and those industries survived and often thrived under regulation:

Tobacco: Can't market to children, must disclose health risks. The tobacco industry still exists.

Food: Nutritional labeling, safety standards, contamination liability. The food industry still exists.

Automotive: Seatbelts, airbags, crash testing, emissions standards. The auto industry still exists—and makes better cars.

Pharmaceuticals: Clinical trials, FDA approval, adverse event reporting. The pharma industry still exists.

Technology platforms are more powerful than any of these. They shape the beliefs, emotions, and behaviors of billions of people daily. The argument that they alone among all industries should operate without proportionate oversight is not a principled position—it's a business position dressed up as a principled one.

Regulation doesn't destroy industries. It channels innovation toward outcomes that serve humanity rather than exploit it.

### Segment 9: The Vision (4 min)

**[40:00-44:00]**

**INTERVIEWER:** Final question, Tristan. You've spent years diagnosing the crisis. Paint the positive picture. If we get the coordination architecture right, what does a humane technology future actually look and feel like?

**HARRIS:** [Pauses, leans forward]

I think about this constantly, because the diagnosis without the vision leads to despair, and despair is paralyzing. So let me describe the world I'm working toward.

**[VISUAL: Montage of humane technology scenarios]**

**HARRIS:** You wake up. Your phone doesn't ambush you with notifications engineered to hijack your first conscious moments. It shows what matters based on priorities you've set—a message from your partner, a calendar reminder. You handle it. You put the phone down. Your morning belongs to you.

On your commute, you open a news app. It doesn't feed you rage bait calibrated to your tribal affiliations. It presents important developments with context, multiple perspectives presented charitably. You arrive at work more informed and less inflamed.

At work, your collaboration tools don't fragment your attention with constant interruptions. They batch communications during natural break points and protect deep work. Your cognitive capacity is treated as precious, not as a resource to be extracted.

In the evening, an entertainment platform helps you find something you'll genuinely enjoy. When it's over, it stops. No autoplay. No algorithmic manipulation. You watch, you enjoy, you close the app.

And social media actually does what it always promised. It helps you maintain friendships across distance. It surfaces moments that matter in the lives of people you care about. Then it gets out of the way so you can be present in your own life.

**INTERVIEWER:** Technology as a tool for human agency, not a trap that undermines it.

**HARRIS:** Technology as coordination infrastructure for human flourishing. It coordinates with your goals, your relationships, your wellbeing—not against them. Every design decision asks the three-body question: how does this feature coordinate user psychology with social dynamics to support human thriving?

This isn't anti-technology. I love technology. I've spent my career in technology. What I'm against is the specific coordination architecture that turns technology into an extraction machine. We can build differently.

**HARRIS:** Achieving this requires all three bodies coordinating:

**Individual responsibility**: Each of us learning to use technology with intention, understanding the manipulation techniques, protecting our attention as the precious and finite resource it is. Cultivating what I'd call attentional sovereignty—the capacity to direct your own mind.

**Platform responsibility**: Companies measuring success by human outcomes—not engagement, not time on platform, but genuine wellbeing, strengthened relationships, informed citizens. Aligning business models with user flourishing so that doing the right thing is also the profitable thing.

**Collective responsibility**: Society setting democratic standards for technology that affects democratic life. Demanding transparency. Enforcing accountability. Treating our collective attention and our shared information environment as commons that deserve protection.

When all three coordinate, we get technology worthy of human potential. When any one fails, we get the extraction economy we're living in now.

**INTERVIEWER:** Coordination infrastructure for civilization.
