# Interview 8: Jimmy Wales

## "Wikipedia's Coordination Architecture: How Distributed Intelligence Creates Knowledge"

**Chapter 5: Human-Systems Intelligence Interaction**

---

## Interview Metadata

**Duration:** 44 minutes
**Location:** Wikimedia Foundation offices, San Francisco
**Setting:** Open office with Wikipedia edit streams visible on screens, whiteboards showing governance structures
**Visual Style:** Transparent and collaborative—showing the mechanics of open coordination
**Key Props:** Live Wikipedia editing demonstration, edit history visualization, discussion page examples, community governance docs

**Guest Bio:**
Jimmy Wales is co-founder of Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia that anyone can edit. What started as an experiment in 2001 has become humanity's largest collaborative knowledge project: 60 million articles in 300+ languages, created by millions of volunteer contributors with no central control. Wikipedia proves that coordination can create quality at scales traditional organizations can't match.

**Core Thesis:**
Wikipedia works not because of the technology (wikis existed before) or despite anonymous editors (the usual concern), but because of three-body coordination: individual contributors, technology platform, and community governance. Most wikis fail because they optimize technology without coordinating community norms.

## ACT I: THE COORDINATION MYSTERY (10 minutes)

### Segment 1: Why Most Wikis Fail

**[01:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Everyone asks the same question about Wikipedia: How does it work with anonymous editors and no central control? But you've said that question itself misses the point. What's the real question we should be asking?

**[01:25] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The real question isn't how Wikipedia works. The real question is why it's so rare. Thousands of organizations have tried to build wikis. Corporations, governments, universities, nonprofits. They install wiki software, they invite people to contribute, and then... nothing. Or worse, chaos. The vast majority of wikis fail.

**[01:45] [B-CAM] WALES:**
And they fail because they think wiki success comes from the technology. The reasoning goes: we'll install wiki software, people will collaborate, knowledge will emerge. But that's two-body thinking. Technology on one side, users on the other. And it doesn't work.

**[02:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
What do those failures actually look like in practice?

**[02:05] [B-CAM] WALES:**
I've watched this play out hundreds of times, and the failures fall into three very predictable patterns.

**[02:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The first is chaos. No coordination norms. People edit randomly, contradict each other, argue without any mechanism for resolution. The wiki becomes noise instead of knowledge. Contributors get frustrated, readers can't trust anything, and within a few months it's abandoned. Too frustrating to use.

**[VISUAL: Examples of failed wikis - abandoned pages, edit wars, spam]**

**[02:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The second is domination. A few loud voices take over. They control what gets published, they revert other people's edits, they drive contributors away through sheer persistence. And eventually everyone else leaves. Why contribute if your work just gets deleted by the same three people?

**[03:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The third is stagnation. This one is actually the most common in corporate settings. There's initial enthusiasm, a burst of activity, and then decline. Pages go out of date. Nobody maintains them. The wiki becomes a graveyard of good intentions. Why visit if the information is from 2019?

**[03:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So the technology platform alone is necessary but not sufficient. What's the missing piece?

**[03:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The third body. Community governance. Wikipedia works because we coordinate three bodies, not two.

**[03:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Body one is the individual contributors. The people who actually write, edit, argue, improve. Body two is the technology platform. The wiki software that makes collaboration mechanically possible. And body three is community governance. The norms, the policies, the dispute resolution processes that coordinate how contribution actually happens.

**[04:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Most wikis have bodies one and two. Wikipedia has all three. That's the difference. That's the whole secret, honestly. It's not more complicated than that, though the details of how you build body three are extremely complicated.

### Segment 2: The Three-Body Architecture

**[04:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Let's unpack each of those bodies. Walk me through how they actually coordinate to produce quality at scale.

**[04:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
[Moves to whiteboard, draws three circles]

Let me start with the contributors, because people always get this wrong. They imagine Wikipedia editors as obsessive nerds in basements. Some of us are, sure. But the contributor base is millions of people worldwide with incredibly diverse expertise, perspectives, and motivations.

**[04:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
And here's what's remarkable about the motivation question: we don't pay anyone. Zero. Not a cent. These are all volunteers. So what drives them? Intrinsic motivation. They care about topics and want to share knowledge. They genuinely enjoy the craft of writing and editing. They value contributing to a public good. And they appreciate the community. There's a real sense of belonging. This is the human knowledge layer of the system.

**[05:15] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the technology platform? What makes wiki software specifically suited to this kind of coordination?

**[05:25] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The technology layer provides the coordination infrastructure. And the key features aren't the ones people think about. Yes, anyone can edit, and that low barrier matters. But what really makes it work is the transparency infrastructure.

**[05:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Every single change is tracked. Complete edit history. You can see exactly who changed what, when, and you can revert it instantly. Discussion pages make coordination visible and transparent. Watchlists let contributors monitor topics they care about. Recent changes feeds give the community real-time oversight. And then there are bots and automated tools handling routine tasks. All of this is coordination infrastructure. It doesn't create knowledge. It makes coordination possible.

**[06:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the third body, the governance layer. This is where it gets interesting.

**[06:20] [B-CAM] WALES:**
This is where the magic is. The governance layer is the set of social norms and policies that coordinate contribution. And these weren't designed top-down by me or anyone else. They emerged through community deliberation over years.

**[06:35] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The core policies are: Neutral Point of View, which we call NPOV, meaning you present all significant viewpoints fairly. Verifiability, meaning information must be sourced to reliable publications. No Original Research, meaning you report what others have published rather than adding your own theories. Assume Good Faith, meaning you treat other editors as well-intentioned until proven otherwise. And then there's a whole ecosystem of dispute resolution processes and a community-elected administrator system for the hard cases.

**[07:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
This is the coordination wisdom. And it's what turns a wiki from a technology into a knowledge-creation system.

**[07:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Can you walk me through a concrete example of how all three bodies coordinate in practice?

**[07:20] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Sure. Let's say someone makes a bad edit. Maybe they add unsourced claims to an article about a pharmaceutical company. Here's the coordination chain. The technology tracks it through the edit history and recent changes feed. The community notices it because people are watching that article through their watchlists, and patrollers are monitoring recent changes. An individual contributor reverts it. If the original editor disagrees, they take it to the discussion page, and that's coordination via the talk page. If they can't resolve it there, it escalates to administrator intervention, which is governance kicking in. And the outcome isn't just that the bad edit gets fixed. The outcome improves both the content and the community norms, because now there's a talk page discussion that future editors can reference.

**[08:05] [B-CAM] WALES:**
That's coordination creating quality through distributed intelligence. No one person decided the right answer. The system coordinated multiple perspectives toward a better outcome.

**[08:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You're describing a system that's not just producing knowledge but producing the rules for producing knowledge. It's self-governing in a very literal sense.

**[08:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
That's exactly right. And that self-governing quality is what makes it resilient. If I tried to impose rules from the top, people would leave. The rules work because the community created them, believes in them, and enforces them collectively. That's a fundamentally different kind of coordination than hierarchy.

## ACT II: COORDINATION PRINCIPLES IN ACTION (20 minutes)

### Segment 3: The NPOV Principle

**[08:50] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Let's dig into the governance principles, because I think they contain lessons that extend well beyond Wikipedia. Start with Neutral Point of View. How does NPOV actually function as a coordination mechanism?

**[09:05] [B-CAM] WALES:**
NPOV is, to my mind, the single most important coordination innovation Wikipedia has produced. And I don't say that lightly.

**[09:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Here's the problem it solves. People have different perspectives, especially on controversial topics. Politics, religion, scientific debates, historical interpretation. Without a coordination framework, you get edit wars. Two people with opposing views constantly changing an article back and forth, each trying to make it reflect their viewpoint. I've seen articles get edited fifty times in a day during heated disputes.

**[VISUAL: Edit war example - article being changed back and forth]**

**[09:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The naive solution is to try to determine the true viewpoint and enforce it. But that's impossible and authoritarian. Who decides what's true? Me? Some committee? That's not how knowledge works.

**[09:55] [B-CAM] WALES:**
NPOV provides a completely different coordination framework. The principle says: don't try to determine the correct view. Instead, present all significant viewpoints fairly. Let me give you a concrete example. Climate change.

**[VISUAL: Climate change article showing balanced presentation]**

**[10:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
People have strong views on climate change. If we tried to declare one position correct, we'd have permanent warfare on the article. Instead, NPOV coordinates the contribution like this: present the scientific consensus, which is that human activity is the primary driver of warming. Present the areas of active debate and uncertainty. Weight coverage by prominence in the relevant expert communities, so the consensus view gets proportionally more coverage because it represents the vast majority of the relevant scientific literature. And source everything so readers can verify claims independently.

**[10:45] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So it doesn't eliminate disagreement. It channels it.

**[10:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Exactly. It channels conflict into productive coordination. Instead of fighting about whose view is correct, contributors coordinate around the question of fair representation. That's a completely different kind of conversation. "Is this article fairly representing the range of significant viewpoints?" is a question people with very different beliefs can actually work on together.

**[11:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
And this is brilliant coordination design that applies far beyond Wikipedia. When you can't agree on truth, coordinate around fair representation. This enables collaboration despite deep disagreement. Think about how many institutions are struggling right now because they can't manage disagreement. NPOV is a proven solution. Not to the disagreement itself, but to the coordination problem the disagreement creates.

**[11:35] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
I'm struck by how counterintuitive that is. Most organizations try to resolve disagreement by picking a side. You're saying the coordination architecture should hold the tension rather than collapse it.

**[11:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Right. And here's why that works. When you pick a side, you alienate everyone who disagrees. They leave, or they fight you. Either way, you've lost contributors and created enemies. When you hold the tension through fair representation, people with different views can coexist productively. They're still arguing, but they're arguing within a framework that produces something useful. The article gets better through the argument. The disagreement becomes fuel for quality rather than fuel for destruction.

### Segment 4: Verifiability and Collective Intelligence

**[12:15] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Let's talk about verifiability. It seems simple on the surface: cite your sources. But you've described it as a coordination principle with much deeper implications.

**[12:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Verifiability is the principle that prevents Wikipedia from becoming a platform for original research or personal opinion. The rule is straightforward: everything must be sourced to reliable published sources. You can't just add your own theories or personal knowledge, no matter how expert you are.

**[12:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
And this drives experts crazy sometimes. They say, "I'm a professor of this subject. I know this is true. Why do I need to cite a source?" And I understand the frustration. But verifiability isn't about doubting expertise. It's about enabling coordination at scale.

**[VISUAL: Article with inline citations, references section]**

**[13:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Think about what verifiability actually coordinates. First, it coordinates Wikipedia with the broader ecosystem of published knowledge. We're not creating new knowledge. We're aggregating and synthesizing what's already been published and peer-reviewed and vetted. Wikipedia's quality is bounded by the quality of its sources. That's a feature, not a bug. It means our reliability is anchored to something external and verifiable.

**[13:35] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Second, it coordinates among contributors. When two editors disagree, verifiability gives them a shared standard for resolution. It's not "I think X" versus "I think Y." It's "Source A says X" versus "Source B says Y." Now you're coordinating around evidence rather than opinion. That's a tractable conversation. You can evaluate source reliability, check the context of claims, reach consensus based on evidence.

**[14:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Third, it coordinates with readers. You don't have to trust Wikipedia. You can check the citations yourself. Wikipedia becomes a gateway to sources, not a final authority. That's an incredibly healthy relationship between a knowledge resource and its users.

**[14:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So verifiability is really a trust architecture. It creates trust not by asking people to believe Wikipedia, but by giving them the tools to verify independently.

**[14:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
That's a beautiful way to put it. And it's why Wikipedia has become more trusted over time, not less. As we've gotten better at enforcing verifiability, the gap between Wikipedia and traditional encyclopedias on reliability has narrowed dramatically. Multiple studies have shown that Wikipedia's accuracy on scientific topics rivals or exceeds traditional encyclopedias. Not because we have better writers, but because we have better coordination. Millions of eyes checking millions of citations.

### Segment 5: Assume Good Faith and Community Health

**[15:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Let's talk about the social layer. "Assume Good Faith" sounds almost naive in the context of the internet. Tell me why it's actually essential.

**[15:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
It does sound naive, doesn't it? In an era of trolling and bad actors and coordinated disinformation campaigns, telling people to assume good faith seems almost foolish. But it's actually the most pragmatic policy we have.

**[15:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Here's the calculus. When someone makes an edit you disagree with, you have a choice. You can assume bad faith: "They're vandalizing. They're pushing an agenda. They're incompetent." That leads to hostility, conflict, escalation. People leave. Or you can assume good faith: "They're trying to improve the article. They might be mistaken, but they're well-intentioned." That leads to constructive dialogue, learning, community growth.

**[VISUAL: Comparison of bad faith vs. good faith interactions on talk pages]**

**[15:55] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Let me give you a concrete scenario. A new editor adds poorly written content with weak sources to an article. It's not great work, but they're trying. The bad faith response is: delete everything, leave a harsh message saying "This is terrible. Learn to write before editing Wikipedia." What happens? That new editor leaves, never contributes again. We just lost someone who might have become a great contributor.

**[16:20] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The good faith response is: improve their content, strengthen the sources, and leave a helpful message. "Thanks for contributing. I've refined the writing and added stronger sources. Here are some resources on Wikipedia style if you want to learn more." What happens? That editor learns, improves, sticks around, becomes a valuable contributor. Maybe they become an administrator. Maybe they write a hundred featured articles.

**[16:45] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So it's not about being nice for the sake of being nice. It's about contributor retention as a coordination problem.

**[16:55] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Exactly. Wikipedia runs entirely on volunteer labor. Every contributor we drive away through hostility is a loss we can't afford. The math is ruthless here. We need a constant influx of new contributors to replace those who naturally leave over time. If we create a hostile environment, the pipeline dries up and the project dies.

**[17:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Assume Good Faith coordinates contributor behavior toward community growth and knowledge improvement. It's not idealism. It's survival strategy.

**[17:25] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
But what about the cases where people genuinely are acting in bad faith? State-sponsored editors, PR firms trying to whitewash articles, agenda-driven activists?

**[17:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
You start with the good faith assumption even then. Give people the benefit of the doubt initially. If they continue with problematic behavior after being welcomed and guided, then you escalate. Warnings first. Temporary blocks if the warnings don't work. Permanent bans if necessary. We have that entire infrastructure.

**[18:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
But here's the key insight. Starting with good faith means you don't alienate well-intentioned newcomers who just don't know the norms yet. And the vast majority of people who make problematic edits are in that category. They're not bad actors. They just don't know how Wikipedia works yet. Assume Good Faith gives them the chance to learn rather than driving them away on first contact.

**[18:25] [B-CAM] WALES:**
This is coordination wisdom: optimize for community health, not for punishing bad actors. The punishment infrastructure exists. But it's the last resort, not the first response.

### Segment 6: The Coordination Learning System

**[18:45] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
One thing that strikes me about Wikipedia is its longevity. Most internet projects from 2001 are long dead. What makes Wikipedia's coordination architecture durable enough to survive and grow for over two decades?

**[19:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Wikipedia is a learning system. And I don't just mean it learns knowledge, though obviously it does. I mean it learns how to coordinate knowledge creation. The system improves its own coordination over time. That's why it survives.

**[VISUAL: Diagram showing Wikipedia's learning loops]**

**[19:20] [B-CAM] WALES:**
There are four feedback loops operating simultaneously. The first is the content learning loop. Contributors add content. Readers use it. Problems get discovered. Contributors improve it. Quality increases. This is collective intelligence through distributed error correction. Millions of eyes catching mistakes, millions of hands fixing them. No editorial board on earth can match that throughput.

**[19:45] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The second is the norm learning loop. The community encounters a coordination problem. They discuss solutions, usually through extensive deliberation on policy talk pages. They establish a norm or policy. They apply it. They refine it based on experience. All of our core policies, NPOV, verifiability, assume good faith, all of them emerged through this process. Nobody designed them in advance. The community discovered them through practice.

**[20:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The third is the technology learning loop. Contributors encounter coordination friction. They request new tools. Developers build them. Coordination improves. Watchlists, recent changes patrol, edit filters, the entire bot ecosystem. All of it was built in response to coordination needs identified by the community.

**[20:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
And the fourth is what I'd call the meta-learning loop. We study what works and what doesn't. Academic researchers study us. We share findings. Other communities learn from our experience, and we learn from theirs. Wikipedia has become a living laboratory for understanding coordination at scale.

**[21:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So the system is continuously improving its own coordination architecture through experience. It's not just a knowledge base. It's an evolving institution.

**[21:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
That's exactly right. And that's why Wikipedia has survived and grown for more than twenty years while most wikis, and most internet projects generally, fail within a few years. We're not static. We're a learning system that continuously adapts its coordination architecture based on what we encounter.

**[21:35] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The lesson here is fundamental. If you want long-term coordination success, you don't just design good rules. You build feedback loops that enable learning and adaptation. The rules will need to change. The question is whether your system can learn what changes to make.

### Segment 7: When Coordination Fails

**[21:55] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Wikipedia isn't perfect, and you've been admirably honest about that over the years. Where does the coordination architecture break down? What are the failure patterns?

**[22:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
There are three persistent failure patterns that keep me up at night, frankly.

**[22:20] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The first is systemic bias. Our contributor base is not representative of humanity. It's disproportionately male, disproportionately Western, disproportionately well-educated. This creates systematic coordination bias. Topics popular with our contributor demographic get extraordinary coverage. Topics outside that experience get poor coverage. An article about a minor Star Wars character might be ten times longer than the article about a major African historical figure. Perspectives from underrepresented groups get less attention, less nuance, less care.

**[VISUAL: Visualization of coverage gaps]**

**[22:55] [B-CAM] WALES:**
This is a coordination failure in the deepest sense. We're not coordinating diverse enough voices. The system's output reflects the system's inputs. And our inputs are biased.

**[23:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
How about on the expertise front? There's a well-known tension between the expert who knows more and the enthusiastic amateur who edits more.

**[23:20] [B-CAM] WALES:**
That's the second failure pattern. Expertise coordination. Sometimes experts and highly active amateur editors clash. The expert knows more about the subject, but the amateur is more active on Wikipedia, knows the norms and processes better, and has more persistence. Without effective coordination mechanisms, the amateur's persistence can win over the expert's knowledge. And the expert just leaves in frustration.

**[23:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Result: quality suffers on technical topics. We lose the expert's knowledge, and we lose their trust. It's a coordination failure because we're not effectively matching expertise with contribution. We're letting process knowledge override domain knowledge.

**[24:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The third failure is bad actor persistence. Most people, when they make an edit and it gets reverted, accept the community's judgment and move on. But determined bad actors exploit the Assume Good Faith principle. They persist with problematic edits, they learn the rules just well enough to game them, and they wear down good faith editors who eventually give up because the coordination cost of fighting them exceeds their patience.

**[24:40] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
These sound like fundamental tensions rather than bugs to be fixed. Can they actually be addressed?

**[24:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
They can be improved, and we're working on all three. For systemic bias, we actively recruit underrepresented contributors through outreach programs, edit-a-thons focused on underrepresented topics, and partnerships with organizations that can bring diverse perspectives. That's diversifying body one.

**[25:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
For expertise coordination, we're building better tools to surface and credit domain expertise, creating clearer pathways for experts to contribute effectively, and developing norms that give appropriate weight to demonstrated expertise. That's improving bodies two and three.

**[25:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
For bad actor persistence, we're developing more sophisticated detection tools, including machine learning systems that can identify problematic editing patterns earlier. That's enhancing the coordination between bodies two and three.

**[25:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The key insight is that coordination failures aren't permanent defects. They're design challenges. We can improve the coordination architecture to address them. Not perfectly, maybe never perfectly. But iteratively, continuously, which is the Wikipedia way.

## ACT III: LESSONS FOR THE FUTURE (14 minutes)

### Segment 8: Coordination Principles for AI

**[26:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Let's pivot to the future, because I think Wikipedia's experience has profound implications for how we build AI systems. What can AI learn from Wikipedia's coordination architecture?

**[26:25] [B-CAM] WALES:**
This is something I think about constantly, because the AI community is wrestling with exactly the coordination challenges Wikipedia has been grappling with for twenty-plus years. Just at a different scale and with higher stakes.

**[26:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Wikipedia proves that distributed coordination can create quality at scales centralized control simply cannot match. No small team of experts, no matter how brilliant, could have created sixty million articles in three hundred languages. It required distributed coordination. AI systems should learn from this.

**[VISUAL: Comparison of Wikipedia coordination vs. typical AI systems]**

**[27:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
I see three principles that transfer directly. The first is transparency. Wikipedia makes everything visible. Edit history, discussion, decision-making process. You can trace exactly how any piece of knowledge was created and coordinated. You can see the arguments, the evidence, the compromises.

**[27:25] [B-CAM] WALES:**
AI systems should do the same. Show reasoning. Show data provenance. Show how decisions were made. Right now, most AI systems are black boxes. You get an output but you can't see how it was generated. That's the opposite of what enables trust and coordination. If Wikipedia worked like most AI systems, nobody would trust it, because they couldn't verify anything.

**[27:50] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
What's the second principle?

**[27:55] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Human agency. Wikipedia keeps humans in control. The technology facilitates, but humans decide what content to include, how to present it, what norms to follow. The technology serves human judgment. It doesn't replace it.

**[28:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
AI systems need the same principle. Augment human judgment, don't replace it. Keep humans in the coordination loop. The moment you take humans out of the loop, you lose the ability to course-correct, to exercise judgment about novel situations, to apply wisdom that can't be reduced to an algorithm. Wikipedia's technology is extraordinarily powerful, but it's powerful because it amplifies human coordination, not because it replaces human thought.

**[28:45] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the third?

**[28:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Community governance. Wikipedia is governed by community norms that emerged from the community itself, not by corporate policies imposed from above. The people using the system shape how it works. There's real accountability, real deliberation, real participation.

**[29:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
AI systems desperately need this. Right now, a handful of companies make decisions about how AI behaves, what values it reflects, what it will and won't do. The affected communities, the billions of people who use these systems, have essentially no say. That's the opposite of the governance model that has made Wikipedia successful.

**[29:35] [B-CAM] WALES:**
AI governance should involve affected communities in real, meaningful ways. Not just consultation exercises where you ask for feedback and then ignore it. Actual governance. Actual power-sharing. The Wikipedia model shows this can work at massive scale with diverse, global communities.

**[29:55] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You're essentially arguing that the AI alignment problem is a coordination problem, not just a technical one.

**[30:05] [B-CAM] WALES:**
That's exactly what I'm arguing. Alignment isn't something you solve once in a lab and then deploy. It's an ongoing coordination challenge between the technology, the people building it, and the communities affected by it. Wikipedia's alignment with human values isn't static. It's continuously maintained through coordination. AI alignment needs to work the same way. You need transparency so people can see what the system is doing. You need human agency so people can correct it. And you need community governance so the affected communities have actual input into how it behaves.

### Segment 9: The Future of Collective Knowledge

**[30:40] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
What's the future of Wikipedia itself? How does AI change collective knowledge creation?

**[30:50] [B-CAM] WALES:**
We're exploring how AI can enhance our coordination without replacing human judgment. And I want to emphasize that distinction, because it's crucial.

**[VISUAL: Mockups of AI-enhanced Wikipedia features]**

**[31:05] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The first direction is AI-assisted editing. AI that helps contributors be more effective. It can suggest sources for unsourced claims, identify potential NPOV issues, flag outdated information, propose improvements to clarity or structure. But the human makes the final decision. The AI facilitates. It doesn't control.

**[31:30] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The second is AI-facilitated coordination. AI that improves how contributors work together. Connecting editors working on related topics who might not know about each other. Identifying coordination conflicts before they escalate into full edit wars. Suggesting consensus approaches based on how similar disputes were resolved in the past. Helping onboard new contributors by guiding them through the norms and processes. Again, the AI facilitates human coordination. It doesn't make decisions.

**[32:00] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The third is AI-enhanced verification. AI that supports the verifiability principle. Checking citations for accuracy, because right now a lot of citations are actually broken or don't support the claims they're attached to. Flagging potential misinformation. Suggesting additional sources. Monitoring for vandalism patterns. The AI supports human verification but doesn't replace human judgment about what counts as a reliable source.

**[32:30] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
I notice you keep emphasizing that the AI supports rather than replaces. Is there a real temptation to let AI do more?

**[32:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Of course there is. Somebody asks me every week, "Why don't you just have AI write Wikipedia?" And the answer is that would destroy what makes Wikipedia valuable. Wikipedia's value isn't just the information. It's the coordination process that produces and validates the information. If an AI writes an article, who's responsible for its accuracy? Who argues about fair representation? Who updates it when new evidence emerges? Who catches the subtle errors that only a domain expert would notice?

**[33:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The moment you remove humans from the coordination loop, you lose the error correction, the deliberation, the community judgment that makes the output trustworthy. You might get something that looks like an encyclopedia, but it wouldn't have the same reliability guarantees. It wouldn't have the same ability to handle contested knowledge. And it wouldn't have the same capacity to learn and improve.

**[33:35] [B-CAM] WALES:**
We're not building AI to write Wikipedia. We're building AI to help humans coordinate better to write Wikipedia. That's the right model. AI enhancing human coordination, not replacing human intelligence.

### Segment 10: The Vision

**[33:55] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Final question, Jimmy. Stepping all the way back. What does Wikipedia teach us about human potential? About what we're capable of when the coordination architecture is right?

**[34:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
[Pauses, looks at the edit streams on the screens behind him]

Wikipedia proves that humans can coordinate at massive scale to create public goods without central control or financial incentives. Think about what we've actually built. Sixty million articles in more than three hundred languages. Billions of page views every month. All created by volunteers. All free to access. All coordinated without a central authority deciding what gets published or who gets to participate.

**[34:40] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Traditional organizational theory says that's impossible. You need hierarchy. You need control. You need incentives. You need somebody in charge. And yet here we are. Twenty-five years in, growing every day, more reliable than ever. It turns out that the theory was wrong. Or rather, it was incomplete. It was two-body thinking applied to a three-body world.

**[35:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
What's the implication for the challenges we face going forward?

**[35:15] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The implications are enormous. The problems we face as a civilization, climate change, inequality, polarization, AI alignment, pandemic preparedness, these all require coordination at scales we've never achieved. No single government, no single company, no single institution can solve them alone. They require the kind of distributed, global, multi-stakeholder coordination that Wikipedia demonstrates is possible.

**[35:45] [B-CAM] WALES:**
And the lesson from Wikipedia is clear. Don't optimize individual intelligence. Optimize coordination architecture. Don't centralize control. Enable distributed coordination. Don't replace human judgment with technology. Facilitate human coordination with technology.

**[36:10] [B-CAM] WALES:**
Wikipedia shows that millions of people with different perspectives, different expertise, different motivations, speaking different languages, living in different cultures, can coordinate to create something extraordinarily valuable for humanity. Not because they agree. They often disagree passionately. But because the coordination architecture channels that disagreement productively.

**[36:40] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You make it sound almost simple.

**[36:45] [B-CAM] WALES:**
[Laughs] It's the simplest idea in the world and the hardest thing to actually build. The principle is simple: coordinate three bodies, not two. Contributors, technology, governance. The execution takes decades of continuous learning and adaptation. But the principle is solid. And it scales.

**[37:05] [B-CAM] WALES:**
The future isn't AI replacing human intelligence. The future is humans and AI coordinating to create collective intelligence at civilization scale. Wikipedia is just the proof of concept. The beginning of what's possible when you get the coordination architecture right.

**[37:25] [B-CAM] WALES:**
We built humanity's knowledge base with volunteers and wiki software. Imagine what we can build with AI-enhanced coordination. Not AI doing the thinking for us. AI helping us think together more effectively. That's the future I'm working toward. That's the future I believe in.
