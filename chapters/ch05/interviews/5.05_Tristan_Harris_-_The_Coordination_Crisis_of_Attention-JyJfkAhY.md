# Interview 5: Tristan Harris

## "The Coordination Crisis of Attention: Designing for Human Flourishing"

**Chapter 5: Human-Systems Intelligence Interaction**

---

## Interview Metadata

**Duration:** 44 minutes
**Location:** Center for Humane Technology offices, San Francisco
**Setting:** Modern office with whiteboards covered in system diagrams, screens showing social media interfaces
**Visual Style:** Investigative and urgent—exposing hidden coordination failures in familiar technology
**Key Props:** Live demonstrations of platform mechanics, user behavior data visualizations, design mockups of humane alternatives

**Guest Bio:**
Tristan Harris is co-founder of the Center for Humane Technology and former Google design ethicist. His work exposing how social media platforms exploit human psychology to maximize engagement has made him one of the most influential voices in technology ethics. His TED talk "How a handful of tech companies control billions of minds every day" has been viewed over 10 million times.

**Core Thesis:**
Technology ethics fails when treated as a two-body problem (technology ←→ users). The real question is three-body coordination: how does this feature coordinate user psychology with social dynamics to create systemic outcomes? The Like button isn't bad in isolation—it's bad because of how it coordinates individual behavior with network effects.

## ACT I: THE ATTENTION EXTRACTION CRISIS (12 minutes)

### Opening Sequence (2 min)

**[VISUAL: Harris walking through San Francisco, past people absorbed in their phones, into CHT offices]**

**HOST VOICEOVER:**
"Tristan Harris spent years inside Google watching brilliant engineers build systems that were destroying human wellbeing. Not because they were evil—because they were optimizing the wrong thing. His journey from insider to whistleblower has revealed a crisis most people don't see: the systematic extraction of human attention through coordination exploitation."

**[CUT TO: Harris at whiteboard with platform diagrams]**

**OPENING QUESTION:**
"You've said that social media platforms aren't just addictive—they're coordination traps. What do you mean?"

---

### Segment 1: The Two-Body Trap (4 min)

**HARRIS:**
"When Facebook adds a feature, they ask: 'Is this good for users?' Two-body thinking: technology ←→ users.

'Users like getting notifications about friend activity. Let's send more notifications.'

Seems reasonable. Individual user gets value. But that's not how it actually works."

**[VISUAL: Whiteboard - Harris draws two circles: "Technology" and "Users"]**

**HARRIS:**
"The problem: they're ignoring the third body—social dynamics. How individual behaviors coordinate to create collective outcomes.

Here's what actually happens with notifications:

**Individual level**: You get a notification, you check your phone, you feel connected. Positive experience.

**Social level**: Everyone gets notifications, everyone checks their phones constantly, nobody is present with each other. Negative collective outcome.

The feature that's good for individuals destroys collective presence."

**[VISUAL: Harris adds third circle: "Social Dynamics" - shows coordination arrows]**

**HOST:** "So the feature isn't bad in isolation?"

**HARRIS:**
"Exactly. It's bad because of how it coordinates individual psychology with social dynamics.

This is the pattern everywhere:

**Infinite scroll**: Convenient for individuals, creates compulsive checking collectively

**Autoplay**: Seamless for individuals, creates binge behavior and sleep disruption collectively

**Algorithmic feeds**: Personalized for individuals, creates filter bubbles and polarization collectively

The features aren't designed to be harmful. They're designed to maximize engagement without considering how individual engagement coordinates to create social harm."

---

### Segment 2: The Like Button Case Study (6 min)

**HOST:** "Walk us through a specific example. The Like button—how is that a coordination problem?"

**HARRIS:**
"Perfect example. Let me show you the three-body analysis:"

**[VISUAL: Animation showing Like button evolution]**

**HARRIS:**
"**Body 1: Individual Psychology**

Humans crave social validation. We want to know: 'Do people approve of me? Am I accepted?'

The Like button provides instant feedback: post something, get validation. Dopamine hit. Positive reinforcement loop.

At the individual level, this feels good. You post, you get likes, you feel validated. What's wrong with that?"

**HARRIS:**
"**Body 2: Platform Mechanics**

Facebook wants engagement—more posts, more time on platform, more ad impressions.

The Like button drives engagement: people post more to get likes, check back frequently to see new likes, spend more time on the platform.

At the platform level, this is perfect. Engagement goes up, revenue goes up. Success."

**HARRIS:**
"**Body 3: Social Coordination**

But watch what happens when you coordinate individual psychology with platform mechanics across millions of users:

**Phase 1: Behavior Change**

People start optimizing for likes. They don't post what's authentic—they post what gets likes. Selfies, hot takes, performance.

**Phase 2: Social Norms Shift**

Everyone's performing for likes. Authenticity becomes rare. The norm becomes performance.

**Phase 3: Psychological Impact**

People compare their like counts. Feel inadequate when others get more. Anxiety, depression, FOMO.

**Phase 4: Collective Outcome**

Social media becomes a performance platform, not a connection platform. Authenticity dies. Mental health suffers. But everyone's trapped because leaving means losing social connection."

**[VISUAL: Diagram showing cascade from individual behavior to collective harm]**

**HOST:** "The Like button created this?"

**HARRIS:**
"The Like button coordinated individual psychology with platform mechanics with network effects to create this.

It's not that the button is evil. It's that it creates a coordination pattern that optimizes for engagement at the expense of authenticity and wellbeing.

This is the three-body problem: you can't evaluate the button in isolation. You have to ask: how does this coordinate user psychology with social dynamics to create systemic outcomes?"

## ACT II: THE COORDINATION PATTERNS OF HARM (20 minutes)

### Segment 3: Outrage and Polarization (5 min)

**HOST:** "Let's look at another pattern: political polarization. How is that a coordination problem?"

**HARRIS:**
"Polarization is the perfect example of coordination creating emergent harm. Let me walk you through it:"

**[VISUAL: Screen showing social media feed with political content]**

**HARRIS:**
"**The Algorithmic Coordination:**

Social media algorithms optimize for engagement: clicks, shares, comments, time on platform.

Research shows: outrage content gets 2-3x more engagement than neutral content. Divisive content gets shared more than nuanced content.

So the algorithm learns: show people content that makes them outraged. That's what maximizes engagement."

**HARRIS:**
"**The Psychological Coordination:**

Humans have negativity bias—we pay more attention to threats than opportunities. And we have tribal instincts—we bond with our in-group against out-groups.

Outrage content triggers both: 'The other side is threatening us!' Your attention locks on, you share it with your tribe, you engage.

The algorithm coordinates with your psychology to keep you outraged."

**HARRIS:**
"**The Social Coordination:**

But here's where it gets dangerous. When millions of people are all being shown outrage content, all sharing it with their tribes, all engaging with divisive narratives:

**Phase 1**: Filter bubbles form—you only see content that confirms your worldview

**Phase 2**: The other side becomes caricatured—you only see the worst examples

**Phase 3**: Empathy breaks down—you can't understand how anyone could think differently

**Phase 4**: Society fractures—we lose shared reality, shared values, shared humanity"

**[VISUAL: Animation showing polarization cascade across network]**

**HARRIS:**
"This is emergent harm from coordination:

- Algorithm optimizes for engagement


- Psychology responds to outrage


- Social dynamics amplify division



Result: Democracy itself is threatened. Not because any individual component is evil, but because their coordination creates systemic harm."

**HOST:** "How do we break this coordination pattern?"

**HARRIS:**
"We have to coordinate all three bodies differently:

**Technology**: Stop optimizing for engagement. Optimize for understanding, nuance, bridge-building.

**Psychology**: Design for our better angels—curiosity, empathy, wisdom—not our worst instincts.

**Social**: Measure success by collective outcomes—shared understanding, democratic health, social cohesion—not individual engagement.

This requires redesigning the coordination architecture."

---

### Segment 4: The Business Model Problem (5 min)

**HOST:** "You're saying the business model is the root cause?"

**HARRIS:**
"The advertising business model creates misaligned coordination. Let me show you:

**[VISUAL: Diagram of advertising business model]**

**HARRIS:**
"**The Coordination Trap:**

**Body 1**: Users want connection, information, entertainment

**Body 2**: Platforms need revenue from advertising

**Body 3**: Advertisers want attention and influence

The platform coordinates these by maximizing time on platform and attention capture. More attention = more ad impressions = more revenue.

But this creates perverse coordination:

- Platforms optimize for addiction, not wellbeing


- Content that's engaging (outrage, anxiety, FOMO) beats content that's valuable


- User wellbeing and platform profit are misaligned"



**HOST:** "So what's the alternative?"

**HARRIS:**
"Business models that align platform success with user wellbeing:

**Subscription models**: Users pay for the service. Platform succeeds by serving users, not advertisers. Coordination becomes: user wellbeing = platform success.

**Public utility models**: Platform is funded publicly, optimizes for social good. Coordination becomes: democratic health = platform success.

**Time well spent models**: Platform is measured by whether users accomplish their goals and feel good about time spent. Coordination becomes: user satisfaction = platform success."

**[VISUAL: Alternative business model diagrams]**

**HARRIS:**
"The key: align the incentives so that coordinating user psychology with platform mechanics with social dynamics creates wellbeing, not harm.

This isn't about making platforms less profitable. It's about changing what they profit from. Profit from user flourishing, not user exploitation."

---

### Segment 5: The Humane Technology Principles (5 min)

**HOST:** "What does humane technology look like? What are the design principles?"

**HARRIS:**
"We've developed three core principles for coordinating technology with human wellbeing:

**Principle 1: Time Well Spent**

Technology should help people accomplish their goals and disconnect, not maximize time on platform."

**[VISUAL: Comparison of current vs. humane design]**

**HARRIS:**
"Current design: Infinite scroll, autoplay, notifications—all designed to keep you engaged forever.

Humane design: Clear goals, completion markers, natural stopping points. 'You've seen everything new. Go live your life.'

The coordination: User intentions ←→ Platform features ←→ Life goals

Success metric: Did the user accomplish what they came to do and feel good about their time spent?"

**HARRIS:**
"**Principle 2: Authentic Social Connection**

Technology should strengthen genuine human relationships, not replace them with performance and comparison."

**HARRIS:**
"Current design: Public metrics (likes, followers), broadcast communication, performance incentives.

Humane design: Private interactions, small groups, meaningful conversation. No metrics that enable comparison. Features that encourage face-to-face connection.

The coordination: Human connection needs ←→ Platform features ←→ Relationship health

Success metric: Are people's real relationships stronger?"

**HARRIS:**
"**Principle 3: Information Integrity**

Technology should promote understanding and shared reality, not polarization and misinformation."

**HARRIS:**
"Current design: Engagement optimization, filter bubbles, algorithmic amplification of extreme content.

Humane design: Nuance promotion, diverse perspective exposure, bridge-building features. Slow down viral spread. Reward understanding over outrage.

The coordination: Human understanding ←→ Platform features ←→ Democratic health

Success metric: Do people understand issues more deeply and empathize with different perspectives?"

**HOST:** "These principles require coordinating multiple values?"

**HARRIS:**
"Exactly. You can't just optimize one thing. Humane technology coordinates:

- Individual user wellbeing


- Platform sustainability


- Social health


- Democratic functioning


- Human dignity



All at once. That's harder than just maximizing engagement. But it's the only path to technology that serves humanity."

---

### Segment 6: The Implementation Challenge (5 min)

**HOST:** "Why haven't platforms adopted these principles?"

**HARRIS:**
"Three reasons:

**First: Competitive dynamics**

If one platform adopts humane design and competitors don't, users might flow to the more addictive platforms. It's a race to the bottom—whoever exploits human psychology most effectively wins market share.

This is a coordination failure at the industry level. We need collective action: regulation, standards, cultural pressure."

**[VISUAL: Game theory diagram showing race to the bottom]**

**HARRIS:**
"**Second: Measurement challenges**

It's easy to measure engagement: time on platform, clicks, shares. It's hard to measure wellbeing: life satisfaction, relationship quality, democratic health.

Platforms optimize what they measure. If we want them to optimize for wellbeing, we need better ways to measure it.

This requires new metrics, new research, new accountability mechanisms."

**HARRIS:**
"**Third: Coordination complexity**

Humane technology requires coordinating multiple stakeholders with different values:

- Users want different things (some want entertainment, some want productivity)


- Society has competing values (free speech vs. harm prevention)


- Platforms have business constraints (need to be sustainable)



There's no simple optimization. It requires ongoing coordination and judgment.

This is why we need frameworks like virtue ethics (as Shannon Vallor discussed) and regulatory oversight. The platforms can't solve this alone."

**HOST:** "What's the path forward?"

**HARRIS:**
"Three coordinated interventions:

**Individual level**: Digital literacy, attention practices, conscious consumption. Users learning to resist manipulation.

**Platform level**: Humane design principles, business model innovation, transparency. Platforms coordinating with user wellbeing.

**Societal level**: Regulation, standards, accountability. Society coordinating platforms with collective values.

All three have to happen together. Individual action alone won't work—platforms are too powerful. Platform self-regulation alone won't work—competitive dynamics prevent it. Regulation alone won't work—it's too slow and crude.

We need coordinated action at all three levels."

## ACT III: THE FUTURE OF HUMAN-TECHNOLOGY COORDINATION (12 minutes)

### Segment 7: What Success Looks Like (4 min)

**HOST:** "Paint the picture. What does a humane technology future look like?"

**HARRIS:**
"Imagine waking up tomorrow in a world where technology coordinates with human flourishing:"

**[VISUAL: Montage of humane technology scenarios]**

**HARRIS:**
"**Morning**: Your phone doesn't bombard you with notifications designed to hijack your attention. It shows you what's actually important based on your stated priorities. You check it once, handle what matters, put it away.

**Commute**: Social media doesn't show you outrage bait to maximize engagement. It shows you content that helps you understand issues deeply, exposes you to diverse perspectives, builds empathy.

**Work**: Collaboration tools don't interrupt you constantly. They coordinate with your attention patterns to batch communications during natural breaks, protect deep work time.

**Evening**: Entertainment platforms don't autoplay forever. They help you find what you actually want to watch, then stop. You watch, you enjoy, you disconnect.

**Social**: Online platforms facilitate real-world connection rather than replacing it. You use them to organize meetups, maintain friendships, coordinate activities. Then you close the app and be present with people."

**HOST:** "Technology as a tool, not a trap?"

**HARRIS:**
"Technology as coordination infrastructure for human flourishing. It coordinates with your goals, your relationships, your wellbeing—not against them.

This isn't anti-technology. It's pro-human technology. Technology designed to serve us, not extract from us."

---

### Segment 8: The Regulatory Imperative (4 min)

**HOST:** "You mentioned regulation. What does that look like?"

**HARRIS:**
"We need regulation that aligns platform incentives with social wellbeing. Three key areas:"

**[VISUAL: Graphics showing regulatory frameworks]**

**HARRIS:**
"**1. Fiduciary Duty**

Platforms should have a legal duty to serve user wellbeing, like doctors have a duty to patient health.

This means: Can't knowingly design for addiction. Can't optimize for engagement at the expense of mental health. Can't exploit psychological vulnerabilities.

Enforcement: Regular audits, transparency requirements, penalties for violations."

**HARRIS:**
"**2. Algorithmic Accountability**

Platforms should be required to study and report the social effects of their algorithms.

This means: What's the algorithm optimizing for? What are the downstream effects on polarization, mental health, democracy?

Transparency enables public oversight. Sunlight is the best disinfectant."

**HARRIS:**
"**3. Business Model Regulation**

Consider restricting advertising-based business models for certain platforms, especially those serving children.

This means: Subscription-only for youth platforms. Public funding for critical information infrastructure. Breaking the coordination trap of attention extraction.

The goal: Align business success with user wellbeing."

**HOST:** "Won't platforms fight this?"

**HARRIS:**
"Of course. But we've regulated other industries that created systemic harm:

- Tobacco: Can't market to children, must disclose health risks


- Food: Nutritional labeling, safety standards


- Finance: Fiduciary duties, transparency requirements


- Pharmaceuticals: Clinical trials, approval processes



Technology platforms are more powerful than any of these. They shape billions of minds daily. We need proportionate oversight.

This isn't about destroying the tech industry. It's about channeling innovation toward human flourishing rather than attention extraction."

---

### Segment 9: The Vision (4 min)

**HOST:** "Final question: What's your vision for human-technology coordination?"

**HARRIS:**
"I envision a future where technology coordinates with our better angels—our capacity for wisdom, empathy, creativity, connection—rather than exploiting our worst instincts.

Where platforms compete on how well they serve human flourishing, not how effectively they capture attention.

Where we use technology intentionally to accomplish our goals, then disconnect to be present with life.

Where social media brings us together in understanding rather than dividing us in outrage.

Where AI augments human wisdom rather than replacing human judgment.

Where children grow up with technology that supports their development rather than exploiting their vulnerabilities."

**[VISUAL: Montage of positive technology use—learning, creating, connecting]**

**HARRIS:**
"This requires coordinating three bodies:

**Individual responsibility**: Each of us learning to use technology wisely, resisting manipulation, protecting our attention.

**Platform responsibility**: Companies designing for wellbeing, measuring success by human outcomes, aligning business models with user flourishing.

**Collective responsibility**: Society setting standards, demanding accountability, regulating coordination failures.

When all three coordinate, we get technology that serves humanity. When any one fails, we get the extraction economy we have now."

**HOST:** "Technology that coordinates with human flourishing."

**HARRIS:**
"Exactly. The technology isn't the problem. The coordination architecture is the problem.

We built systems that coordinate user psychology with platform mechanics with social dynamics to maximize engagement and extraction.

We can build systems that coordinate those same elements to maximize understanding, wellbeing, and flourishing.

It's a choice. And it's the most important choice of our time.

Because the platforms that control billions of minds every day aren't just technology companies. They're coordination infrastructure for human civilization.

The question is: What are they coordinating us toward? Extraction or flourishing? Division or understanding? Addiction or freedom?

That's the coordination crisis. And solving it is how we save ourselves."
