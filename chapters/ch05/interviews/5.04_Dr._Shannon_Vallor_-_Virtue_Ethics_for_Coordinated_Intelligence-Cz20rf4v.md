# Interview 4: Dr. Shannon Vallor

## "Virtue Ethics for Coordinated Intelligence: Beyond Rules and Optimization"

**Chapter 5: Human-Systems Intelligence Interaction**

---

## Interview Metadata

**Duration:** 40 minutes
**Location:** University of Edinburgh, Philosophy Department
**Setting:** Traditional academic office with modern technology visible—books on ethics alongside AI papers
**Visual Style:** Philosophical depth meets practical wisdom—abstract concepts grounded in concrete examples
**Key Props:** Whiteboard for ethical frameworks, historical philosophy texts, modern AI ethics papers

**Guest Bio:**
Dr. Shannon Vallor is Professor of Philosophy at the University of Edinburgh and author of "Technology and the Virtues: A Philosophical Guide to a Future Worth Wanting." Her work bridges ancient virtue ethics with contemporary technology ethics, arguing that AI challenges require not new ethical rules but renewed cultivation of human wisdom, courage, and practical judgment.

**Core Thesis:**
AI ethics fails when it tries to encode ethics into systems as rules or utility functions. Ethics is coordination between personal virtue, technological capability, and social context. We need AI that participates in ethical reasoning while humans maintain ethical responsibility.

### Segment 1: Why Current AI Ethics Fails (4 min)

**VALLOR:**
"We're trying to solve a coordination problem with optimization tools. Let me explain:

**The utilitarian approach**: Program AI to maximize good outcomes. Sounds reasonable—until you ask: good for whom? Measured how? Over what timeframe? With what tradeoffs?

Facebook optimized for 'engagement'—a proxy for user satisfaction. Result: addiction, polarization, mental health harm. They optimized the wrong thing because they reduced ethics to a single metric."

**[VISUAL: Whiteboard - Vallor writes "Ethics = Optimization?" with a large X through it]**

**VALLOR:**
"**The deontological approach**: Program ethical rules into AI. 'Don't lie. Respect privacy. Treat people fairly.'

Sounds good—until AI encounters situations where rules conflict. Should an autonomous vehicle prioritize passenger safety or pedestrian safety? Should a content moderation AI prioritize free speech or harm prevention?

Rules can't handle the contextual complexity of real ethical situations. They're too rigid for the messy reality of human life."

**[VISUAL: Vallor writes "Ethics = Rules?" with another X]**

**HOST:** "So if ethics isn't optimization or rules, what is it?"

**VALLOR:**
"Ethics is coordination between multiple values in context. It's the practiced ability to navigate competing goods, understand particular situations, and make wise judgments that serve human flourishing.

This is what virtue ethics teaches: ethics isn't following rules or maximizing outcomes—it's developing character traits that enable good judgment in context."

**[VISUAL: Vallor writes "Ethics = Coordinated Wisdom"]**

---

### Segment 2: The Three Bodies of Ethical Coordination (4 min)

**HOST:** "Explain this three-body coordination framework for ethics."

**VALLOR:**
"Every ethical situation involves three bodies that must coordinate:"

**[VISUAL: Whiteboard - three circles appearing]**

**VALLOR:**
"**Body 1: Individual Virtues** - the personal layer

These are character traits: wisdom, courage, compassion, justice, temperance. Not rules you follow, but capacities you develop through practice.

Wisdom lets you see situations clearly. Courage lets you act despite difficulty. Compassion lets you understand others' experiences. Justice lets you balance competing interests.

These are the human ethical foundation—developed over a lifetime through experience and reflection."

**VALLOR:**
"**Body 2: AI Systems** - the technological layer

AI can process vast data, recognize patterns, maintain consistency at scale, optimize within defined parameters. These are powerful capabilities.

But AI can't develop wisdom or compassion. It can't understand context the way humans do. It can't make value judgments about what matters.

This is the technological ethical amplifier—it scales human decisions but can't replace human judgment."

**VALLOR:**
"**Body 3: Social Context** - the reality layer

Ethical situations happen in specific social contexts: cultural norms, power dynamics, historical injustices, systemic effects. What's ethical in one context might not be in another.

A content moderation decision that's appropriate in one cultural context might be harmful in another. A hiring algorithm that seems fair in isolation might perpetuate systemic inequalities in practice.

This is the coordination reality—where actual outcomes happen and ethics is judged."

**[VISUAL: Arrows connecting all three circles]**

**VALLOR:**
"Ethics emerges from coordinating all three: human virtue guiding AI capability in social context to create outcomes that serve flourishing.

When we try to reduce ethics to one body—just optimize the AI, just follow rules, just respect cultural norms—we fail. Ethical coordination requires all three."

## ACT II: VIRTUE ETHICS FOR AI (20 minutes)

### Segment 3: What Virtue Ethics Offers (5 min)

**HOST:** "Why is virtue ethics better suited to AI challenges than other ethical frameworks?"

**VALLOR:**
"Because virtue ethics is inherently about coordination, not optimization or rule-following. Let me show you with a concrete example: content recommendation algorithms."

**[VISUAL: Graphics showing social media recommendation flow]**

**VALLOR:**
"**Utilitarian approach to recommendations:**

Maximize user engagement—time on platform, clicks, shares. Simple optimization problem.

Result: Algorithms recommend increasingly extreme content because outrage drives engagement. Users become polarized, anxious, addicted.

The optimization succeeded. The ethics failed. Because engagement isn't the same as flourishing."

**VALLOR:**
"**Deontological approach:**

Program rules: 'Don't recommend misinformation. Don't recommend harmful content. Respect user privacy.'

Sounds good—but how do you define misinformation? What counts as harmful? What if users explicitly want controversial content?

Rules can't handle the contextual complexity. Every rule needs exceptions, every exception needs judgment."

**VALLOR:**
"**Virtue ethics approach:**

Ask: What virtues should guide recommendation systems? What character traits, if the algorithm could have them, would lead to ethical recommendations?

**Wisdom**: Understanding what genuinely serves user wellbeing, not just what maximizes engagement

**Temperance**: Recommending in moderation, not overwhelming users or exploiting vulnerabilities

**Justice**: Ensuring diverse perspectives, not just confirming biases

**Compassion**: Considering emotional impact, not just behavioral response"

**HOST:** "But algorithms don't have character traits."

**VALLOR:**
"Exactly. So we coordinate:

**Humans provide**: The virtues—wisdom, judgment, values

**AI provides**: Pattern recognition, consistency, scale

**Context provides**: Feedback on actual outcomes

The algorithm doesn't need wisdom—but the humans designing, training, and overseeing it do. The algorithm amplifies human virtue or vice. That's why cultivating virtue in AI developers and users is crucial."

---

### Segment 4: Coordinating Human and Artificial Intelligence (5 min)

**HOST:** "How do we design AI systems that coordinate with human virtue rather than replacing it?"

**VALLOR:**
"Three design principles:

**Principle 1: Transparency**

AI systems should make their reasoning visible to humans. Not black boxes that make inscrutable decisions, but systems that show their work.

This enables human judgment. A doctor using an AI diagnostic system needs to see why the AI recommends a particular diagnosis—so they can apply their medical wisdom and contextual understanding."

**[VISUAL: Example of transparent AI interface showing reasoning]**

**VALLOR:**
"**Principle 2: Human-in-the-loop**

AI should support human decision-making, not replace it. Keep humans in the coordination loop, especially for consequential decisions.

Example: An AI system helping judges with bail decisions. The AI can analyze recidivism patterns and flag relevant factors. But the judge makes the final decision, applying wisdom about this particular person in this particular context.

The coordination: AI pattern recognition + human judgment = better decisions than either alone."

**VALLOR:**
"**Principle 3: Feedback loops**

Design systems that learn from ethical outcomes, not just technical metrics. Include feedback about whether AI decisions actually served human flourishing.

Example: A hiring AI. Don't just measure 'prediction accuracy'—measure whether hired candidates thrive, whether the process felt fair, whether diversity improved.

This creates ethical learning: the AI learns what actually serves flourishing, not just what optimizes a proxy metric."

**HOST:** "This requires changing how we build AI?"

**VALLOR:**
"Completely. Current AI development optimizes technical performance: accuracy, speed, scale. Ethical AI development optimizes coordination: how well does this system coordinate with human virtue and social context to serve flourishing?

That's a different design paradigm. It means:

- Including ethicists and affected communities in design, not just engineers


- Measuring success by human outcomes, not just technical metrics


- Iterating based on ethical feedback, not just performance optimization


- Accepting 'good enough' technical performance if it enables better ethical coordination"



---

### Segment 5: Cultivating Virtue in the AI Age (5 min)

**HOST:** "If AI amplifies human virtue or vice, how do we cultivate virtue in the age of AI?"

**VALLOR:**
"This is the deeper challenge: technology is changing what virtues we need and how we develop them.

Let me give you three examples:"

**[VISUAL: Historical progression of virtue cultivation]**

**VALLOR:**
"**Attention and Focus**

Historically, temperance meant moderating physical appetites—food, drink, pleasure. Today, it means moderating digital consumption.

Social media, streaming platforms, infinite scroll—all designed to capture attention and maximize engagement. Developing temperance now means cultivating the ability to resist algorithmic manipulation.

This requires new practices: digital sabbaths, attention training, conscious consumption. The virtue is ancient, but the practices must be updated for algorithmic environments."

**VALLOR:**
"**Empathy and Understanding**

Historically, we developed empathy through face-to-face interaction—reading body language, hearing tone, experiencing shared presence.

Today, much interaction is mediated through technology. Text messages, social media, video calls. The bandwidth is reduced—we lose subtle cues that enable empathy.

Developing empathy now requires intentional practice: seeking face-to-face interaction, assuming good faith online, remembering there's a human behind every screen name.

The virtue is ancient, but technology makes it harder to cultivate."

**VALLOR:**
"**Practical Wisdom**

Historically, wisdom came from lived experience—making mistakes, learning from consequences, developing judgment over time.

Today, AI systems can provide answers instantly. Why struggle with a problem when you can ask an AI? Why develop expertise when you can outsource judgment?

The risk: we become dependent on AI judgment without developing our own. We lose the ability to coordinate with AI wisely because we haven't cultivated wisdom ourselves.

Developing wisdom now requires resisting the temptation to outsource judgment. Using AI as a tool while maintaining our capacity for independent thought."

**HOST:** "Technology makes virtue harder to cultivate?"

**VALLOR:**
"Technology changes the landscape of virtue cultivation. Some virtues become harder, some easier, some new ones emerge.

But the fundamental principle remains: human flourishing requires virtue. If we want AI to serve flourishing, we need humans with the virtue to coordinate with AI wisely.

This means education, practice, cultural support for virtue development. Not just building ethical AI, but cultivating ethical humans who can coordinate with AI."

### Segment 6: The Medical AI Example (5 min)

**HOST:** "Let's ground this in a concrete example. How does virtue ethics apply to medical AI?"

**VALLOR:**
"Perfect example because medicine has always been about coordinating technical knowledge with human judgment. Let me walk you through it:"

**[VISUAL: Medical consultation room with AI diagnostic system]**

**VALLOR:**
"**The Scenario:**

A doctor is using an AI diagnostic system. A patient presents with symptoms. The AI analyzes the symptoms, medical history, latest research, and recommends a diagnosis and treatment plan.

**The utilitarian temptation:**

Just follow the AI recommendation—it's based on millions of cases, it's more accurate than human judgment, it maximizes expected outcomes.

But this ignores patient context: their values, their life situation, their risk tolerance. Optimizing expected outcomes isn't the same as serving this patient's flourishing."

**VALLOR:**
"**The deontological temptation:**

Follow medical ethics rules: 'respect patient autonomy, do no harm, maintain confidentiality.'

But rules don't tell you how to balance competing principles. If the AI recommends an aggressive treatment that might harm quality of life but extend survival—how do you balance autonomy, beneficence, and non-maleficence?

Rules provide constraints but not guidance for wise judgment."

**VALLOR:**
"**The virtue ethics approach:**

The doctor coordinates three bodies:

**Their medical virtue**: Years of clinical experience, pattern recognition from treating patients, wisdom about what treatments actually help

**The AI's capability**: Pattern recognition across millions of cases, latest research synthesis, statistical analysis

**The patient's context**: Their values, their life goals, their tolerance for risk and side effects, their social support system

The doctor doesn't just follow the AI. They coordinate:

- Use AI insights to inform their clinical judgment


- Apply their wisdom to understand this particular patient


- Engage the patient in shared decision-making


- Make a recommendation that serves this patient's flourishing, not just optimizes expected outcomes"



**[VISUAL: Diagram showing coordination between doctor virtue, AI capability, patient context]**

**VALLOR:**
"The result: A decision that's better than the doctor alone (benefits from AI's pattern recognition), better than the AI alone (benefits from human wisdom and contextual understanding), and better than the patient alone (benefits from medical expertise).

This is ethical coordination: all three bodies contributing their strengths to serve flourishing."

**HOST:** "This requires doctors to maintain their virtue?"

**VALLOR:**
"Exactly. If doctors become dependent on AI recommendations without maintaining their own clinical judgment, the coordination fails. The AI becomes a crutch, not a tool.

This is why virtue cultivation is crucial: we need humans with the wisdom to coordinate with AI, not humans who've outsourced judgment to AI."

## ACT III: BUILDING COORDINATED ETHICS (10 minutes)

### Segment 7: Practical Implementation (4 min)

**HOST:** "How do organizations actually implement this virtue ethics approach?"

**VALLOR:**
"Three practical steps:

**Step 1: Virtue-centered design process**

Instead of asking 'what can this AI do?' ask 'what virtues should guide this AI's development and use?'

Gather stakeholders—developers, users, affected communities—and identify relevant virtues: What wisdom is needed? What justice considerations? What temperance?

This becomes the ethical framework for the project—not a list of rules, but a set of virtues to coordinate around."

**[VISUAL: Workshop scene with diverse stakeholders discussing virtues]**

**VALLOR:**
"**Step 2: Coordination architecture**

Design the system to enable coordination between human virtue and AI capability:

- Make AI reasoning transparent so humans can apply judgment


- Keep humans in the loop for consequential decisions


- Create feedback mechanisms for ethical outcomes


- Build in reflection points where humans reassess whether the system is serving flourishing



This isn't just technical architecture—it's ethical architecture."

**VALLOR:**
"**Step 3: Ongoing virtue cultivation**

Invest in developing virtue in everyone involved:

- Train developers in ethical reasoning and contextual awareness


- Support users in developing wisdom about AI coordination


- Create communities of practice for sharing ethical challenges


- Regularly reflect on whether the system is serving flourishing



This is continuous work, not a one-time ethics review."

**HOST:** "This sounds more demanding than just following ethics principles."

**VALLOR:**
"It is more demanding. Virtue ethics requires continuous judgment, reflection, and adaptation. You can't just check the 'ethics box' and move on.

But that's appropriate—because ethics is hard. The problems we're facing with AI are genuinely difficult. They require wisdom, not just rules or optimization.

The virtue ethics approach acknowledges that difficulty and provides a framework for navigating it through coordinated human judgment."

---

### Segment 8: The Limitations of AI Ethics (3 min)

**HOST:** "What can't AI do ethically, even with perfect coordination?"

**VALLOR:**
"AI can't develop or exercise virtue. It can participate in ethical coordination, but it can't replace human ethical agency.

Three fundamental limitations:"

**VALLOR:**
"**First: AI can't understand meaning**

AI processes patterns in data. But it doesn't understand what those patterns mean for human flourishing. It doesn't grasp the significance of suffering, the value of dignity, the importance of justice.

This means AI can recognize patterns associated with ethical concerns, but it can't understand why those concerns matter. That understanding requires human consciousness."

**VALLOR:**
"**Second: AI can't make value judgments**

When values conflict—privacy vs. security, freedom vs. safety, individual rights vs. collective good—someone has to decide which matters more in this context.

AI can show you the tradeoffs, but it can't tell you which to prioritize. That requires human judgment informed by values, experience, and wisdom."

**VALLOR:**
"**Third: AI can't take moral responsibility**

When ethical decisions cause harm—and sometimes they will—someone must be accountable. AI can't be held responsible because it doesn't have agency or moral standing.

This means humans must remain in the ethical loop. We can use AI to inform our decisions, but we can't outsource responsibility to AI."

**HOST:** "So there's an ethical limit to AI capability?"

**VALLOR:**
"Yes. And recognizing that limit is crucial. The fantasy that we can build AI systems that make ethical decisions autonomously is dangerous.

We need to maintain human ethical agency and responsibility while coordinating with AI's capabilities. That's the only path to ethical AI."

---

### Segment 9: The Vision (3 min)

**HOST:** "What's your vision for ethical human-AI coordination?"

**VALLOR:**
"I envision a future where AI amplifies human virtue rather than replacing it. Where technology enables us to be wiser, more compassionate, more just—not by doing our ethical thinking for us, but by coordinating with our ethical capacities to create better outcomes."

**[VISUAL: Montage of ethical AI applications—medical, educational, civic]**

**VALLOR:**
"This means:

**In medicine**: AI that helps doctors apply their clinical wisdom at scale, serving more patients more effectively

**In education**: AI that helps teachers apply their pedagogical judgment to each student's needs, enabling personalized learning

**In governance**: AI that helps citizens and officials apply democratic values to policy decisions, enabling wiser collective choices

**In daily life**: AI that helps us resist manipulation, maintain attention, connect authentically—enabling virtue cultivation, not undermining it"

**VALLOR:**
"The key: AI as coordination infrastructure for human virtue, not as replacement for human judgment.

This requires:

- Designing AI for coordination, not autonomy


- Cultivating virtue in developers and users


- Maintaining human ethical agency and responsibility


- Continuously reflecting on whether our systems serve flourishing



It's harder than just optimizing metrics or following rules. But it's the only approach that respects both human dignity and the genuine difficulty of ethics."

**HOST:** "Ethics as coordination, not optimization."

**VALLOR:**
"Exactly. And that changes everything about how we build and use AI.

The question isn't 'how do we make AI ethical?' It's 'how do we coordinate AI capability with human virtue in social context to serve flourishing?'

That's the virtue ethics approach. And it's our best path to technology that genuinely serves human good."
