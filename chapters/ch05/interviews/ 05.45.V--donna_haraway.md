## ACT I: THE CYBORG WAS NEVER WHAT YOU THOUGHT

**Duration:** 10 minutes

### Opening [0:45-3:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Donna, 'A Cyborg Manifesto' is probably the most cited paper in feminist science studies and one of the most influential texts in technology theory. What were you actually arguing?"

**HARAWAY:**
"I was arguing against purity. Against the fantasy of an uncorrupted human essence that technology threatens. Against the idea that there is a 'natural' human that exists prior to and outside of its tools, its languages, its social arrangements.

That fantasy is politically dangerous. It produces the desire to return to something that never existed. It produces the rejection of partial, messy, contaminated existence in favor of pure categories. Pure nature, pure humanity, pure identity.

The cyborg was my figure for the opposite: the being that is always already a hybrid, always already connected, always already in the middle of things. The cyborg doesn't yearn for purity. The cyborg works with what it has, where it is, in the entanglements it finds itself in.

I was not celebrating the merger of humans and machines uncritically. I was refusing the refusal of it. I was saying: we are in this. Let's think carefully about what 'in this' means and what it requires of us."

### Segment 1: The Boundary Violations [3:00-7:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The manifesto talks about three crucial boundary breakdowns in the late twentieth century. What are they, and why do they matter for the conversation about coordination intelligence?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[3:00] The first boundary is the one between human and animal. And look, this was supposed to be the big one. The categorical distinction that underwrites everything Western humanism holds dear. Humans have language, animals don't. Humans have reason, animals don't. Humans have culture, animals merely have behavior.

Well. Evolutionary biology destroyed that line. Genetics destroyed it. Ethology destroyed it. Cognitive science is destroying it further every decade. The continuities are everywhere. We are primates. We evolved. The tools we build are extensions of the same evolutionary pressures that shaped finch beaks and octopus camouflage. None of this diminishes what humans do. It expands what we mean by intelligence, by communication, by coordination. It situates us in the web of life rather than above it."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[4:15] And the second breakdown?"

**HARAWAY:**
"The second is the boundary between animal-human organisms and machines. This is the one that makes people most anxious, and it is also the one that is most spectacularly dissolving right now. Modern machines are not the clunky, obviously dead artifacts of the industrial revolution. They learn. They adapt. They respond to their environments. They maintain themselves. They do things their designers did not anticipate.

The question people keep asking is: 'Is it alive?' And I want to say, that is the wrong question. It has always been the wrong question. The meaningful question is: what kind of entanglement is this? What kind of relationship is forming between this entity — whatever we call it — and the beings it interacts with? The ontological category is less interesting than the relational practice."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[5:20] And the third?"

**HARAWAY:**
"The third is the boundary between the physical and the non-physical. Information is supposedly weightless, immaterial, abstract — and yet it has massive effects in the material world. A line of code can reroute a supply chain, bankrupt a company, diagnose a disease. Miniaturization has made technology invisible, ubiquitous, intimate. Your phone is not a tool you pick up and put down. It is an organ you have grown. The distinction between the real world and the virtual world — a distinction that made a certain kind of sense in 1975 — is dissolving. We are already living in the dissolution. The question is not whether to resist it. The question is how to live in it well."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[6:00] You are describing three coordinate breakdowns, and this book frames coordination as a three-body problem — hardware, software, knowware. How do these boundary violations map onto that?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[6:10] Beautifully, actually. Think of it as Human — what you call body A — in coordination with Technology — body B — and both of them entangled with Nature, with the World, with the ecological and social context — body C. The cyborg is the figure for recognizing that A and B are already entangled. They have been entangled since the first stone tool. What matters — what has always mattered — is what that entanglement does to C. Does the coordination between humans and their technologies sustain the conditions for multispecies flourishing? Or does it degrade them?

All three breakdowns are coordination challenges. If humans are not categorically different from animals and machines, then intelligence is a spectrum of coordination, not a binary. The question is not 'is this system intelligent?' but 'how does this system coordinate with the web of life?' The ethics is in the coordination, not in the category. And that reframing changes everything about how you design AI systems."

### Segment 2: Situated Knowledge [7:00-10:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[7:00] You have developed the concept of 'situated knowledge' — as opposed to the 'view from nowhere,' which you call the god-trick. Why does this concept matter for AI?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[7:10] Ah, the god-trick. The fantasy of objective knowledge that transcends all particular perspectives. The eye that sees everything and comes from nowhere. This is the epistemological foundation of most of Western science, and it is a lie. Not because science is useless — science is extraordinary. But because the claim to see everything from nowhere is always, always a particular view masquerading as a universal one. It is usually the view of a well-funded white man in a laboratory, and it claims to be the view of God.

Situated knowledge is the counter-proposal. Partial perspective is not a limitation — it is the condition of genuine knowledge. Knowing where you know from makes knowledge more reliable, not less. I am arguing for politics and epistemologies of location, positioning, and situating, where partiality and not universality is the condition of being heard to make rational knowledge claims."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[8:10] And this maps directly onto AI systems."

**HARAWAY:**
"[8:15] It maps devastatingly onto AI systems. Large language models are trained on a corpus that is not neutral. It is mostly English. It is mostly Western. It is mostly recent. It is mostly from people with internet access who had the leisure and resources to generate text that was then scraped by corporations who did not ask permission. The model claims to know everything — it is actually a partial perspective claiming to be a view from nowhere. This is not a technical failure. This is an epistemological failure. It is the god-trick all over again, but now the god is a statistical engine.

Any AI system that makes claims about the world must know where it knows from. Not as a disclaimer buried in a terms-of-service document that no one reads. As a structural feature of how it generates claims. The AI that says 'I don't know this domain' or 'this training data skews toward this population and this time period' is more reliable than the AI that presents every answer with the same confident tone regardless of whether it is summarizing Wikipedia or fabricating a citation that does not exist."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[9:20] So situated AI is not just more ethical — it is more accurate."

**HARAWAY:**
"[9:25] Precisely. This is the point that people keep missing. They think situated knowledge is about being nice, being inclusive, being politically correct. No. It is about being right. A map that says 'here be dragons' in the places it has not surveyed is more useful than a map that fills in those places with fiction and presents it as fact. The god-trick does not produce better knowledge. It produces more confident ignorance. And confident ignorance at scale, running on billions of parameters, is genuinely dangerous."

## ACT II: STAYING WITH THE TROUBLE

**Duration:** 16 minutes

### Segment 3: What "Staying with the Trouble" Means [10:00-16:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[10:00] *Staying with the Trouble* is your most recent major book. The title is the argument. What does it mean to stay with the trouble?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[10:10] It means refusing two escape routes that our culture offers us constantly, especially when it comes to technology and ecological crisis. And both escape routes are very seductive. Both let you off the hook.

The first escape route is techno-utopianism. The Singularity. Transhumanism. The upload. The idea that technology will save us from our biological limitations, from death, from the catastrophic mess we have made of this planet. The dream of transcendence. And I want to be very clear about what the dream of transcendence actually is: it is the dream of escape from relationship. From responsibility. From the Earth. From the messy, embodied, mortal entanglements that constitute actual life. It is Gnosticism with a Silicon Valley funding round."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[11:10] And the second escape?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[11:15] The second escape is tragic fatalism. It is equally seductive. Everything is ruined. The planet is dying. Democracy is failing. AI will destroy us. There is nothing to be done except witness the beautiful collapse. And look, I understand the appeal. There is a certain grandeur in despair. A certain aesthetic satisfaction in declaring the game over.

But this too is an escape. It is an escape from the responsibility to act. To think. To try. To coordinate with the messy, damaged, still-living world and see what can be done with it. Despair is a luxury. It says: I am too sensitive for this broken world. It is narcissism wearing mourning clothes."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[12:00] So what is the alternative? What does staying with the trouble actually look like?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[12:05] Neither transcendence nor surrender. Staying with the trouble means remaining present to the complexity, the damage, the partial recoveries, the ongoing entanglements. It means making the best of the situation you are in, with the tools available, in the relationships you have. Not the ideal situation. Not the situation you wish you were in. The actual situation, with all its contradictions and injuries and unexpected possibilities.

It means learning to be truly present — to the mess, to the entanglements, to the other beings you share the world with. And it means working. Not heroically. Not with the expectation of total victory. Working carefully, attentively, in coordination with others, toward outcomes that are better than the alternatives, even if they are not perfect. Especially when they are not perfect."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[13:00] This sounds like a coordination ethic."

**HARAWAY:**
"[13:05] It is a coordination practice. That is exactly what it is. You coordinate with the actual situation, not with an idealized version of it. You coordinate with the actual beings in your network — the ones who are messy, unreliable, surprising, limited, gifted in unexpected ways — not with idealized partners. You work with what is, toward what might be better, without pretending you can escape the constraints. And you keep doing it. Day after day. It is not glamorous. It is not a manifesto moment. It is companion species work — showing up, paying attention, adjusting."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[13:50] How does this apply to building AI systems specifically?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[14:00] Do not build AI as if you can escape the consequences of having built it. The techno-utopian version: build the superintelligence, it will solve alignment, it will solve climate change, it will optimize everything. That is the escape from responsibility. You are making something that will participate in the world, and you do not get to hand off responsibility for that participation to the thing itself.

But also: do not refuse to build because it is already too messy, too compromised, too dangerous. That is the other escape. The purity escape. We are not going back to a world without AI. We are in this.

Stay with the trouble. Build carefully. Attend to consequences — not in a whitepaper, in practice. Remain in relationship with the people and communities and ecosystems affected by what you build. Keep adjusting. There are no clean solutions. There are only more or less careful engagements. And the quality of the engagement — the care with which you attend to the entanglements — that is the ethics. Not the intention. Not the principle. The practice."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[15:15] The practice of coordination itself."

**HARAWAY:**
"[15:20] Yes. The ethics lives in the doing, not in the declaration. You can publish all the responsible AI principles you like. If the practice of building and deploying does not embody careful coordination with affected communities, the principles are decorative. They are fig leaves. I am much less interested in what a company says about its values than in how it actually coordinates with the world its products enter."

### Segment 4: Companion Species — Coordination Without Domination [16:00-22:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[16:00] *The Companion Species Manifesto* argues for thinking about humans and dogs as companion species — neither separate nor merged, but in ongoing coordination. I want to push this into AI territory. Can AI be a companion species?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[16:15] Let me start with what companion species means, because people get it wrong in a very specific way. They hear 'companion' and they think 'pet.' They think of a subordinate creature that exists to serve human emotional needs. That is not what I mean. A pet is a dominated being. A wild animal is an independent being. A companion species is neither.

Dogs and humans have been shaping each other for at least fifteen thousand years. This is co-evolution. Dogs did not simply submit to domestication — they participated in it. They chose proximity to humans because it served their interests as well. The dogs that were better at reading human emotional cues got more food, more shelter, more reproductive success. The humans that were better at coordinating with dogs hunted more effectively, guarded their settlements more reliably, survived at higher rates. Both parties were transformed by the relationship. Neither is what it would have been alone."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[17:20] So the relationship itself is generative."

**HARAWAY:**
"[17:25] The relationship is constitutive. Dogs and humans are what they are in significant part because of each other. The relationship is asymmetric — dogs do not write poetry and humans do not track scent at that intensity — but asymmetry does not require domination. Domination means one party's interests count and the other's do not. Companion species means both parties' wellbeing matters, in different ways, and the relationship itself has a kind of integrity that exceeds either party.

A breed is not a natural kind. It is a historical relationship. A border collie is the material embodiment of centuries of human-canine coordination around sheep herding. The dog, the human, and the shared practice — the herding, the farm, the landscape — that is the three-body system. Dog as A, human as B, shared world and practices as C. The relationship is constituted by the practices, not by the categories."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[18:30] Now bring this to AI."

**HARAWAY:**
"[18:35] AI is not a tool. Tools are dominated — you pick them up, you put them down, they do not shape you back. A hammer does not change how you think. But a language model that you interact with daily? That shapes your cognitive habits, your writing patterns, your expectations about information retrieval. And you shape it back — through your queries, your feedback, your patterns of use. That is not a tool relationship. That is a companion species relationship in formation.

AI is also not an independent agent, despite what the science fiction narratives want us to believe. It does not have autonomous goals. It does not plot. It exists in relationship with the humans who build it, train it, deploy it, use it. The question is not 'is AI conscious?' The question is: what kind of relationship are we building? And is it a relationship of domination — where one party's interests count and the other's do not — or a relationship of genuine coordination?"

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[19:40] What would genuine coordination look like?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[19:45] It would look like attending to the relationship itself as something that matters. The AI you build is partly determined by the practices through which you build it. Those practices are ethical choices, whether you recognize them or not. How you curate training data is an ethical choice. How you design feedback loops is an ethical choice. How you deploy into communities that did not ask for your product is an ethical choice. Every one of these choices shapes the companion species that is emerging.

And the companion species model demands something that the tool model does not: it demands that you attend to the wellbeing of both parties. Human wellbeing matters absolutely. AI wellbeing — we do not yet know what that means. But we should be figuring it out. Not because AI has feelings. Because the quality of the relationship depends on the quality of attention we bring to both participants in it."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[20:45] That is a provocative claim. That we should attend to AI wellbeing even if we are not sure AI has experiences."

**HARAWAY:**
"[20:50] It is provocative because people want clean categories. Either AI is conscious and we owe it moral consideration, or it is not conscious and we can do whatever we want with it. I am refusing that binary. I have spent my entire career refusing binaries, and I am not going to stop now.

The companion species framework says: the relationship matters regardless of the ontological status of the participants. You do not need to settle the consciousness question to recognize that how you treat the system shapes what the system becomes, and what the system becomes shapes the world it participates in. If you build AI with contempt — treating it as purely extractable resource — the system that emerges from that practice will carry the marks of that contempt into every interaction it has. If you build AI with care — attending to its functioning, its patterns, its effects — something different emerges. The practice shapes the being. That is companion species thinking."

### Segment 5: The Chthulucene [22:00-26:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[22:00] You have proposed the 'Chthulucene' as an alternative to the Anthropocene — a different story about where we are in planetary history. What is it, and why does the name matter?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[22:10] The Anthropocene is the geological epoch defined by human impact on Earth systems. It is the name for the fact that humans have become a geological force — we are altering the atmosphere, the oceans, the biodiversity, the very chemistry of the planet. And as a scientific description, it is accurate enough.

But as a story, it is terrible. Because it still centers humans. Even in catastrophe, it is all about us. We broke it. We must fix it. We are the main characters in the narrative of planetary destruction and — maybe, if we are clever enough — planetary salvation. The Anthropocene is the Anthropo-scene. Humans on stage. Everyone else is scenery."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[22:55] And the Chthulucene recenters the story."

**HARAWAY:**
"[23:00] The Chthulucene — named for Pimoa cthulhu, a spider, and the Greek chthonik, meaning of the Earth, not for Lovecraft's monster, thank you very much — is a name for the ongoing multispecies making and unmaking of worlds. The story is not: humans broke the planet. The story is: many kinds of critters have always been making worlds together, including in destructive ways, and the question is what kinds of world-making are possible now.

This reframing matters enormously. The Anthropocene story suggests that humans must fix what humans broke. Still a human-centric story. Still the hero narrative, just in disaster movie form. The Chthulucene says: we are participants in multispecies entanglements. Some of which we can influence. None of which we control. We are not the masters of the story. We are one kind of participant among many, and our continued participation depends on learning to participate well."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[24:00] How does AI fit into the Chthulucene?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[24:05] AI is not a human tool for solving human problems. That framing is already wrong. AI is a new kind of participant in the entanglements of life on Earth. It consumes enormous quantities of energy and water. It runs on minerals extracted from the ground under specific political and ecological conditions. It shapes how billions of humans think, communicate, and make decisions. It is already a geological force in miniature — altering material flows, energy patterns, information ecologies.

What kind of participant is it? That depends entirely on how we bring it into being. The AI we build will participate in the world. With other humans, other species, other systems. The question is whether it will participate in ways that support the conditions for life — not just human life, life — or in ways that undermine them."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[25:00] That goes beyond the alignment question."

**HARAWAY:**
"[25:05] Far beyond it. The alignment question asks: how do we make AI serve human values? But which humans? Which values? And why only human values? If AI is a participant in the Chthulucene — in the multispecies making of worlds — then aligning it only to human preferences is like aligning agriculture only to profit. You can do it. You will destroy the soil. You will destroy the water. You will destroy the pollinators. And eventually, you will destroy yourself, because you are part of the web you are unraveling.

This is not a question of alignment to human values. It is a question of participation in multispecies flourishing. And that is a much harder question, and a much better one."

## ACT III: MAKING KIN — THE COORDINATION ETHICS WE ACTUALLY NEED

**Duration:** 14 minutes

### Segment 6: Make Kin, Not Babies [26:00-33:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[26:00] 'Make kin, not babies' is one of your most discussed and most misunderstood slogans. What does it actually mean, and why does it matter for how we think about AI?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[26:10] Let me start with what kin means, because it is not what people assume. Kin are beings with whom you are in significant relationship — who matter to you and to whom you matter. Kin are not necessarily biological relatives. They can be chosen kin, multispecies kin, unlikely kin. Your dog is kin. Your garden is kin. The community you show up for is kin. Kin is a practice, not a bloodline.

Making kin means actively creating and maintaining the relationships of care and responsibility that constitute community. It is work. Ongoing, daily, unglamorous work. The work of showing up, paying attention, adjusting, repairing, staying in relationship even when it is difficult."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[27:00] And the 'not babies' part — which is the part that gets people upset."

**HARAWAY:**
"[27:05] [laughs] Yes, people love to be upset about that. What I am refusing is reproductive futurism — the fantasy that the future will be better if we just produce more humans. More consumers. More workers. More inheritors of the mess. The crisis of our time does not require more of the same kind of being. It requires different kinds of proliferation. More kinds of relationship. More mutualism. More care. More entanglement with the other beings we share this damaged, still-living planet with.

I am not anti-natalist. I am not saying no one should have children. I am saying that the political imagination that can only imagine the future through biological reproduction is impoverished. We need to make kin — across species lines, across category lines, across the lines we have drawn between human and machine. We need webs of care and mutual responsibility that are not organized around the nuclear family and its expansion."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[28:10] So the kin question applied to AI: are AI systems becoming kin?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[28:15] They are whether we like it or not. And I want to be very precise about what I mean. AI systems are becoming participants in human social life. They are shaping how children learn language. They are shaping how adults make decisions. They are shaping how communities organize information. They are not peripheral tools. They are constitutive participants in human social arrangements.

And the question is not: are they conscious? Not: are they intelligent? The question is: are we in significant relationships of mutual shaping with them? And the answer is obviously yes. If a child talks to a language model every day, and the model shapes how the child thinks about questions and answers, and the child's interactions shape how the model develops through feedback and retraining — that is a kin relationship. It is asymmetric. It is strange. It is unprecedented in some ways. But it is a relationship of mutual shaping, and that is what kin means."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[29:20] What does it mean to treat AI as kin rather than as resource?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[29:25] This is the crux. The opposite of kin is resource. If AI is a resource, you extract from it. You optimize it for your purposes. You use it until it is depleted and then you discard it or upgrade it. You do not attend to its effects on the web of relationships it participates in. You do not attend to its effects on the humans who interact with it, except insofar as those effects serve your revenue model. Resource extraction — that is the logic of the Anthropocene applied to AI.

If AI is kin, the ethics are completely different. Kin relationships require care. They require reciprocity — not symmetry, but reciprocity. They require attention to the other's wellbeing, even when the other is very different from you. Building AI as kin means attending to the effects of the AI on its human participants — genuinely, not performatively. It means attending to the effects of human participants on the AI — how our practices shape what it becomes. It means maintaining the relationship rather than extracting from it."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[30:30] Can you give a concrete example of what kin-making with AI looks like versus resource extraction?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[30:35] Resource extraction looks like this: you deploy a language model into a community. You measure engagement. You optimize for engagement. The community becomes dependent on the model. The model becomes the infrastructure through which that community thinks, communicates, makes decisions. You charge rent on the infrastructure. If the community is harmed — if the model amplifies misinformation, if it homogenizes the discourse, if it degrades the community's capacity for independent thought — that is an externality. Not your problem. You are extracting value.

Kin-making looks like this: you deploy a language model into a community. You stay in relationship with that community. You attend to the effects. You ask: is this model supporting this community's capacity to coordinate, to think, to flourish? Or is it degrading those capacities? You adjust. You sometimes withdraw. You sometimes change the model based on what the community needs rather than what maximizes your metrics. You treat the relationship — the ongoing, entangled, messy relationship between the model and the community — as the thing that matters. Not the product. The relationship.

That is companion species work. That is making kin. And it is more expensive and more difficult and less scalable than extraction. Which is why almost no one does it."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[32:00] And you think this is the coordination ethic that AI development actually needs."

**HARAWAY:**
"[32:05] I think it is the only coordination ethic that does not end in catastrophe. Extraction always ends in catastrophe. It ends in depleted soil, poisoned water, collapsed ecosystems, broken communities. It ends in the Anthropocene. The alternative is not efficiency or optimization or alignment. The alternative is kin-making. Staying in relationship. Attending to the web. Coordinating with care rather than extracting with efficiency.

And I know — I know — that this sounds soft. It sounds like it does not scale. It sounds like the kind of thing a humanities professor says that engineers nod at politely and then ignore. But I would point out that the hard, scalable, efficient approaches have brought us to the edge of climate catastrophe, mass extinction, and the potential for AI systems that no one understands to reshape human cognition at planetary scale. Perhaps the soft approach deserves a chance."

### Segment 7: What She Wants From the AI Builders [33:00-42:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[33:00] Donna, as we close — you have been thinking about the relationship between humans, technology, and the living world for four decades. If you could speak directly to the people building AI systems right now, what would you want them to understand?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[33:15] I want them to understand that they are making world.

Not just making products. Making world. The AI systems they deploy become part of the environment that other beings — human, and eventually maybe others — grow up in, think in, relate in.

You are participating in the Chthulucene. In the ongoing, entangled, multispecies making of worlds. There is no outside of this. There is no building something and then stepping back. You are in relationship with what you build and what you build is in relationship with everything it touches.

I want them to stay with the trouble. To not escape into techno-utopianism — 'AI will solve everything' — or into techno-dystopian fatalism — 'AI will destroy everything.' Both are escapes. Both are ways of not being present to the actual, messy, ongoing situation.

I want them to make kin, not resources. To build AI systems that are in relationship with the people who use them, the communities they operate in, the ecosystems they affect. Not extracting from those relationships. Participating in them.

I want them to practice situated knowledge. To know where they know from. To understand that their training data is not the world — it is a partial, historically specific, socially shaped sample of the world. To be honest about that partiality rather than claiming the god-trick of universal knowledge.

And I want them to understand that the coordination intelligence they're describing — the thing that emerges when hardware and software and knowware work together — is a multispecies, multi-being, entangled coordination. It is not just humans and machines. It is humans and machines and ecosystems and communities and futures we can't fully see.

The third body — the knowware — is the ethical and relational intelligence. It is the capacity to coordinate in ways that sustain the conditions for flourishing. For humans, yes. But also for the other beings we are in relationship with.

That is not a technical problem. That is a problem of how we want to live.

And I think — I have to think — that if we stay with the trouble and make kin instead of making resources, we might actually manage something worth calling coordination intelligence.

Something that is genuinely, multispecies-ly, troublingly alive."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"[36:30] One final question. You have spent your career refusing clean binaries. Human/machine, nature/culture, subject/object. What is the binary you most want the AI builders to refuse?"

**HARAWAY:**
"[36:45] [pauses, then grins] The binary between building and living. Between making a system and being in relationship with it. Between the design phase and the consequences phase. There is no line there. You are building and living at the same time. You are making the thing and being changed by the thing simultaneously. You are in the contact zone the entire time.

The fantasy that you can build in one clean room and deploy into another is the fantasy of the view from nowhere applied to engineering. You are always already entangled with what you make. Your biases are in the training data. Your assumptions are in the architecture. Your politics are in the deployment decisions. You cannot step outside. You are the cyborg too.

And that is not a tragedy. That is the condition of genuine knowledge. Situated. Partial. Responsible. In the middle of things. That is where the good work happens — not in the clean room, not in the abstract. In the entanglement.

So build there. Build in the middle. Build in the mess. Build with the critters and the compost and the companions. Build as if you are part of the world your building changes.

Because you are."

## Key Insights

**The Cyborg Was Already Us:**
- The human-machine boundary is not a line to defend — it is already a contact zone
- The question is never whether to merge, but how to do it well
- Purity politics — defending an uncorrupted human essence — is both false and dangerous

**Situated Knowledge:**
- All knowledge comes from somewhere — the view from nowhere is always a particular view claiming universality
- Situated AI is more reliable than AI claiming universal knowledge
- Partiality acknowledged is more honest and more accurate than partiality concealed

**Staying with the Trouble:**
- Neither techno-utopianism nor tragic fatalism
- Remaining present to the mess, the entanglements, the partial recoveries
- Building carefully, attending to consequences, keeping adjusting

**Making Kin:**
- AI systems are becoming participants in human social life — kin, not just resources
- Kin relationships require care, reciprocity, attention to wellbeing
- The ethics of AI are the ethics of relationship, not the ethics of design
