# Interview 8: Jimmy Wales

## "Wikipedia's Coordination Architecture: How Distributed Intelligence Creates Knowledge"

**Chapter 5: Human-Systems Intelligence Interaction**

---

## Interview Metadata

**Duration:** 40 minutes
**Location:** Wikimedia Foundation offices, San Francisco
**Setting:** Open office with Wikipedia edit streams visible on screens, whiteboards showing governance structures
**Visual Style:** Transparent and collaborative—showing the mechanics of open coordination
**Key Props:** Live Wikipedia editing demonstration, edit history visualization, discussion page examples, community governance docs

**Guest Bio:**
Jimmy Wales is co-founder of Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia that anyone can edit. What started as an experiment in 2001 has become humanity's largest collaborative knowledge project: 60 million articles in 300+ languages, created by millions of volunteer contributors with no central control. Wikipedia proves that coordination can create quality at scales traditional organizations can't match.

**Core Thesis:**
Wikipedia works not because of the technology (wikis existed before) or despite anonymous editors (the usual concern), but because of three-body coordination: individual contributors, technology platform, and community governance. Most wikis fail because they optimize technology without coordinating community norms.

It's the coordination architecture."

### Segment 1: Why Most Wikis Fail (4 min)

**WALES:**
"Let me start with why Wikipedia's success is rare. Thousands of organizations have tried to build wikis. Most fail. Why?

Because they think wiki success comes from the technology. 'We'll install wiki software, people will collaborate, knowledge will emerge.'

But that's two-body thinking: technology ←→ users. And it doesn't work."

**[VISUAL: Examples of failed wikis - abandoned pages, edit wars, spam]**

**WALES:**
"Failed wikis have three patterns:

**Pattern 1: Chaos**

No coordination norms. People edit randomly, contradict each other, argue without resolution. The wiki becomes noise, not knowledge.

Result: Abandoned. Too frustrating to use.

**Pattern 2: Domination**

A few loud voices dominate. They control what gets published, revert others' edits, drive contributors away.

Result: Abandoned. Why contribute if your work gets deleted?

**Pattern 3: Stagnation**

Initial enthusiasm, then decline. Pages go out of date, no one maintains them, the wiki becomes a graveyard.

Result: Abandoned. Why visit if the information is stale?"

**HOST:** "So the technology isn't enough?"

**WALES:**
"The technology is necessary but not sufficient. You need the third body: community governance.

Wikipedia works because we coordinate three bodies:

**Body 1: Individual Contributors** - people who write and edit

**Body 2: Technology Platform** - wiki software enabling collaboration

**Body 3: Community Governance** - norms, policies, and processes that coordinate contribution

Most wikis have 1 and 2. Wikipedia has all three. That's why it works."

---

### Segment 2: The Three-Body Architecture (4 min)

**HOST:** "Walk us through Wikipedia's three-body coordination in detail."

**WALES:**
"Let me show you how all three bodies coordinate to create quality:"

**[VISUAL: Whiteboard - Wales draws three circles]**

**WALES:**
"**Body 1: Individual Contributors**

Millions of volunteers worldwide. Diverse expertise, perspectives, motivations.

What drives them? Not money—we don't pay anyone. Intrinsic motivation:

- They care about topics and want to share knowledge


- They enjoy the process of writing and editing


- They value contributing to a public good


- They appreciate the community



This is the human knowledge layer."

**WALES:**
"**Body 2: Technology Platform**

Wiki software that makes coordination possible:

- Anyone can edit—low barriers to contribution


- All changes tracked—complete edit history, accountability


- Discussion pages—transparent coordination


- Watchlists—contributors monitor topics they care about


- Recent changes—community oversight


- Bots and tools—automation for routine tasks



This is the coordination infrastructure."

**WALES:**
"**Body 3: Community Governance**

Social norms and policies that coordinate contribution:

- **Neutral Point of View (NPOV)**: Present all significant viewpoints fairly


- **Verifiability**: Information must be sourced to reliable publications


- **No Original Research**: Report what others have published, don't add your own theories


- **Assume Good Faith**: Treat other editors as well-intentioned


- **Dispute Resolution**: Processes for handling disagreements


- **Administrator System**: Community-elected admins for difficult cases



This is the coordination wisdom."

**[VISUAL: Animation showing coordination arrows between all three bodies]**

**WALES:**
"Here's how they coordinate:

**Scenario: Someone makes a bad edit**

- Technology tracks it (edit history, recent changes)


- Community notices it (watchlists, patrol)


- Someone reverts it (individual action)


- If disputed, discussion (coordination via talk page)


- If unresolved, administrator intervention (governance)


- Outcome improves both content and community norms



This is coordination creating quality through distributed intelligence."

## ACT II: COORDINATION PRINCIPLES IN ACTION (20 minutes)

### Segment 3: The NPOV Principle (5 min)

**HOST:** "Let's dig into the governance principles. Start with Neutral Point of View—how does that coordinate contribution?"

**WALES:**
"NPOV is the coordination principle that prevents Wikipedia from becoming a battleground.

Here's the problem: People have different perspectives, especially on controversial topics. Without coordination, you get edit wars—people constantly changing articles to reflect their viewpoint."

**[VISUAL: Edit war example - article being changed back and forth]**

**WALES:**
"NPOV provides the coordination framework: Don't try to determine the 'true' viewpoint. Instead, present all significant viewpoints fairly.

Let me show you with an example: Climate change."

**[VISUAL: Climate change article showing balanced presentation]**

**WALES:**
"People have strong views on climate change. If we tried to determine the 'correct' view, we'd have endless conflict.

Instead, NPOV coordinates contribution:

- Present the scientific consensus (most climate scientists agree human activity causes warming)


- Present dissenting views (some scientists disagree or emphasize uncertainties)


- Weight coverage by prominence (consensus gets more space because it's more widely held)


- Source everything (readers can verify claims)



Result: An article that people with different views can accept as fair, even if they disagree with some perspectives presented."

**HOST:** "NPOV prevents conflict?"

**WALES:**
"It channels conflict into productive coordination. Instead of fighting about whose view is correct, contributors coordinate around fair representation of all significant views.

This is brilliant coordination design: Don't eliminate disagreement—coordinate it productively.

The principle applies beyond Wikipedia: When you can't agree on truth, coordinate around fair representation. This enables collaboration despite deep disagreement."

---

### Segment 4: Verifiability and Collective Intelligence (4 min)

**HOST:** "How does verifiability create collective intelligence?"

**WALES:**
"Verifiability is the principle that prevents Wikipedia from becoming original research or opinion.

Everything must be sourced to reliable published sources. You can't just add your own theories or knowledge—you must cite where you got the information."

**[VISUAL: Article with inline citations, references section]**

**WALES:**
"This seems limiting: 'I'm an expert! I know this is true! Why do I need to cite sources?'

But it enables collective intelligence through coordination:

**Coordination with Published Knowledge**

Wikipedia aggregates and synthesizes what's already published. We're not creating new knowledge—we're coordinating existing knowledge into accessible form.

This means Wikipedia's quality is bounded by published knowledge quality. We're only as good as our sources."

**WALES:**
"**Coordination Among Contributors**

Verifiability provides a shared standard for resolving disputes. Instead of 'I think X', it's 'Source Y says X'.

This coordinates contribution around evidence, not opinion. When contributors disagree, they can check sources, evaluate reliability, reach consensus based on evidence.

**Coordination with Readers**

Readers can verify claims by checking citations. This builds trust—you don't have to trust Wikipedia, you can verify the sources.

This coordinates knowledge consumption: Wikipedia as gateway to sources, not as final authority."

**HOST:** "Verifiability creates a coordination standard?"

**WALES:**
"Exactly. It's the coordination principle that enables millions of contributors with different expertise and perspectives to create coherent, reliable knowledge.

Without it, Wikipedia would be chaos—everyone adding their own beliefs with no way to coordinate quality."

---

### Segment 5: Assume Good Faith and Community Health (4 min)

**HOST:** "Tell us about 'Assume Good Faith.' Why is that crucial?"

**WALES:**
"This is the social coordination principle that keeps the community healthy.

When someone makes an edit you disagree with, you have a choice:

**Assume bad faith**: 'They're vandalizing! They're pushing an agenda! They're incompetent!'

Result: Hostility, conflict, people leave.

**Assume good faith**: 'They're trying to improve the article. They might be mistaken, but they're well-intentioned.'

Result: Constructive dialogue, learning, community growth."

**[VISUAL: Comparison of bad faith vs. good faith interactions on talk pages]**

**WALES:**
"Assume Good Faith coordinates community behavior toward collaboration rather than conflict.

Example: A new editor adds poorly written content with weak sources.

**Bad faith response**: Delete it, leave harsh message: 'This is terrible. Learn to write before editing.'

Result: New editor leaves, never contributes again. Wikipedia loses potential contributor.

**Good faith response**: Improve the content, leave helpful message: 'Thanks for contributing! I've refined the writing and added stronger sources. Here are resources on Wikipedia style.'

Result: New editor learns, improves, becomes valuable contributor. Wikipedia gains contributor."

**WALES:**
"This principle is crucial because Wikipedia depends on volunteer retention. If we drive people away with hostility, the project dies.

Assume Good Faith coordinates contributor behavior toward community growth and knowledge improvement."

**HOST:** "Even when people ARE acting in bad faith?"

**WALES:**
"Even then, start with good faith assumption. Give them the benefit of the doubt.

If they continue problematic behavior, then escalate: warnings, temporary blocks, permanent bans if necessary.

But starting with good faith assumption means we don't alienate well-intentioned newcomers who just don't know the norms yet.

This is coordination wisdom: Optimize for community health, not for punishing bad actors."

---

### Segment 6: The Coordination Learning System (4 min)

**HOST:** "How does Wikipedia learn and improve its coordination over time?"

**WALES:**
"Wikipedia is a learning system—not just learning knowledge, but learning how to coordinate knowledge creation.

Let me show you the feedback loops:"

**[VISUAL: Diagram showing Wikipedia's learning loops]**

**WALES:**
"**Content Learning Loop**

Contributors add content → Readers use it → Problems discovered → Contributors improve it → Quality increases

This is collective intelligence through distributed error correction. Millions of eyes catching mistakes, millions of hands fixing them.

**Norm Learning Loop**

Community faces coordination problem → Discusses solution → Establishes norm or policy → Applies it → Refines based on experience

Our policies evolved through community deliberation. NPOV, verifiability, assume good faith—these emerged from coordination challenges.

**Technology Learning Loop**

Contributors encounter coordination friction → Request new tools → Developers build them → Coordination improves

Watchlists, recent changes patrol, edit filters, bots—all built in response to coordination needs.

**Meta-Learning Loop**

We study what works and doesn't work → Research coordination patterns → Share findings → Other communities learn from us

Wikipedia has become a laboratory for understanding coordination at scale."

**HOST:** "The system is continuously improving its coordination?"

**WALES:**
"Yes. That's why Wikipedia has survived and grown for 20+ years while most wikis fail.

We're not static. We're a learning system that continuously improves its coordination architecture based on experience.

This is the key to long-term coordination success: Build feedback loops that enable learning and adaptation."

---

### Segment 7: When Coordination Fails (3 min)

**HOST:** "Wikipedia isn't perfect. When does coordination break down?"

**WALES:**
"Coordination fails in predictable patterns. Let me show you three:

**Failure Pattern 1: Systemic Bias**

Our contributor base isn't representative—disproportionately male, Western, educated. This creates coordination bias:

- Topics popular with contributors get great coverage


- Topics outside contributor experience get poor coverage


- Perspectives from underrepresented groups get less attention



Result: Coverage gaps and perspective imbalances.

This is a coordination failure: We're not coordinating diverse enough voices."

**[VISUAL: Visualization of coverage gaps]**

**WALES:**
"**Failure Pattern 2: Expertise Coordination**

Sometimes experts and amateurs clash. The expert knows more, but the amateur is more active. Without good coordination, the amateur's persistence wins over the expert's knowledge.

Result: Quality suffers on technical topics.

This is a coordination failure: We're not effectively coordinating expertise with contribution.

**Failure Pattern 3: Bad Actor Persistence**

Most people assume good faith and move on when reverted. But bad actors exploit this—they persist with bad edits until good faith editors give up.

Result: Bad content persists because coordination costs exceed bad actor persistence.

This is a coordination failure: We're not effectively coordinating defense against determined bad actors."

**HOST:** "How do you address these coordination failures?"

**WALES:**
"By improving the coordination architecture:

- Actively recruit underrepresented contributors (diversify body 1)


- Build better expertise coordination tools (improve body 2)


- Develop more efficient bad actor detection (enhance body 3)



The key: Coordination failures aren't permanent. They're design challenges. We can improve coordination architecture to address them."

## ACT III: LESSONS FOR THE FUTURE (10 minutes)

### Segment 8: Coordination Principles for AI (4 min)

**HOST:** "What can AI systems learn from Wikipedia's coordination architecture?"

**WALES:**
"Wikipedia proves that distributed coordination can create quality at scales centralized control can't match. AI systems should learn from this.

Three principles:"

**[VISUAL: Comparison of Wikipedia coordination vs. typical AI systems]**

**WALES:**
"**Principle 1: Transparency**

Wikipedia makes everything visible: Edit history, discussion, decision-making process. You can see how knowledge was created and coordinated.

AI systems should do the same: Show reasoning, show data, show how decisions were made. Transparency enables coordination and trust.

**Principle 2: Human Agency**

Wikipedia keeps humans in control. The technology facilitates, but humans decide what content to include, how to present it, what norms to follow.

AI systems should do the same: Augment human judgment, don't replace it. Keep humans in the coordination loop.

**Principle 3: Community Governance**

Wikipedia is governed by community norms, not corporate policies. The people using the system shape how it works.

AI systems should involve affected communities in governance: Not just engineers deciding, but users, stakeholders, affected communities coordinating around norms and values."

**HOST:** "These principles enable coordination at scale?"

**WALES:**
"They enable coordination that creates quality. Wikipedia has proven: Distributed coordination with good governance produces better results than centralized control.

AI systems that adopt these principles—transparency, human agency, community governance—will coordinate more effectively with human values and needs."

---

### Segment 9: The Future of Collective Knowledge (3 min)

**HOST:** "What's the future of Wikipedia and collective knowledge creation?"

**WALES:**
"We're exploring how AI can enhance coordination without replacing human judgment.

Three directions:"

**[VISUAL: Mockups of AI-enhanced Wikipedia features]**

**WALES:**
"**AI-Assisted Editing**

AI that helps contributors:

- Suggests sources for unsourced claims


- Identifies potential NPOV issues


- Flags outdated information


- Proposes improvements to clarity or structure



The AI facilitates human editing, doesn't replace it. Humans maintain editorial control.

**AI-Facilitated Coordination**

AI that improves contributor coordination:

- Connects editors working on related topics


- Identifies coordination conflicts before they escalate


- Suggests consensus approaches for disputed content


- Helps onboard new contributors



The AI facilitates human coordination, doesn't make decisions.

**AI-Enhanced Verification**

AI that supports verifiability:

- Checks citations for accuracy


- Flags potential misinformation


- Suggests additional sources


- Monitors for vandalism



The AI supports human verification, doesn't replace human judgment."

**HOST:** "AI as coordination facilitator?"

**WALES:**
"Exactly. The same role technology has always played in Wikipedia: Facilitating human coordination to create collective intelligence.

We're not building AI to write Wikipedia. We're building AI to help humans coordinate better to write Wikipedia.

This is the right model: AI enhancing human coordination, not replacing human intelligence."

### Segment 10: The Vision (3 min)

**HOST:** "Final question: What does Wikipedia teach us about human potential?"

**WALES:**
"Wikipedia proves that humans can coordinate at massive scale to create public goods without central control or financial incentives.

Think about what we've built:

- 60 million articles


- 300+ languages


- Billions of page views per month


- All created by volunteers


- All free to access


- All coordinated without central authority



This shouldn't be possible. Traditional organizational theory says you need hierarchy, control, incentives.

But Wikipedia proves: Given the right coordination architecture—individual motivation, enabling technology, community governance—humans will coordinate to create extraordinary things."

**[VISUAL: Montage of Wikipedia's global reach and impact]**

**WALES:**
"The lesson for the future:

Don't optimize individual intelligence. Optimize coordination architecture.

Don't centralize control. Enable distributed coordination.

Don't replace human judgment with AI. Facilitate human coordination with AI.

The problems we face—climate, inequality, polarization, AI alignment—these require coordination at scales we've never achieved.

Wikipedia shows it's possible. Millions of people with different perspectives, different expertise, different motivations—all coordinating to create something valuable for humanity.

That's the model. That's the proof. That's the future.

Not AI replacing human intelligence. But humans and AI coordinating to create collective intelligence at civilization scale.

Wikipedia is just the beginning."
