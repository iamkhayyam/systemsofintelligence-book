## ACT I: FROM NEUROSCIENCE TO COORDINATED AI

**Duration: 10 minutes**

### SEGMENT 1: The Neuroscience Foundation (3 min)

**[00:45 - 00:50] [A-CAM: Interviewer, warm and engaged.]**

**[00:50 - 01:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, leaning forward, enthusiastic about neuroscience.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"My PhD research focused on the hippocampus—this small structure deep in the brain that's critical for memory. And what I discovered completely changed how I think about intelligence."

**[01:10] [Visual: Brain scan highlighting hippocampus, then zooming in to show its structure.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"The hippocampus doesn't store memories. That was the conventional view, but it's wrong. What it actually does is coordinate memory storage across the cortex. It's a coordination hub, not a storage unit."

**[01:30 - 01:40] [A-CAM: Interviewer, intrigued.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**

**[01:40 - 02:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining the distributed architecture.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Exactly. When you remember a beach vacation, different aspects are stored in different brain regions. The visual memory—what the beach looked like—is in visual cortex. The sound of waves is in auditory cortex. The feeling of sand is in somatosensory cortex. The emotion you felt is in the amygdala."

**[02:00] [Visual: Brain diagram showing different regions lighting up, each holding a piece of the memory.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"The hippocampus coordinates all these distributed pieces into a unified memory. It doesn't contain the memory—it coordinates the retrieval and integration. Memory emerges from coordination, not from any single location."

**[02:30 - 02:40] [A-CAM: Interviewer, making the connection.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And that taught you that intelligence is about coordination?"

**[02:40 - 03:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, nodding, this is the key insight.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Absolutely. The brain isn't a monolithic processor. It's specialized subsystems coordinating. The hippocampus coordinates memory. The prefrontal cortex coordinates planning. The thalamus coordinates sensory information. Intelligence emerges from coordination architecture, not from any single region."

**[03:00] [Visual: Brain network visualization showing different regions coordinating through connection hubs.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This was the blueprint for how I've approached AI ever since. Don't build monolithic intelligence. Build specialized capabilities that coordinate. The coordination architecture is what creates intelligence."

## ACT II: THE COORDINATION ARCHITECTURE OF INTELLIGENCE

**Duration: 24 minutes**

### SEGMENT 5: AlphaGo's Three-Body Architecture (8 min)

**[07:45 - 07:50] [SCREEN CAM: Hassabis begins presenting AlphaGo architecture on large display.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"AlphaGo has three coordinating components. Let me show you each one, then how they work together."

**[07:50 - 09:20] [SCREEN CAM: Diagram showing Component 1: Policy Network.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Component One: The Policy Network.** This is AlphaGo's intuition."

**[08:00 - 08:40] [B-CAM: Hassabis explaining while gesturing to screen.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"We trained a deep neural network on 100,000 human expert games. It learned to recognize patterns—what moves look promising in different positions. When you show it a board position, it instantly outputs a probability distribution over all possible moves."

**[08:20] [Visual: Animation showing Go board → Policy Network → Probability distribution over moves, highlighting the most promising ones.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"It's fast—milliseconds. It's intuitive—based on pattern recognition, not calculation. And it mimics how human Go masters think: 'This move feels right.' It's strategic intuition, learned from human expertise."

**[08:40 - 09:00] [B-CAM: Hassabis, acknowledging limitations.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"But intuition alone isn't enough. It's fast but can be wrong. It misses deep tactical sequences. That's why we need the second component."

**[09:00 - 09:05] [A-CAM: Interviewer, following the logic.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"The value network?"

**[09:05 - 09:20] [B-CAM: Hassabis, nodding.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Exactly. **Component Two: The Value Network.** This is AlphaGo's judgment."

**[09:20 - 10:20] [SCREEN CAM: Diagram showing Component 2: Value Network.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"The value network evaluates board positions without searching. You give it a position, it tells you: 'White is winning' or 'Black is winning' with a probability. It's learned from millions of self-play games—positions that led to wins versus losses."

**[09:40] [Visual: Go board → Value Network → Win probability percentage.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is positional judgment. Human Go masters develop this over years—they can look at a position and just know who's winning, even if they can't calculate the exact winning sequence. The value network mimics that judgment."

**[10:00 - 10:20] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining the limitation.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"But judgment alone isn't enough either. It tells you who's winning, but not how to get there. It doesn't show the path to victory. That's where the third component comes in."

**[10:20 - 10:30] [SCREEN CAM: Diagram showing Component 3: Monte Carlo Tree Search.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Component Three: Monte Carlo Tree Search.** This is AlphaGo's calculation engine."

**[10:30 - 11:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining the search process.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Tree search is systematic exploration of possible futures. 'If I play here, opponent plays there, I play here, opponent plays there...' It builds a tree of possibilities, searching for the best sequence of moves."

**[10:50] [Visual: Animated tree growing from current position, branches representing possible move sequences.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"But Go's branching factor is enormous—hundreds of possible moves per turn. You can't search everything. That's where the coordination comes in."

**[11:10 - 11:30] [SCREEN CAM: Diagram showing all three components with coordination arrows between them.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"The policy network tells search which branches to explore—the promising moves. The value network tells search when to stop exploring a branch—this position is clearly winning or losing. Search finds the best sequences within the space the other two components define."

**[11:30 - 11:40] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking the key question.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So none of them alone could beat a world champion?"

**[11:40 - 12:40] [B-CAM: Hassabis, emphatic, this is the coordination proof.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"None. We tested each component individually. The policy network alone? Plays at strong amateur level, but makes mistakes. The value network alone? Can't actually play—just evaluates. Tree search alone? Too slow, searches wrong branches, can't finish games in reasonable time."

**[12:00] [Visual: Three separate performance graphs showing each component's individual performance—all below world champion level.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"But coordinate all three—intuition guiding search, search refining intuition, evaluation focusing both—and you get superhuman performance. Move 37 emerged from that coordination. None of the components alone would have found it."

**[12:20 - 12:40] [SCREEN CAM: Coordination diagram with all three components, showing how Move 37 emerged from their interaction.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Intelligence isn't in any component. It's in the coordination. That's the breakthrough."

**[12:40] [Production note: This is the proof of concept—coordination creates superhuman intelligence. Mark it visually.]**
