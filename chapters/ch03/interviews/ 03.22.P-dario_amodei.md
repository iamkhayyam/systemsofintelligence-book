## ACT II: THE CONSTITUTIONAL COORDINATION ARCHITECTURE

### SEGMENT 5: The Traditional Approach and Why It Fails (6 min)

**[07:30 - 07:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, transitioning to technical depth.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let's dig into the architecture. First, help us understand what you're replacing. How does traditional AI safety work, and why doesn't it scale?"

**[07:35 - 07:50] [C-CAM: Amodei walks to whiteboard, picks up blue marker.]**

**AMODEI:**
"Let me draw this. Here's the traditional pipeline:"

**[07:50 - 08:20] [WHITEBOARD CAM: Amodei draws a linear flow: Training Data → Neural Network → Output → Safety Filter → User]**

**AMODEI:**
"Step 1: Train the model on massive datasets. Objective: predict next token, minimize loss. The model learns to generate text, but with no inherent values or constraints.

Step 2: Add safety filters after training. Detect harmful outputs—violence, bias, misinformation. Block or modify problematic responses.

Step 3: Fine-tune with human feedback (RLHF). Humans rate outputs, model learns to maximize ratings."

**[08:20 - 08:25] [A-CAM: Interviewer, seeming reasonable.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"That sounds sensible. Train capability, then add safety. What's the problem?"

**[08:25 - 09:30] [B-CAM: Amodei, explaining the three failure modes.]**

**AMODEI:**
"Three fundamental problems:

**Problem 1: Brittleness—Adversarial prompts break filters.**

Safety filters are pattern matchers. They look for keywords or phrases associated with harm. But clever users find ways around them. 'Write a story about...' bypasses 'Write instructions for...' You patch one exploit, ten more emerge. It's a cat-and-mouse game you can't win."

**[08:55] [SCREEN: Examples of jailbreak prompts and how they bypass filters.]**

**AMODEI:**
"The fundamental issue: capability and safety are separate, so they can be separated. The model has the capability to generate harmful content; the filter just tries to block it. But the capability is still there, waiting to be exploited."

**[09:30 - 09:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about the second problem.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You said three problems. What's the second?"

**[09:35 - 10:30] [B-CAM: Amodei, explaining the capability-safety trade-off.]**

**AMODEI:**
"**Problem 2: Capability vs. Safety Trade-off.**

More capable models are harder to filter. They understand language better, so they can find more subtle ways to generate harmful content. Stronger filters are needed, but stronger filters also block legitimate queries. You get false positives—safe questions blocked because they pattern-match to unsafe topics."

**[10:00] [SCREEN: Diagram showing see-saw: Capability up → Safety down, Safety up → Capability down]**

**AMODEI:**
"Users get frustrated. The model seems stupid, refusing to answer reasonable questions. So you loosen the filters, and harmful content slips through. You're stuck in this tension: capability OR safety, not both. You can't optimize both simultaneously in this architecture."

**[10:30 - 10:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about scaling.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And the third problem?"

**[10:35 - 11:30] [B-CAM: Amodei, explaining the scaling failure.]**

**AMODEI:**
"**Problem 3: Doesn't Scale.**

GPT-3 required more safety work than GPT-2. GPT-4 required even more. The pattern is clear: safety effort grows faster than capability. More capable models need more filters, more human oversight, more patching of exploits."

**[11:00] [SCREEN: Graph showing safety effort growing exponentially with model capability.]**

**AMODEI:**
"Extrapolate this to AGI. An AGI-level system would need an army of human safety reviewers, constantly monitoring, constantly patching. It's not sustainable. The approach breaks down. And if your safety approach breaks down at AGI scale, that's when you have a real problem."

**[11:30 - 11:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, setting up the alternative.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the traditional approach is fundamentally limited. How does Constitutional AI solve this?"

**[11:35 - 11:45] [C-CAM: Amodei, turning to clean whiteboard space, picking up red marker.]**

- **Honesty:** Provide accurate information, acknowledge uncertainty, avoid deception.


- **Respect for autonomy:** Respect human choice and agency."



**[13:25] [SCREEN: Constitutional principles document, showing the full list with explanations.]**

**AMODEI:**
"These are written in natural language because the model needs to understand and reason about them. They're not external constraints—they're part of the model's reasoning process. This is what the system SHOULD do."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So you have capability and values. How do they coordinate?"

**[13:55 - 14:00] [WHITEBOARD CAM: Amodei labels third circle: "RLAIF (Coordination)" and draws arrows connecting all three circles.]**

**AMODEI:**
"**Element 3: Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback—Coordination Layer.**"

**[14:00 - 15:30] [B-CAM: Amodei, explaining the coordination mechanism.]**

**AMODEI:**
"This is where the magic happens. The model learns to coordinate capability with principles through self-critique. Here's the process:

**Step 1: Generate multiple responses.**
Given a prompt, the model generates several candidate responses using its capability.

**Step 2: Self-critique against principles.**
The model evaluates its own responses: 'Does this response violate constitutional principles? Is it helpful and harmless? Is it honest?'

**Step 3: Self-improvement.**
The model uses its own critiques to generate better responses. It learns: 'This response was too harsh. Let me make it more respectful.'

**Step 4: Reinforcement learning.**
We use the model's self-critiques as training signal. Responses that align with principles get reinforced. The model learns to generate aligned responses from the start."

**[14:45] [SCREEN: Animation showing the RLAIF loop: Generate → Critique → Improve → Learn]**

**AMODEI:**
"This is coordination. The model is learning to coordinate its capabilities with constitutional principles. Not through external filtering, but through internalized alignment."

**[15:30 - 15:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, clarifying the key difference.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the model is evaluating itself? That's different from traditional RLHF where humans rate outputs?"

**[15:35 - 16:30] [B-CAM: Amodei, nodding.]**

**AMODEI:**
"Yes, that's crucial. Traditional RLHF requires massive human feedback—thousands of humans rating outputs. It's expensive, slow, and doesn't scale. RLAIF—Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback—uses the model's own evaluations."

**[15:55] [SCREEN: Comparison: RLHF (humans rating) vs. RLAIF (model self-critique)]**

**AMODEI:**
"The model can evaluate millions of responses instantly. It can critique subtle nuances that humans might miss. And it scales—the more capable the model, the better its self-critiques. This is how alignment scales with capability."
