## ACT II: THE COORDINATION ARCHITECTURE OF SUPERHUMAN INTELLIGENCE

**Duration: 24 minutes**

### SEGMENT 5: AlphaGo's Three-Body Architecture (8 min)

**[10:30 - 10:40] [SCREEN: Clean, animated diagram of AlphaGo's architecture appears. Three main components clearly labeled.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"AlphaGo has three coordinating elements. Each one does something different. None of them alone could beat a world champion. But coordinated together, they created superhuman performance."

**[10:40 - 10:45] [WHITEBOARD CAM: Hassabis picks up a blue marker, draws first element.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Element 1: Policy Network—Intuition.**"

**[10:45 - 11:50] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining the policy network.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is a deep neural network trained on 30 million positions from human expert games. We showed it millions of examples of strong players making moves, and it learned the patterns. Given a board position, it outputs a probability distribution over all possible moves—which moves are most promising."

**[11:10] [SCREEN: Animation showing a Go board, the policy network analyzing it, and highlighting several promising moves with probability percentages.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is intuition—fast pattern recognition. It's like a Go master glancing at a board and immediately sensing 'these three moves feel right.' It doesn't calculate deeply; it recognizes patterns from experience. Fast, efficient, human-like."

**[11:50 - 11:55] [A-CAM: Interviewer, clarifying.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the policy network gives you candidate moves, but it doesn't tell you which one is actually best?"

**[11:55 - 12:05] [B-CAM: Hassabis, nodding.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Right. It narrows the search space—instead of considering hundreds of moves, you focus on the top 10 or 20. But you still need to evaluate them. That's where the value network comes in."

**[12:05 - 12:10] [WHITEBOARD CAM: Hassabis draws second element with a red marker.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Element 2: Value Network—Evaluation.**"

**[12:10 - 13:00] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining the value network.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is another neural network, but it's trained to evaluate positions, not moves. You give it a board position, and it outputs a single number: the probability that this position leads to a win. It's learned to judge 'is this position good or bad?' without calculating all possible continuations."

**[12:35] [SCREEN: Animation showing the value network analyzing a board position and outputting a win probability: 67%.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is strategic judgment. A Go master can look at a position and say 'I'm ahead' or 'I'm behind' without calculating every variation. The value network does the same thing—holistic evaluation based on learned patterns."

**[13:00 - 13:05] [A-CAM: Interviewer, seeing the coordination emerging.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So you have intuition suggesting moves and evaluation judging positions. But you said calculation was the third element?"

**[13:05 - 13:10] [WHITEBOARD CAM: Hassabis draws third element with a green marker, then connects all three with arrows.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Element 3: Monte Carlo Tree Search—Calculation.**"

**[13:10 - 14:20] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining MCTS.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is systematic exploration of possible futures. It's a planning algorithm that builds a tree of possibilities: 'If I play here, opponent plays there, then I play here...' It explores millions of these sequences, using the policy network to guide which branches to explore and the value network to evaluate the end positions."

**[13:40] [SCREEN: Animation showing a tree structure growing from the current position, with branches representing different move sequences. The tree is guided by the policy network and evaluated by the value network.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"This is deep calculation—computational lookahead. It's what computers do well: systematically exploring possibilities. But without guidance, it would waste time on bad moves. That's where coordination becomes critical."

**[14:20 - 14:25] [WHITEBOARD CAM: Hassabis draws arrows between the three elements, creating a coordination loop.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Here's the magic—the coordination:"

**[14:25 - 15:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, now animated, gesturing to show the coordination dance.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Step 1:** Policy network suggests promising moves based on intuition. 'These 10 moves feel right.'

**Step 2:** Monte Carlo Tree Search explores those moves deeply. 'Let me calculate what happens if we play these.'

**Step 3:** Value network evaluates the resulting positions. 'After this sequence, the position is 70% winning.'

**Step 4:** MCTS uses that evaluation to decide which branches to explore more. 'This line looks good, explore it deeper.'

**Step 5:** As MCTS explores, it discovers new insights. 'Actually, this unexpected move leads to a great position.'

**Step 6:** Those insights feed back to refine future searches. The system learns from its own exploration.

This is coordination. Intuition guides calculation. Calculation refines intuition. Evaluation focuses both. They're not separate modules—they're a coordinated system."

**[15:30 - 15:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, summarizing the key insight.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So each element compensates for the others' weaknesses?"

**[15:35 - 16:00] [B-CAM: Hassabis, emphatic.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Exactly. Intuition alone misses tactics. Calculation alone is too slow without guidance. Evaluation alone doesn't show the path. But coordinated together—intuition providing guidance, calculation providing depth, evaluation providing judgment—they create superhuman performance. The whole is greater than the sum of the parts."

### SEGMENT 8: AlphaFold—Coordination Generalizes Across Domains (5 min)

**[22:00 - 22:05] [A-CAM: Interviewer, transitioning to biology.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"AlphaGo and AlphaZero were about games. Then you took the coordination principle to biology—protein folding. How did that work?"

**[22:05 - 22:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining the protein folding challenge.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Protein folding is one of the grand challenges in biology. You have an amino acid sequence—a one-dimensional string of letters—and you need to predict how it folds into a three-dimensional structure. The structure determines the function, so if you can predict structure, you can understand biology and design drugs."

**[22:20] [SCREEN: Animation showing an amino acid sequence folding into a complex 3D protein structure.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"But it's incredibly hard. There are astronomical numbers of possible configurations. Scientists had been working on this for 50 years with limited success."

**[22:30 - 22:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, curious about the approach.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How did you apply coordination thinking to this problem?"

**[22:35 - 23:50] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining AlphaFold's architecture.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"We asked: what three sources of information need to coordinate to solve this? And we identified three bodies:

**Body 1: Physical Constraints (Chemistry/Thermodynamics)**
Proteins fold to minimize energy. Chemical bonds have preferred angles. Atoms can't overlap. Physics determines what's possible. But there are too many possibilities to search.

**Body 2: Evolutionary Information (Multiple Sequence Alignments)**
Related proteins have similar structures. Evolution preserves structural patterns. If two amino acids interact in one protein, they'll co-evolve in related proteins. This gives us clues about 3D contacts. But the evolutionary signal is noisy.

**Body 3: Geometric Reasoning (Spatial Structure, Attention)**
3D structure has geometric constraints. Residues nearby in sequence are often nearby in space. Attention mechanisms can learn which residues interact. But we need to learn what relationships matter."

**[23:20] [SCREEN: Three-body diagram showing Physics ↔ Evolution ↔ Geometry, with arrows indicating coordination.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"The breakthrough was coordinating all three. The neural network processes evolutionary information, applies geometric attention to learn spatial relationships, and stays constrained by physical plausibility. None sufficient alone—all necessary together."

**[23:50 - 23:55] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about results.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And it worked?"

**[23:55 - 24:40] [B-CAM: Hassabis, a proud smile.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"It solved protein folding. AlphaFold2, released in 2020, achieved atomic-level accuracy on most proteins. We predicted structures for 200 million proteins—essentially all known proteins in biology. It's revolutionizing drug discovery, disease understanding, and biological research. We won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for this in 2024."

**[24:20] [SCREEN: Gallery of protein structures predicted by AlphaFold, including complex multi-chain proteins.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"But the deeper lesson is this: the same coordination principle that worked for Go worked for protein folding. Intuition ↔ calculation ↔ evaluation became physics ↔ evolution ↔ geometry. Same architecture, different domain. Coordination generalizes."

**HASSABIS:**
"Based on what we've learned, AGI requires coordinating three types of intelligence:"

**[27:25 - 28:30] [B-CAM: Hassabis, explaining each element.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"**Element 1: Learning Systems**
The ability to acquire knowledge from data and experience. Current AI excels at this—deep learning, self-supervised learning, reinforcement learning. We can learn patterns from massive datasets. This is what AI CAN do well.

**Element 2: Reasoning Systems**
The ability to manipulate knowledge symbolically. Logical inference, causal reasoning, abstraction, analogy. Current AI struggles with this. We can pattern-match, but we can't reason flexibly. This is what AI NEEDS to improve.

**Element 3: Planning Systems**
The ability to apply knowledge to achieve goals. Long-horizon planning, goal decomposition, strategy, adaptation. Current AI barely does this. We can optimize locally, but we can't plan strategically. This is what AI MUST develop."

**[28:00] [SCREEN: Three-body diagram: Learning ↔ Reasoning ↔ Planning, with arrows showing coordination.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"AGI emerges when we coordinate all three. Learning without reasoning is pattern-matching without understanding. Reasoning without learning is brittle and can't adapt. Planning without learning or reasoning has no knowledge to work with. All three must coordinate."

**[28:30 - 28:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, clarifying.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So AGI isn't just scaling up current systems?"

**[28:35 - 29:00] [B-CAM: Hassabis, emphatic shake of head.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"No. Scaling helps—bigger models learn more patterns. But scale alone won't give you reasoning and planning. You need architectural innovation. You need coordination architectures that integrate learning, reasoning, and planning. That's the path to AGI."

### SEGMENT 12: Coordinated Hybrid Intelligence (2 min)

**[30:30 - 30:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about the vision.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've said the future isn't artificial general intelligence—it's coordinated hybrid intelligence. What do you mean?"

**[30:35 - 31:20] [B-CAM: Hassabis, articulating the vision.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Most people think AGI means building AI that replaces humans—machines that can do everything humans can do, but better. I don't think that's the right vision. The right vision is coordinated hybrid intelligence—systems where human expertise, AI capability, and domain knowledge coordinate to solve problems that neither could tackle alone."

**[31:00] [SCREEN: Diagram showing Human Expertise ↔ AI Capability ↔ Domain Knowledge, with arrows indicating coordination.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Think about AlphaFold. It doesn't replace biologists—it amplifies them. Biologists provide domain knowledge and research questions. AlphaFold provides prediction capability. Together, they're discovering new biology faster than either could alone. This is the future: coordination, not replacement."

**[31:20 - 31:25] [A-CAM: Interviewer, asking about implications.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Why does this matter? Why is coordination better than replacement?"

**[31:25 - 32:00] [B-CAM: Hassabis, serious and thoughtful.]**

**HASSABIS:**
"Because replacement is a zero-sum game. If AI replaces humans, humans lose agency, purpose, value. But coordination is positive-sum. If AI coordinates with humans, both become more capable. Humans keep agency and purpose while gaining superhuman tools. Society benefits from amplified intelligence rather than displaced intelligence. This is the path that works for everyone."
