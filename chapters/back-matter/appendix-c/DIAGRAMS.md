## Why Appendix C has no figures

Appendix C is a curated bibliography and reading guide: cited books, papers, courses, labs, communities, funding sources, and recommended reading paths across thirty-two sections. It is catalogue content, not argument. It does not advance causal or structural claims that a reader would recover more easily from a node-and-arrow diagram than from the list itself.

The arc_engine convention in this book reserves diagrams for genuine three-body or feedback-loop claims — places where a reader gains something by seeing variables, polarities, and loop sign made explicit. Bibliography entries do not meet that bar. A "knowledge lineage" sketch (Wiener → Ashby → Bateson → Pask) or a "reading path" flowchart would be decorative: it would restate what the prose already says in ordered list form, without exposing any feedback structure, reinforcing loop, or coordination asymmetry that the text itself hides.

The ruthless-cut test asks: *would removing this diagram make the chapter worse?* For every candidate considered in Appendix C the answer was no. Authoring zero diagrams here is the correct outcome, not a gap.

## Candidates considered and cut

| Candidate | Why cut |
|---|---|
| Cybernetics lineage (Wiener → Ashby → Bateson → Pask → Pangaro) | A genealogy, not a causal loop. A bullet list conveys the same information with less ceremony. |
| Cross-discipline coordination map (systems thinking ↔ AI ↔ consciousness ↔ HCI) | The prose in C.29 ("Interdisciplinary Connections") already does this work; a diagram would duplicate, not reveal. |
| Reader-path flowchart (Technical / Researcher / Leader / Explorer) | C.10 already enumerates four ordered paths. Turning them into a diagram adds visual weight without adding meaning. |
| 30-day → 90-day → lifetime practice ladder (C.20) | Linear progression, no feedback — a diagram would misrepresent it as a loop. |
| Knowware community flywheel | Would be an aspirational marketing diagram, not a grounded causal claim from the text. |
