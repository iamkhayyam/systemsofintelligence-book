# Dan Doctoroff: Urban Governance Coordination

**Former CEO Sidewalk Labs, Former Deputy Mayor NYC**

**Interview Duration**: 38-42 minutes  
**Location**: Doctoroff's office, New York City  
**Visual Elements**: NYC transformation photos, Sidewalk Labs Quayside plans, governance diagrams

---

## Pre-Interview Context

**Expertise**: Urban innovation, public-private coordination, city transformation, policy and governance, economic development

**Key Achievements**:
- Deputy Mayor of NYC under Bloomberg (2002-2007): Led $30B+ in infrastructure investment
- CEO of Sidewalk Labs (2015-2021): Attempted first coordination-based urban development at scale
- Led NYC's 2012 Olympic bid: Created framework for urban transformation
- Current: Chairman of Sidewalk Infrastructure Partners

**Coordination Insight**: Urban transformation requires coordinating three bodies that usually work against each other: government (long-term, accountable, slow), private sector (short-term, innovative, fast), and civil society (experiential, diverse, powerful). Most urban projects fail by optimizing any two while ignoring the third.

**Why This Interview Matters**: Doctoroff has worked on cities from all three perspectives—government, business, and now philanthropy. He's seen urban transformation succeed and fail from every angle. His Sidewalk Labs experiment was the most ambitious attempt to build coordination infrastructure for urban development, and its failure revealed exactly why three-body coordination is so difficult at political scale.

## Opening: The Three-Body Urban Challenge (6 minutes)

**[Interviewer]**: Dan, you've worked on cities from three perspectives: government as Deputy Mayor of New York, private sector as CEO of Sidewalk Labs, and now philanthropy. That's a rare vantage point. What have you learned about why urban transformation is so hard?

**[Doctoroff]**: Urban transformation fails when you optimize any two bodies while ignoring the third. And we do this constantly.

I've seen it from every angle. In government, I watched brilliant public projects fail because they ignored market realities or community needs. At Sidewalk Labs, I watched innovative private projects fail because they lacked democratic legitimacy. And now, I watch community movements fail because they lack resources and expertise to scale.

The pattern is universal: optimize two, ignore one, fail.

**[Interviewer]**: Walk us through these three bodies and why they work against each other.

**[Doctoroff]**: Think about the fundamental tensions:

**Government** thinks in decades, is accountable to voters, controls land use and regulation. But government is slow, risk-averse, politically constrained. Every decision requires consensus across agencies, approval from elected officials, compliance with regulations. By the time government acts, the problem has often changed.

**Private sector** thinks in quarters, is accountable to shareholders, controls capital and innovation. But private sector is fast, risk-taking, market-driven. Companies can pivot quickly, deploy resources rapidly, innovate without permission. But they lack democratic legitimacy and long-term thinking.

**Civil society**—citizens, communities, advocacy groups—has lived experience, local knowledge, democratic voice. They know what works on the ground, what serves community needs, what creates neighborhood vitality. But they lack resources, technical expertise, and often struggle to coordinate at scale.

These three bodies have fundamentally different timescales, accountability structures, and incentive systems. They naturally work against each other.

**[Interviewer]**: Give us an example of this failure pattern.

**[Doctoroff]**: The Big Dig in Boston. Perfect example of government-only optimization.

Twenty-two billion dollars. Fifteen years. Solved 1960s traffic problems in 2007. By the time it was finished, traffic patterns had completely changed. The city had evolved. New problems had emerged.

Why? Because government optimized infrastructure without coordinating with market dynamics—how businesses and residents were actually evolving—or community needs—what neighborhoods actually wanted.

The result: technically impressive, functionally obsolete, politically exhausting.

Or take Hudson Yards in New York. Perfect example of private-only optimization.

Twenty-five billion dollars. Architectural marvel. Fastest-growing neighborhood in the city. And... a social desert. Beautiful buildings, no community. Serves tourists and wealthy residents, doesn't serve the city.

Why? Because private sector optimized return on investment without coordinating with government's long-term vision or community's social needs.

The result: economically successful, socially hollow.

**[Interviewer]**: So you need all three?

**[Doctoroff]**: You need all three coordinating. Not just present—coordinating. That's the challenge I spent my career trying to solve.

## Act I: The Coordination Bodies (14 minutes)

**[Interviewer]**: Let's go deeper into each body and understand why coordination is so difficult. Start with government—what can only government do?

**[Doctoroff]**: Government has three unique powers: long-term thinking, public accountability, and regulatory authority.

**Long-term thinking**: Government can think in generations. Infrastructure that pays off over fifty years. Social investments that create value over decades. Environmental protection that serves future citizens.

Private sector can't do this—shareholders demand quarterly returns. Communities can advocate for it, but can't implement it.

**Public accountability**: Government represents all citizens, not just customers or stakeholders. It must balance competing interests—economic growth and environmental protection, development and preservation, efficiency and equity.

Private sector serves customers and shareholders. Communities represent members. Only government serves everyone.

**Regulatory authority**: Government controls land use, building codes, environmental standards, safety regulations. It can coordinate private action toward public goals through regulation.

But here's the problem: these strengths become weaknesses.

**[Interviewer]**: How so?

**[Doctoroff]**: Long-term thinking becomes paralysis. Every decision requires extensive planning, environmental review, community input, political approval. By the time you act, conditions have changed.

Public accountability becomes risk aversion. Politicians can't afford failure. So they choose safe, proven approaches over innovation. They optimize for not losing rather than for winning.

Regulatory authority becomes bureaucracy. Every good regulation spawns ten bad ones. Every safety rule creates ten compliance requirements. The system becomes so complex that only large, established players can navigate it.

This is why government-led urban development is often slow, expensive, and misaligned with actual needs.

**[Interviewer]**: What about private sector—what can only business do?

**[Doctoroff]**: Private sector has three unique capabilities: innovation speed, capital access, and risk tolerance.

**Innovation speed**: Companies can experiment, fail, pivot, and scale rapidly. No need for public approval or political consensus. If something works, scale it. If it fails, try something else.

**Capital access**: Private markets can deploy billions quickly. Infrastructure, technology, development—if there's a return, capital flows.

**Risk tolerance**: Companies can take risks that government can't. Try unproven technologies. Test new business models. Fail and learn.

These are superpowers for urban innovation. But they come with fatal flaws.

**[Interviewer]**: Which are?

**[Doctoroff]**: Short-term thinking. Quarterly earnings pressure means you optimize for immediate returns, not long-term value. You build what's profitable now, not what the city needs in twenty years.

Narrow accountability. You serve customers and shareholders, not citizens. You optimize for market segments that can pay, not for community needs that can't.

No democratic legitimacy. You can innovate rapidly, but if citizens don't trust you, if communities don't want you, if government doesn't support you—you fail.

This is why private-led urban development often creates economic value but social hollowness.

**[Interviewer]**: And civil society—what can only communities do?

**[Doctoroff]**: Communities have three irreplaceable assets: lived experience, local knowledge, and democratic voice.

**Lived experience**: People who actually live in neighborhoods know what works and what doesn't. They know which streets feel safe, which parks get used, which businesses serve local needs. This knowledge is invisible to planners and developers.

**Local knowledge**: Communities understand social dynamics, cultural patterns, historical context. They know why certain interventions failed in the past, what values matter to residents, what changes will be embraced or resisted.

**Democratic voice**: In a democracy, community consent is essential. Projects that lack community support fail politically, even if they're technically sound and economically viable.

But communities face constraints.

**[Interviewer]**: Such as?

**[Doctoroff]**: Limited resources. Community groups can't deploy billions in capital. They can't hire armies of engineers and planners. They can't build infrastructure at scale.

Limited technical expertise. Understanding what's technically feasible, what regulatory frameworks allow, what economic models work—this requires specialized knowledge.

Limited coordination capacity. Communities are diverse, with competing interests and perspectives. Building consensus is hard. Coordinating action at scale is harder.

This is why community-led urban development often has authenticity but lacks scale.

**[Interviewer]**: So each body has superpowers and fatal flaws. How do you coordinate them?

**[Doctoroff]**: That was the Sidewalk Labs experiment. And it's why our failure is so instructive.

## Act II: The Sidewalk Labs Experiment (12 minutes)

**[Interviewer]**: Quayside in Toronto was designed to coordinate all three bodies—government, private sector, civil society. Walk us through the coordination architecture.

**[Doctoroff]**: Quayside was supposed to be the first truly coordinated urban development at scale. We spent three years designing the coordination infrastructure.

The project: twelve acres of Toronto waterfront. Not just buildings—a new model for urban development that coordinated government authority, private innovation, and citizen voice.

**Government coordination**: Waterfront Toronto, a public agency, provided land and regulatory framework. They represented long-term public interest and democratic accountability.

Their role: Set goals (housing affordability, carbon neutrality, job creation). Approve plans. Ensure public benefit. Maintain democratic oversight.

**Private coordination**: Sidewalk Labs brought capital, technology, and innovation speed.

Our role: Design solutions. Deploy technology. Take financial risk. Innovate without permission. Move fast.

**Civil society coordination**: Citizens shaped priorities, tested ideas, demanded accountability.

Their role: Define what "livable" means. Challenge assumptions. Ensure community benefit. Provide democratic legitimacy.

The coordination architecture had three innovations:

**[Interviewer]**: Start with the civic data trust—what was that?

**[Doctoroff]**: This was our answer to the question: "Who controls urban data?"

Traditional smart city: private company collects data, owns it, monetizes it. Citizens have no control. This creates surveillance capitalism.

Our alternative: civic data trust—an independent entity that governs urban data on behalf of citizens.

**How it worked**: Sidewalk Labs collects data through sensors and systems. But we don't own it. The trust owns it. Citizens, through the trust, decide:
- What data gets collected
- How it gets used
- Who can access it
- How privacy is protected

This coordinated private capability (data collection and analysis) with public accountability (democratic governance) with citizen control (direct voice in decisions).

**[Interviewer]**: Outcome-based procurement?

**[Doctoroff]**: Traditional procurement: government specifies inputs. "Build this road, install these sensors, deploy this system." You're paid for delivering what's specified, not for achieving outcomes.

The problem: optimizes for compliance, not results. You can deliver everything specified and still fail to achieve the goal.

Our alternative: outcome-based procurement. Government specifies outcomes: "Reduce carbon emissions 80%, create affordable housing for 40% of residents, generate X jobs."

How you achieve it is up to you. You're paid based on results, not inputs.

This coordinated government goals (public benefit) with private innovation (freedom to experiment) with citizen priorities (measurable outcomes).

**[Interviewer]**: And iterative development?

**[Doctoroff]**: Traditional development: plan everything upfront, build it all, hope it works. If it doesn't, too late.

Our alternative: pilot, learn, adapt—with all three bodies participating.

Start small. Test ideas. Measure results. Get feedback from residents. Adjust based on data. Scale what works. Kill what doesn't.

This coordinated government oversight (approval at each stage) with private agility (rapid iteration) with citizen participation (continuous feedback).

**[Interviewer]**: This sounds brilliant. So why did it fail?

**[Doctoroff]**: Because we couldn't maintain three-body coordination at political scale.

Here's what happened:

**Government shifted priorities**: New political leadership had different goals. The original vision—innovation-driven development—gave way to concerns about privatization and control.

**Private capital got nervous**: As timelines extended and political support wavered, investors worried about returns. The business case depended on coordination holding together.

**Community trust eroded**: Despite extensive engagement, concerns about surveillance, gentrification, and corporate control grew. We couldn't build trust fast enough.

When one body's commitment wavered, the coordination collapsed. You can't have three-body coordination with only two bodies committed.

**[Interviewer]**: What did you learn?

**[Doctoroff]**: That coordination architecture is necessary but not sufficient. You also need coordination maintenance.

We designed beautiful coordination mechanisms—civic data trust, outcome-based procurement, iterative development. But we didn't design for coordination resilience.

When political leadership changed, we had no mechanism to maintain government commitment.

When community concerns arose, we had no process to rebuild trust at the speed required.

When private investors got nervous, we had no structure to align short-term pressures with long-term coordination.

The lesson: coordination infrastructure must be designed for political and social turbulence, not just technical elegance.

## Act III: The Future of Urban Coordination (6 minutes)

**[Interviewer]**: Despite Quayside's failure, you still believe urban transformation requires three-body coordination. What needs to change?

**[Doctoroff]**: We need to design coordination infrastructure that survives political cycles, maintains trust through turbulence, and aligns incentives across different timescales.

Three principles:

**First: Embedded coordination structures**. Don't rely on individual relationships or political alignment. Build coordination into governance structures that persist through leadership changes.

Example: Independent authorities with representation from all three bodies. Board seats for government, private sector, and civil society. Decisions require coordination, not consensus.

**Second: Progressive trust building**. Don't try to build everything at once. Start with small wins that demonstrate coordination value. Build trust through results, not promises.

Example: Pilot projects that show coordination creating value for all three bodies. Government sees public benefit. Private sector sees viable returns. Citizens see community improvement.

**Third: Flexible commitment structures**. Don't lock everyone into rigid long-term plans. Create coordination frameworks that allow adaptation while maintaining alignment.

Example: Outcome-based agreements with milestone reviews. Parties commit to coordination process and outcomes, not specific solutions. This allows pivoting when conditions change.

**[Interviewer]**: How do you build coordination infrastructure that enables government authority, private innovation, and citizen voice to work together?

**[Doctoroff]**: You need coordination platforms, not just projects.

Traditional urban development: one-off projects. Each project negotiates coordination from scratch. No learning across projects. No coordination infrastructure that persists.

Future urban development: coordination platforms that enable multiple projects.

The platform provides:
- **Governance structures** that ensure all three bodies have voice
- **Data infrastructure** that enables coordination through transparency
- **Decision processes** that balance speed with accountability
- **Feedback mechanisms** that enable continuous learning

Projects use the platform. They don't reinvent coordination each time.

**[Interviewer]**: What becomes possible when these three bodies coordinate through emergence rather than compromise?

**[Doctoroff]**: Urban transformation that serves all three simultaneously—not through trade-offs, but through emergence.

**Government** gets long-term public benefit without slow bureaucracy.

**Private sector** gets viable returns without short-term optimization.

**Citizens** get community value without sacrificing voice.

This doesn't happen through compromise—everyone giving up something. It happens through emergence—coordination creating value that none could create alone.

Example: Affordable housing that's also financially viable and community-desired. Traditional approach: trade-offs. Mandate affordability (government), sacrifice returns (private), accept design compromises (community).

Coordination approach: innovative construction methods (private) + land use flexibility (government) + community design input (citizens) = affordable housing that's financially viable and community-embraced.

The value emerges from coordination.

## Closing: Lessons from the Arena (4 minutes)

**[Interviewer]**: You've been in government, business, and now philanthropy. What's the most important thing each sector misunderstands about the others?

**[Doctoroff]**: **Government misunderstands** that private sector isn't evil—it's just optimizing for different goals. When you treat business as the enemy, you lose access to innovation and capital.

**Private sector misunderstands** that government isn't stupid—it's just optimizing for different values. When you treat regulation as obstruction, you lose democratic legitimacy.

**Civil society misunderstands** that both government and business face real constraints. When you demand perfection, you prevent progress.

These misunderstandings prevent coordination. Each body sees the others as obstacles rather than coordination partners.

**[Interviewer]**: If you could redesign urban governance from scratch with coordination thinking, what would be different?

**[Doctoroff]**: I'd build coordination infrastructure into governance itself.

Instead of government, business, and community as separate actors that occasionally interact, I'd create governance structures where coordination is the default.

**Coordination boards** with mandatory representation from all three bodies. Not advisory—decision-making.

**Coordination budgets** that fund projects requiring all three bodies. If you can't demonstrate coordination, you don't get funding.

**Coordination metrics** that measure how well the three bodies work together, not just individual outcomes.

**Coordination platforms** that make it easy for the three bodies to coordinate, not hard.

The goal: make coordination the path of least resistance, not the path of most resistance.

Right now, our governance structures make it easier to work in silos than to coordinate. We need to flip that.

**[Interviewer]**: Final thought: what gives you hope?

**[Doctoroff]**: That we're learning. Quayside failed, but we learned why. Other cities are learning from our mistakes. The next generation of urban leaders understands coordination in ways my generation didn't.

The future of cities depends on coordinating government authority, private innovation, and citizen voice. We're still learning how. But we're learning.

And that's how progress happens: not through perfect plans, but through coordinated learning.

## Key Insights

**The Three Bodies of Urban Transformation**:
- **Government**: Long-term thinking, public accountability, regulatory authority (slow, risk-averse, politically constrained)
- **Private Sector**: Innovation speed, capital access, risk tolerance (short-term, narrow accountability, lacks legitimacy)
- **Civil Society**: Lived experience, local knowledge, democratic voice (limited resources, expertise, coordination capacity)

**Why Single-Body Optimization Fails**:
- **Government-only** (Big Dig): Slow, expensive, misaligned with needs by completion
- **Private-only** (Hudson Yards): Economically successful, socially hollow
- **Community-only**: Authentic but lacks resources and expertise to scale

**Sidewalk Labs Coordination Innovations**:
- **Civic Data Trust**: Citizens control data governance, not government or corporations
- **Outcome-Based Procurement**: Pay for results (affordability, carbon reduction), not inputs
- **Iterative Development**: Pilot, learn, adapt with all three bodies participating

**Why Quayside Failed**:
- Government priorities shifted with political leadership
- Private capital nervous about extended timelines and wavering support
- Community trust eroded faster than it could be rebuilt
- Coordination infrastructure wasn't designed for political turbulence

**Lessons for Coordination at Scale**:
- **Embedded structures**: Build coordination into governance, not relationships
- **Progressive trust**: Start small, demonstrate value, build through results
- **Flexible commitment**: Commit to coordination process and outcomes, not rigid plans

**Future of Urban Coordination**:
- Coordination platforms that enable multiple projects
- Governance structures ensuring all three bodies have voice
- Decision processes balancing speed with accountability
- Emergence creating value none could create alone
