## ACT I: THE TURNAROUND WAS A COORDINATION PROBLEM, NOT A TECHNOLOGY PROBLEM

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Objective:** Establish that AMD's comeback was architected as a coordination solution — and that this framing reveals something universal about engineering leadership

### Opening [0:45-3:00]

**[00:45] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Dr. Su, when you took over as CEO in 2014, AMD was in genuine trouble. The conventional analysis was: AMD's chips aren't good enough. But you've described what you found as something different — a strategic problem, not a technical one. What did you actually see?

**[01:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
When I came in, the engineers were excellent. The technology was capable. What was broken was the coordination between what we were building and why we were building it.

**[01:20] [B-CAM] SU:**
We had CPU teams and GPU teams working in parallel, both trying to compete with their respective dominant competitors, both resource-constrained, both making decisions in isolation. The CPU team was optimizing against Intel. The GPU team was optimizing against Nvidia. Neither was asking: what does the system need? What does the customer's workload actually require? What are the three things that have to work together for this to be valuable?

**[01:50] [B-CAM] SU:**
That's a coordination failure. Not a technology failure. And coordination failures have a different fix than technology failures. You don't solve them by spending more on R&D. You solve them by changing what R&D is pointed at.

**[02:00] [Visual: The org chart of AMD 2014 — siloed divisions. Then the post-Zen structure — integrated roadmap, shared architecture decisions.]**

**[02:05] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Can you be specific about what that looked like day to day? When you say the coordination was broken, what were the symptoms an engineer inside AMD would have felt?

**[02:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Slight pause, choosing words carefully] The clearest symptom was that our roadmaps didn't talk to each other. The CPU roadmap was built to answer Intel. The GPU roadmap was built to answer Nvidia. Each roadmap made sense on its own. But nobody was asking: when a customer buys a server, what do they actually need from both of these things working together?

**[02:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
We had teams that were very good at their individual jobs but were not designing for what the market needed as a system. And in semiconductors, the penalty for that kind of misalignment is brutal, because you commit to a design years before it ships. If the coordination is wrong at the architecture stage, no amount of execution can fix it later. The silicon is the silicon.

### Segment 1: The Zen Lesson [3:00-7:00]

**[03:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
The Zen architecture was the turning point. What made it different from what AMD had been doing before?

**[03:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
So, to understand Zen, you have to understand what we were doing before Zen. The prior architectures were what I would call locally optimized designs. Better cache here, better branch predictor there, faster clock somewhere else. Each decision was reasonable in isolation. Each subsystem was good on its own metrics. But the overall chip was not coherent. It was not designed as a system.

**[03:45] [B-CAM] SU:**
Zen was fundamentally different because we started from the coordination problem, not from the component problem. The question was not: how do we make a faster core? The question was: how do we make CPU cores, cache hierarchy, and memory bandwidth work together as a coordinated system? Those are very different design questions, and they lead to very different architectures.

**[04:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And that led to the chiplet approach, which was genuinely novel at the time. Can you walk through how the coordination thinking led to that specific architectural choice?

**[04:20] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Leans forward slightly] The traditional approach in our industry was the monolithic die. You put everything on one large piece of silicon. One manufacturing process, one die, one set of trade-offs. Intel did this. We did this. Everyone did this.

**[04:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
The problem with monolithic dies is that they force every component to make the same compromise. Your CPU cores, your I/O, your memory controller — they all have to be on the same process node. But a CPU core benefits enormously from being on the latest, smallest process node. An I/O controller does not. It works perfectly well on a mature node that costs a fraction of the price.

**[05:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
The monolithic approach optimizes for one thing: integration simplicity. The chiplet approach optimizes for coordination between different things. You build the CPU cores on the newest, most advanced node. You build the I/O die on a mature, cost-effective node. And then you connect them with a high-bandwidth interconnect — what we call Infinity Fabric.

**[05:30] [Visual: Zen chiplet architecture diagram — the dies, the Infinity Fabric interconnect, the coordination made physical in silicon.]**

**[05:35] [B-CAM] SU:**
Each chiplet is one body. The interconnect is what coordinates between them. And the adjacent chiplet is the third body. It is literally a three-body architecture in silicon. The dies have to coordinate their data flow, their power management, their timing — all through that shared fabric.

**[05:55] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the manufacturing economics of this were significant.

**[06:00] [B-CAM] SU:**
The economics were transformative. A large monolithic die has a yield problem — the bigger the die, the higher the chance of a defect landing on it. With chiplets, each individual die is small, so yields are high. If one chiplet has a defect, you discard that one chiplet, not an entire massive die. The cost per good unit drops substantially.

**[06:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
But the real win was not cost. The real win was that we could coordinate the best of each process node rather than compromising everything to a single node. We could move the CPU cores to the next process node without redesigning the I/O. We could scale the core count by adding more chiplets without redesigning the architecture. That flexibility — that ability to evolve different parts of the system at different rates — that is what coordination gives you.

**[06:50] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the market results bore this out.

**[06:55] [B-CAM] SU:**
Zen 1 made us competitive with Intel for the first time in years. Zen 2 gave us performance leadership in many workloads. Zen 3 gave us clear leadership. And this was not because we suddenly hired better engineers. Our engineers were always excellent. What changed was that the coordination architecture unlocked what they already had. It let them do their best work in a framework that added up to something coherent at the system level.

### Segment 2: The Xilinx Move [7:00-10:00]

**[07:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
The $49 billion Xilinx acquisition in 2022 was the largest in semiconductor history at the time. Xilinx makes FPGAs — field-programmable gate arrays — which are a completely different product category. Why?

**[07:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
The straightforward answer is that FPGAs fill a gap in our portfolio. But the real answer is about coordination architecture at the company level.

**[07:35] [B-CAM] SU:**
Think about what we had before Xilinx. We had CPUs — general-purpose sequential processing. Very good at a broad range of tasks. We had GPUs — massively parallel processing. Excellent at matrix operations, at AI training, at graphics. But the computing landscape is not just those two things.

**[07:55] [B-CAM] SU:**
An FPGA is reconfigurable hardware. It can be programmed after manufacture to behave like a specialized accelerator for a specific workload. It is not fixed-function like a CPU or GPU. It adapts to the context of deployment. That makes it the third body in the compute architecture. The CPU handles general computation. The GPU handles parallel workloads. The FPGA adapts to the specific, changing needs of the deployment.

**[08:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So the acquisition was about completing a three-body architecture at the corporate level?

**[08:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
Exactly. We did not acquire Xilinx to enter the FPGA market. We acquired Xilinx to complete a coordination architecture. CPU, GPU, and adaptive compute — each one necessary, none sufficient alone. Together, they can address virtually any computing workload. And more importantly, they can coordinate with each other in ways that no single product type can achieve alone.

**[08:50] [B-CAM] SU:**
This mattered enormously for AI. Training a model is a GPU-dominated, known workload. But running that model in production — inference — is diverse, variable, often latency-sensitive. Sometimes you need the GPU. Sometimes you need the CPU. Sometimes you need something that can be reconfigured for the specific model architecture. The FPGA coordinates between the trained model and its specific deployment context.

**[09:00] [Visual: The AMD product portfolio after Xilinx — CPU, GPU, FPGA in one company. The three-body compute architecture made corporate.]**

**[09:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You saw this before the AI boom made it obvious.

**[09:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Measured] We saw the trajectory. The Xilinx deal closed in early 2022, before ChatGPT, before the public explosion of interest in large language models. But the underlying trend — that AI workloads would become the dominant driver of compute demand, and that those workloads would require diverse, coordinated hardware — that was already clear from what our data center customers were telling us. We built the portfolio for where the market was going, not where it was.

## ACT II: THE THREE-BODY ARCHITECTURE OF AI COMPUTING

**Duration:** 18 minutes
**Objective:** Su's framework for AI computing infrastructure — and why the hardware problem is always a coordination problem

### Segment 3: The MI300X and the Coordination of Memory [10:00-16:00]

**[10:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
The MI300X is your flagship AI accelerator. It is unusual because it puts CPU, GPU, and memory on a single package — what is called a unified memory architecture. Why does that matter?

**[10:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
Let me walk you through the problem it solves. In a traditional AI training setup, you have a GPU with its own memory, a CPU with its own memory, and data that has to move between them. Every time data moves from the CPU's memory to the GPU's memory — or back — you pay a cost. Latency. Energy. Bandwidth.

**[10:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
People tend to think the bottleneck in AI computing is raw compute power — how many floating-point operations per second can your GPU do. And for certain workloads, that is true. But for an increasing number of the most important workloads, the bottleneck is not compute at all. It is the coordination between compute and memory. It is how fast you can get data to the place where it needs to be processed.

**[11:05] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
The memory wall.

**[11:08] [B-CAM] SU:**
That is the common term, yes. And it has been a problem in computing for decades. But it has become particularly acute with large language models, because these models are enormous. A 70-billion parameter model requires roughly 140 gigabytes just to store the weights in 16-bit precision. That is before you account for the activations, the attention cache, the optimizer states during training.

**[11:35] [B-CAM] SU:**
In a traditional multi-GPU setup, you have to split that model across multiple GPUs, each with its own memory. And then those GPUs have to communicate with each other to coordinate the computation. Every communication step is overhead. Every data transfer is latency. The system spends a significant fraction of its time just moving data around, not doing useful computation.

**[12:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the MI300X addresses this how?

**[12:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
The MI300X puts CPU and GPU compute on the same package with 192 gigabytes of unified high-bandwidth memory. The CPU and GPU share the same memory pool. There is no data transfer between separate CPU memory and GPU memory, because they are the same memory.

**[12:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
That 70-billion parameter model I mentioned? It fits entirely in the memory of a single MI300X. No model partitioning across devices. No cross-device communication overhead. The compute and the data are co-located, coordinated within a single package. The three-body problem — GPU, data, CPU — does not disappear. But it gets solved by making memory a shared coordination layer rather than a boundary between separate systems.

**[12:55] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You are describing a principle that goes beyond the specifics of this one chip.

**[13:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
Correct. The principle is this: every AI workload has three bodies. The model — what you are computing. The compute — the hardware doing the work. And the memory — where the data lives. If you optimize any two of those without coordinating the third, you will create a bottleneck at the uncoordinated boundary.

**[13:30] [B-CAM] SU:**
The history of AI hardware is, in many ways, a history of two-body optimizations running into three-body walls. People built faster GPUs — optimized the compute body. Then they discovered the memory body was the bottleneck. People added more memory — optimized the memory body. Then they discovered the communication between compute and memory was the bottleneck. The architectural progress, the real progress, is always in recognizing and resolving the coordination constraint. Not in making any single body faster in isolation.

**[14:00] [Visual: Memory bandwidth comparison — traditional multi-GPU setup vs. MI300X unified memory. The coordination overhead made visible as latency and energy.]**

**[14:05] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
When you sit down to design a next-generation chip, is that the framing? Where is the coordination constraint?

**[14:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Nods] That is the first question. Not: how do we add more compute? Not: how do we get to the next process node? Those matter. But the first question is always: for the workloads our customers are running two to three years from now, where will the coordination constraint be? What three things will need to work together, and where will the bottleneck between them sit? That determines the architecture. Everything else follows from that.

### Segment 4: The Ecosystem Question [16:00-22:00]

**[16:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Nvidia's dominance in AI computing is not just about hardware. It is about the CUDA software ecosystem — twenty years of developer investment. How do you coordinate with that reality?

**[16:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
This is one of the most important strategic questions in our industry, and I want to be precise about it.

**[16:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
CUDA is Nvidia's proprietary programming model for GPU computing. Over twenty years, they have built an enormous ecosystem around it. Libraries, tools, frameworks, tutorials, community expertise. Every major AI framework — PyTorch, TensorFlow — is deeply optimized for CUDA. Practically every AI researcher has been trained on CUDA. The switching cost for an individual developer or a research lab is enormous.

**[16:55] [B-CAM] SU:**
Now, a lot of people look at that and say: that is an insurmountable moat. You cannot compete with twenty years of ecosystem development. And if you frame it as a competition problem — AMD trying to out-CUDA Nvidia — they are probably right. We would lose that competition. You do not win by trying to replicate someone else's two-decade head start.

**[17:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So what is the alternative framing?

**[17:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
The coordination framing. We do not need to replace CUDA. We need to coordinate with the existing ecosystem in a way that lowers the barrier for developers to use AMD hardware.

**[17:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
Our platform is called ROCm. It is open source. And one of its key features is a tool called HIP — Heterogeneous-compute Interface for Portability — which is essentially a translation layer. Code written for CUDA can be converted to run on AMD hardware with minimal changes. In many cases, it is an automated process.

**[18:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
The strategic insight is: do not fight the ecosystem. Coordinate with it. The ecosystem exists. The code exists. The developer knowledge exists. Build a bridge, not a competing island.

**[18:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
There is a three-body pattern here as well.

**[18:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
There is. Hardware is one body. The developer ecosystem is the second body. And the deployment context — the specific customer, the specific workload, the specific cost and performance requirements — is the third body.

**[18:45] [B-CAM] SU:**
Nvidia optimized the first two bodies very tightly: their hardware and their ecosystem. That is a powerful two-body optimization. Our approach is to coordinate our hardware with the existing ecosystem through an open translation layer, and then to coordinate that with the specific deployment context. Different customers have different needs. Some need the absolute highest performance regardless of cost. Some need the best performance per dollar. Some need the best performance per watt. Some need vendor diversification for supply chain resilience.

**[19:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
A single-vendor ecosystem is a single-body optimization from the customer's perspective. It is powerful but brittle. Diversification across hardware platforms creates the kind of coordination that produces resilience. And our hyperscale customers understand this very well. They are investing in multiple hardware platforms — our products, custom silicon, different architectures — because they know that diversity of the hardware layer is a strategic necessity.

**[19:45] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You have said publicly that the AI chip market is large enough for multiple winners. That is not typically how semiconductor competition is discussed.

**[19:55] [B-CAM] SU:**
Because most people discuss it as a competition problem. Who has the best chip? Who wins the benchmark? And those things matter. We work very hard to have the best performance. But the AI compute demand is growing so fast that the real question is not who wins the market — it is whether the entire supply chain can coordinate to meet the demand. The world needs more AI compute than any single company can provide. That is not a competition argument. It is a coordination argument.

**[20:00] [Visual: The AI chip ecosystem map — Nvidia, AMD, custom silicon (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Apple Neural Engine) — all coordinating within the larger software and deployment context.]**

**[20:25] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
How do you see the open-source software ecosystem playing into this?

**[20:30] [B-CAM] SU:**
Open source is, in many ways, the natural coordination mechanism for this industry. PyTorch is open source. The model architectures are largely open. The training techniques are published. When the software layer is open, the hardware layer can compete on merit — performance, efficiency, cost — rather than on ecosystem lock-in.

**[20:55] [B-CAM] SU:**
Our investment in ROCm as an open-source platform is part of this. We want the software ecosystem to be a coordination layer that works across hardware, not a barrier that locks developers into one vendor. That serves our customers. And honestly, it serves the entire industry, because it means hardware progress anywhere benefits everyone.

### Segment 5: The Power Problem [22:00-28:00]

**[22:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
AI data centers are consuming extraordinary amounts of power. The constraint on AI development may not be chips — it may be electricity. How does that change the hardware architecture problem?

**[22:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
It changes it fundamentally. For most of the history of semiconductor design, the primary metric was performance. How fast can the chip go? How many operations per second? Power was a consideration, but it was secondary. You designed for performance first and managed power consumption as a constraint.

**[22:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
That era is ending. The AI workloads being deployed today consume megawatts per cluster. The data centers being built to run these workloads are being sited based on access to power infrastructure, not based on proximity to customers or talent. Power is becoming the binding constraint on AI infrastructure, and that means power is becoming the binding constraint on AI progress.

**[23:05] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
So the optimization target has shifted.

**[23:10] [B-CAM] SU:**
It has. The right metric is no longer raw performance. It is not even performance per watt, though that is closer. The real metric is performance per watt per dollar per unit of physical infrastructure. How much useful AI computation can you deliver within a fixed power envelope, at a given cost, in a given amount of data center space?

**[23:35] [B-CAM] SU:**
That is a coordination optimization, not a single-variable optimization. You are coordinating compute capability with energy availability, with cooling capacity, with cost constraints, with physical space. And different deployment contexts weight these factors differently. An edge deployment has a very different power budget than a hyperscale data center. An inference workload has very different power characteristics than a training workload.

**[24:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
How does this affect the chip design itself?

**[24:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
In several concrete ways. First, the chiplet architecture helps here. Because we can mix process nodes — the latest node for the cores that benefit from it, mature nodes for the components that do not — we can optimize the overall power efficiency of the package in ways that a monolithic design cannot.

**[24:30] [B-CAM] SU:**
Second, each generation of Zen has delivered significant performance-per-watt improvements. Not just performance improvements — performance-per-watt improvements. That distinction matters. Zen 4 was not just faster than Zen 3; it delivered more computation for the same energy budget. And Zen 5 continues this trajectory. We measure our architecture teams on efficiency, not just speed.

**[25:00] [B-CAM] SU:**
Third, the MI300X was designed from the beginning with power efficiency at scale as a primary design target. When you consolidate what would have been a multi-GPU setup into a single unified package, you eliminate all the power consumed by cross-device communication. That power savings compounds at scale. A thousand-GPU cluster built with more efficient devices saves megawatts of power — enough to run additional computation, or enough to reduce operating cost.

**[25:30] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You are describing a future where energy infrastructure shapes chip architecture more than Moore's Law does.

**[25:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Pauses, then nods] That is not quite how I would put it, but the direction is right. Moore's Law is about transistor density. It is about what you can fit on a chip. That still matters. But the question of what you should put on that chip — how you use those transistors — is increasingly answered by the energy constraint.

**[26:00] [Visual: Power consumption curves for AI training clusters. The inflection point where energy became the constraint. The three-body system: compute, software, grid.]**

**[26:05] [B-CAM] SU:**
The three-body system here is compute architecture, software workloads, and energy infrastructure. Most chip design has historically optimized the first two. Now the third body — the grid, the power supply, the physical infrastructure — is asserting itself as the binding constraint. The engineers who design the most important systems of the next decade will be the ones who can coordinate across all three. Not the ones who can make the fastest chip. The ones who can make the chip that coordinates best with its energy reality.

**[26:35] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And this applies beyond AI.

**[26:40] [B-CAM] SU:**
It applies everywhere computation happens at scale. Cloud computing, telecommunications, autonomous vehicles, edge devices. Energy is a universal constraint. The question is always: how much useful work can you accomplish within a given energy budget? That is a coordination question. It is not a question any single engineering discipline can answer alone. It requires hardware designers, software architects, and infrastructure planners working together. And the companies that can coordinate across those boundaries will define the next generation of computing.

## ACT III: WHAT HARDWARE LEADERSHIP ACTUALLY MEANS NOW

**Duration:** 14 minutes
**Objective:** Su's view of what the hardware layer means for AI civilization — and why coordination at the silicon level matters for everything above it

### Segment 6: Chips Designing Chips [28:00-34:00]

**[28:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You have said that AI will help design better chips. That is a recursive loop — chips making chips. What does that coordination look like?

**[28:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
Let me describe the current chip design process, because the complexity is often underappreciated. A modern processor has billions of transistors. Designing one involves millions of individual decisions about layout, routing, timing, power distribution. The process takes three to five years and costs billions of dollars. And the margin for error is essentially zero, because once you fabricate the silicon, you cannot change it.

**[28:45] [B-CAM] SU:**
Today, most of those decisions are made by a combination of human engineers — who define the high-level architecture and make the strategic trade-offs — and Electronic Design Automation tools, which handle the lower-level optimization problems. The EDA tools are sophisticated, but they are fundamentally rule-based optimizers. They explore the design space methodically, but slowly.

**[29:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And AI changes this?

**[29:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
AI changes the speed and quality of exploration. Take placement and routing — the problem of deciding where on the chip to physically place each functional block and how to wire them together. This is an enormously complex optimization problem. There are more possible placements than atoms in the universe. Traditional EDA tools use heuristics to search that space. They produce good results, but it takes weeks or months.

**[29:45] [B-CAM] SU:**
AI-based approaches — using reinforcement learning, for example — can explore that space differently. They can find placements that the heuristic-based tools would never consider, and they can find them in hours rather than weeks. Google published work on this with chip floorplanning. We and others are pursuing similar approaches. The results are genuinely better — not just faster to achieve, but better in terms of the actual quality of the placement.

**[30:15] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
And the recursive element — better chips enabling better AI enabling better chip design.

**[30:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
That is the coordination feedback loop. Better AI chips allow you to train larger, more capable AI models. Those models become better design tools. Those design tools help you create the next generation of chips faster and with fewer errors. Those chips enable the next generation of AI models. The hardware layer and the intelligence layer are coordinating to co-evolve.

**[30:50] [B-CAM] SU:**
But — and this is important — the loop is not self-sufficient. AI can optimize within a design space that humans define. It can find better solutions to well-posed problems. What it cannot yet do is the architectural insight. The decision to move from monolithic to chiplet architecture — that was not an optimization problem. It was a reframing of the problem. The decision to put CPU and GPU on the same package with unified memory — that was a coordination insight, not an optimization insight.

**[31:20] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
You are drawing a line between optimization and architecture.

**[31:25] [B-CAM] SU:**
I am. AI is very good at optimization. It will get better. It will continue to accelerate our design cycles and improve our designs. But the architectural decisions — what three-body problem to solve next, what coordination to prioritize, what constraints to embrace rather than fight — those still require human judgment. The coordination between human architectural vision and AI optimization capability — that is the productive frontier.

**[31:55] [B-CAM] SU:**
I have said publicly that AI is a great foundational technology, but it is nowhere near as good as it can be. That applies to AI as a design tool as well. We are in the early stages of learning how to coordinate human and machine intelligence in the design process. The companies that figure out that coordination — not just the ones with the best AI tools, but the ones that coordinate human insight and AI capability most effectively — will build the best chips.

**[32:00] [Visual: The chip design feedback loop — AI-assisted layout, faster iteration, better chips, more AI capability, better design tools. The coordination made circular.]**

**[32:25] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
What does this mean for your engineering teams? Are they worried?

**[32:30] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Slight smile] No. And I will tell you why. Because the bottleneck in chip design has never been the tedious optimization work that AI is now beginning to do well. The bottleneck has always been the architectural creativity — the ability to see a coordination problem that nobody else has framed, and to design a system that resolves it. AI makes our engineers more productive by handling the optimization. That frees them to spend more time on the architecture. More time on the part that matters most.

**[33:00] [B-CAM] SU:**
Our best engineers are not the ones who are fastest at layout. They are the ones who see coordination constraints that others miss. AI does not replace that ability. It amplifies it by removing the friction between having an architectural idea and being able to test it in silicon.

### Segment 7: The Hardware Foundation of Everything [34:00-42:00]

**[34:00] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**
Every interview in this book assumes computation as the substrate — the thing everything runs on. You build that substrate. What do you want the people building everything above it to understand about what you are doing?

**[34:15] [B-CAM] SU:**
[Sits back, considers] The first thing I would want them to understand is that good infrastructure is invisible. Nobody thinks about the power grid until it fails. **[34:40] [B-CAM] SU:**

Not necessarily visibly. The AI model does not crash. It is just slow. Or expensive. Or limited in capability. The coordination failure at the hardware level manifests as a ceiling on what can be built above it.

**[35:10] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**

Three things.

**[35:55] [B-CAM] SU:**

Large language model training is different from real-time inference, which is different from edge deployment, which is different from robotics. **[36:30] [A-CAM] INTERVIEWER:**

**[36:35] [B-CAM] SU:**
The physical is not optional. This is the one that I think is most often forgotten by people who work primarily in software. AI runs on physical infrastructure. Chips. Data centers. Power grids. Cooling systems. Water. Copper. Silicon. Every choice about model architecture has a physical consequence. Every choice about training scale has an energy consequence. Every choice about inference latency has a hardware consequence.

**[37:00] [B-CAM] SU:**

The coordination between the virtual — the model, the algorithm, the software — and the physical — the chip, the data center, the grid — is the engineering challenge that does not go away by abstraction. And the quality of that hardware coordination determines the ceiling of what your software can accomplish.

**[37:30] [B-CAM] SU:**
