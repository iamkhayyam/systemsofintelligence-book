### SEGMENT 2: Double Exponential Scaling (3 min)

**[03:20 - 03:25] [A-CAM: Interviewer, shifting to a specific technical achievement.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"You've observed something called 'double exponential scaling'—what you call Neven's Law. Performance doesn't just double, it doubles the doubling. That sounds impossible. Walk us through what this means."

**[03:25 - 04:05] [B-CAM: Neven, excited to explain this phenomenon.]**

**NEVEN:**
"Yes! This is where it gets really fascinating. Classical computing follows Moore's Law—performance roughly doubles every 18-24 months. That's exponential growth: 2, 4, 8, 16, 32... written mathematically as 2^n, where n is time."

**[03:50] [Visual: Graph showing exponential growth curve (Moore's Law) rising steadily.]**

**NEVEN:**
"But in quantum computing, we're seeing double exponential growth—not 2^n but 2^(2^n). So: 2, 4, 16, 256, 65,536... The growth accelerates far faster than classical exponential growth."

**[04:05 - 04:15] [Visual: Second graph showing double exponential curve shooting upward dramatically, dwarfing the exponential curve.]**

**[04:15 - 04:20] [A-CAM: Interviewer, slightly incredulous.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"How is that even possible? What creates this acceleration?"

**[04:20 - 05:15] [B-CAM: Neven, leaning in, this is the key insight.]**

**NEVEN:**
"This is the coordination insight. The double exponential comes from two exponentials multiplying:\n\nFirst exponential: Quantum hardware improves exponentially—better qubits, longer coherence times, lower error rates. That's 2^n.\n\nSecond exponential: Classical algorithms for controlling quantum systems improve exponentially—better error correction, better optimization, better hybrid strategies. That's also 2^n.\n\nWhen you *coordinate* both improvements, you don't just add them—you multiply them. (2^n) × (2^n) = 2^(2n), which is double exponential."

**[04:55] [Visual: Animation showing two separate exponential curves (quantum hardware, classical algorithms) then merging and multiplying into a much steeper double exponential curve.]**

**NEVEN:**
"This only happens when both bodies coordinate. Quantum improvement alone is limited by classical control. Classical improvement alone can't access quantum advantage. But coordinated? You get double exponential—a phase transition in computational capability."

**[05:15 - 05:20] [A-CAM: Interviewer, pressing on the coordination theme.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So the coordination creates capabilities that seem to violate computational theory?"

**[05:20 - 05:35] [B-CAM: Neven, emphatic nod.]**

**NEVEN:**
"Exactly. If you only looked at quantum or classical separately, this growth rate shouldn't be possible. But the coordination between them creates emergence—capabilities that neither has alone."

### SEGMENT 3: The Three Bodies of Quantum Computing (3 min)

**[05:35 - 05:40] [A-CAM: Interviewer, diving into the framework.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Let's break down the three elements you coordinate. First: quantum coherence. What is it and why is it so fragile?"

**[05:40 - 06:30] [B-CAM: Neven, explaining the delicate nature of quantum states.]**

**NEVEN:**
"Quantum coherence is the ability of qubits to maintain their quantum properties—superposition and entanglement. It's incredibly fragile. Any interaction with the environment—heat, vibration, electromagnetic fields—destroys it almost instantly. This is called decoherence."

**[06:05] [Visual: Animation of a pristine quantum state (represented as a delicate, shimmering sphere) being disrupted by environmental noise (represented as chaotic particles), causing it to collapse.]**

**NEVEN:**
"This is why our quantum processors operate at near absolute zero—about minus 273 degrees Celsius, colder than outer space. Even at that temperature, coherence times are only microseconds to milliseconds. It's like trying to build with soap bubbles in a hurricane."

**[06:30 - 06:35] [A-CAM: Interviewer, moving to the second body.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Second element: classical error correction. How does classical computing preserve quantum information?"

**[06:35 - 07:20] [B-CAM: Neven, explaining the coordination between quantum and classical.]**

**NEVEN:**
"This is where coordination becomes critical. Quantum states decohere constantly—errors accumulate. Classical systems monitor those errors and correct them before they destroy the quantum information. It's like having a team of repair workers constantly fixing a building while you're still constructing it."

**[06:55] [Visual: Animation showing quantum errors appearing (small glitches in the quantum state) and classical error correction systems (represented as repair mechanisms) fixing them in real-time.]**

**NEVEN:**
"The beautiful thing is that classical computers are extremely good at this kind of monitoring and correction—they're stable, reliable, fast. So we coordinate quantum fragility with classical stability. The quantum does what it does best—explore superposition. The classical does what it does best—maintain control and correct errors."

**[07:20 - 07:25] [A-CAM: Interviewer, completing the triad.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"And third: hybrid algorithms. What are these and why can't you just run everything on the quantum processor?"

**[07:25 - 08:15] [B-CAM: Neven, explaining the orchestration layer.]**

**NEVEN:**
"Hybrid algorithms are the coordination intelligence. They determine what runs where—which parts of the computation benefit from quantum parallelism, and which parts are better suited for classical processing."

**[07:45] [Visual: Flowchart showing a computation being divided between quantum (represented in blue, glowing) and classical (represented in orange, solid) processing, with hybrid algorithm (green) orchestrating the coordination.]**

**NEVEN:**
"Example: In quantum chemistry simulation, the quantum processor handles the exponentially complex molecular state calculations—that's where quantum superposition gives massive advantage. But the classical computer handles optimization of parameters, analysis of results, and iterative refinement. The hybrid algorithm coordinates both, deciding when to use quantum, when to use classical, and how to integrate the results."

**[08:15 - 08:20] [C-CAM: Neven, summarizing the three-body coordination.]**

**NEVEN:**
"So you have quantum coherence providing the raw computational power, classical error correction preserving it, and hybrid algorithms orchestrating both. Remove any one element and the system fails. Coordinate all three and you get quantum advantage."

**NEVEN:**

**[11:05 - 11:10] [A-CAM: Interviewer, making the connection.]**

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So quantum computing is really about coordination control?"

**[11:10 - 11:50] [B-CAM: Neven, excited by this framing.]**

**NEVEN:**
"Yes! That's a profound insight. We're not just building quantum processors—we're building coordination architectures that control how quantum systems coordinate with classical systems and with their environment. Error correction is about preventing bad coordination. Hybrid algorithms are about enabling good coordination. The entire enterprise is coordination engineering."

**[11:30] [Visual: Diagram showing "Desired Coordination" (quantum ↔ classical control) in green arrows, and "Undesired Coordination" (quantum ↔ environmental noise) in red arrows, with error correction blocking the red arrows.]**

**NEVEN:**
"When coordination is controlled—when we successfully coordinate quantum with classical while preventing coordination with environment—we get quantum computation. When coordination fails—when environment disrupts the quantum-classical coordination—we get decoherence and the computation fails."
