**A NOTE ON THIS INTERVIEW:**
*Professor Lu is the person who named Knowware. In 2005 he published the first formal definition of knowware as an independent, commercialized knowledge module — separable from software, operable by computer, embeddable in hardware and software systems — and argued that hardware, software, and knowware should be considered the three equally important underpinnings of the IT industry. This book exists in the space that paper opened. This interview is both synthesis and acknowledgment. The word belongs to him.*

## ACT I: THE PROBLEM THAT NEEDED A NEW WORD

**Duration:** 12 minutes
**Objective:** Establish why the word was necessary — what gap it filled in 2005, and why that gap had been invisible

### Opening [0:00-3:00]

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Professor Lu, you coined the word knowware in 2005. Before we discuss what it means, I want to ask about the moment you realized a new word was needed. What were you seeing that didn't have a name?"

**LU:**
"I had been working for many years on automatic generation of expert systems — systems that could acquire knowledge from technical books and produce expert consultations. The CONBES project, and later Tianfeng. And I kept encountering the same problem.

The knowledge was in the software. The two things were fused together. If you wanted to use the knowledge in a different application, you had to rewrite the software. If you wanted to update the knowledge, you had to touch the code. If you wanted to sell the knowledge separately — to commercialize it, to make it portable, to let it travel independently — you could not. Software and knowledge were bound to each other in a way that served neither well.

I thought: we separated data from programs a long time ago. That separation — the database, the data warehouse — was one of the great liberations of computing. Why had we not done the same for knowledge? Why was knowledge still imprisoned inside software?

That was the problem. The name came later."

**[02:00] [Visual: The CONBES system architecture — showing knowledge embedded inside software. Then a diagram of the proposed separation: knowledge as independent module.]**

### Segment 1: IT's Third Liberation [3:00-8:00]

**[03:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Your 2005 paper was subtitled 'IT's Third Liberation.' What were the first two liberations, and why was a third one overdue?"

**[03:10] LU:**
"The history of information technology is a history of separations. Each time we separate something that was fused together, an entire industry is born. This has happened twice. I was arguing it needed to happen a third time.

The first liberation was the separation of software from hardware. In the earliest computers, the program was wired into the machine. The ENIAC — you changed the program by reconnecting cables. The logic was physical. If you wanted the machine to do something different, you rebuilt the machine.

Then came stored-program architecture. Von Neumann's insight. The program became something you could write down, store in memory, change without touching the hardware. Software became separable, transportable, an object in its own right. You could sell it. You could improve it independently of the machine it ran on. This separation created the entire software industry."

**[04:30] [Visual: Early computing images — ENIAC with cables, then a transition to punch cards, magnetic tape, a disk with stored programs. The first liberation visualized.]**

**[04:35] INTERVIEWER:**
"And the second liberation?"

**[04:40] LU:**
"The second liberation was the separation of data from programs. In early software, the data was inside the code. If you had a payroll program, the employee names, the salary figures, the tax rates — all written into the program itself. To change a salary, you changed the code. The data and the logic that operated on it were one thing.

Then came the relational database. Codd's insight in the 1970s. Data became independent. You stored it in tables. You queried it with a standard language — SQL. You could update the data without touching the program. You could use the same data with different programs. You could build an entire infrastructure just for managing data.

This second separation created Oracle, the modern enterprise, the entire business intelligence industry. All from one act of separation."

**[05:50] INTERVIEWER:**
"So you saw a third separation waiting to happen."

**[06:00] LU:**
"Yes. And the question was: why had nobody named it? The pattern was clear. First we separated programs from hardware. Then we separated data from programs. The next separation — the obvious next separation — was knowledge from software. But nobody was talking about it.

In 2005, if you had an expert system, the knowledge was inside the software. The inference engine and the knowledge base were technically separate in theory, but in practice they were deployed as one system. You could not take the medical knowledge out of a medical expert system and put it into a different platform without substantial rewriting. You could not sell the knowledge independently of the software that processed it. You could not update the knowledge without risking the stability of the code.

The knowledge was imprisoned in exactly the same way that data had once been imprisoned in programs, and programs had once been imprisoned in hardware. The liberation was overdue. What I realized was that nobody in the software industry was looking at the problem from this angle. Hardware companies think about hardware. Software companies think about software. Nobody was thinking about knowledge as independent infrastructure. That required someone standing outside both — in knowledge engineering — to see the gap."

**[07:30] [Visual: Three panels side by side — the three liberations as structural diagrams. Each showing what was fused, what got separated, and what became possible after separation.]**

### Segment 2: The Definition [8:00-12:00]

**[08:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"So you saw the gap. You saw the pattern. How did you define knowware precisely? Not the intuition — the formal definition."

**[08:10] LU:**
"I was very deliberate about this. A new concept in engineering must be precise, or it becomes a metaphor. And metaphors do not build industries. So I defined knowware with five properties.

First: it is an independent knowledge module. Independent — meaning it does not belong to any particular software system. It exists on its own.

Second: it is commercialized. This was important to me. Knowledge that cannot be bought and sold, licensed and traded, is not infrastructure. It is a research artifact. Knowware must be a commodity.

Third: it is computer-operable. A textbook contains knowledge, but a computer cannot operate on it directly. A human must read it, interpret it, translate it into something the machine can use. Knowware requires no such translation. The machine can process it directly.

Fourth: it is free of any built-in control mechanism. This is the critical distinction from software. A software component contains logic — it tells the computer what to do. Knowware contains knowledge — it tells the computer what is true, what is relevant, what the relationships are. The control remains in the software. The knowledge travels independently.

Fifth: it meets industrial standards and is embeddable in both hardware and software systems. Portable. Interoperable. Verifiable by some authority. Not a one-off creation but a standardized product."

**[09:30] INTERVIEWER:**
"Help me understand what knowware is not. Because I think many people would hear this and think — isn't that just a database? Or an expert system?"

**[09:40] LU:**
"No, and the distinctions are important. A database stores data — facts, figures, records. But data is not knowledge. Data tells you that a patient's temperature is 38.5 degrees. Knowledge tells you what that temperature means in the context of their symptoms, their history, the current literature on their condition. A database stores observations. Knowware stores understanding.

An expert system is closer, but an expert system fuses the knowledge with the inference engine. The knowledge base and the reasoning mechanism are deployed together. You cannot take the knowledge out and use it in a different reasoning framework without significant re-engineering. An expert system is software that contains knowledge. Knowware is knowledge that any software can use.

A document — a textbook, a manual — contains knowledge, but it requires a human to read it. Knowware does not. It is structured for machine processing."

**[10:30] LU:**
"I used an analogy in my book that I still find useful. Consider an MP3 player. The player itself is hardware. The software that runs the player — the interface, the decoder — is software. But the songs are neither hardware nor software. The songs are what the system is for. They are the content, the knowledge, the value that the user actually wants.

Now, if you package those songs in a standard format — MP3 — that package is knowware. It works with any compliant player. It can be bought and sold independently. It has its own lifecycle, its own economy. The music industry already understood this separation. A record label does not need to manufacture its own CD players. The music travels independently of the hardware and software that plays it.

The IT industry had not yet understood this for knowledge. Knowledge was still bound to the software that processed it. I was saying: separate them. Create the standard format. Build the industry."

**[11:30] INTERVIEWER:**
"You also introduced the concept of mixware — the seam where knowware connects to software."

**[11:40] LU:**
"Yes. Mixware is what you get when you take a software component and replace its internal knowledge with a knowware module. The logic stays in the software. The knowledge becomes independent and pluggable. The software becomes a shell — still functional, still providing the control mechanism, but the knowledge it operates on can be swapped, updated, replaced without touching the code. Mixware is the interface between the second star and the third star. It is where the separation becomes operational."

**[12:00] [Visual: The MP3 analogy animated. Player (hardware) + player software (software) + music library (knowware). Three bodies. Each separable. Each with its own market.]**

## ACT II: THE ARCHITECTURE OF THE THIRD STAR

**Duration:** 17 minutes
**Objective:** Walk through Lu's actual architectural proposals — furnace model, crystallization model, spiral model — and connect them to coordination intelligence

### Segment 3: Three Life Cycles [12:00-19:00]

**[12:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Professor Lu, you proposed three life cycle models for knowware engineering — the furnace model, the crystallization model, and the spiral model. These are unusual names for engineering models. Where did they come from?"

**[12:15] LU:**
"They came from the recognition that knowledge is not built the way software is built. Software engineering has the waterfall model, the agile model, Boehm's spiral. These work because software is constructed — you design it, you code it, you test it, you ship it. The metaphors are architectural. You build software.

But knowledge is not built. Knowledge is refined. It is grown. It is accumulated through cycles of use and correction. The lifecycle models had to reflect this different nature. I could not take software engineering models and simply apply them to knowware. The metaphors had to change because the underlying process is different."

**[13:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Start with the furnace model."

**[13:05] LU:**
"The furnace model comes from metallurgy. When you make steel, you do not build it. You take raw ore — heterogeneous, impure, mixed with other materials — and you put it through a furnace. The furnace extracts, purifies, refines. What comes out is something fundamentally different from what went in. The structure has changed. The impurities have been removed. The material is now useful for engineering.

Knowware engineering works the same way. You begin with raw knowledge materials — technical documents, expert interviews, research papers, operational data. This material is heterogeneous, unstructured, often contradictory. It is domain-specific and context-dependent. It is ore, not steel.

The knowware furnace takes this raw material and processes it. Extraction: identifying the knowledge structures within the raw material. Purification: removing contradictions, resolving ambiguities, standardizing representations. Structuring: organizing the knowledge into machine-operable formats that meet industrial standards.

What comes out is a refined knowware module, ready for deployment. And the furnace runs continuously — new knowledge enters as raw material, refined knowware modules emerge. It is a production process, not a construction project."

**[14:30] [Visual: The furnace model animated — raw knowledge documents entering one side, refined knowware modules emerging from the other. The industrial metaphor made explicit.]**

**[14:35] INTERVIEWER:**
"And the crystallization model — that is a different metaphor entirely."

**[14:40] LU:**
"Yes. Crystallization describes a different process for a different situation. In crystallography, a crystal forms around a seed. The seed has a structure — a lattice pattern — and that structure determines what can attach to it and how. As surrounding material is introduced, it organizes itself according to the seed's pattern. The crystal grows. But the growth is not random. The architecture of the seed determines the architecture of the crystal.

For knowware, the crystallization model works like this. You begin with a core definition — a seed structure that captures the essential ontology of the knowledge domain. What are the fundamental concepts? What are the relationships between them? This seed is small but precisely structured.

Then knowledge accretes around it in organized layers. Each layer is consistent with the layers beneath it. The knowware module grows as more knowledge is incorporated, but the growth is disciplined by the original architecture.

This model is particularly useful for domains where the core structure is well understood but the detailed knowledge is vast and evolving. Medical knowledge, for instance. The fundamental anatomy and physiology provide the seed. The constantly evolving clinical literature crystallizes around it. Good architecture enables growth. Poor architecture produces brittleness — the crystal fractures."

**[16:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"The third model is the spiral. This one you adapted from Boehm's software spiral, but with what you called knowware's specific character."

**[16:10] LU:**
"Boehm's spiral model for software is about iterative development with risk management. You plan, you build a prototype, you evaluate, you plan again, each time going further. I adapted this for knowware, but with a crucial difference: knowledge evolves with use.

Software, once deployed, does what it was programmed to do. It does not learn from deployment. But knowledge — real knowledge, operational knowledge — changes when it meets reality. A medical knowledge module deployed in a hospital will encounter cases that test its boundaries. A financial knowledge module deployed in a trading system will encounter market conditions that reveal gaps.

So the knowware spiral works like this. First cycle: deploy the knowware module. Second: observe how it performs in its deployment context — what questions does it answer well, where does it fail, what knowledge is missing. Third: refine the module based on this feedback. Fourth: redeploy. Each cycle produces a more complete, more accurate, more robust knowware module.

The knowledge module learns from its deployment context. Not in the machine learning sense — I am not talking about neural networks adjusting weights. I am talking about knowledge engineers observing how structured knowledge performs in practice and refining it accordingly. The spiral is a coordination between the knowware, the deployment context, and the feedback from use. Three bodies, interacting, producing something none of them could produce alone."

**[17:40] INTERVIEWER:**
"You were describing what this book calls coordination intelligence. In 2005."

**[17:45] LU:**
"I was describing what I saw. The language you use — coordination, three-body dynamics — I did not have that language. But the structure was the same. Knowledge does not exist in isolation. It exists in relationship with the systems that use it and the contexts in which it is used. The spiral model was my way of saying: this relationship is not static. It evolves. And the evolution follows a pattern that can be engineered."

**[18:00] [Visual: Three diagrams side by side — furnace model (linear refinement), crystallization model (nucleation and growth), spiral model (iterative deployment and refinement). Each one a different coordination architecture.]**

### Segment 4: The Tower and the Ladder [19:00-25:00]

**[19:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"You also proposed two development schemas for mixware — the tower model and the ladder model. What is the difference, and why did you need both?"

**[19:15] LU:**
"The tower model and the ladder model address different starting conditions. The question is: how do you integrate knowware into a system? And the answer depends on whether you are building a new system or modifying an existing one.

The tower model is vertical integration. Imagine a tower with three layers. Hardware at the base. Software above it. Knowware at the top. Each layer is built on the layer below it, and each layer fully supports the layer above it. Development proceeds bottom-up: you establish the hardware infrastructure, you build the software layer on top of it, and then you integrate the knowware modules at the top.

The tower is stable because each layer is designed with full awareness of what will sit above it. The software layer knows it will need to interface with knowware modules, so it is designed with the appropriate interfaces from the beginning. This model is appropriate for greenfield systems — when you are starting from nothing and you control all three layers."

**[20:15] INTERVIEWER:**
"And the ladder model is for situations where you don't have that luxury."

**[20:20] LU:**
"Exactly. Most systems in the real world already exist. They have hardware. They have software. They do not have knowware, and they were not designed with knowware in mind. The ladder model addresses this reality.

Imagine a ladder leaning against an existing software system. Each rung of the ladder is a knowware module — a different knowledge domain, a different capability. The software system climbs the ladder by incorporating these modules one at a time. First rung: a basic knowledge module, perhaps a simple domain ontology. Second rung: a more sophisticated module, perhaps incorporating inference rules. Third rung: a comprehensive knowledge service.

The ladder is flexible. You add rungs as needed. You remove them. You replace them. Development proceeds knowledge-first: you identify what knowledge the existing system needs, and you build the ladder accordingly. The system does not need to be redesigned from the ground up. It grows its knowledge capabilities incrementally."

**[21:15] INTERVIEWER:**
"These are two different coordination architectures."

**[21:20] LU:**
"Yes. The tower model is three-body coordination built from the ground up — hardware, software, and knowware designed together. The ladder model is knowware coordinating with existing software, which coordinates with existing deployment contexts. Two different coordination architectures for two different starting conditions. Most of the real world, I should say, is a ladder problem. Very few organizations have the luxury of building towers."

**[21:50] INTERVIEWER:**
"You extended this thinking to web services — knowware delivered over a network."

**[22:00] LU:**
"This was the natural extension. If knowware is independent, portable, and standardized, then it can travel over a network. I proposed knowware-based web services — services that deliver knowledge as a commodity. Any software system can call the service, receive the knowledge module, and use it. The knowledge travels independently over the infrastructure.

In 2005, this was speculative engineering. I was proposing something that did not yet have the infrastructure to support it at scale. The standard knowledge representations were not mature enough. The network bandwidth was not sufficient for large knowledge modules. The market mechanisms did not exist.

But the architecture was sound. A web service that delivers knowledge to any client that requests it. The knowledge maintained centrally, updated continuously, delivered on demand. The client software provides the control mechanism. The service provides the knowledge. They coordinate through a standard interface."

**[23:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Professor Lu, I have to point something out. What you just described — a web service that delivers knowledge to any client, maintained centrally, updated continuously, delivered on demand through a standard interface — that is exactly what a large language model API is. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini. You described this infrastructure in 2005."

**[23:20] LU:**
"I described the architecture. I did not predict the specific technology that would implement it. Large language models achieve something close to what I envisioned, but through a very different mechanism. I imagined structured, formally represented knowledge modules — ontologies, rule bases, verified knowledge packages. What the industry built instead was statistical knowledge — patterns learned from vast corpora, represented as neural network weights.

The architecture is the same. The implementation is different. And I would say — here I am perhaps showing the bias of a knowledge engineer — that the implementation has not yet caught up with the architecture. A large language model delivers knowledge, yes. But can you inspect that knowledge? Can you verify it? Can you separate one domain's knowledge from another? Can you update the medical knowledge without affecting the legal knowledge? These were requirements in my original framework. The current implementations do not fully meet them."

**[24:20] [Visual: Tower model and ladder model diagrams side by side. Then: a modern LLM API call. The knowware-based web service made real, twenty years later.]**

### Segment 5: What Lu's Knowware and This Book's Knowware Share [25:00-32:00]

**[25:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"This brings us to a delicate question. This book uses the word you named — knowware. But it uses it somewhat differently than you defined it. Your knowware is an engineering concept: a portable knowledge module. This book's knowware is a coordination concept: the emergent intelligence between hardware and software. How do you see the relationship?"

**[25:20] LU:**
"I have thought about this carefully, because when someone takes your word and gives it new meaning, you must decide whether to be protective or to be curious. I have chosen to be curious.

My definition is an engineering definition. Knowware as a commodity: separable, portable, standards-compliant. Knowware as infrastructure: the third underpinning of the IT industry. Knowware as a product: something that can be manufactured, bought, sold, licensed. My focus was on making knowledge independently operable. I wanted to build an industry."

**[26:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"And this book's definition?"

**[26:05] LU:**
"This book asks a different question. It asks: what happens when the three stars are in the sky together? Not what each star is individually, but what emerges from their coordination. Your knowware is not a module — it is an emergence. It is what arises when hardware and software coordinate in a way that produces something neither contains alone. Intelligence. Understanding. What you call the third body.

These are different definitions. They operate at different levels of abstraction. Mine is structural. Yours is dynamic. Mine describes what knowware is. Yours describes what knowware does."

**[26:45] INTERVIEWER:**
"Are they compatible?"

**[27:00] LU:**
"They are more than compatible. They are complementary. Think of it this way. I separated knowledge from software so that it could travel independently. This book asks: what happens when it arrives? What does independent knowledge do when it coordinates with hardware and software in a live system? I built the module. You are asking about the emergence from deploying it.

My definition is necessary for yours. You cannot have emergent coordination intelligence without independent knowledge modules that are capable of participating in that coordination. And your definition gives purpose to mine. A knowledge module that sits in a repository is an artifact. A knowledge module that coordinates with hardware and software to produce intelligence — that is alive, in some meaningful sense."

**[27:45] INTERVIEWER:**
"Where do the architectures converge?"

**[27:50] LU:**
"In several places, and this is what gave me confidence that the relationship is genuine, not merely terminological. My concept of mixware — software whose internal knowledge has been replaced by an independent knowware module — is the same thing this book calls a coordination system. The structure is identical. Software provides the logic. Knowledge provides the content. The two coordinate through a defined interface.

My spiral model — deploy the knowware, observe how it performs, refine it, redeploy — is the same thing this book calls three-body coordination. The knowware, the deployment context, and the feedback loop form three interacting elements that produce outcomes none of them could produce alone.

And my knowledge service — knowware delivered over a network to any client that requests it — is the same thing this book calls coordination infrastructure. The word was right. The architecture was right. What was needed, perhaps, was the expansion of the frame."

**[28:50] [Visual: Two columns — Lu's definitions alongside this book's definitions. Side by side. The same structure from two angles. Converging.]**

## ACT III: WHAT THE WORD BECAME — AND WHAT IT STILL COULD

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Objective:** The gap between Lu's vision and current reality — and what this book proposes to do about it

### Segment 6: Why Nobody Noticed [29:00-35:00]

**[29:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"Professor Lu, your 2005 paper and 2007 book were rigorous and original. You published in IEEE Intelligent Systems. You put the book on arXiv. The ideas were accessible. But the concept did not take off the way relational databases or object-oriented programming did. Why not?"

**[29:20] LU:**
"I will give you the honest assessment, because at my age there is no reason for anything else. The idea was ahead of the infrastructure to realize it.

Each of the previous liberations happened when the enabling conditions were in place. The first liberation — software from hardware — happened when hardware was standardized enough to separate from. You needed stored-program architecture, you needed reliable memory, you needed a stable platform on which software could be written and rewritten without hardware changes. Those conditions took years to mature.

The second liberation — data from programs — happened when storage was cheap enough and query languages mature enough to make independent data management practical. Codd published the relational model in 1970. But Oracle did not become an industry force until the mid-1980s. Fifteen years for the infrastructure to catch up with the idea.

The third liberation — knowledge from software — needed three things that did not exist at scale in 2005. First: a standard knowledge representation that was truly portable. We had OWL, we had various ontology languages, but nothing with the ubiquity and simplicity of SQL for data. Second: scalable knowledge processing infrastructure. The computing power to process large knowledge modules in real time. Third: market mechanisms for trading knowledge. You cannot have a knowware industry without a knowledge market."

**[30:40] INTERVIEWER:**
"None of those existed in 2005."

**[30:45] LU:**
"None of them existed at scale. And this is the timing problem that every visionary — if I may use that word without too much self-regard — encounters. The concept was correct. The ecosystem was not ready. I was describing a future that required infrastructure that had not yet been built.

But here is what I find remarkable. In 2024 — twenty years later — all three conditions are substantially met. Not in the form I imagined, but in substance. Large language models are, effectively, knowledge modules at scale. They are portable — the same model can serve any application through an API. And they are commercialized — there is now a market for knowledge services worth billions.

The IT industry had its third liberation. It just did not call it that."

**[31:45] [Visual: Lu's 2005 architecture diagram alongside a 2024 LLM API diagram. The isomorphism is striking. The knowledge module became the model. The knowledge service became the API.]**

**[31:50] INTERVIEWER:**
"So the AI field built your infrastructure without knowing you had named it."

**[32:00] LU:**
"This is the fate of many ideas in science. The person who names a thing is not always the person who builds it. And the person who builds it does not always know that someone named it first. I do not say this with bitterness. I say it as an observation about how ideas travel.

What I would add is that what was built does not yet fully match what I described. My knowware was verifiable — you could inspect it, test it, confirm its accuracy. A large language model is not verifiable in this sense. My knowware was modular — you could update the medical knowledge without affecting the legal knowledge. A large language model is not modular in this sense.

The architecture arrived. The discipline has not yet caught up. And I believe strongly that the next phase of AI development will involve exactly the kind of discipline I was proposing. Separating knowledge from the systems that process it. Making it inspectable, verifiable, modular. This is not a solved problem. It is an open problem that my 2005 framework was designed to address."

**[33:00] [Visual: A timeline — 2005 IEEE paper, 2007 book, then silence, then the explosion of LLMs from 2020 onward. The gap between naming and arrival.]**

### Segment 7: The Invitation [33:00-42:00]

**[33:00] INTERVIEWER:**
"This book takes your word and extends it. The question it asks is not just what knowware is structurally, but what it enables when it coordinates with hardware and software dynamically. You have read the framework. What would you affirm, and what would you correct?"

**[33:15] LU:**
"I affirm the three-body structure. This is what I saw. Hardware, software, and knowledge — three equally important underpinnings. You call them three bodies. I called them three stars. The geometry is the same.

I affirm the independence of knowledge as a layer. This is the liberation. As long as knowledge is fused with software, it cannot participate fully in the coordination you describe. The separation I proposed is a precondition for the emergence you describe.

I affirm the engineering implications. The mixware concept, the lifecycle models, the service architecture — these are not merely historical. They are blueprints. If you want to build the coordination systems this book envisions, you will need to engineer the knowware modules that participate in them. My work tells you how."

**[34:15] INTERVIEWER:**
"And what would you add that you feel is missing?"

**[34:20] LU:**
"Several things. First, the standards problem. My 2005 vision required industrial standards for knowledge representation — a common format, a common interface, a common way to verify that a knowledge module is what it claims to be. We still do not have this. SQL standardized data and made the data industry possible. We need the equivalent for knowledge. Without it, knowware remains artisanal — each module custom-built, each interface bespoke. You cannot have an industry on that basis.

Second, the verification problem. In my framework, knowware should be verified by some authority — tested, certified, validated. Who verifies the knowledge in a large language model? When the model says something about medicine, or law, or engineering, who certifies that this knowledge is accurate? This is not a theoretical problem. It is a safety problem. And my framework — with its emphasis on verifiable, standards-compliant knowledge modules — offers an architecture for solving it.

Third, the watermarking and provenance problem. I foresaw knowledge patents, knowledge provenance — the ability to trace where knowledge came from, who created it, who owns it. The AI industry is still wrestling with this. When a language model generates an answer, whose knowledge is it drawing on? My framework said: knowware modules should have clear provenance, clear ownership, clear licensing. This remains unsolved."

**[35:45] INTERVIEWER:**
"And what would you gently correct about how this book uses your word?"

**[35:55] LU:**
