## Pre-Interview Context

**Who is Philip Rosedale:**
- Founder of Second Life (2003) and High Fidelity (VR social platform)
- Pioneer in virtual worlds and digital presence
- 25+ years building technologies for human connection in virtual spaces
- Advocate for virtual presence as legitimate form of human connection
- Bridge between physical and digital reality through coordination lens

**Why This Interview Matters:**
This interview challenges the binary "virtual vs. real" thinking, showing how both physical and virtual presence coordinate with psychological reality to create genuine human connection. Rosedale's decades building virtual worlds demonstrate that "real" isn't about medium—it's about whether the experience coordinates with human needs for connection, meaning, and belonging.

**Key Themes:**
- Three bodies: physical reality, virtual reality, psychological reality
- Virtual presence as real (not fake)
- Coordination determines "realness," not medium
- Metaverse as coordination opportunity
- Design principles for coordinated presence

### Segment 8: Example - Zoom Fatigue (5 minutes)
**[27:00]**
**PHILIP ROSEDALE:** "To make this even more concrete, let's look at a pervasive modern example of coordination failure: Zoom fatigue."

**[27:10]**
*[C-CAM: Visuals: Zoom fatigue research data, charts showing increased exhaustion from video calls. Montage of people looking tired, stressed, or disengaged during video calls.]*

**PHILIP ROSEDALE:** "The phenomenon is well-known: video calls feel draining, often more exhausting than physical meetings, leading many to avoid video altogether. The common, simplistic explanation is, 'Virtual isn't as good as physical.' But that's fundamentally wrong. It's not about the medium being inherently inferior; it's about poor coordination."

**[27:50]**
*[C-CAM: Graphic: "ZOOM COORDINATION FAILURE ANALYSIS." Break down the points visually.]*

**PHILIP ROSEDALE:** "Video calls, as currently designed, fail to coordinate with our natural human interaction patterns in several critical ways. First, **natural interaction rhythms**."

**PHILIP ROSEDALE:** "In physical conversations, we have natural turn-taking, subtle eye contact signals, and body language cues that allow for smooth, effortless coordination. This creates a constant, low-level cognitive load as our brains struggle to manage these broken interaction rhythms."

**[28:50]**
