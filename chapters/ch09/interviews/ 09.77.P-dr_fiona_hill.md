## ACT I: WHAT THE CHIP WAR IS ACTUALLY ABOUT

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Objective:** Establish the semiconductor geopolitical conflict as a three-body coordination failure — not a bilateral US-China competition

### Opening [0:00-3:00]

**(0:45) INTERVIEWER:** "Dr. Hill, the conventional framing of the semiconductor conflict is US versus China — a bilateral technology competition. You've pushed back on that framing. Why is it wrong?"

**(0:52) HILL:** "Because it treats a three-body problem as a two-body problem. And three-body problems don't respond to two-body solutions.

You have the United States, with its design capabilities, its chip companies, its export control apparatus. You have China, with its manufacturing ambitions, its domestic market, its state investment programs. That's the framing everyone uses.

But then there's Taiwan. Ninety percent of the world's most advanced chips. One company — TSMC — producing the semiconductors that every AI system, every smartphone, every military system depends on. Taiwan is not a pawn in a US-China game. Taiwan is the third body whose position determines the stability of the entire system.

Remove Taiwan from the equation and you don't have a bilateral competition with a clear winner. You have a chaotic three-body system where the outcome is genuinely unpredictable. That's the problem we actually face. And almost nobody in Washington is thinking about it that way."

**[02:00] [Visual: The three-body geopolitical diagram — US, China, Taiwan as three bodies, with arrows showing the mutual dependencies and tensions.]**

**(2:05) INTERVIEWER:** "That's a striking claim — that Washington isn't thinking in three-body terms. You've been in those rooms. What do you see instead?"

**(2:12) HILL:** "Binary thinking. Hawks and doves. Containment or engagement. Confrontation or accommodation. These are all two-body frames. They assume you pick a stance relative to one adversary and everything else is a supporting variable. But the semiconductor supply chain doesn't work that way. It's a web of mutual dependencies that spans a dozen sovereign actors, each with their own agency, and the moment you try to reduce it to us-versus-them, you start making decisions that produce cascading effects you never anticipated."

### Segment 1: The Supply Chain as Coordination Architecture [3:00-7:00]

**(3:00) INTERVIEWER:** "The global semiconductor supply chain spans dozens of countries and companies. Walk us through why it's structured the way it is — and what that structure reveals about coordination."

**(3:08) HILL:** "Right. So let's map this properly. Design — that's primarily American. AMD, Nvidia, Qualcomm, Apple. Intel straddles design and manufacturing. These companies produce the architectures, the intellectual property, the blueprints for what gets built.

Then equipment. This is where it gets extraordinary. The machines required to manufacture advanced chips are some of the most complex objects human beings have ever built. ASML in the Netherlands makes the extreme ultraviolet lithography machines. Tokyo Electron and Shin-Etsu in Japan supply critical components. These are not fungible. You cannot substitute them.

Manufacturing — Taiwan, overwhelmingly. TSMC is the centre of gravity. Samsung and SK Hynix in South Korea play significant roles but are not at the same leading edge for logic chips. Then assembly and testing happen largely in Southeast Asia — Malaysia, Vietnam, Thailand. And raw materials are globally distributed.

Now, the critical thing to understand is that this structure was not planned. Nobody sat down and designed it. It evolved over decades through comparative advantage, through trust relationships, through long-term contracts and shared technical standards. Each node became the best in the world at its particular function. And together they created something remarkable — coordination intelligence at civilisational scale."

**(4:25) INTERVIEWER:** "You call it coordination intelligence. What does that mean in this context?"

**(4:30) HILL:** "It means that no single country controls the entire chain. Every country is dependent on every other country. The United States designs the chips but cannot manufacture them at leading edge without Taiwan. Taiwan manufactures but cannot do so without Dutch lithography equipment and Japanese chemicals. The Netherlands builds the machines but requires components from five thousand suppliers across forty countries. This is a system where everyone needs everyone else. That mutual dependency is itself a form of intelligence — it creates incentives for cooperation, for maintaining relationships, for investing in shared standards."

**(5:05) INTERVIEWER:** "But you've also argued this creates extraordinary fragility."

**(5:10) HILL:** "Precisely. Because the optimisation produced a highly efficient system, it also produced a highly concentrated one. ASML is the only company in the world — the *only* company — that can make the extreme ultraviolet lithography machines required for advanced chips. One company. In the Netherlands. Producing machines that require components from five thousand suppliers across forty countries. Each machine costs upwards of three hundred million dollars and takes months to assemble.

And TSMC — one company, one island, producing ninety percent of the world's most advanced chips. The supply chain optimised for efficiency and, in doing so, concentrated risk at critical nodes. This is what happens when you optimise two bodies — efficiency and cost — and ignore the third: resilience and geopolitical stability."

**[05:30] [Visual: The supply chain map again — but now with concentration indicators. The nodes where a single point of failure could cascade globally.]**

**(5:40) INTERVIEWER:** "The CHIPS Act was supposed to address this. Is it working?"

**(5:45) HILL:** "The CHIPS Act is a recognition — a belated recognition — that the supply chain is a coordination vulnerability. It commits substantial US investment to reshoring semiconductor manufacturing. And I give it credit for that. The diagnosis is correct.

But here is the challenge: you cannot recreate sixty years of coordinated expertise in five years. TSMC's Arizona fab — yes, the equipment is there, the buildings are there, the engineers are being trained. But the ecosystem — the thousands of suppliers, the institutional knowledge, the coordinated industrial culture — that cannot be imported. It cannot be purchased. It was built through generations of accumulated practice and relationship.

I've said this before and I'll say it again: you cannot lay down forty billion dollars and buy your way to what Taiwan has built over three generations. Money buys equipment. It does not buy an ecosystem. An ecosystem is a coordination achievement, and coordination takes time."

### Segment 2: Export Controls as Coordination Disruption [7:00-10:00]

**(7:00) INTERVIEWER:** "The US has imposed sweeping export controls on advanced semiconductors and chip-making equipment to China. Is this a coordination strategy or a coordination disruption?"

**(7:08) HILL:** "It is intended as a coordination strategy. It operates as a coordination disruption. And the gap between those two things is where the problem lives.

Let me walk through the logic. The controls restrict the sale of advanced chips above certain performance thresholds to China. They restrict the sale of chip-making equipment — especially those ASML EUV machines — to China. They restrict US persons from supporting advanced chip manufacturing in China. The intent is to freeze China at a certain capability level. To maintain the technology gap."

**(7:42) INTERVIEWER:** "And the logic seems straightforward — deny China access to the best tools."

**(7:47) HILL:** "It does seem straightforward, and that's the problem. It's two-body logic. US capability — call it A — versus China's capability — call it B. Widen the gap. Deny China access to A's best tools. This is optimisation thinking: control the bilateral balance.

But here's where the third body enters. The export controls require coordination from the Netherlands, from Japan, from South Korea, from Taiwan. Each of those allies has its own economic interests in the Chinese market. ASML was selling equipment to China. Tokyo Electron was selling equipment to China. These were significant revenue streams. Each ally has its own geopolitical calculations, its own domestic politics, its own industrial priorities.

The export controls work only if the coalition holds. And the coalition — that's the third body. It is under constant strain."

**(8:35) INTERVIEWER:** "And how has China responded to these controls?"

**(8:38) HILL:** "Exactly as a three-body analysis would predict — by routing around the two-body logic. China has poured massive domestic investment into semiconductor manufacturing. SMIC achieved seven-nanometre chips using older equipment through remarkably creative engineering. Huawei's Kirin 9000S was a domestic advanced chip that genuinely surprised Western analysts. China is not frozen. It is finding paths around the constraints.

And this is the deeper problem. Export controls can slow China's access to leading-edge chips. They cannot slow China's accumulation of mature-node chips, which still run most of the world's electronics. They cannot prevent China from developing its own ecosystem over time. And — this is crucial — they create pressure for China to accelerate exactly what the controls are trying to prevent. You impose controls to slow indigenous development, and instead you provide the political justification and the strategic urgency for precisely that indigenous development.

This is the classic three-body dynamic: a two-body intervention creates unpredictable cascade effects in the third body. Every time."

## ACT II: THE THREE-BODY GEOPOLITICAL COORDINATION PROBLEM

**Duration:** 18 minutes
**Objective:** The specific coordination failures Hill has observed — from Russia to the NSC to technology policy — and the pattern they reveal

### Segment 3: What Putin Taught Her About Coordination [10:00-16:00]

**(10:00) INTERVIEWER:** "You spent decades studying Putin and Russian foreign policy. What did that teach you about coordination failure at the geopolitical level?"

**(10:08) HILL:** "Putin is the purest two-body thinker I have ever studied. His worldview is structured around a bilateral contest between Russia and the West. Every negotiation is zero-sum. When he perceives a Russian gain, he assumes a Western loss — and vice versa. There is no conceptual space in his framework for mutual benefit, for positive-sum coordination. It simply does not exist in his mental architecture.

Now, in some ways this makes him a predictable negotiating partner. You know his frame. You know his assumptions. You can anticipate his moves within that frame. But it also makes him a catastrophically bad actor when the situation involves a third body — because he literally cannot see it."

**(10:55) INTERVIEWER:** "Ukraine being the most devastating example."

**(11:00) HILL:** "The most devastating example. Putin's calculation in February 2022 was pure two-body logic. Russia — body A — invades Ukraine — body B. Expected outcome: Ukraine collapses in days. Kyiv falls. NATO fractures under the shock. The West accepts the fait accompli because the cost of reversing it is too high.

He had his intelligence services, his military planners, his political advisers — all of them feeding him a two-body model. Russia is strong, Ukraine is weak. The variable is Western reaction, and Western reaction will be divided and slow.

But the third body he excluded — Ukrainian national identity and will to resist — was the body that determined everything. Ukraine was not a passive object sitting between Russia and the West. Ukraine was an active coordination system with its own agency, its own military culture that had been quietly professionalising since 2014, its own democratic identity that had deepened through two revolutions. The three-body system behaved nothing like the two-body model predicted. The war's trajectory was determined by the body Putin excluded from his calculations."

**(12:10) INTERVIEWER:** "Wasn't that an intelligence failure as much as an analytical one?"

**(12:15) HILL:** "It was both, and they're related. Good intelligence would have told Putin that Ukrainian coordination capacity was strong — that the military had reformed, that national identity had hardened, that the population would fight. But the intelligence services told him what he wanted to hear. They told him Ukraine was weak because that's what the boss wanted to hear.

And this is a coordination failure within the decision architecture itself. When an organisation optimises for pleasing the leader rather than for coordinating with reality, the third body — objective assessment — gets removed from the system. The intelligence apparatus ceases to function as a reality-testing mechanism and becomes a confirmation mechanism. The result is always the same: catastrophic miscalculation."

**(12:55) INTERVIEWER:** "You've said this pattern recurs across major geopolitical failures. Can you trace it?"

**(13:00) HILL:** "I've observed this pattern in every major geopolitical miscalculation I have studied. Leaders who think in two bodies consistently mispredict outcomes that are determined by third bodies.

The Korean War. MacArthur's two-body calculation — UN forces versus North Korean forces — excluded China. He pushed north to the Yalu River. China entered the war. Three hundred thousand Chinese troops crossing the border was the third body his model had no room for.

The Iraq War. The two-body calculation was US military capability versus Iraqi military capability. That equation resolved quickly. Baghdad fell in three weeks. But the sectarian coordination dynamics — Sunni, Shia, Kurdish — were the third body. They determined everything that happened for the next decade.

Western sanctions against Russia after 2014 and again after 2022. The two-body calculation: sanctions pressure versus Russian economic resilience. What it underestimated was Russia's coordination with non-Western partners — China, India, Turkey, the Gulf states — who were willing to keep buying Russian energy, keep selling Russian goods, keep providing financial channels. The third body — the non-Western economic order — absorbed the shock the two-body model said would be crippling.

And the semiconductor export controls we just discussed. Same pattern. Same structural blindness."

**[14:00] [Visual: A timeline of major geopolitical miscalculations — each one annotated with the third body that was ignored. The pattern is visible.]**

**(14:15) INTERVIEWER:** "Is this a problem of intelligence, or of the conceptual frameworks leaders use to interpret intelligence?"

**(14:22) HILL:** "Frameworks. Always frameworks. Intelligence agencies often have the data. What they lack — what their principals lack — is a conceptual architecture that can accommodate three-body dynamics. The data about Chinese troop movements was available to MacArthur. The data about Iraqi sectarianism was available before the invasion. The data about Ukrainian military reform was available to Putin. In each case, the framework could not accommodate the data. The framework said two bodies. The data said three. The framework won, and the outcome was determined by the body the framework excluded.

This is why I find the three-body problem metaphor so useful. It's not just an analogy. It names the structural error. Two-body models are solvable. They give you clean predictions. They're comforting. Three-body systems are chaotic. They resist prediction. They require you to hold uncertainty. And leaders — particularly leaders under political pressure — reach for the solvable model every time."

### Segment 4: The NSC and the Coordination Architecture of US Foreign Policy [16:00-22:00]

**(16:00) INTERVIEWER:** "You served on the National Security Council. The NSC is supposed to coordinate US foreign policy across agencies. How well does it actually coordinate?"

**(16:08) HILL:** "The NSC was designed to do exactly what we've been discussing — to be the coordination layer. It's supposed to integrate foreign policy across State, Defence, CIA, Treasury, Commerce, and the White House. Each agency has its equities, its expertise, its bureaucratic interests. The NSC process — the Deputies Committee, the Principals Committee — is meant to take those separate inputs and produce coherent, integrated policy.

In theory, it's three-body coordination at institutional scale. Each agency is a body with its own gravitational pull. The NSC is the mechanism that keeps them in productive orbit rather than collision.

In practice — and I can only speak from my own experience — it often collapses into two-body optimisation."

**(16:55) INTERVIEWER:** "What does that collapse look like from inside?"

**(17:00) HILL:** "Each agency optimises for its own equities. State optimises for diplomatic relationships — it wants to maintain access, maintain dialogue, maintain the fabric of alliance. Defence optimises for military capability and deterrence — it wants credible threats and force posture. CIA optimises for intelligence collection and covert action. Treasury and Commerce optimise for economic tools and market access.

Each one is running its own A-B relationship. State's relationship with an ally. Defence's deterrence calculation against an adversary. Treasury's sanctions pressure on a target.

The NSC is supposed to be the C — the coordination layer that integrates all of these into something coherent. When it works — and I've seen it work — you get genuine integration. Emergent policy that no single agency would have produced on its own. Policy that surprises everyone, including its authors, with its effectiveness. Those are moments of genuine three-body coordination.

But when it doesn't work — and this is more common than it should be — what you get is each agency pursuing its own two-body optimisation, and the NSC rubber-stamping whichever voice is loudest or whichever agency has the most bureaucratic weight in that particular administration."

**(18:05) INTERVIEWER:** "Can you give a concrete example? The semiconductor policy, perhaps?"

**(18:10) HILL:** "Semiconductor policy is an excellent example. Commerce wants to protect US economic interests and, critically, wants to protect allies' market access — because allies with healthy economies are better allies. Defence wants to deny China military capability — full stop, as aggressively as possible. State wants to maintain alliance relationships, because those alliances are the foundation of everything else.

The NSC should coordinate these into a coherent three-body policy. What often happened — and I'm speaking generally about the pattern, not revealing classified deliberations — is that the strongest voice won. In some administrations, Defence dominated. In others, Commerce. The third body — allied coordination — was treated as an afterthought. Something to be managed rather than consulted.

ASML found out about export control decisions from news reports, not from prior consultation. Think about what that communicates. You're asking the Netherlands to sacrifice significant revenue from Chinese sales, to enforce controls that will hurt their most important company — and you didn't bother to consult them before announcing it. When allies feel like objects rather than participants, the coalition frays. And the coalition is the only thing that makes the controls work."

**(19:15) INTERVIEWER:** "What does effective NSC coordination look like when it works?"

**(19:20) HILL:** "When it works, it's genuinely impressive. Agencies bring their positions. The NSC process — properly run — forces genuine integration. State says, 'This will damage the alliance.' Defence says, 'We cannot afford the capability gap.' Treasury says, 'The economic costs are here.' And the process produces a policy that accounts for all three dimensions.

The result is a policy that none of the agencies would have written alone. Something that is simultaneously strategically sound, diplomatically viable, and economically sustainable. That's emergence from coordination. The policy is more than the sum of its parts.

Those moments are rarer than they should be, but they happen. And when they do, American foreign policy is extraordinarily effective — because it has the institutional depth and the alliance structure to execute on genuinely integrated policy. The machinery exists. The question is whether the political leadership allows the machinery to function."

### Segment 5: The Taiwan Question as Three-Body Problem [22:00-26:00]

**(22:00) INTERVIEWER:** "Taiwan is simultaneously the world's most important chip manufacturer and the most dangerous geopolitical flashpoint. How do you think about the coordination architecture for managing that?"

**(22:10) HILL:** "Start with the official frame. The United States has maintained a policy of strategic ambiguity since 1979. We neither confirm nor deny that we will defend Taiwan militarily. China claims Taiwan as sovereign territory. The United States recognises there is one China while supporting Taiwan's de facto independence through arms sales, diplomatic engagement, and economic ties.

This is deliberate two-body ambiguity. It's designed to deter both sides — deter China from invading and deter Taiwan from a formal independence declaration that would provoke invasion. And for decades it worked. It was an elegant piece of diplomatic architecture."

**(22:55) INTERVIEWER:** "But you've argued strategic ambiguity is a two-body solution to a three-body problem."

**(23:00) HILL:** "Precisely. The frame is: the United States — body A — managing its relationship with China — body B — over Taiwan. Taiwan is the object of policy rather than a participant in it. But Taiwan is twenty-three million people with a democratic government, a world-class technology industry, and increasingly strong national identity. Taiwan's agency — its own choices about its future, its own democratic politics, its own strategic calculations — is the third body.

Strategic ambiguity works only if Taiwan doesn't make choices that force the American hand. And Taiwan increasingly has reasons to make those choices. Democratic deepening, generational shift — younger Taiwanese identify overwhelmingly as Taiwanese rather than Chinese — and the growing awareness that their semiconductor capability gives them leverage they didn't have before."

**(23:50) INTERVIEWER:** "The silicon shield argument."

**(23:53) HILL:** "Yes. TSMC's concentration is not just a military vulnerability — it is a coordination leverage point. Taiwan's semiconductor capability gives it a silicon shield: both sides need TSMC to survive. China cannot invade Taiwan without destroying the very capability it wants to acquire. You cannot seize a semiconductor fab intact. The precision required, the institutional knowledge, the supply chain dependencies — all of it is destroyed by conflict. And the United States cannot abandon Taiwan without losing ninety percent of its advanced chip supply.

This creates a three-body stability — but a fragile one. The stability depends on all three parties maintaining their current positions and calculations. Any disruption — military conflict, natural disaster, political crisis, even a shift in one party's risk calculus — triggers cascade effects the global economy cannot absorb. We saw a taste of this during the pandemic chip shortage, and that was a disruption of months. A Taiwan Strait crisis would be a disruption of years."

**(24:45) INTERVIEWER:** "So what does coordination intelligence look like here? What would you actually advocate?"

**(24:50) HILL:** "Not containment — that's two-body: US versus China. Not pure engagement — that's also two-body: US plus China versus shared problems. Genuine three-body coordination: the United States, China, and Taiwan managing shared interests in a way that treats all three as active participants.

The semiconductor supply chain is itself a coordination incentive. Everyone needs it to keep working. That shared dependency is an asset, not just a vulnerability. The CHIPS Act is partial diversification — reducing the single-point-of-failure risk, which is sensible. But it must be paired with diplomatic engagement that treats Taiwan as an active participant, not an object to be managed or defended.

The answer to the Taiwan question is not military deterrence alone. Deterrence is necessary, but insufficient. The answer is building coordination interests strong enough that conflict becomes irrational for everyone. That means economic interdependence, diplomatic inclusion, institutional integration — making Taiwan's participation in the global system so deep and so valuable that disrupting it becomes unthinkable. That's what coordination intelligence looks like in practice."

## ACT III: WHAT COORDINATION FAILURE LOOKS LIKE FROM INSIDE THE ROOM

**Duration:** 14 minutes
**Objective:** Hill's personal witness to coordination failure — and what she believes genuine coordination intelligence in foreign policy would require

### Segment 6: The Impeachment Testimony and What It Revealed [26:00-32:00]

**(26:00) INTERVIEWER:** "Your impeachment testimony in 2019 was notable for its precision and its explicit rejection of conspiracy theories about Ukraine. You were describing a coordination failure in real time. What were you seeing?"

**(26:10) HILL:** "What I was seeing — and what I tried to communicate as clearly as I could in that hearing room — was a US foreign policy toward Ukraine being run on two tracks simultaneously. And the two tracks were not coordinating with each other.

The official track was the established framework. The State Department, the National Security Council, the interagency process. This track had clear objectives: support Ukrainian sovereignty, support Ukrainian reform, maintain the alliance framework, deter Russian aggression. These were bipartisan objectives supported by decades of policy continuity.

The unofficial track was a parallel foreign policy with different objectives entirely. I won't relitigate the specifics — the record is public and extensive. What matters for our discussion is the structural pattern: two tracks, two sets of objectives, no coordination between them."

**(27:05) INTERVIEWER:** "And what was the coordination failure, specifically?"

**(27:10) HILL:** "The three-body failure was this: US national security interests — body A — and Ukraine's security — body B — and the domestic political context — body C. The official track was coordinating A and B. That's what the interagency process is designed to do. The unofficial track was optimising C — domestic political benefit — without coordinating with A or B.

The result was contradictory signals. Ukraine was receiving one message from the State Department and NSC and a different message from the unofficial channel. Russia was watching both tracks and drawing its own conclusions — and Russia is very good at reading coordination failures as opportunity. Our allies were confused. The Ukrainians were confused. The interagency professionals were confused.

And the cost was real. It was not abstract. Every contradictory signal weakened the deterrence framework that was holding. Every month of frozen security assistance — while the unofficial track operated — was a month that Ukraine's military readiness eroded. These are not theoretical costs."

**(28:15) INTERVIEWER:** "You've said the domestic political dimension is legitimate — that it can't simply be excluded."

**(28:20) HILL:** "It is legitimate, and this is important. I'm not arguing that domestic politics should be absent from foreign policy. That's naive and it's also impossible. Every president, every administration, operates within a domestic political context. Electoral pressures, Congressional dynamics, public opinion — these are real constraints and real inputs.

The failure is not that domestic politics existed. The failure is that it was optimised independently rather than integrated. What coordination intelligence would have looked like — what it should have looked like — is not two tracks, but one track that genuinely integrated A, B, and C. A policy toward Ukraine that served national security interests, supported Ukrainian sovereignty, and was politically sustainable at home.

When C is optimised while A and B are ignored, you get exactly what happened: a policy that achieved none of its stated objectives and weakened the coordination architecture it depended on. The alliance was damaged. The deterrence was damaged. And the domestic political benefit turned out to be illusory anyway — it produced an impeachment."

**(29:20) INTERVIEWER:** "Is this pattern unique to that episode, or have you seen it elsewhere?"

**(29:25) HILL:** "Every major US foreign policy failure I have observed follows the same pattern. Multiple actors optimising their own two-body relationships. Ignoring the coordination between them. The third body — whether it's an ally's trust, a country's agency, the overall coalition's coherence, or the domestic political sustainability of the policy — asserting itself as failure.

Vietnam. Iraq. The response to the Arab Spring. The withdrawal from Afghanistan. In each case, you can identify the third body that was excluded from the coordination architecture. And in each case, it was the excluded body that determined the outcome."

### Segment 7: What Coordination Intelligence in Foreign Policy Requires [32:00-42:00]

**(32:00) INTERVIEWER:** "If you were designing the foreign policy architecture for managing the AI and semiconductor geopolitical challenge — what would genuine coordination intelligence look like?"

**(32:10) HILL:** "I'd start with four principles. They sound straightforward. Implementing them is extraordinarily difficult. But the framework has to be right before the implementation can be right.

First principle: include all three bodies. US semiconductor and AI policy involves US interests — body A — Chinese competition — body B — and allied coordination — body C. The third body cannot be an afterthought. It cannot be the phone call you make after the press conference. Every export control decision should be preceded by allied consultation, not followed by it.

The coalition is not a multiplier of US power. I need to say that again because it's the conceptual error I see most often. The coalition is not a multiplier of US power. It is a participant in the coordination. It has its own interests, its own agency, its own democratic constraints. Treat it like one. Consult before you announce. Negotiate before you impose. Build consensus before you demand compliance."

**(33:15) INTERVIEWER:** "And Taiwan specifically?"

**(33:18) HILL:** "Second principle: Taiwan as participant, not object. Foreign policy that treats Taiwan as an object to be managed produces fragile stability. Foreign policy that treats Taiwan as a coordination participant produces durable stability.

This means genuinely consulting Taiwan on semiconductor policy — not informing them of decisions already made. This means treating TSMC's decisions as sovereign industrial policy, not as chips on a geopolitical board. The stability of the supply chain depends on Taiwan's willingness to maintain it. And that willingness depends on Taiwan feeling like a partner rather than a pawn. If Taiwan concludes that the United States views it purely as an asset to be defended or denied to China rather than as a sovereign partner, the coordination architecture weakens from within."

**(34:05) INTERVIEWER:** "Your third principle?"

**(34:08) HILL:** "The domestic third body. Every foreign policy has a domestic political context, and the coordination failure happens when domestic politics is optimised independently rather than integrated.

The CHIPS Act is actually an example of getting this right — or at least approaching it correctly. It's domestic industrial policy: jobs, manufacturing, economic revitalisation of communities that lost their industrial base. It's simultaneously national security policy: supply chain resilience, reducing the Taiwan concentration risk. And it's alliance policy: reshoring without abandoning allies, building complementary capacity rather than zero-sum competition with allied manufacturers.

Not perfect. But genuinely three-body in design. It integrates domestic politics, national security, and alliance management into a single policy framework. That's rare, and it's worth recognising as a model."

**(34:55) INTERVIEWER:** "And the fourth principle — you've written extensively about this — deindustrialisation."

**(35:00) HILL:** "Deindustrialisation as coordination failure. My book *There Is Nothing For You Here* argues that the gutting of industrial communities — in the United States, in Britain, in Russia — is itself a three-body coordination failure. Technology — body A — and capital efficiency — body B — were optimised without coordinating with community resilience — body C.

I grew up in a mining village in County Durham, in the northeast of England. I watched this happen. The mines closed. The community's economic purpose disappeared. And what replaced it was not a new economic purpose — it was nothing. Or worse than nothing: it was dependency, it was loss of identity, it was communities becoming available for political coordination by movements that offered identity in place of opportunity.

The same thing happened across the American Rust Belt. The same thing happened in post-Soviet Russia, at national scale — the collapse of the Soviet system destroyed not just an economy but a coordination architecture that millions of people depended on for their daily lives, their identities, their sense of purpose."

**(36:00) INTERVIEWER:** "And you see AI replicating this pattern."

**(36:05) HILL:** "At larger scale and higher speed. AI will create the largest wave of labour market disruption since industrialisation. The coordination challenge is AI capability — body A — economic growth — body B — and social and political stability — body C.

If C is not in the coordination architecture from the beginning — not added later as an afterthought, not addressed through retraining programmes designed after the displacement has already happened — we get exactly what deindustrialisation produced. Enormous efficiency gains. Enormous concentrations of wealth. Enormous communities of people whose coordination with the economic system has been severed.

And we know what happens then. Because we've seen it. Those communities don't just quietly accept their obsolescence. They find political expression. They find leaders who name their anger even if those leaders have no solutions. They vote for disruption because disruption is at least *something* in a world that has offered them nothing.

The semiconductor reshoring is partly an attempt to repair this — to rebuild the third body that deindustrialisation destroyed. New fabs in Ohio, in Arizona, in New York. These are not just national security investments. They are attempts to reconnect communities to the economic system. To give people in those places a role in the coordination architecture rather than leaving them outside it."

**(37:15) INTERVIEWER:** "The geopolitics of AI, then — you've argued this isn't really about which country wins."

**(37:20) HILL:** "It is not about which country wins. That's the two-body frame again — US versus China, who gets to advanced AI first, who dominates. The actual question, the three-body question, is whether any country manages the transition without tearing its social fabric apart.

China faces this challenge too. China's industrial communities are also vulnerable. China's political stability depends on continued economic growth and employment. If AI disrupts Chinese manufacturing at scale — and it will — China faces the same third-body problem we do, filtered through a different political system.

The countries that will navigate this successfully are not the ones with the most advanced AI. They are the ones that build the coordination architectures — the institutional, political, economic architectures — that integrate technological change with social stability. That's three-body work. It's harder than building better models. And it's more important."

**(38:10) INTERVIEWER:** "We're approaching the end. I want to give you space for a final reflection. You've seen coordination failure from more vantage points than almost anyone — from County Durham to the Kremlin to the NSC to the semiconductor supply chain. What's the through-line?"

**(38:25) HILL:** "The through-line is always the same.

I grew up in a mining village. I watched deindustrialisation take the coordination out of my community — the economic coordination, the social coordination, the sense that we were part of something that worked and that needed us. The mines weren't just jobs. They were the organising principle. When they closed, what collapsed was not just employment but the entire architecture of mutual dependency that held the community together.

I studied Russia for decades and watched the same thing happen at national scale. The Soviet system was brutal and inefficient, but it was a coordination architecture that two hundred and fifty million people had built their lives around. When it collapsed, what replaced it was not freedom — it was chaos. And from that chaos came Putin, who offered a different kind of coordination: authoritarian, militarised, zero-sum.

I served in the NSC and watched coordination failures in real time. Agencies optimising independently. Allies treated as afterthoughts. The domestic political dimension hijacking the interagency process. Good people with good intentions producing bad outcomes because the coordination architecture was broken."

**(39:40) HILL:** "And now I watch the semiconductor and AI challenge from Brookings and I see the same pattern reasserting itself. Two-body thinking applied to three-body problems. Export controls without allied consultation. Industrial policy without social policy. Technology competition without coordination architecture.

The common thread is always the same: someone optimised two bodies and forgot the third. The third always comes back. It always has a cost. The question is whether you pay it in advance — through coordination, through inclusion, through the hard slow work of building systems that account for all the bodies in the system — or whether you pay it afterward, through catastrophe."

**(40:20) INTERVIEWER:** "Which are we going to get?"

**(40:23) HILL:** "I'm a pessimist by training and an optimist by temperament. I've seen enough coordination failures to know how common they are. But I've also seen enough moments of genuine coordination — the CHIPS Act, certain alliance frameworks, certain moments in the NSC process — to know that it's possible. The machinery exists. The institutional knowledge exists. The question is always political will.

The three-body problem in physics has no general analytical solution. But it can be managed numerically, iteratively, through constant recalculation and adjustment. That's what coordination intelligence looks like in practice. Not a grand strategy that solves everything. Constant adjustment. Constant inclusion of all the bodies. Constant attention to the third body that the optimisers want to ignore.

It's not elegant. It's not satisfying. But it's what works. And given the stakes — given what AI and semiconductors will do to the global order over the next decade — it had better be what we do."

- When it works: genuine agency integration producing emergent policy

- Technology + capital efficiency optimised without community resilience
- The third body (community cohesion, economic purpose) severed
- The political consequences: populist movements fill the coordination vacuum
- AI risks replicating this at larger scale and higher speed
